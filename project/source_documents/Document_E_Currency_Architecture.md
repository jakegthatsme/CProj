The seventh document of the original project arc, *Minds, Markets, and the Sacred*, developed the analytical-philosophical apparatus on sacred values, incommensurability, and the moral limits of markets that the present document's Part One extends from the diagnostic recognition that the universal-fungibility regime is structurally inadequate to the architectural commitment that the constitutional response is the architectural separation of public-good provision from the universal-fungibility regime. The ninth document, *The Political Economy of Transformative Legislation*, developed the broader political-economy architecture within which the present document's currency-architectural reconstruction operates. The present document's currency architecture is the load-bearing companion to Document D's constitutional architecture, to Document F's e-democracy as structural public check, to Document H's multi-generational rectification, and to the broader project's architectural reconstruction.

The methodological commitment of this document is the multi-eyed seeing commitment that organizes the project arc, extending Albert Marshall (Mi'kmaq) and Murdena Marshall's (Mi'kmaq) Two-Eyed Seeing (Etuaptmumk) — the bilateral practice articulated in Cheryl Bartlett, Murdena Marshall, and Albert Marshall's *Two-Eyed Seeing and Other Lessons Learned within a Co-Learning Journey of Bringing Together Indigenous and Mainstream Knowledges and Ways of Knowing* (2012) — into the multilateral architecture the project's equal-weight commitment requires, deployed with explicit care to honor the Marshall framework as a learning process rather than a perfected outcome. The commitment is consequential at three levels for the present document. At the level of analytical authority, the currency-architectural reconstruction is co-produced through the simultaneous engagement of the Western political-economy tradition's heterodox monetary scholarship (Modern Monetary Theory, the sovereign-money tradition, the public-banking tradition, the Minsky-Mehrling financial-stability tradition, Mazzucato's entrepreneurial-state and value-of-everything contributions); the political-philosophical tradition on sacred values, incommensurability, and the moral limits of markets (Tetlock and Atran, Sandel, Anderson, Radin, Satz, Polanyi); the Indigenous gift-economy and ecological-economy traditions (the wampum, potlatch, and Anishinaabe gift-economy traditions whose substantive content Kimmerer, Trosper, LaDuke, Whyte, and the broader contemporary Indigenous economic scholarship engages); the Black-radical political-economy tradition on monetary architecture (Baradaran's *The Color of Money* and *How the Other Half Banks*, Darity and Mullen's *From Here to Equality*, the broader Black-radical scholarship on currency, banking, and economic exclusion); the dalit and Latinx political-economy traditions whose substantive contributions on caste-economic operation, remittance-economy structure, and the broader political-economic configuration this document engages; the cooperative-and-worker-ownership tradition (Kelly, Wright, Alperovitz, Scholz on platform cooperativism); the cryptocurrency-and-blockchain-critique tradition (Hilary Allen's *Driverless Finance*, Gerard's *Attack of the 50 Foot Blockchain* and *Libra Shrugged*, de Vries's energy-consumption research, Schneier's analysis of transactional metadata); and the broader cross-traditional analytical apparatus the project's diagnostic engages. The substantive commitment the document advances is structural and warrants explicit articulation at the outset. The contemporary American currency architecture operates on the analytical premise that money is a universal medium of exchange whose substantive content is fungible across all domains of social life — that healthcare and housing are appropriately purchased through the same monetary medium that purchases consumer goods, that education and political-participatory access are appropriately purchased through the same monetary medium that purchases luxury commodities, that the substantive content of public-good provision is appropriately determined through the substantive content of private-purchasing-power distribution. ## Part One — The Critique of Universal Fungibility

### The Universal-Fungibility Regime Articulated

The contemporary American currency architecture operates on what the present argument names the universal-fungibility regime: the analytical-political configuration under which a single monetary medium — the dollar — is treated as the universal medium of exchange across all substantive domains of social and economic life. Under the universal-fungibility regime, the same dollar that purchases a luxury automobile may purchase healthcare, education, housing, political-participatory access, environmental commons, public-research outputs, and the broader substantive content of public-good provision; the substantive content of access to these goods is determined principally through the substantive content of private-purchasing-power distribution; and the structural-architectural arrangement under which these exchanges operate has been treated, across the broader political-economic and constitutional-doctrinal apparatus, as the natural and unavoidable architectural condition of contemporary economic and political life.

The argument the present part advances against the universal-fungibility regime is structural and runs through several analytical moves the subsequent subsections develop. The first move is the substantive recognition that the universal-fungibility regime is not the natural and unavoidable architectural condition of contemporary economic and political life but a specific historical-political-architectural configuration whose substantive content has been constructed through specific institutional, doctrinal, and political-economic developments and whose substantive premises have been substantively contested across multiple analytical traditions. The second move is the substantive engagement with the political-philosophical tradition on sacred values, incommensurability, and the moral limits of markets — Tetlock and Atran on sacred values, Sandel on the moral limits of markets, Anderson on value pluralism and the corruption thesis, Radin on contested commodities, Satz on noxious markets, Polanyi on the embedded economy and the commodity fictions — that articulates the substantive case for the moral-political distinctions the universal-fungibility regime systematically erases. The third move is the substantive engagement with the Indigenous articulation of the sacred-versus-commodified distinction at the level of land, ecological commons, and the broader substantive content of Indigenous economic philosophy whose substantive engagement the multi-eyed seeing commitment requires. The fourth move is the substantive engagement with the historical record of the universal-fungibility regime's operation under chattel slavery, articulated through Saidiya Hartman's *Scenes of Subjection* and Walter Johnson's *River of Dark Dreams*, as the constitutive case against the universal-fungibility commitment rather than as illustration of its excess. The fifth move is the substantive transition from the diagnostic recognition that the universal-fungibility regime is structurally inadequate to the architectural commitment that the constitutional response is the architectural separation of public-good provision from the universal-fungibility regime through the unidirectional dual-currency architecture Part Four develops.

## Part Two — Public Money: The Heterodox Monetary Tradition

The argument the present part advances is that the substantive analytical contributions of the heterodox monetary tradition — Modern Monetary Theory, the sovereign-money tradition, the public-banking tradition, the Minsky-Mehrling financial-stability tradition, and Mazzucato's entrepreneurial-state and value-of-everything contributions — are load-bearing for the substantive understanding of public money the architectural reconstruction requires, while the unidirectional dual-currency architecture is structurally distinct from the substantive content of these contributions in ways the present part articulates explicitly. ### Modern Monetary Theory: The Operational Reality of Sovereign Currency

Modern Monetary Theory, articulated principally through L. Randall Wray's *Modern Money Theory: A Primer on Macroeconomics for Sovereign Monetary Systems* (second edition 2015), Stephanie Kelton's *The Deficit Myth: Modern Monetary Theory and the Birth of the People's Economy* (2020), Warren Mosler's *Soft Currency Economics* (1995) and *The 7 Deadly Innocent Frauds of Economic Policy* (2010), and Pavlina Tcherneva's *The Case for a Job Guarantee* (2020) and the broader body of her work on employer-of-last-resort architecture, supplies the foundational contemporary heterodox analytical apparatus on the operational reality of sovereign currency in the contemporary American context. The substantive analytical contribution of Modern Monetary Theory that bears centrally on the present argument is the demonstration that the substantive operational mechanics of contemporary sovereign-currency issuance are substantively different from the political-architectural narrative under which the contemporary configuration has been substantively understood, and that the substantive analytical engagement with the operational reality articulates the substantive political-architectural possibilities the configuration supports.

The substantive contributions Modern Monetary Theory articulates that the present argument engages include several specific analytical moves. The first is the demonstration that a sovereign-currency-issuing government — that is, a government that issues its own non-convertible fiat currency, that holds the substantive monopoly on the issuance of the currency, that does not commit to substantive convertibility of the currency to gold or other currencies at fixed rates, and that issues debt only in its own currency — operates under substantive operational conditions in which it cannot become substantively insolvent in its own currency in the sense the conventional political-architectural narrative has substantively presumed. The substantive operational reality is that the government's substantive spending creates the currency the substantive private sector subsequently uses; the government's substantive taxation withdraws currency from circulation; the substantive operational balance between spending and taxation produces the substantive monetary operating conditions; and the substantive constraint on government spending is not the substantive availability of currency (which the government issues at substantive operational discretion) but the substantive constraint of inflationary pressure when spending is conducted in conditions of substantive resource constraint. The substantive analytical apparatus has been substantively confirmed by the substantive empirical operations of the Federal Reserve and the United States Treasury across multiple substantive episodes — including the substantive operational conduct of quantitative easing across the post-2008 period and the substantive operational conduct of pandemic-period fiscal operations — and the substantive analytical content has been substantively engaged across the contemporary heterodox monetary literature.

The second analytical contribution is Stephanie Kelton's substantive articulation in *The Deficit Myth* of the structural-political case that the substantive deficit-and-debt narrative under which contemporary American fiscal policy has been substantively constrained articulates a structural-political constraint that is not the substantive operational constraint of the substantive fiscal configuration. The substantive content of the deficit-and-debt narrative — the substantive concern that government deficits will produce substantive financial-collapse outcomes, the substantive concern that government debt will produce substantive financial-burden outcomes for future generations, the substantive concern that government spending must be substantively financed through prior taxation or debt issuance — articulates the structural-political configuration through which the substantive fiscal capacity of the sovereign currency has been substantively constrained, and the substantive analytical apparatus articulates the substantive political-architectural case that the substantive fiscal capacity is substantively available for the substantive public-good provision the contemporary configuration has substantively constrained.
