# Document Nine — The Political Economy of Transformative Legislation: Historical Waves, Contemporary Conditions, and the Strategic Prescription for the Multi-Decade Project

The eight prior documents of this project arc have constructed a cumulative argument whose structure is now substantially complete in its diagnostic dimension. Document One assembled the constitutional text and amendments as the textual foundation. Document Two analyzed all eighty-five Federalist Papers for what they reveal about the founders' assumptions and the internal tensions within the founders' theory. Document Three engaged the founders' post-ratification reconsiderations and what they show about how the designers themselves understood the limits of their work. Document Four developed the biological and anthropological critique of the founders' assumptions about human nature, social organization, and human-environment relations. Document Five surveyed the biomimicry, polycentric governance, ecological-economics, and adaptive-law scholarship that offers a positive design vocabulary for governance. Document Six synthesized the diagnostic phase into a unified critique. Documents Seven and Eight extended the framework — Document Seven into the cognitive, macroeconomic, and incommensurability dimensions of governance failure, Document Eight into the specific structural failures of the constitutional architecture as a climate-response system and the categories of constitutional redesign that would address them. The diagnostic and extension phases together have specified what is wrong with the inherited architecture and what the analytical and ecological foundations of an alternative would require. They have not yet specified what is to be done under the political conditions the contemporary period actually presents. That is the work this Document Nine begins.

The strategic question Document Nine takes up is one that the diagnostic documents could defer but cannot finally avoid. If the inherited constitutional architecture is substantially inadequate to the empirical conditions of the twenty-first century, and if the redesign categories the project's framework prescribes are substantial enough that no plausible amendment process under existing constitutional rules can accomplish them on a relevant time horizon, then what is the strategic posture the project's framework should adopt under the conditions actually obtaining? The temptation to answer the question by prescribing the legislation that should be enacted — the Green New Deal, the Voting Rights Advancement Act, an environmental constitutional amendment, a wealth-tax constitutional amendment, the abolition of the filibuster — is real, and the project's framework would support each of these in some form. But the temptation answers a different question than the one the strategic posture requires. The strategic question is not what should be enacted in a political environment that does not exist; it is what the work of the present consists of given the political environment that does exist, and what the relationship is between that work and the eventual transformation whose conditions the present period must construct. This document specifies the answer through four analytical operations. First, it develops a conceptual framework for transformative legislation that improves on the Ackermanian "constitutional moments" tradition by integrating the panarchy framework that prior documents have established. Second, it conducts historical analysis of the principal transformative legislative waves of the past century and a half — the Progressive Era, the New Deal, the civil rights wave, the environmental wave, and the Great Society — for what they reveal about the political-economic conditions under which transformative legislation has actually been produced. Third, it assesses the contemporary period against those historical conditions across four dimensions — empirical pressure, movement infrastructure, institutional opportunity structure, intellectual readiness — to specify what the contemporary moment is and is not. Fourth, it articulates the strategic prescription that the analysis produces, including the multi-scale, multi-decade infrastructure-building program that the conditions actually permit and the three operational layers that the next document in the arc will develop in detail.

## PART ONE — TRANSFORMATIVE LEGISLATION: DEFINITION, HISTORICAL PATTERN, AND DECISION QUALITY

### 1.1 Defining transformative legislation: beyond the Ackermanian constitutional moment

Bruce Ackerman's three-volume *We the People* — *Foundations* (1991), *Transformations* (1998), and *The Civil Rights Revolution* (2014) — constitutes the most institutionally substantive theoretical account of constitutional change outside the formal Article V amendment process that contemporary American constitutional theory has produced. Ackerman's framework distinguishes between "normal politics," in which legislative and executive action operates within an inherited constitutional baseline, and "constitutional moments," in which sustained popular mobilization, presidential leadership, congressional supermajorities, and judicial reorientation cumulatively transform the constitutional baseline itself without formal Article V amendment. Ackerman identifies three such moments in American history: the Founding itself, Reconstruction, and the New Deal. *The Civil Rights Revolution* extends the framework to the legislative wave of 1964 to 1965, treating it as a fourth constitutional moment whose institutional consolidation through the Civil Rights Act of 1964, the Voting Rights Act of 1965, and the Fair Housing Act of 1968 reordered the constitutional meaning of the Fourteenth and Fifteenth Amendments more profoundly than any judicial decision since *Brown v. Board of Education*.

The Ackermanian framework has substantial value. It directs attention to the fact that the American constitutional order has changed substantially since 1787 in ways the formal amendment process does not fully capture, that the changes are produced by political-mobilization processes that operate over decades rather than by single legislative enactments, and that the constitutional doctrine through which subsequent courts interpret the resulting transformations is itself a product of the political processes rather than an independent foundation. The framework also has limitations the project's analytical lens makes legible. Three are particularly consequential.

The first limitation is that the Ackermanian framework imports a conception of constitutional change as discrete moments with defined onset, peak, and resolution that does not match the empirical pattern of how transformative change has actually occurred. The New Deal "constitutional moment" in Ackerman's account runs from approximately 1933 through the "switch in time" of 1937 and the consolidation of the Roosevelt Court through the early 1940s. But the substantive intellectual, political, and institutional preparation for the New Deal extended back at least to the 1880s — through the populist movement, the Progressive Era, the labor movement, the institutionalist tradition in academic economics — and the institutional consolidation of the New Deal extended forward through the Great Society and beyond. Treating the moment as 1933 to 1937 truncates both the antecedent infrastructure and the subsequent consolidation in ways that misrepresent the actual dynamics.

The second limitation is that the framework's reliance on the formal categories of presidential leadership, congressional supermajorities, and judicial reorientation as the markers of a constitutional moment systematically underweights legislative waves that produced substantial constitutional transformation without filling all three categories simultaneously. The civil rights legislative wave of 1964 to 1968, which Ackerman accommodates by extending the framework, fits awkwardly because the judicial reorientation it requires had already occurred in *Brown v. Board of Education* (1954) and the related desegregation cases, and because the legislative phase relied on a Democratic congressional supermajority that was assembled through the contingent collapse of the Republican opposition in the 1964 election rather than through a sustained ideological realignment. The environmental legislative wave of 1969 to 1976 — the National Environmental Policy Act, the Clean Air Act amendments, the Clean Water Act, the Endangered Species Act, the Resource Conservation and Recovery Act, the Toxic Substances Control Act, and a half-dozen other major statutes — fits even more awkwardly. It was enacted under a Republican administration with bipartisan congressional majorities, it produced administrative and constitutional transformations whose magnitude rivals the New Deal's regulatory architecture, and it is barely visible in the Ackermanian framework. The framework's categories systematically underweight legislative waves whose political coalitions do not match the New Deal template, even when the substantive constitutional transformation those waves produced is comparable.

The third limitation, and the one most directly grounded in the project's analytical framework, is that the Ackermanian account of constitutional change as discrete moments with defined onset, peak, and resolution does not capture the ecological reality of how complex adaptive systems actually transform. The panarchy framework that Lance Gunderson and C. S. Holling developed in *Panarchy: Understanding Transformations in Human and Natural Systems* (2002), engaged across Documents Five and Six, describes systems as cycling through phases of growth (r), conservation (K), release (Ω), and reorganization (α), with transformations occurring not as singular events but as cascading reconfigurations across multiple scales. Applied to constitutional change, the panarchy framework suggests that transformative legislation is the visible legislative phase of a longer cycle that includes movement infrastructure-building, elite ideological reorientation, judicial doctrinal preparation, and the slow accumulation of administrative and bureaucratic capacity. The legislation itself is the moment of release and reorganization, but it is unintelligible without the longer accumulation that precedes it and the slower consolidation that follows.

A more useful definition of transformative legislation, for the purposes of this analysis, is one that specifies four characteristics. A legislative wave is transformative when it (first) creates institutions or rights that did not previously exist in federal law and that subsequent administrations and Congresses cannot easily dismantle without sustained counter-mobilization; when it (second) reorders the relationship between federal authority, state authority, and private authority over a substantive domain in ways that subsequent constitutional doctrine treats as the new baseline; when it (third) generates an administrative and regulatory infrastructure capable of sustained implementation across multiple presidential administrations; and when it (fourth) reorients public expectation about the legitimate scope of governmental responsibility in the relevant domain. By this fourfold test, the New Deal, the civil rights wave, and the environmental wave clearly qualify. The Affordable Care Act partially qualifies. The Inflation Reduction Act qualifies on dimensions one and three but is contested on dimensions two and four. The Progressive Era reforms qualify, with some complications discussed below. The Great Society partially qualifies. Most other major legislation does not.

The strategic implication of this definitional refinement is that the question for the contemporary period is not whether the present moment is a constitutional moment in Ackerman's sense — that is partly the question Ackerman's framework asks but is partly an artifact of the framework — but rather where the constitutional system is in the panarchy cycle, what conservation-phase rigidities have accumulated, what release-phase conditions are emerging, and what reorganization possibilities the longer cumulative work of movements and intellectual traditions has prepared. The historical analysis that follows is organized to produce material for that latter assessment rather than to identify constitutional moments in the Ackermanian sense.

### 1.2 The Progressive Era wave: antecedent conditions and institutional preparation

The Progressive Era regulatory wave runs from approximately 1887, with the Interstate Commerce Act, through 1920, with the ratification of the Nineteenth Amendment, and includes the Sherman Antitrust Act (1890), the Pure Food and Drug Act (1906), the Federal Reserve Act (1913), the Sixteenth Amendment authorizing the federal income tax (1913), the Seventeenth Amendment establishing direct election of senators (1913), the Clayton Antitrust Act (1914), the Federal Trade Commission Act (1914), the Eighteenth Amendment establishing Prohibition (1919), and the Nineteenth Amendment establishing women's suffrage (1920). The wave is sometimes treated as preliminary to the New Deal rather than as transformative in its own right, but this treatment understates both its substantive achievement and its strategic significance for what came after.

The political conditions that made the Progressive Era wave possible included a sustained economic transformation — the consolidation of industrial capitalism, the rise of the great trusts, the urbanization that created mass working-class concentrations in northeastern and midwestern cities — that produced widely visible social dislocations no inherited governance framework could address. Theda Skocpol's work in *Protecting Soldiers and Mothers: The Political Origins of Social Policy in the United States* (1992) and in *Diminished Democracy: From Membership to Management in American Civic Life* (2003) has shown how the political infrastructure that made Progressive legislation possible was built incrementally across decades, through the Civil War pension system, the women's club movement, the settlement house movement, the elite reform organizations whose membership overlapped extensively with the legislators and executives who would later draft the Progressive statutes, and the dense network of cross-class fraternal associations that constituted the American civic infrastructure of the late nineteenth and early twentieth centuries. The infrastructure was substantially in place by 1900; the legislative output of 1906 to 1920 was its activation, not its origination. Skocpol's broader theoretical claim — that durable social-policy legislation in the United States has historically required the combination of mass membership organizations, elite reform networks, and federal structural-political openings — has been engaged by the subsequent literature including Kim Phillips-Fein's *Invisible Hands: The Businessmen's Crusade against the New Deal* (2009) and Kim Voss's work on labor movement organization, and constitutes the principal sociological framework through which transformative legislative waves can be understood.

The decision-quality of Progressive legislation, examined through the analytical lens that the project's framework engages from Documents Four and Seven, is mixed in instructive ways. The Federal Reserve Act of 1913 represents one of the most institutionally durable and operationally sophisticated pieces of Progressive legislation, drawing on more than a half-century of policy work descending from the National Banking Acts of 1863 and 1864 and from the academic monetary economics of the period. The Act's design, including the regional-bank structure, the dual public-private governance, and the relative independence from direct presidential control, reflected a substantial body of accumulated institutional learning and produced an architecture that has survived more than a century of subsequent monetary and political stress. The Pure Food and Drug Act of 1906, by contrast, was substantially the product of acute crisis politics — the publication of Upton Sinclair's *The Jungle* and the resulting public revulsion at the meatpacking industry's practices — and produced legislation whose institutional design relied on enforcement structures that proved substantially weaker than its drafters anticipated, requiring multiple subsequent amendments through the Federal Food, Drug, and Cosmetic Act of 1938 and beyond. The Sherman Antitrust Act, drafted in language so general that its operational meaning was substantially constructed by judicial interpretation across the subsequent half-century, demonstrates the pattern in which Progressive legislation often delegated the substantive specification of regulatory authority to courts whose ideological commitments would shape the doctrine in directions the original drafters had not anticipated.

The constitutional architecture within which Progressive legislation operated was itself substantially transformed by the wave. The Sixteenth Amendment overcame the Supreme Court's holding in *Pollock v. Farmers' Loan & Trust Co.* (1895) that the federal income tax was unconstitutional, expanding the federal fiscal capacity in ways that the New Deal and all subsequent regulatory and welfare-state architecture would depend on. The Seventeenth Amendment removed the structural protection of state-legislative selection of senators, partially democratizing the Senate while producing the federalism implications that subsequent constitutional theory has continued to engage. The Nineteenth Amendment, the political achievement of the suffrage movement whose organizational infrastructure had been built across the seven decades since the Seneca Falls Convention of 1848, expanded the franchise to women and produced the demographic transformation of the electorate that subsequent legislative coalitions would have to engage. The constitutional amendments of the Progressive Era are sometimes treated as separate from the regulatory legislation, but the four amendments and the regulatory wave constitute one cumulative constitutional transformation whose institutional architecture would be the foundation on which the New Deal built.

### 1.3 The New Deal: crisis-driven legislation, durable institutional architecture, and the constitutional crisis of 1937

The New Deal legislative wave runs from the Hundred Days legislation of March-June 1933 through the consolidation of the Second New Deal in 1935 and the legislative aftermath through 1938. The wave includes the Agricultural Adjustment Act (1933, struck down by the Supreme Court in *United States v. Butler* (1936) and re-enacted in 1938), the National Industrial Recovery Act (1933, struck down in *A.L.A. Schechter Poultry Corp. v. United States* (1935)), the Glass-Steagall Banking Act (1933), the Securities Act (1933) and Securities Exchange Act (1934), the Federal Emergency Relief Act (1933), the Tennessee Valley Authority Act (1933), the Civilian Conservation Corps Act (1933), the Public Works Administration legislation (1933), the Social Security Act (1935), the National Labor Relations Act (1935), the Banking Act (1935), the Public Utility Holding Company Act (1935), the Soil Conservation and Domestic Allotment Act (1936), the Fair Labor Standards Act (1938), and a substantial body of additional regulatory and relief legislation. The wave's substantive scope, its regulatory ambition, and its institutional consequences exceed those of any other legislative period in American history with the possible exception of the Reconstruction amendments and statutes.

The political conditions that produced the New Deal included the catastrophic economic collapse of 1929-1933, the bank crisis of February-March 1933 that brought the financial system to operational failure, the political collapse of the Republican coalition that had governed for most of the previous seven decades, the assembly under Franklin Roosevelt of a Democratic coalition combining the southern white Democratic establishment, the urban-immigrant constituencies of the northern industrial cities, the labor movement whose membership had grown substantially during the 1920s and would grow more dramatically during the 1930s, and the agricultural constituencies whose economic conditions had been deteriorating since the post-World War I farm depression. The combination of acute crisis, political collapse of the prior governing coalition, and the assembly of a substantially new coalition produced the political conditions for transformative legislation that no subsequent period has fully replicated.

The decision-quality of New Deal legislation varied substantially across statutes in ways that the project's analytical framework can specify. The legislation of the Hundred Days was substantially produced under acute crisis conditions, with policy ideas drawn from the New York progressive tradition, from the Brain Trust's academic networks (Rexford Tugwell, Adolf Berle, Raymond Moley), from the institutionalist tradition in academic economics (John R. Commons, Wesley Mitchell, Walton Hamilton), and from the international experience of European welfare-state and regulatory legislation. The legislation was drafted rapidly, often in the form of broad delegations of authority to administrative agencies that would specify the substantive content through subsequent rulemaking, and several of the central statutes proved constitutionally vulnerable. The National Industrial Recovery Act's central regulatory mechanism was struck down in *Schechter Poultry* on non-delegation and Commerce Clause grounds. The Agricultural Adjustment Act was struck down in *Butler* on spending-clause grounds. The legislation of the Second New Deal, drafted with the lessons of the constitutional invalidations and with more deliberate institutional design, proved substantially more durable. The Social Security Act of 1935, the National Labor Relations Act of 1935, the Securities Exchange Act of 1934, the Federal Deposit Insurance Corporation legislation, and the Tennessee Valley Authority all survived constitutional challenge and produced the institutional architecture that has persisted to the present.

The constitutional crisis of 1935-1937 deserves engagement on its own terms because it constitutes one of the principal historical episodes in which the constraints of the inherited constitutional architecture confronted the substantive demands of transformative legislation. The Supreme Court's *Lochner*-era jurisprudence, developed across the four decades from *Lochner v. New York* (1905) through *Schechter Poultry* and *Butler* and *Carter v. Carter Coal Co.* (1936), operated on a substantive due process framework that constrained federal and state regulatory authority in ways the New Deal's drafters confronted as a structural barrier. Roosevelt's response — the court-packing plan of February 1937, formally the Judicial Procedures Reform Bill — proposed adding up to six new Justices to the Supreme Court, ostensibly on the basis of judicial efficiency but transparently to overcome the constitutional invalidation of New Deal legislation. The plan produced one of the most consequential political-constitutional confrontations in American history. The plan failed legislatively, but the political pressure it produced, combined with the cumulative academic and judicial work of the legal-realist tradition that had been delegitimating *Lochner*-era jurisprudence for two decades, produced the "switch in time that saved nine": Justice Owen Roberts's vote shifts in *West Coast Hotel Co. v. Parrish* (1937) and *NLRB v. Jones & Laughlin Steel Corp.* (1937) that effectively ended the *Lochner* era and inaugurated the New Deal constitutional settlement under which the Commerce Clause, the Spending Clause, and the administrative state would operate without serious constitutional constraint for approximately the next four decades.

The strategic significance of the 1937 episode for the project's analytical framework is substantial. The episode demonstrates that constitutional doctrine is responsive to political pressure of sufficient magnitude, that the doctrinal framework constraining transformative legislation is not a stable feature of American constitutionalism but a contingent product of judicial composition and political conditions, and that judicial transformation is essential to legislative durability. It also demonstrates that the cumulative legal-academic work that prepared the doctrinal ground for the post-1937 settlement — the legal-realist tradition associated with Karl Llewellyn, Felix Cohen, Roscoe Pound, Louis Brandeis, and the Harvard and Columbia legal academies of the 1920s and 1930s — operated on a multi-decade time horizon during which its strategic significance was not legible to the actors involved. The strategic implication for the contemporary period is that the cumulative work of legal scholarship, advocacy, and judicial pressure that would constitute the constitutional preparation for any future transformative wave operates on a comparable time horizon, and the work of building it must be conducted during periods in which its eventual strategic significance is not yet visible.

### 1.4 The civil rights wave: movement-forced legislation and constitutional transformation

The civil rights legislative wave runs from the Civil Rights Act of 1957 through the Civil Rights Act of 1968 and includes the Civil Rights Act of 1957, the Civil Rights Act of 1960, the Civil Rights Act of 1964, the Voting Rights Act of 1965, the Fair Housing Act of 1968, and the substantial body of related civil-rights and antidiscrimination legislation. The wave is centered on the 1964 to 1965 legislative period when the political conditions assembled for substantial enactment, but its political-economic structure cannot be understood without attention to the longer arc of movement infrastructure-building that preceded the legislative period and to the constitutional transformations the legislation produced.

The political-economic structure of the civil rights wave differs from the New Deal's structure in ways that are diagnostic for the project's analytical framework. Where the New Deal coalition was assembled by the political collapse of the prior governing coalition and the contingent leadership of Franklin Roosevelt, the civil rights coalition was assembled through movement pressure that forced legislative action on a political establishment whose median position would have preferred non-action. Charles Payne's *I've Got the Light of Freedom: The Organizing Tradition and the Mississippi Freedom Struggle* (1995) and Taylor Branch's three-volume *America in the King Years* — *Parting the Waters* (1988), *Pillar of Fire* (1998), *At Canaan's Edge* (2006) — together constitute the most institutionally substantive analytical accounts of how movement infrastructure built across the half-century from the founding of the NAACP in 1909 to the legislative climax of 1964-1965 actually produced the political conditions for the legislation. The infrastructure included the NAACP's litigation strategy descending from Charles Hamilton Houston's tenure at Howard Law School in the 1930s and Thurgood Marshall's development of the litigation campaign through the 1940s and 1950s, the Black church-based organizing infrastructure that produced the Southern Christian Leadership Conference and the Montgomery Bus Boycott, the student organizing of the Student Nonviolent Coordinating Committee and the Congress of Racial Equality, the local-leader networks documented in Payne's work that constituted the organizational substrate of the Mississippi and Alabama campaigns, and the cross-racial coalitions that produced the religious and labor support for federal legislation.

The political pressure that forced the 1964 legislation was substantially the product of the visible violence at Birmingham in May 1963 — the police dogs, the fire hoses, the children's crusade — and the moral pressure that the Birmingham images produced on the American political establishment. The Kennedy administration's civil rights bill, introduced in June 1963, was substantially the product of that pressure. The legislative passage of the Civil Rights Act after Kennedy's assassination, under Lyndon Johnson's leadership and with the political moment of post-assassination national mourning, produced the supermajority that overcame the Senate filibuster led by southern Democrats. The Voting Rights Act of 1965 was the product of further movement pressure — the Selma to Montgomery marches, the violence at the Edmund Pettus Bridge in March 1965, the moral pressure on the political establishment that produced Johnson's "We Shall Overcome" address to Congress and the Voting Rights Act passage that followed.

The constitutional consequences of the civil rights legislation extended substantially beyond the statutory provisions themselves. The Civil Rights Act of 1964's Title II (public accommodations) was sustained against constitutional challenge in *Heart of Atlanta Motel, Inc. v. United States* (1964) and *Katzenbach v. McClung* (1964) on Commerce Clause grounds, expanding the constitutional understanding of federal commerce authority in ways that have been substantially constrained but not reversed by subsequent doctrine. The Voting Rights Act's Section 5 preclearance provisions, sustained in *South Carolina v. Katzenbach* (1966), constructed a constitutional architecture in which historically discriminating jurisdictions were subject to ongoing federal supervision of voting changes, an architecture that operated for forty-eight years until the Supreme Court's 5-4 decision in *Shelby County v. Holder* (2013) effectively dismantled it. The Fair Housing Act's protection against housing discrimination, the subsequent expansions through the Equal Credit Opportunity Act (1974) and related legislation, and the broader constitutional jurisprudence under the Fourteenth and Fifteenth Amendments that the civil rights wave produced together constitute one of the most substantial constitutional transformations in American history.

The decision-quality and durability of civil rights legislation, examined through the project's framework, are mixed in instructive ways. The Voting Rights Act's institutional design, with its preclearance architecture targeting historically discriminating jurisdictions and its statutory triggering formula, proved substantially durable for nearly a half-century but ultimately vulnerable to the doctrinal innovation in *Shelby County* that the conservative legal countermovement constructed. The Civil Rights Act's employment provisions, administered through the Equal Employment Opportunity Commission, have proved more institutionally durable but operationally constrained by the EEOC's chronic resource limitations and by judicial decisions including *Wards Cove Packing Co. v. Atonio* (1989) that narrowed the disparate-impact framework before the Civil Rights Act of 1991 partially restored it. The Fair Housing Act's enforcement architecture has proved among the weakest of the civil rights statutes, with structural under-resourcing that has constrained its capacity to address the persistent residential segregation that contemporary research including Richard Rothstein's *The Color of Law: A Forgotten History of How Our Government Segregated America* (2017) and Patrick Sharkey's *Stuck in Place: Urban Neighborhoods and the End of Progress toward Racial Equality* (2013) has documented.

### 1.5 The environmental wave: legislation under bipartisan conditions and the limits of regulatory architecture

The environmental legislative wave runs from the National Environmental Policy Act of 1969 through the Resource Conservation and Recovery Act of 1976 and the Toxic Substances Control Act of 1976, and includes the Clean Air Act amendments of 1970, the Clean Water Act amendments of 1972, the Marine Mammal Protection Act of 1972, the Endangered Species Act of 1973, the Safe Drinking Water Act of 1974, and approximately thirty additional major environmental statutes enacted across the eight-year period. The wave produced one of the most substantial regulatory transformations of the twentieth century and constructed the institutional architecture — the Environmental Protection Agency, established by executive reorganization in 1970, and the related administrative infrastructure — through which contemporary American environmental governance operates.

The political conditions that produced the environmental wave differ from the New Deal and civil rights conditions in ways that are diagnostic. The wave occurred under a Republican administration (Nixon's), with bipartisan congressional majorities, and without the kind of acute economic crisis that produced the New Deal or the moral-pressure crisis that produced the civil rights wave. The conditions for the wave were substantially the product of a different mix of factors: the visible environmental dislocations produced by post-World War II industrial expansion (the Cuyahoga River fires, the Santa Barbara oil spill of 1969, the Love Canal contamination that became visible by the late 1970s), the cultural mobilization produced by Rachel Carson's *Silent Spring* (1962) and the broader environmental literature of the 1960s, the institutional preparation by the conservation movement infrastructure descending from the Progressive Era (the Sierra Club, the Audubon Society, the Wilderness Society, the conservation academic infrastructure), the new environmental movement organizations of the 1960s and 1970s (the Environmental Defense Fund, the Natural Resources Defense Council, Friends of the Earth, Greenpeace), and the political-strategic calculation by the Nixon administration that environmental legislation could neutralize an issue on which Democratic competitors had been mobilizing.

The decision-quality of environmental legislation has been engaged extensively in subsequent literature. The Clean Air Act amendments of 1970 produced one of the most institutionally substantive regulatory architectures in American legislative history, with the National Ambient Air Quality Standards framework, the State Implementation Plan structure, the New Source Performance Standards, and the technology-forcing provisions that produced the catalytic-converter mandate and the subsequent reduction in lead, sulfur dioxide, nitrogen oxides, and particulate emissions across the subsequent half-century. The Clean Water Act produced a substantially less successful regulatory architecture, with the technology-forcing structure for point-source pollution achieving substantial reductions in industrial-effluent discharge but the non-point-source pollution architecture (agricultural runoff principally) remaining substantially unregulated and the resulting nutrient pollution of waterways constituting one of the principal ongoing failures of American environmental governance. The Endangered Species Act produced an institutionally durable but politically vulnerable regulatory architecture whose substantive operation has been constrained by chronic under-resourcing of the Fish and Wildlife Service and by judicial doctrines including the *Bennett v. Spear* (1997) standing innovations that the conservative legal countermovement has constructed.

The strategic significance of the environmental wave for the project's analytical framework is twofold. First, the wave demonstrates that transformative legislation can be produced under political conditions substantially different from the New Deal or civil rights templates, with bipartisan congressional majorities and without acute crisis politics, when the cumulative cultural and movement preparation has produced sufficient political pressure on a non-aligned political establishment. Second, the wave demonstrates the limitations of regulatory architecture as a strategy for transformative change in domains where the underlying causal structure is distributed across many actors and many emission sources rather than concentrated in identifiable industries. The Clean Air Act's success against point-source emissions and the Clean Water Act's relative failure against non-point-source pollution together constitute a natural experiment about what regulatory architecture can and cannot accomplish, with implications for the contemporary period's climate-policy challenges that the project's framework engages.

### 1.6 The Great Society: elite-designed legislation without movement constituency

The Great Society legislative wave overlapped chronologically with the civil rights wave and was enabled by the same Democratic supermajorities, but its origins and political logic were substantially different. Where the civil rights legislation was forced by movement pressure, the Great Society was substantially designed by the Johnson administration and its allied policy experts, drawing on policy ideas accumulated through decades of liberal academic and foundation work but without the kind of mass movement constituency that the civil rights wave commanded. The wave includes the Economic Opportunity Act of 1964, the Elementary and Secondary Education Act of 1965, the Higher Education Act of 1965, the Social Security Amendments of 1965 establishing Medicare and Medicaid, the Immigration and Nationality Act of 1965, the Older Americans Act of 1965, the Head Start program, the Model Cities program, and the substantial body of additional anti-poverty and social-welfare legislation enacted under the broad rubric of the War on Poverty.

The decision-quality of Great Society legislation varied substantially across statutes. Medicare's institutional design, drawing on the precedent of Social Security and on a half-century of policy work descending from the Progressive Era's failed efforts at national health insurance, has proven remarkably durable; the program's basic structure has persisted across nine presidential administrations and remains popular across partisan lines despite repeated efforts to restructure it. Medicaid's design, by contrast, replicated the federalist concession structure of unemployment insurance — federal funding combined with state administration and substantial state-level discretion — that produced systematic underprotection in the southern states whose conservative congressional members required the federalist structure. The result has been six decades of inequality in Medicaid coverage and benefits across states, with the Affordable Care Act's Medicaid expansion of 2010 and its constitutional vulnerability under *NFIB v. Sebelius*, 567 U.S. 519 (2012), continuing to play out the consequences of the original 1965 design choice. The Elementary and Secondary Education Act has produced an institutional architecture that has been substantially altered through subsequent reauthorizations including the No Child Left Behind Act of 2001 and the Every Student Succeeds Act of 2015, illustrating the pattern in which Great Society legislation lacked the movement-constituency protection that the New Deal's most durable statutes (Social Security in particular) commanded.

The constitutional and institutional obstacles that the Great Society overcame were less substantial than those overcome by the civil rights or environmental waves; the Commerce Clause, the spending power, and the developing administrative-law framework were broadly available, and the legislation was enacted before the conservative legal movement had assembled the doctrinal and institutional resources that would later challenge federal regulatory authority. The political coalition supporting the Great Society, however, was substantially weaker than the coalitions that had produced the New Deal and the civil rights wave. Without the labor movement's capacity that had produced the New Deal coalition, and without the civil rights movement's pressure that had produced the 1964 and 1965 Acts, the Great Society depended substantially on Lyndon Johnson's personal political skill, on the post-Kennedy assassination political moment, and on the temporary disorganization of the conservative coalition. When the political conditions changed — with the polarization produced by the Vietnam War, the Watts riots and subsequent urban uprisings, and the white backlash that George Wallace's 1968 candidacy crystallized — the Great Society's legislative momentum collapsed, and much of its substantive program was substantially altered or dismantled by subsequent administrations beginning with Nixon's New Federalism and accelerating through the Reagan administration's reorientation.

### 1.7 The Affordable Care Act and the Inflation Reduction Act: contemporary partial cases

Two contemporary legislative episodes deserve engagement as partial cases that illuminate the conditions for and limits of transformative legislation under contemporary political conditions. The Patient Protection and Affordable Care Act of 2010 and the Inflation Reduction Act of 2022 represent two of the most consequential pieces of legislation enacted in the past three decades. Neither fully meets the fourfold definition of transformative legislation specified above, but each meets it on some dimensions and not on others, and the analysis of which dimensions and why is diagnostic for the contemporary strategic question.

The Affordable Care Act produced substantial expansion of health-insurance coverage, the Medicaid expansion (originally mandatory, made effectively optional by *NFIB v. Sebelius*), the establishment of state and federal insurance exchanges, the prohibition of pre-existing-condition exclusions, the dependent-coverage extension to age 26, the elimination of lifetime coverage caps, and the substantial subsidies for individual-market premiums. The Act's institutional architecture has proven substantially durable: the principal repeal effort of 2017 failed despite Republican control of Congress and the presidency, the Medicaid expansion has been adopted in a substantial majority of states (though not all, with the holdout states concentrated in the Deep South), and the Act's basic structure has persisted across three subsequent administrations of varying partisanship. The Act partially meets the definitional fourfold test: it created institutions that subsequent administrations have been unable to easily dismantle (dimensions one and three), it has reoriented public expectations about health-insurance access (dimension four), but it has not substantially reordered the relationship between federal authority, state authority, and private authority in the health-care domain (dimension two), and the Medicaid expansion's optionality after *NFIB v. Sebelius* limits the federalism transformation it might otherwise have produced.

The Inflation Reduction Act of 2022 produced the largest single climate-policy investment in American history — approximately $370 billion in tax credits, grants, and loan-program authority for clean-energy deployment, electric-vehicle infrastructure, residential and commercial building electrification, agricultural conservation, and environmental justice — along with the Medicare drug-price-negotiation provisions and the corporate alternative minimum tax. The Act's structural choice to operate through tax credits and spending mechanisms rather than through regulatory mandates was deliberate: tax and spending provisions face fewer constitutional doctrinal constraints than regulatory provisions, the major-questions doctrine that the Supreme Court constructed in *West Virginia v. Environmental Protection Agency* (2022) operates principally against regulatory mechanisms, and the standing doctrine that constrains environmental litigation operates principally against regulatory rather than fiscal provisions. The Act's institutional architecture has proven partially durable through the early implementation phase, but its political-economic vulnerability to subsequent administrations is substantial: tax credits can be rescinded, grant programs can be defunded, and the implementation infrastructure depends on annual appropriations that hostile administrations can constrain. The Act meets the definitional test on dimensions one and three (it created tax-credit and spending architectures that have produced substantial private-sector investment, and it generated administrative infrastructure for climate-finance deployment), but it is contested on dimensions two and four: the relationship between federal and private authority over energy systems has not been fundamentally reordered, and public expectation about governmental responsibility for climate response has not been transformed in ways that make subsequent climate legislation politically obligatory.

The strategic significance of the Affordable Care Act and the Inflation Reduction Act for the project's framework is that they constitute the maximum that contemporary political conditions have permitted, that they fall substantially short of the transformative thresholds the project's diagnostic phase identified as necessary, and that the explanation for both the achievement and the shortfall lies in the political-economic conditions of the contemporary period that the next part will engage in detail.

### 1.8 Six patterns from the historical analysis

The historical analysis above produces six patterns that should structure the analysis of the contemporary period in Part Two.

The first pattern is that movement infrastructure built over decades is the necessary condition for transformative legislation, with crisis conditions accelerating but not creating the political coalition. Every transformative wave examined was the visible legislative phase of a longer cycle of organizing, ideological development, litigation, and institutional preparation. The New Deal harvested the work of the populist movement, the Progressive movement, the labor movement, and the institutionalist tradition in academic economics. The civil rights wave harvested the half-century of NAACP litigation and the half-century of Black community organizing. The environmental wave harvested the conservation movement infrastructure descending from the Progressive Era. Without the prior infrastructure, the political coalition for legislative transformation does not assemble.

The second pattern is that the decision-quality of legislation produced under acute crisis conditions is systematically lower than the decision-quality of legislation produced under conditions that permit sustained deliberation, but acute crisis conditions are often necessary for the political coalition to form. The New Deal of 1933 produced the operationally weak and constitutionally vulnerable legislation of the first hundred days; the New Deal of 1935 to 1938 produced the institutionally durable Social Security Act and National Labor Relations Act. The strategic implication is that movement infrastructure capable of sustaining political pressure beyond the acute crisis phase is essential to producing legislation whose institutional architecture will endure.

The third pattern is that judicial transformation is essential to legislative durability, and the constitutional doctrine that limits transformative legislation is responsive to political pressure of sufficient magnitude. The "switch in time" of 1937 was produced by the political pressure of court-packing combined with the cumulative legal-academic work of legal realism that had been delegitimating *Lochner*-era jurisprudence for two decades. The civil rights wave's constitutional sustainability rested on the Warren Court's reorientation, itself the product of the cumulative work of the civil rights legal infrastructure. Any transformative wave the contemporary period might eventually produce will require comparable judicial preparation that the conservative composition of the contemporary federal judiciary makes substantially difficult.

The fourth pattern is that legislation operating through positive institutional construction (Social Security, Medicare, the EPA's regulatory architecture) tends to produce more durable transformation than legislation operating through prohibitions and rights enforcement (the Voting Rights Act's preclearance, antidiscrimination enforcement). Positive institutional construction creates constituencies and administrative capacity that defend the legislation across political cycles; prohibitions and rights enforcement create only the political coalition that secured the legislation, which can dissipate when the immediate political moment passes.

The fifth pattern is that the constitutional architecture within which transformative legislation operates is itself transformed by the legislation, but the transformations are reversible by sufficient counter-mobilization. The post-1937 New Deal settlement was substantially reversed by the conservative legal countermovement of the 1980s through the present, with the Commerce Clause framework narrowed in *United States v. Lopez* (1995) and *United States v. Morrison* (2000), the spending-power framework constrained in *NFIB v. Sebelius* (2012), and the administrative-state framework substantially constrained by the major-questions doctrine and the post-*Chevron* deference framework. Constitutional transformations are not permanent achievements but ongoing political contests whose direction depends on which side has the legal-academic and judicial-appointment infrastructure to maintain or reverse them.

The sixth pattern is that legislative waves are visible phases of longer panarchy cycles whose multi-decade dynamics structure both the conditions for the wave and the consolidation that follows. The wave's strategic significance is unintelligible without the longer cycle. The contemporary strategic question is therefore not whether contemporary conditions permit legislation comparable to the historical waves — they generally do not — but where the contemporary period is in the panarchy cycle of which any future legislative wave will be the visible phase, and what the strategic work of the contemporary phase consists of given the panarchy-cycle position the empirical analysis specifies.

## PART TWO — CONTEMPORARY CONDITIONS ASSESSED AGAINST THE HISTORICAL RECORD

The historical analysis of Part One produces a framework against which the contemporary period can be assessed. The four dimensions the framework specifies — empirical pressure, movement infrastructure, institutional opportunity structure, and intellectual readiness — operate as a diagnostic across which the contemporary period's conditions can be mapped. The honest assessment requires neither the optimism that overstates contemporary readiness for transformative legislation nor the despair that understates the substantial assets the contemporary period has accumulated. The analytical task is to specify what is and is not present, what each presence and absence implies for strategy, and what the integration of the four dimensions reveals about the panarchy-cycle position the contemporary moment occupies.

### 2.1 Empirical pressure: severity, recognition, and the political-force gap

The empirical conditions of the contemporary period are, by any reasonable measure, more severe than the conditions that produced the historical legislative waves. The climate trajectory documented in successive reports of the Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change, including the Sixth Assessment Report (2021-2023), specifies global warming on track to exceed the 1.5°C target of the Paris Agreement within the next decade and to reach 2.5° to 2.9°C by century-end on current policy trajectories, with associated impacts on sea-level rise, agricultural productivity, water availability, biodiversity, human migration, and human mortality that exceed the empirical reference cases in human historical experience. The biodiversity collapse documented in the Intergovernmental Science-Policy Platform on Biodiversity and Ecosystem Services Global Assessment (2019) specifies extinction rates running at tens to hundreds of times the natural background rate, with approximately one million species facing extinction risk, and the planetary-boundaries framework's 2023 update finding that humanity has now transgressed six of the nine boundaries — climate change, biosphere integrity, biogeochemical flows, land-system change, freshwater change, and novel entities. The democratic-governance trajectory documented by Freedom House, the Varieties of Democracy Institute, and the broader democratic-decline literature specifies measurable democratic backsliding in approximately seventy countries over the past decade, with the United States itself classified as a "flawed democracy" or "backsliding democracy" by various indices and with the constitutional norm-violations of the past decade constituting a body of empirical evidence that the inherited American constitutional architecture has substantial vulnerabilities the framers did not anticipate. The economic-inequality trajectory documented by Thomas Piketty, Emmanuel Saez, Gabriel Zucman, and the broader inequality-research literature specifies wealth concentration and income inequality at levels comparable to the Gilded Age peak and substantially exceeding any other period in American history.

The empirical severity is not in dispute among those qualified to assess it. The political-force gap that Document Ten will engage in detail — the gap between the empirical severity and the political pressure actually mobilized in response — is also not in dispute, though its causes and remedies are. The contemporary period has produced empirical pressure substantially exceeding the conditions of the historical waves without producing the political-force response the historical waves generated. The Depression of 1929-1933 produced unemployment of approximately twenty-five percent, the visible failure of the banking system, and the immediate threat of further economic collapse — empirical conditions that produced political-force response within months. The civil rights crisis of Birmingham 1963 produced visible state violence against children — empirical conditions that produced political-force response within weeks. The environmental crisis visible by 1969 — the Cuyahoga River fires, the Santa Barbara oil spill, the documented air-pollution mortality — produced political-force response within months. The contemporary climate crisis, despite empirical severity exceeding any of these reference cases, has not produced political-force response remotely comparable to the historical waves' response. The same is true of the biodiversity crisis, the inequality crisis, and the democratic-erosion crisis.

The cognitive and informational mechanisms that produce the political-force gap are engaged in detail in Document Ten and were preliminarily developed in Document Seven. The construal-level theory of Yaacov Trope and Nira Liberman specifies that psychological distance — temporal, spatial, social, and hypothetical — systematically activates abstract construal that reduces the urgency and concreteness with which issues are processed. The empirical features of the contemporary crises — temporally distant impacts (decades hence), spatially distant impacts (other regions), socially distant impacts (out-groups, future generations), hypothetically distant impacts (probabilistic projections) — produce abstract construal that systematically weakens the political-force response. The cultural-cognition framework of Dan Kahan and the Cultural Cognition Project at Yale Law School specifies that empirical questions entangled with cultural-identity questions are processed through identity-protective cognition that systematically resists empirical evidence that threatens the cultural-political identity of those processing it. The contemporary crises have been substantially entangled with cultural-political identity in ways that the historical crises generally were not, with the result that empirical evidence that should produce political-force response is filtered through the identity-protective cognition that contemporary political alignment produces. The argumentative-theory account of reason developed by Hugo Mercier and Dan Sperber specifies that human reasoning operates principally in social-deliberative contexts rather than in individual evidence-weighing, and that the standard informational-deficit communication strategies premised on individual evidence-weighing systematically underperform.

The structural implication is that the empirical-political linkage that produced the historical waves has been substantially weakened by the cognitive and informational conditions of the contemporary period, with the result that empirical severity does not translate into political-force response at the rates the historical analysis would have predicted. The implication is not that empirical severity has become irrelevant — it remains the substrate from which political mobilization must work — but that the translation of empirical severity into political force requires methodologies that the historical waves did not require. Those methodologies are the subject of Document Ten's third operational layer; their relevance for the contemporary strategic assessment is that the political-force gap is not a temporary aberration that empirical severity will eventually overcome, but a structural feature of the contemporary informational and cognitive environment that requires specific strategic engagement.

The honest assessment on the empirical-pressure dimension is therefore that the contemporary period has substantially greater empirical severity than the historical waves but has not produced the empirical-political linkage that empirical severity alone, in prior periods, was sufficient to generate. The strategic implication is that empirical severity is not, by itself, the political asset that historical analogy would suggest, and that the strategic work of the present must include the construction of the cognitive and informational conditions through which empirical severity translates into political force.

### 2.2 Movement infrastructure: substantial assets, structural limitations

The movement infrastructure of the contemporary period presents a more complex picture than the empirical-pressure dimension. The contemporary period has accumulated substantial assets across several categories that the project's framework should specify in detail before assessing the limitations.

The intellectual infrastructure of the contemporary period is unprecedented in scope and sophistication. The scientific understanding of climate change, biodiversity, planetary boundaries, ecological economics, decision quality, cognitive bias, polycentric governance, biomimicry, and Indigenous knowledge traditions provides analytical resources that the historical waves' intellectual infrastructure did not approach. The work of scholars across these fields, including the scholars whose work this project's documents have engaged — Holling and Gunderson on panarchy, Ostrom on polycentric governance, Meadows on systems and leverage points, Benyus on biomimicry, Raworth on doughnut economics, Rockström and Steffen on planetary boundaries, Kimmerer and Whyte on Indigenous environmental thought, Kahneman and Tversky on cognitive bias, Tetlock and Atran on sacred values and incommensurability, the Cultural Cognition Project on identity-protective cognition — has produced an analytical foundation for transformative legislation that is substantially more sophisticated than the foundations the historical waves operated from. The intellectual readiness exceeds the political readiness in ways that the historical comparisons would not have predicted.

The advocacy and policy-development infrastructure of the contemporary period is substantial. The environmental sector includes the Environmental Defense Fund, the Natural Resources Defense Council, the Sierra Club, the World Resources Institute, Earthjustice, the Center for Biological Diversity, 350.org, the Sunrise Movement, and approximately a hundred additional national and regional organizations with substantial accumulated capacity. The democratic-reform sector includes Common Cause, the Brennan Center for Justice, the Campaign Legal Center, FairVote, RepresentUs, and a comparable number of organizations. The economic-justice sector includes the Center on Budget and Policy Priorities, the Economic Policy Institute, the Roosevelt Institute, the Center for American Progress, and the policy-research infrastructure of organized labor including the Aspen Institute's Future of Work program. The racial-justice sector includes the NAACP Legal Defense Fund, the Leadership Conference on Civil and Human Rights, the Movement for Black Lives and its constituent organizations, Color of Change, UnidosUS, the Asian Americans Advancing Justice network, and the broader infrastructure of community-organizing groups working on racial-justice issues. The Indigenous sovereignty and rights infrastructure includes the National Congress of American Indians, the Native American Rights Fund, the Indigenous Environmental Network, Honor the Earth, and the broader network of tribal nation governments whose institutional capacity has grown substantially across the past several decades.

The state-level infrastructure that Document Ten engages in detail represents another substantial category of accumulated capacity. The State Innovation Exchange, the National Caucus of Environmental Legislators, the Public Leadership Institute, and the broader network of state-level legislative-support organizations operate as infrastructure that did not exist in comparable form during the historical waves. The state-level executive infrastructure, including the U.S. Climate Alliance (the coalition of states committed to Paris Agreement compliance), the Regional Greenhouse Gas Initiative, and various state attorneys-general coalitions, similarly operates as infrastructure that exceeds what was available during the historical waves.

The labor-organizing infrastructure has been substantially weakened across the past four decades but has begun to show signs of revival in the contemporary period. The decline in union density from approximately thirty-five percent of the private-sector workforce in the mid-1950s to approximately six percent in the contemporary period represents one of the most consequential structural changes in American political economy across the post-war period, and the corresponding loss of political-economic capacity has been substantial. The contemporary signs of revival — the Amazon Labor Union victory at JFK8 in 2022, the Starbucks Workers United campaign, the United Auto Workers Stand Up Strike of 2023, the Writers Guild and SAG-AFTRA strikes of 2023 — represent meaningful institutional development that the project's framework should not understate. The aggregate labor capacity remains substantially below the levels that produced the New Deal coalition, but the trajectory has shifted from sustained decline to early-stage revival in ways the project's strategic framework should engage.

The structural limitations of contemporary movement infrastructure, despite the substantial accumulated assets, are equally important to engage. The first limitation is the substantial decline in cross-class mass-membership civic associations that Skocpol's *Diminished Democracy: From Membership to Management in American Civic Life* (2003) documented. The fraternal associations, the labor unions, the parent-teacher organizations, the women's clubs, the religious congregations whose membership and political capacity declined dramatically across the second half of the twentieth century constituted the civic infrastructure within which Progressive Era and New Deal political coalitions were assembled. The contemporary movement infrastructure is substantially more professionalized and substantially less rooted in cross-class mass-membership organizations than the infrastructure that produced the historical waves. The professionalization has produced substantial accumulated expertise but has not produced the political-mobilization capacity that mass-membership organizations historically generated.

The second limitation is the substantial geographic concentration of contemporary movement infrastructure in coastal urban areas, with limited reach into the rural, exurban, and small-city communities whose political weight the inherited federalism structure substantially over-represents. Jonathan Rodden's *Why Cities Lose: The Deep Roots of the Urban-Rural Political Divide* (2019) and the broader political-geography literature have documented how the geographic concentration of progressive political-cultural identity in urban areas, combined with single-member-district legislative representation, produces structurally adverse legislative geography for the kinds of transformative legislation the project's framework prescribes. The infrastructure that would translate the empirical and intellectual readiness of the contemporary period into legislative-coalition assembly is structurally constrained by the geographic distribution of its base.

The third limitation is what Hahrie Han's *How Organizations Develop Activists: Civic Associations and Leadership in the 21st Century* (2014) and her subsequent work with Elizabeth McKenna and Michelle Oyakawa on *Prisms of the People: Power and Organizing in Twenty-First-Century America* (2021) have specified as the systematic shift from organizing to mobilizing across the contemporary movement-infrastructure ecosystem. Mobilizing operates by aggregating individuals to act on positions they already hold, through channels (mass communication, online petition, digital ad-targeting) that minimize the friction of participation. Organizing operates by building relationships among people through which positions, identities, and political capacities are developed over time, through channels (face-to-face conversation, membership organizations, collective action) that require substantial friction to participate. Han's empirical work demonstrates that organizations that produce durable political power and durable issue-shift are those that engage in genuine organizing rather than relying on mobilizing infrastructure. The contemporary funder ecosystem, the digital-organizing technology stack, and the consultant infrastructure have all systematically biased toward mobilizing — which is more measurable, more scalable, and more legible to funders — at the cost of the organizing that Han's empirical work identifies as the mechanism for durable power-building. The aggregate effect is that the contemporary movement infrastructure has substantial reach but limited depth, with the consequence that the political-force capacity it can mobilize falls short of what the empirical pressure would warrant.

The synthesis on the movement-infrastructure dimension is that the contemporary period has substantial intellectual depth and considerable absolute scale, but with organizational forms substantially different from those of the historical waves and with rank-and-file organizing capacity in working-class communities, in racially diverse communities, and in geographies outside coastal urban areas that is substantially less developed than the historical waves required. The contemporary movement infrastructure is sufficient to support legislative episodes of the Inflation Reduction Act's scale and durability profile, but probably not yet sufficient to support transformative legislation of the New Deal's or the civil rights wave's scale.

### 2.3 Institutional opportunity structure: the conservative legal countermovement and the contemporary judiciary

The institutional opportunity structure within which the contemporary period must operate has been substantially constructed by the four-decade conservative legal countermovement whose strategic significance the project's framework must engage in detail. The countermovement is the constitutional reaction to the legislative waves of the 1930s through the 1970s, and its success has been substantial enough that the institutional opportunity structure for transformative legislation in the contemporary period is substantially less hospitable than it was during any of the historical waves.

The infrastructure of the conservative legal countermovement is the most institutionally substantive product of right-of-center political organization in American history, and the project's framework should specify it with the same analytical seriousness with which the New Deal and civil rights infrastructures have been engaged. The countermovement's foundational institutional development includes the founding of the Federalist Society in 1982 by law students at Yale, Chicago, and Harvard, with subsequent expansion to chapters at virtually every American law school and to the Lawyers Division that operates as the principal professional network of conservative attorneys. Steven Teles's *The Rise of the Conservative Legal Movement: The Battle for Control of the Law* (2008) documents how the Federalist Society's strategic design — explicitly modeled on the perceived success of the liberal legal infrastructure of the post-war period — created a recruiting and credentialing pipeline that produces conservative legal talent at scale, with the Society's membership now constituting the principal source of conservative judicial nominations across Republican administrations. The institutional infrastructure also includes the Olin Foundation's law-and-economics program, which funded the establishment of law-and-economics centers at most major American law schools and produced the academic foundations of the deregulatory and property-rights jurisprudence the contemporary Court has substantially adopted. Jane Mayer's *Dark Money: The Hidden History of the Billionaires Behind the Rise of the Radical Right* (2016) documents the broader funder infrastructure including the Bradley Foundation, the Scaife family foundations, the Koch network, and the related organizations that have funded the cumulative institutional development of the countermovement.

The doctrinal output of the countermovement has been substantial. The major-questions doctrine, articulated formally in *West Virginia v. Environmental Protection Agency*, 597 U.S. 697 (2022), holds that administrative agencies cannot make decisions of "vast economic and political significance" without clear congressional authorization, with the Court itself determining what counts as "vast" and what counts as "clear." The doctrine has been applied to invalidate the Clean Power Plan, to constrain the OSHA vaccine-mandate, to invalidate the Biden student-loan forgiveness program in *Biden v. Nebraska*, 600 U.S. 477 (2023), and to constrain administrative discretion across the regulatory domain. The doctrine's structural effect is to require congressional re-authorization for substantial regulatory action that the inherited statutory framework had previously authorized, and to do so under congressional procedural conditions (the filibuster, the supermajority requirements for substantial legislation) that make such re-authorization substantially infeasible. The structural effect, in operation, is the constitutional dismantling of the regulatory architecture the historical waves produced.

*Loper Bright Enterprises v. Raimondo*, 603 U.S. 369 (2024), overturned the *Chevron* deference framework that had governed judicial review of administrative interpretation since 1984, holding that courts must independently determine the best reading of statutory provisions rather than deferring to reasonable agency interpretations. The doctrinal innovation transfers substantial regulatory authority from administrative agencies (whose technical capacity and democratic accountability through presidential appointment had constituted the post-New Deal administrative architecture) to courts (whose technical capacity for regulatory determination is structurally limited and whose democratic accountability operates through a substantially different mechanism). The structural effect, in operation, is to shift regulatory contests from administrative rulemaking to litigation, with the implications for regulatory delay, regulatory inconsistency across circuits, and regulatory capture by litigation-resourced interests that the administrative-law literature has anticipated.

*Trump v. United States*, 603 U.S. 593 (2024), constructed a doctrine of presidential immunity from criminal prosecution for official acts that has no clear constitutional textual foundation and that substantially expands executive power in ways that the unitary-executive theory the project rejects has long sought. The structural effect is to establish constitutional protection for executive conduct that the inherited constitutional architecture had treated as subject to criminal accountability, and to do so on grounds whose textual basis is contested by substantial constitutional scholarship including the dissents in the case itself.

*Shelby County v. Holder*, 570 U.S. 529 (2013), effectively dismantled the preclearance architecture of the Voting Rights Act of 1965 by invalidating the coverage formula that determined which jurisdictions were subject to federal supervision of voting changes. The doctrinal innovation, that the coverage formula reflected outdated conditions, has produced the predictable result that the jurisdictions previously subject to preclearance have implemented voting changes — voter-identification requirements, voter-roll purges, polling-place closures, restrictions on absentee and early voting — that the preclearance architecture would substantially have prevented. The structural effect is the constitutional dismantling of one of the principal institutional achievements of the civil rights wave.

*Citizens United v. Federal Election Commission*, 558 U.S. 310 (2010), constructed a constitutional architecture in which corporate political spending is constitutionally protected speech subject to limited regulatory constraint, producing the post-2010 transformation of American political-finance structure that the small-dollar online-fundraising infrastructure has only partially counterbalanced. The structural effect is the substantial increase in the political-economic capacity of concentrated wealth relative to dispersed political-mobilization capacity, with the implications for legislative capture and policy-feedback loops that the political-economy literature has documented.

*Dobbs v. Jackson Women's Health Organization*, 597 U.S. 215 (2022), overturned *Roe v. Wade* (1973) and *Planned Parenthood v. Casey* (1992), eliminating the constitutional protection for abortion access and producing the post-2022 state-level reorganization of abortion law that has produced the most substantial reproductive-rights transformation in fifty years. The structural effect is not only the immediate substantive consequences for reproductive rights but the demonstration that constitutional precedents the conservative legal movement has identified as targets can be overturned when the Court's composition permits, with implications for the durability of other precedents (including substantive due process foundations of contraception, marriage equality, and related rights) that the conservative legal movement has expressed varying degrees of intent to revisit.

*NFIB v. Sebelius*, 567 U.S. 519 (2012), upheld the Affordable Care Act's individual mandate as a tax but invalidated the Act's mandatory Medicaid expansion as a coercive use of the spending power, producing the optional-Medicaid-expansion structure whose consequences have been the persistent coverage gap in non-expansion states. The case's significance for the project's framework extends beyond the specific Medicaid-expansion holding: it demonstrates that the spending-power framework that constituted one of the principal constitutional foundations of New Deal and Great Society legislation is now subject to judicial constraint in ways that prior periods did not anticipate.

The cumulative pattern is one of constitutional doctrine actively constructing constraints on the kind of transformative legislation that this document examines. The doctrines are not stable features of American constitutionalism that the project must work around; they are the contemporary judicial output of a four-decade conservative legal movement that has had as one of its explicit objectives the constitutional dismantling of the administrative and rights-protective architecture that the historical waves produced. The Federalist Society infrastructure, the Olin Foundation network, the Heritage Foundation's legal work, the work of scholars including Antonin Scalia, Robert Bork, and Richard Epstein, and the systematic appointment strategy that has produced the contemporary Court are the visible products of the kind of long-term ideological infrastructure that the historical analysis above identified as preceding transformative waves. The conservative legal movement is the constitutional countermovement to the legislative waves of the 1930s through the 1970s, and its success has been substantial.

The strategic implications for transformative legislation are several. The first is that any major regulatory legislation must anticipate constitutional challenges grounded in the major-questions doctrine, the regulatory takings doctrine, the standing doctrine, and the post-*Chevron* deference framework, with substantial probability that significant portions of the legislation will be invalidated or narrowed in ways that the legislation's drafters did not intend. The second is that legislation operating through the spending power and tax-credit mechanisms — the structural choice that the Inflation Reduction Act largely made — has greater constitutional resilience than legislation operating through regulatory mandates, because spending and tax provisions face fewer doctrinal constraints than regulatory provisions do, but the spending-power framework itself is now subject to the constraints *NFIB v. Sebelius* introduced. The third is that any legislation seeking to address voting rights, racial justice, climate, or administrative reform must include explicit constitutional reasoning in its legislative findings and operational provisions, with attention to the specific doctrinal arguments the Court has used to invalidate prior legislation in these areas. The fourth is that the constitutional preparation for any future transformative wave will require the construction of a counter-infrastructure of legal scholarship, advocacy, and judicial appointment that operates on a multi-decade time horizon comparable to the Federalist Society's infrastructure, and that this construction is itself part of the strategic work the contemporary period requires.

The legislative procedural structure compounds the constitutional constraint. The Senate filibuster's effective requirement of sixty votes for substantial non-budget legislation, the budget reconciliation process's narrow scope under the Byrd Rule, the House Rules Committee's procedural control over legislative consideration, and the broader procedural architecture under which contemporary legislation is enacted produce structural conditions in which transformative legislation comparable to the historical waves is substantially more difficult to enact than it was during any of the prior waves. The historical waves operated under filibuster rules that required two-thirds for cloture (until 1975) but under congressional procedural conditions that permitted substantial bipartisan cooperation on substantive issues; the contemporary period operates under filibuster rules requiring three-fifths for cloture but under partisan-polarization conditions that have made the threshold effectively higher in operation than the formal rules require. The cumulative procedural architecture, combined with the constitutional architecture the conservative legal countermovement has constructed, produces an institutional opportunity structure for transformative legislation that is substantially less hospitable than the structures within which the historical waves operated.

The synthesis on the institutional-opportunity-structure dimension is therefore that the contemporary period operates under substantially less hospitable conditions than the historical waves. The federal judiciary has been actively constructing constitutional constraints on the kind of legislation that the historical waves produced. The legislative procedural structure requires supermajorities that contemporary partisan conditions do not produce. The administrative state has been substantially weakened by four decades of funding cuts and doctrinal constraints. The state-level landscape offers opportunity in some jurisdictions and active hostility in others. The institutional-opportunity-structure assessment is the dimension on which the contemporary period most severely underperforms the historical comparison.

### 2.4 Intellectual readiness: substantial assets, integration challenges

The intellectual readiness of the contemporary period is the dimension on which the contemporary period most clearly exceeds the historical waves. The scientific understanding of the substantive domains the project's framework engages — climate, biodiversity, ecological economics, decision quality, cognitive bias, polycentric governance, biomimicry, Indigenous knowledge — provides analytical resources that the historical waves' intellectual infrastructure did not approach. The implication is not that intellectual readiness is sufficient for transformative legislation — it manifestly is not — but that the intellectual foundation for any future transformative wave the contemporary period might eventually produce is substantially more sophisticated than the foundations the historical waves operated from.

The substantive intellectual assets are extensive. The climate-science infrastructure including the Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change, the World Climate Research Programme, and the network of national climate-research programs has produced empirical understanding of the climate system whose precision and sophistication exceed any prior period. The ecological-economics literature including the work of Herman Daly, Nicholas Georgescu-Roegen, Robert Costanza, Joan Martinez-Alier, and the broader scholarly community has produced an analytical framework for the embedded-economy paradigm that constitutes a substantive alternative to the inherited neoclassical framework. The decision-science literature including the work of Daniel Kahneman, Amos Tversky, Gerd Gigerenzer, Philip Tetlock, Cass Sunstein, Bent Flyvbjerg, Robert Lempert, and the broader scholarly community has produced analytical resources for evaluating decision quality that the historical waves did not have available. The polycentric-governance literature descending from Elinor Ostrom's work has produced an institutional framework that explicitly integrates the multi-scale dynamics that contemporary governance challenges require. The biomimicry framework descending from Janine Benyus's work and operationalized through Kate Raworth's doughnut economics has produced a positive design vocabulary for governance that the historical waves did not have. The Indigenous-knowledge integration descending from Robin Wall Kimmerer, Vine Deloria Jr., Kyle Whyte, Glen Coulthard, Leanne Betasamosake Simpson, Daniel Wildcat, and the broader Indigenous-scholarship community has produced institutional vocabulary that the historical waves did not engage at all.

The integration challenge for the intellectual-readiness dimension is the gap between the substantive intellectual development across these fields and the integration of the development into the political-coalition assembly the strategic project requires. The historical waves were preceded by intellectual movements that were substantially integrated with the political coalitions that produced the legislation — the institutionalist tradition with the New Deal, the legal-realist tradition with the post-1937 settlement, the civil-rights legal infrastructure with the Warren Court reorientation, the environmental academic infrastructure with the environmental wave. The contemporary intellectual readiness is more substantial than any of these prior intellectual movements but is substantially less integrated with the political coalitions that would translate it into legislative outcomes. The integration challenge is one of the principal strategic questions the project's framework engages, and it is the bridge between the intellectual readiness of the contemporary period and the political-coalition assembly that transformative legislation would require.

The strategic implication of the intellectual-readiness assessment is that the contemporary period has accumulated substantial intellectual assets that exceed the historical waves' intellectual foundations, but that the assets are not yet integrated into political-coalition infrastructure capable of translating them into legislative outcomes. The work of integration is itself part of the strategic work the contemporary period requires, and the project's documents are themselves contributions to that integration work.

### 2.5 The synthesis: intellectual readiness exceeds political readiness

The integration of the four-dimensional assessment produces a synthesis the project's framework can specify with reasonable confidence. The contemporary period has substantially greater empirical pressure than the historical waves but has not produced the empirical-political linkage that empirical severity in prior periods generated. The contemporary period has substantial movement infrastructure with substantial accumulated capacity but with structural limitations in cross-class organizing, geographic distribution, and the organizing-versus-mobilizing balance that constrain its political-force generation. The contemporary period operates under substantially less hospitable institutional opportunity structure than the historical waves, with the conservative legal countermovement having constructed constitutional and procedural constraints that substantially exceed the constraints prior waves operated within. The contemporary period has substantially greater intellectual readiness than any prior period in American history, with the substantive intellectual assets exceeding the intellectual foundations of any of the historical waves.

The synthesis is that the contemporary moment is not analogous to the New Deal moment of 1933 or the civil rights moment of 1963 in the immediate sense; the political conditions for transformative legislation comparable to the historical waves are not present, and the strategic work of the present must be infrastructure-building for an eventual moment whose timing cannot be predicted. The contemporary moment is, however, a moment of comparable historical significance — what the panarchy framework would identify as the late conservation phase of an adaptive cycle, in which institutional rigidity has accumulated to levels that the system can no longer absorb, in which empirical conditions are creating pressures that the existing institutional structure cannot manage, and in which the conditions for eventual release and reorganization are accumulating even though the release phase has not yet arrived. The strategic question is not whether the present is the moment of transformation; it is whether the work of the present is being conducted in ways that will permit the transformation when it eventually occurs.

The honest assessment that this analysis has produced is one that neither optimism nor despair captures accurately. Optimism that the present empirical severity will produce immediate political force comparable to the historical waves underestimates the cognitive and structural conditions that have weakened the empirical-political linkage. Despair that the present conditions are inadequate to the historical task underestimates the long-cycle dynamics through which transformative change has actually occurred and the substantial intellectual and organizational infrastructure that the contemporary period has accumulated. The strategic posture that the analysis prescribes is one of patient, multi-scale, multi-decade work, taking the contemporary conditions seriously as the actual operating environment rather than the conditions one might wish for, and recognizing that the work of building the conditions for transformation is itself the necessary work of the present even when the transformation cannot yet occur.

## PART THREE — THE PANARCHY REFRAMING AND THE STRATEGIC PRESCRIPTION

The four-dimensional assessment of Part Two produces a diagnostic that the contemporary period substantially exceeds the historical waves on intellectual readiness and empirical severity but substantially underperforms on the empirical-political linkage, on the political dimensions of movement infrastructure, and on the institutional opportunity structure. The diagnostic is honest but is also incomplete as a strategic guide. The remaining analytical work is to specify what the strategic prescription that follows from the diagnostic actually consists of, what its time horizons and operational layers are, and how it connects to the prior documents in the arc and to the documents that will follow. This Part conducts that strategic specification through the panarchy framework that Document Five and Document Six established as the project's analytical lens for systemic transformation.

### 3.1 The contemporary moment in the panarchy cycle

The panarchy framework, articulated in Lance Gunderson and C. S. Holling's *Panarchy: Understanding Transformations in Human and Natural Systems* (2002), specifies that complex adaptive systems cycle through four phases: the rapid-growth phase (r) in which novel components accumulate and exploit available resources; the conservation phase (K) in which the system becomes increasingly efficient, increasingly connected, and increasingly rigid; the release phase (Ω) in which accumulated rigidities collapse under the pressure of disturbance the system can no longer absorb; and the reorganization phase (α) in which the components released by collapse recombine into novel configurations that may or may not resemble the prior system. The cycle then returns to a new growth phase. The framework's central insight, as Document Five elaborated, is that the most stable-seeming, mature institutional systems — those with the highest K-phase accumulation of precedent, procedure, and political investment — are also the most brittle and the most resistant to the adaptive reorganization that changing conditions require.

Applied to the American constitutional system as engaged across this project's prior documents, the panarchy framework specifies a diagnostic that the empirical and analytical material assembled supports. The American constitutional system is not in a r-phase or K-phase early-conservation state; it has accumulated the institutional rigidities, the precedent-density, and the political-investment thickness that characterize late-K-phase or early-Ω-phase systems. The signs the framework specifies are visible. The constitutional doctrine is increasingly contested, with central precedents being overturned (*Dobbs*, the *Chevron* framework, *Roe*, the Voting Rights Act preclearance) and with constitutional authority increasingly disputed. The legitimacy of the institutional architecture is increasingly contested across the political spectrum, with the Court's approval ratings at historic lows, with congressional approval ratings consistently below twenty percent across the past two decades, and with the executive branch's institutional norms increasingly violated and contested. The administrative state is increasingly contested both doctrinally and politically, with the unitary-executive theory the project rejects gaining ground in constitutional doctrine and with the Schedule F executive-order architecture proposing the structural transformation of the civil service. The federalism architecture is increasingly contested, with state-level innovation in some jurisdictions running far ahead of federal capacity and with state-level retrenchment in others producing constitutional confrontations the framework had not anticipated. The empirical conditions the constitutional architecture must manage — climate, biodiversity, demographic transformation, technological transformation, the fiscal pressures of an aging population — are accumulating at rates the inherited institutional architecture is structurally unable to absorb.

The panarchy framework specifies that systems in late-K or early-Ω phases are not in equilibrium and not in stable adaptation; they are accumulating the conditions for release and reorganization that the framework predicts will eventually occur. The framework also specifies that the timing and form of the release-reorganization phase are not predictable in detail. The release can be triggered by any of a wide variety of disturbances that the rigidified system cannot absorb — economic crisis, ecological catastrophe, geopolitical confrontation, electoral discontinuity, judicial-legitimacy collapse, mass-casualty event, or some combination of these — and the reorganization that follows can take a wide variety of forms depending on which institutional and intellectual resources are available at the time of release. The strategic implication is that the work of the present cannot be the prediction of the release event or the prefiguration of the reorganization form, but the construction of the institutional and intellectual resources that will be available to the reorganization phase when it eventually arrives.

The framework's specification of the "remember" cross-scale interaction is particularly relevant. In Holling's panarchy, "remember" describes the way larger and slower scales provide the memory and context that allow smaller, faster scales to reorganize after collapse. In a forest fire, the soil seed bank persists through the fire and re-seeds the forest in the reorganization phase that follows; the fire does not destroy the forest's reorganizing capacity if the slower-cycle resources have been preserved. Applied to constitutional reorganization, the "remember" function is performed by the institutional resources, the intellectual frameworks, the demonstration cases, and the personnel networks that the present period accumulates and that will be available as resources to the reorganization phase whenever the release phase eventually concludes. The strategic significance is that the work of the present is the construction of the "remember" resources, not the construction of the reorganized constitutional architecture itself, and that the appropriate strategic posture is one of patient, multi-decade preparation under the recognition that the immediate political-legislative work the historical analogies might suggest is not feasible under current conditions.

### 3.2 The strategic prescription: multi-scale, multi-decade infrastructure-building

The strategic prescription that the panarchy framework produces is multi-scale, multi-decade infrastructure-building under hostile federal conditions, oriented toward the eventual reorganization phase that the panarchy framework specifies follows the release phase the inherited constitutional system has now entered. The prescription has specific components that distinguish it from the alternative strategic postures that contemporary political conversation has produced.

The prescription differs from the strategic posture of immediate-legislative-action that the historical analogies might suggest. The diagnosis of the four-dimensional assessment specifies that the political conditions for transformative legislation comparable to the historical waves are not present, and that legislative action under contemporary conditions can produce the partial achievements of the Affordable Care Act and the Inflation Reduction Act but cannot produce the transformative architecture that the project's diagnostic phase specified as necessary. The prescription does not relinquish legislative work — substantial legislative work at federal and state scales is part of the multi-decade infrastructure-building — but it does recognize that legislative achievement under contemporary conditions is partial and infrastructure-building rather than transformative.

The prescription differs from the strategic posture of constitutional convention or formal Article V amendment that some contemporary commentary has urged. The Article V amendment process, requiring two-thirds of both houses of Congress and three-fourths of the states for ratification, is structurally infeasible under contemporary conditions for substantive amendments addressing the project's framework's concerns. The constitutional-convention pathway, also requiring two-thirds of state legislatures to call and three-fourths of states to ratify any proposals, has not been used since 1787 and is procedurally untested in ways that produce substantial uncertainty about its operational feasibility, and the political-coalition that would assemble such a convention under contemporary conditions would not produce the substantive amendments the project's framework would support; if anything, a contemporary constitutional convention would be more likely to produce a constitutional architecture more hostile to the project's framework than the current architecture is. The strategic prescription therefore does not include the Article V or constitutional-convention pathways as principal components of the multi-decade work.

The prescription differs from the strategic posture of "exit" that some libertarian and some left-accelerationist commentary has urged. The proposal to abandon the federal constitutional framework in favor of state-level secession, regional confederation, or sub-federal community-construction underestimates the integration of the American economy, the cross-state externalities of climate and other ecological systems, the constitutional protections that exit-strategies would forfeit, and the historical record of secessionist proposals in American constitutional history. The strategic prescription operates within the assumption that the federal constitutional framework will continue to be the operating environment within which the multi-decade work is conducted, even as that framework is increasingly contested and increasingly transformed by the cycle the panarchy framework specifies.

The prescription differs from the strategic posture of "after-the-collapse" prefiguration that some apocalyptic commentary has urged. The proposal to focus exclusively on the construction of post-constitutional institutional architecture, on the assumption that the inherited federal framework will collapse imminently and the work of the present is to prefigure what comes after, underestimates the substantial political-economic resources of the inherited framework, the durability that complex institutional systems exhibit even in late-K phases, and the strategic costs of disengaging from the present institutional architecture before its collapse has actually occurred. The strategic prescription operates within the assumption that the inherited framework will continue to function — increasingly imperfectly, increasingly contested, increasingly transformed — across the multi-decade time horizon of the strategic project, and that the work of the present must engage the inherited framework rather than disengage from it.

The strategic prescription that the panarchy framework actually produces is multi-scale infrastructure-building across the operational layers that the next document in the arc will specify in detail. The infrastructure-building is multi-decade in time horizon — anchored in the recognition that the work of the present is to prepare the conditions for an eventual transformation whose specific timing and form cannot be predicted. The infrastructure-building is multi-scale — operating simultaneously at sub-national, international, and cognitive-individual scales because the panarchy cycle the project's framework operates within is multi-scale in its own dynamics. The infrastructure-building is integrative — recognizing that the substantive intellectual development across the project's analytical fields must be integrated with the political-coalition assembly that translates intellectual development into legislative and institutional outcomes.

### 3.3 The components of the multi-decade infrastructure-building

The components of the multi-decade infrastructure-building that the strategic prescription specifies can be enumerated through the historical pattern that Part One identified. The historical waves were the visible legislative phases of longer panarchy cycles whose components were specifiable: movement infrastructure-building, intellectual reorientation, litigation strategy, institutional preparation, and the political-coalition assembly that translates these components into legislative outcomes. The contemporary period must engage all of these components, with the recognition that some are substantially developed and others are substantially under-developed, and that the work of the present is to develop the components whose under-development constrains the eventual legislative possibilities.

Movement infrastructure-building under contemporary conditions must address the limitations the four-dimensional assessment identified. The decline in cross-class mass-membership civic associations, the geographic concentration of progressive infrastructure in coastal urban areas, and the systematic shift from organizing to mobilizing all constitute strategic challenges that the multi-decade infrastructure-building must address. The labor-organizing revival of the contemporary period — the Amazon Labor Union, Starbucks Workers United, the UAW Stand Up Strike, the Writers Guild and SAG-AFTRA strikes — represents one of the principal substantive developments that the multi-decade project should engage and support. The community-organizing revival represented by the People's Action network, the Center for Popular Democracy, the Working Families Party, the Movement for Black Lives, and the various state-level organizing infrastructures represents another substantive development. The strategic discipline is to support the organizing-rather-than-mobilizing methodologies that produce durable power-building, to channel resources toward cross-class and geographically dispersed organizing rather than toward the professionalized advocacy that has been the dominant mode of contemporary movement infrastructure, and to integrate the substantive issue-specific organizing across the climate, democracy, racial-justice, and economic-justice domains that contemporary movement infrastructure has often kept separate.

Intellectual reorientation under contemporary conditions involves the integration of the substantive intellectual development across the fields the project's framework engages — ecological science, decision theory, polycentric governance, biomimicry, doughnut economics, Indigenous knowledge, cognitive bias and political psychology — with the political-coalition assembly that translates intellectual development into political force. The historical pattern of the legal-realist tradition's integration with the post-1937 New Deal settlement, of the civil-rights legal infrastructure's integration with the Warren Court reorientation, of the environmental academic infrastructure's integration with the environmental wave, all specify that intellectual development without political-coalition integration does not produce legislative transformation. The contemporary intellectual development is substantially more sophisticated than any of these prior intellectual movements but is substantially less integrated with political-coalition infrastructure. The work of integration is itself part of the multi-decade infrastructure-building, and it requires the construction of institutions — research-and-advocacy organizations, foundation-funded networks, academic-practitioner partnerships, credentialing pipelines for the next generation of practitioner-scholars — that operate as bridges between the intellectual development and the political-coalition assembly.

Litigation strategy under contemporary conditions must operate within the constitutional architecture that the conservative legal countermovement has constructed. The strategic implication is that contemporary litigation strategy cannot replicate the offensive-litigation posture of the civil-rights and environmental waves, which operated within constitutional architectures substantially more hospitable to expansive interpretation of federal regulatory and rights-protective authority. The contemporary litigation strategy must include defensive litigation to preserve the institutional achievements of prior waves against the ongoing dismantling — the Voting Rights Act litigation, the Clean Air Act and Clean Water Act litigation, the antidiscrimination litigation — alongside offensive litigation in the substantive domains where the contemporary constitutional architecture permits advancement (state-level constitutional litigation, tribal-court Rights of Nature litigation, international human-rights litigation that operates outside the American constitutional architecture). The strategic discipline is to recognize that litigation strategy in the contemporary period is principally infrastructure-defensive rather than infrastructure-creative, and that the litigation work must integrate with the broader multi-decade project rather than operate as a separable strategic component.

Institutional preparation under contemporary conditions involves the construction of the regulatory, technical, and administrative capacity that any eventual transformative wave will require for sustained implementation. The historical pattern specifies that legislation operating through positive institutional construction (Social Security, Medicare, the EPA's regulatory architecture) tends to produce more durable transformation than legislation operating through prohibitions and rights enforcement, and that the institutional capacity that supports positive institutional construction must be built in advance of the legislation rather than constructed within the legislative process. The contemporary period has substantial institutional preparation in some domains — the climate-policy technical capacity built across state agencies, the policy-research capacity of the contemporary advocacy organizations, the administrative capacity that the Affordable Care Act and Inflation Reduction Act have begun to construct — and substantial gaps in others. The strategic discipline is to invest in institutional capacity-building that will be available as resources to eventual transformative legislation, including state-level regulatory agency capacity, the technical-academic infrastructure that supports rigorous policy development, and the personnel pipelines through which the next generation of public-administration practitioners will be trained.

Political-coalition assembly under contemporary conditions is the most challenging of the components, because the contemporary political-economic conditions — the partisan polarization, the geographic-political asymmetries, the cultural-cognition entrenchment — produce structural constraints on coalition assembly that the historical waves did not face in the same form. The historical pattern specifies that political coalitions for transformative legislation are assembled through movement pressure that crosses cultural-political identities — the New Deal coalition's combination of southern white Democrats, urban-immigrant constituencies, the labor movement, and agricultural constituencies; the civil rights coalition's combination of the Black community, religious networks, organized labor, and northern liberal voters — and that the assembly of such coalitions under contemporary conditions requires the systematic engagement of cultural-cognition divides that contemporary mobilization methodology has often failed to produce. The race-class narrative work of Anat Shenker-Osorio, the deep-canvassing methodology of Joshua Kalla and David Broockman, the More in Common research on hidden tribes and shared values, and the broader contemporary cognitive-aware mobilization methodology that Document Ten will engage in detail constitute the contemporary substantive resources for political-coalition assembly across cultural-cognition divides. The strategic discipline is to invest in these methodologies as substantive infrastructure rather than as messaging consultancy, and to integrate the methodologies with the organizing-rather-than-mobilizing approach that produces durable power-building.

### 3.4 The time horizon and the question of patience

The strategic prescription's specification of multi-decade time horizon deserves engagement on its own terms because it confronts a tension that the contemporary political moment has made acute. The empirical severity of the contemporary crises — climate change advancing on a trajectory that will exceed 1.5°C within the decade, biodiversity collapse accelerating, democratic erosion producing constitutional discontinuities the framers did not anticipate — produces urgency that the multi-decade time horizon may seem to inadequately respect. The tension between the urgency of the empirical conditions and the patience of the strategic prescription is not a tension the framework can resolve through clever formulation; it is a structural feature of the strategic situation that the project's framework must engage honestly.

The honest engagement specifies several considerations. The first is that the multi-decade time horizon does not imply the deferral of substantive action; it implies the recognition that substantive action under contemporary conditions can only be infrastructure-building rather than transformative, and that the substantive action of infrastructure-building must begin immediately rather than be deferred to the moment of transformation. The strategic prescription is not "wait until conditions improve"; it is "build the conditions through the substantive work of the present, recognizing that the transformation the work prepares is on a multi-decade horizon."

The second consideration is that the multi-decade horizon does not imply that the empirical severity of the contemporary crises will be tractable to whatever transformation eventually occurs. The climate trajectory the contemporary period has set in motion will produce substantial impacts regardless of when transformative legislation eventually occurs, and the impacts that occur during the multi-decade infrastructure-building period will be substantial and irreversible. The strategic prescription does not promise that the multi-decade work will avert the impacts the empirical analysis predicts; it promises only that the multi-decade work will construct the institutional and intellectual resources that the eventual reorganization phase will draw on, and that those resources are necessary even though they are not sufficient to prevent the empirical impacts the contemporary trajectory has set in motion. The honest assessment is that substantial damage will occur during the period the strategic prescription specifies, and the work of the present is to minimize the damage where possible and to construct the conditions for the eventual transformation that will determine what comes after the damage period.

The third consideration is that the multi-decade horizon has historical precedent. The Progressive Era's institutional preparation extended back at least to the 1880s; the New Deal harvested four decades of accumulated infrastructure; the civil rights wave harvested half a century of NAACP litigation and Black community organizing; the environmental wave harvested seven decades of conservation movement infrastructure descending from the Progressive Era. The historical pattern specifies that transformative legislation operates on multi-decade time horizons in its preparation, and the contemporary period's strategic situation is not anomalous in requiring multi-decade preparation but is simply at the early-to-middle stage of the cycle that historical analysis specifies. The patience the strategic prescription requires is not a counsel of resignation but a recognition of the time scales on which transformative change actually operates.

The fourth consideration is that the multi-decade work has the property that its components remain valuable even if the eventual transformation occurs in a form different from the form that present strategic analysis would prefer. The institutional capacity-building, the intellectual development, the movement infrastructure-building, the personnel pipelines, the demonstration cases — all of these remain valuable across a wide range of possible eventual transformation pathways, including pathways that the contemporary strategic analysis cannot fully anticipate. The strategic posture is therefore one of constructing components whose value is robust across multiple eventual scenarios rather than constructing components whose value depends on a specific scenario whose probability cannot be reliably assessed. The decision-quality literature's emphasis on robust decision-making applies to the strategic-prescription level: the multi-decade infrastructure-building should be designed for robustness across multiple eventual transformation pathways rather than optimized for a specific pathway whose probability the contemporary analysis cannot reliably specify.

### 3.5 The forward implications: three operational layers and the constitutional design horizon

The strategic prescription's articulation of multi-scale, multi-decade infrastructure-building leaves substantial questions about the operational specification that the prescription requires. The components enumerated above — movement infrastructure, intellectual reorientation, litigation strategy, institutional preparation, political-coalition assembly — operate as analytical categories rather than as operational layers. The translation of the strategic prescription into operational specification is the work of the document that should follow this one, and the closing of this Part identifies the three operational layers that the next document will develop in detail.

The first operational layer is state-level legislative and institutional innovation as the primary venue for operationalizing the project's framework under contemporary federal conditions. The contemporary federal political environment does not permit transformative legislation comparable to the historical waves, but the state-level political environment in some jurisdictions does permit substantial legislative and institutional innovation. The state-level layer constructs the demonstration cases, the regulatory and technical-agency capacity, and the political constituencies that any eventual federal transformation will require. The substantive domains for state-level innovation include climate, democratic reform, labor, Rights of Nature and ecological constitutionalism, doughnut economics and post-growth experiments, and Indigenous co-governance arrangements. The next document must engage the question of which categories of state-level innovation are most generative of the longer panarchy cycle, what design features distinguish durable from fragile innovation, and how state-level innovation integrates with the international and cognitive layers of the multi-decade project.

The second operational layer is the international and Indigenous-governance dimension that this document has not extensively developed but that constitutes a substantial component of the strategic project. The international climate and biodiversity governance frameworks — the Paris Agreement, the Kunming-Montreal Global Biodiversity Framework, the Loss and Damage Fund, the High Seas Treaty — operate as venues for normative development that influences American politics indirectly through specific causal pathways. The Rights of Nature jurisprudence developed across multiple jurisdictions — Ecuador, Bolivia, New Zealand, Colombia, the White Earth Band of Ojibwe, the Yurok Tribe — supplies institutional vocabulary that the inherited American constitutional structure cannot generate from within itself. The United Nations Declaration on the Rights of Indigenous Peoples and the Free, Prior and Informed Consent framework supply procedural vocabulary that the American constitutional architecture has not adequately incorporated. The Indigenous governance traditions — the Haudenosaunee Great Law of Peace, the Andean buen vivir tradition, the Ubuntu philosophical tradition, Two-Eyed Seeing, the grammar of animacy — supply load-bearing institutional vocabulary that the multi-decade project must engage on its own terms rather than as ornament. The next document must specify the strategic significance of these venues and traditions, the causal pathways through which they influence American politics, and the integration with the state-level and cognitive-aware mobilization layers of the multi-decade project.

The third operational layer is the methodologies of cognitive-aware political mobilization that close the political-force gap this document has identified as the binding constraint on transformative action. The cognitive science literature engaged across Documents Four and Seven and preliminarily in this document specifies why standard environmental, democratic-reform, and economic-justice messaging has systematically underperformed relative to its empirical basis. The cognitive-aware mobilization methodologies that contemporary practitioners have developed — the public-narrative methodology of Marshall Ganz, the deep-organizing methodology of Jane McAlevey, the organizing-versus-mobilizing distinction of Hahrie Han, the deep-canvassing methodology of Joshua Kalla and David Broockman, the race-class narrative work of Anat Shenker-Osorio, the climate-communication work of George Marshall and the Yale Program on Climate Change Communication — constitute substantive resources for closing the political-force gap. The deliberative-democracy methodologies — the Deliberative Polling work of James Fishkin, the open-democracy work of Hélène Landemore, the practical record of Citizens' Assemblies including the Irish abortion and climate assemblies and the French Convention Citoyenne pour le Climat — supply institutional methodologies for decision-quality production that the standard polling and electoral mechanisms systematically fail to produce. The next document must specify how cognitive-aware mobilization integrates with the state-level and international layers, what methodologies have produced measurable durable attitude change versus only short-term mobilization, and what the strategic discipline is that prevents cognitive-aware mobilization from degenerating into messaging consultancy rather than infrastructure-building.

The three operational layers identified here will be the subject of Document Ten of the project arc. The forward horizon beyond Document Ten is the constitutional-design question that Document Eight engaged in categories rather than in specifics. The eventual constitutional architecture the project's framework would prescribe — the substantive amendments, the institutional reforms, the structural changes that would address the diagnostic failures the prior documents identified — must eventually be articulated as a positive proposal that the multi-decade infrastructure-building is working toward. The articulation of that positive proposal is the appropriate subject of Document Eleven, after Document Ten has specified the operational layers within which the proposal would eventually be deployed.

## CLOSING INTEGRATION AND FORWARD IMPLICATIONS

The nine documents of this project arc, as they now stand, have constructed a cumulative argument whose structure is now substantially complete in its diagnostic dimension and is increasingly turning toward strategic and prescriptive questions. The first six documents built the diagnosis: the constitutional text and its structural pathologies (Document One); the Federalist Papers' internal tensions and what they reveal about the founders' assumptions (Document Two); the founders' post-ratification reconsiderations and what they show about how the designers themselves understood the limits of their work (Document Three); the biological and anthropological critique of the founders' assumptions about human nature, social organization, and human-environment relations (Document Four); the biomimicry, polycentric governance, and ecological-economics scholarship that offers a positive design vocabulary for governance (Document Five); and the capstone synthesis that integrated the diagnosis (Document Six). Documents Seven and Eight extended the framework: Document Seven into the cognitive, macroeconomic, and incommensurability dimensions of governance failure; Document Eight into the specific structural failures of the constitutional architecture as a climate-response system and the categories of constitutional redesign that would address them.

This Document Nine has begun the strategic phase of the work by examining how transformative legislation has actually been produced in American history, what the contemporary conditions permit and constrain, and what the project's framework prescribes as strategy under the conditions that obtain. The document's central conclusions can now be stated. First, the historical waves of transformative legislation were the visible legislative phases of much longer panarchy cycles of movement infrastructure-building, intellectual reorientation, litigation strategy, and institutional preparation; the legislation cannot be understood apart from the cycles, and the cycles cannot be reproduced through legislation alone. Second, the contemporary period has accumulated substantial assets — intellectual infrastructure of unprecedented sophistication, movement organizations of substantial scale and reach, state-level innovation in multiple policy domains, and the beginning of labor movement revival — but has not yet assembled the political coalition or the institutional opportunity structure that transformative legislation comparable to the historical waves would require. Third, the constitutional architecture that the historical waves operated within has been progressively weaponized against the regulatory and rights-protective purposes the legislation was designed to serve, and the contemporary federal judiciary is the visible product of a four-decade conservative legal countermovement that has been largely successful at constructing constitutional constraints on transformative legislation. Fourth, the project's framework prescribes patient, multi-scale, multi-decade work of infrastructure-building rather than premature legislative action, with the recognition that the work of the present is to prepare the conditions for an eventual transformation whose specific timing and form cannot yet be predicted.

The connection back to the prior documents is direct. The diagnosis those documents constructed identified the constitutional architecture as substantially incompatible with the analytical and ecological foundations that contemporary science makes available. The strategic analysis this document has constructed identifies that the constitutional transformation the diagnosis implies cannot be accomplished under contemporary political conditions and that the work of the present is to build the conditions under which it might eventually occur. The two analyses are not in tension; they are sequential phases of a single strategic logic that the panarchy framework makes intelligible. The diagnosis specifies what is wrong with the system. The strategy specifies what the present moment requires given that what is wrong cannot yet be transformed.

The forward implications for the project's continuing work are several. The next document in the arc must engage directly with the question of what kinds of state-level legislative and institutional innovation would be most productive of the conditions that eventual federal transformation will require, drawing on the polycentric governance framework and the actual experience of state-level innovation in the contemporary period. The same document must engage the international dimension that this document has noted but not extensively developed, including the Paris Agreement framework, the planetary boundaries governance discussions, and the international Indigenous rights infrastructure as venues that operate substantially outside the constraints of the American constitutional system. The same document must engage the cognitive-aware mobilization methodologies that close the political-force gap this document has identified as the binding constraint on transformative action, drawing on the cognitive science literature, the contemporary practitioner literature, and the deliberative-democracy institutional methodologies. The integration of these three operational layers as one strategy rather than three separable initiatives is the appropriate subject of Document Ten.

A subsequent document in the arc must engage the constitutional-design question that the prior documents have engaged in categories rather than in specifics. The eventual constitutional architecture that the project's framework would prescribe — the substantive amendments, the institutional reforms, the structural changes that would address the diagnostic failures the prior documents identified — must eventually be articulated as a positive proposal that the multi-decade infrastructure-building is working toward. The articulation of the positive proposal is the appropriate work of a document that follows after the operational layers have been specified, because the constitutional architecture the project will eventually propose is not a blueprint to be drafted in advance of the political conditions that would receive it but a horizon toward which the operational work assembles the conditions.

The honest acknowledgment that this document must offer in closing is that the strategic prescription it has produced is sobering rather than inspiring. The empirical severity of the contemporary crises is substantial; the strategic prescription's recognition that transformative legislation comparable to the historical waves is not feasible under current conditions does not match the urgency that the empirical severity would demand if urgency alone could produce political force. The project's framework's analytical honesty requires acknowledging this gap rather than papering over it with rhetorical optimism. The honest assessment is that substantial damage will occur during the multi-decade period the strategic prescription specifies, that the transformations the eventual reorganization phase produces will not necessarily be the transformations the project's framework would prescribe in the abstract, and that the work of the present is the construction of resources whose deployment will occur in conditions the contemporary analysis cannot fully predict. The strategic posture the project's framework adopts is not optimistic; it is committed. The commitment is to the substantive work of infrastructure-building under the recognition that the work matters across a wide range of possible futures, that the alternative postures (immediate-legislative-action, constitutional-convention, exit, after-the-collapse prefiguration) all underperform on substantive grounds, and that patient multi-decade work has the historical record's support as the strategy through which transformative change has actually been produced. The work of the present is the work the present permits, and the work the present permits is what the strategic prescription specifies.
