# The Anti-Federalist Case Against Ratification
## Arguments In Response To, and Independent Of, Each of the 85 Federalist Papers

---

# PREFATORY NOTE: METHODOLOGY AND SOURCES

This document proceeds paper by paper through all 85 Federalist essays, identifying for each:

1. **Direct responses** — arguments made by Anti-Federalist writers who read and specifically engaged with *Publius*, often citing specific passages, or whose arguments were clearly written in reply to the same constitutional provisions *Publius* was defending.

2. **Independent arguments** — Anti-Federalist positions that addressed the same subject matter but arose from their own analytical tradition rather than as rejoinders to Hamilton, Madison, or Jay.

The major Anti-Federalist sources drawn upon throughout:

- **"Brutus"** (Robert Yates, NY): 16 essays, Oct. 1787 – Apr. 1788, *New York Journal*
- **"Federal Farmer"** (likely Richard Henry Lee, VA): *Letters from the Federal Farmer*, Oct.–Nov. 1787; additional letters Jan.–May 1788
- **"Centinel"** (Samuel Bryan, PA): 24 essays, Oct. 1787 – Nov. 1788, *Independent Gazetteer*
- **"Agrippa"** (James Winthrop, MA): 16 essays, Nov. 1787 – Feb. 1788, *Massachusetts Gazette*
- **"An Old Whig"** (George Bryan or James Workman, PA): 8 essays, Oct.–Nov. 1787
- **"A Plebeian"** (Melancton Smith, NY): *An Address to the People of New York*, Apr. 1788
- **"Philadelphiensis"** (Benjamin Workman, PA): 12 essays, Nov. 1787 – Apr. 1788
- **"The Republican"** (NY): essays, Nov. 1787
- **Patrick Henry**: Virginia Ratification Convention speeches, June 1788
- **George Mason**: *Objections to the Constitution*, Oct. 1787; Virginia Convention speeches
- **Luther Martin**: *Genuine Information*, Nov. 1787 – Jan. 1788
- **Melancton Smith**: New York Ratification Convention speeches, June–July 1788
- **Robert Yates and John Lansing**: *Reasons for Refusing to Sign the Constitution*, Dec. 1787
- **"Cincinnatus"** (Arthur Lee, VA): 6 essays, Nov.–Dec. 1787
- **"A Georgian"**: essays, Nov. 1787
- **"Helvidius Priscus"** (MA): essays, Oct. 1787

Herbert Storing's *The Complete Anti-Federalist* (7 volumes, 1981) is the definitive collection. Cecelia Kenyon's *The Antifederalists* (1966) and Saul Cornell's *The Other Founders* (1999) provide essential scholarly context.

# THE ANTI-FEDERALIST CASE, PAPER BY PAPER

---

## FEDERALIST No. 1 — Hamilton: General Introduction

### Direct Response

**"Centinel" No. 1** (Samuel Bryan, Oct. 5, 1787): Bryan directly attacked Hamilton's rhetorical strategy of casting opponents as self-interested or ignorant. He argued that *Publius*'s framing — that those who oppose must be motivated by ambition or error — was itself a propaganda technique designed to preempt legitimate criticism:

> Such is the form of government which has been calculated for America... The wealthy and ambitious, who in every community think they have a right to lord it over their fellow creatures, have availed themselves very successfully of this favorable conjuncture; it remains for the yeomanry of this country to determine whether they will submit to despotism or assert their liberty.

Bryan accused Hamilton of framing the question as one of reason vs. passion precisely in order to delegitimize popular skepticism. Passion, he argued, was not on the Anti-Federalist side but on the side of those who rushed the Constitution to ratification before the public could deliberate.

**"An Old Whig" No. 1** (Oct. 12, 1787): Challenged Hamilton's claim that America faced a unique historical opportunity. He argued that the very urgency of the case for ratification was suspicious — that genuine reforms are deliberated carefully, while usurpations are hurried through before opposition can organize.

### Independent Arguments

**The Legitimacy of the Convention**: Luther Martin (*Genuine Information*, Nov. 1787) raised an argument Hamilton did not address: the Philadelphia Convention had no authority to produce a new constitution. It was commissioned to amend the Articles of Confederation. The product of its work was therefore revolutionary — a seizure of authority not granted — regardless of its merits. Hamilton's framing of "reflection and choice" versus "accident and force" omitted the crucial middle category: constitutional usurpation through formally peaceful but unauthorized means.

**The Stakes Cut Both Ways**: "Federal Farmer" No. 1 (Oct. 8, 1787) agreed with Hamilton that the stakes were high but drew the opposite conclusion: precisely because the question was so consequential, it demanded slow deliberation, not swift ratification. The argument that urgency required rapid decision-making was, Federal Farmer argued, a manipulation.

## FEDERALIST No. 2 — Jay: Dangers from Foreign Force and Influence

### Direct Response

**"Brutus" No. 2** implicitly engaged Jay's providential argument — the claim that America was a naturally unified nation whose unity divine providence had ratified by giving it a connected territory and common heritage. Brutus responded that geographic and cultural unity do not require political consolidation: Switzerland managed internal diversity and external threats without a consolidated national government. The argument that union requires the proposed Constitution specifically, rather than a reformed confederation, was asserted but not proved.

**"Agrippa" No. 4** (Dec. 3, 1787) directly contested Jay's claim of cultural and social unity. He argued that the thirteen states were profoundly different in their economies, social structures, legal traditions, and interests — New England commercial towns had almost nothing in common with Virginia plantation counties — and that this heterogeneity meant consolidated government would serve some at the expense of others.

### Independent Arguments

**Cultural Heterogeneity as a Constitutional Problem**: "Federal Farmer" No. 2 argued that Jay's vision of a homogeneous American people was a fiction. The actual diversity of the states — in religion, economy, social structure, and law — meant that a single national government would inevitably represent some portions of the country at the expense of others. Genuine representation required proximity between representatives and represented; Jay's providential unity obscured this structural problem.

**Foreign Influence and Republican Corruption**: Patrick Henry (Virginia Convention, June 5, 1788) turned Jay's foreign-influence argument against the Constitution: it was the Constitution itself, with its treaty-making power concentrated in the President and Senate, that created the greatest risk of foreign corruption and influence. A monarch negotiating in secret with foreign powers was exactly what the Constitution authorized. Jay said foreign influence was the danger; Henry said the Constitution was the vector.

## FEDERALIST No. 3 — Jay: Dangers from Foreign Force (continued)

### Direct Response

**"Federal Farmer" No. 3** challenged Jay's assumption that wise, experienced men selected from the whole country would make better foreign policy than state governments. This assumed that national leaders would be qualitatively superior to state leaders — an aristocratic premise that contradicted republican principles. The selection of the "best men" from a continental scale would in practice mean the selection of those with the resources, connections, and visibility to operate nationally: wealthy merchants, large landowners, and men of legal distinction. This was not superior wisdom; it was class bias laundered as expertise.

### Independent Arguments

**Treaty Violations and State Rights**: George Mason's *Objections* noted that the Constitution gave federal courts jurisdiction over all treaty-related disputes, effectively stripping state courts of authority over matters that had previously been within their competence. Jay's concern about state treaty violations was legitimate, but his solution — federal judicial supremacy — was disproportionate to the problem and threatened the entire structure of state sovereignty over domestic relations.

## FEDERALIST No. 4 — Jay: The Same Subject Continued

### Direct Response

**"Brutus" No. 9** (Jan. 17, 1788) contested Jay's argument that a unified nation would be less likely to be attacked because it would present a more formidable military posture. Brutus argued that this logic led directly to the case for a large standing army — which was precisely what republicans most feared. The price of the national security Jay promised was a permanent military establishment that would ultimately threaten domestic liberty more than any foreign power.

### Independent Arguments

**Neutrality as an Alternative**: Several Anti-Federalists argued that the real security against foreign attack was not military strength but commercial neutrality — the policy America had nominally pursued since 1776. A republic that stayed out of European quarrels, maintained open commerce with all parties, and avoided entangling alliances needed no large national army. The Constitution's framework for foreign policy — executive treaty-making, congressional war powers — was more likely to draw America into European wars than to keep it out of them.

---

## FEDERALIST No. 5 — Jay: Dangers from Disunion

### Direct Response

**"Agrippa" Nos. 4–5** directly contested Jay's argument that disunion would produce interstate warfare by citing the actual history of relations among the states since independence. Despite the acknowledged defects of the Articles, the states had not gone to war with each other, had not developed competing military establishments, and had generally maintained peaceful relations. Jay's scenario of interstate rivalry escalating to war was speculative; the actual record was more benign than Jay acknowledged.

### Independent Arguments

**The Multiple Confederacies Alternative**: "Federal Farmer" No. 2 proposed what no single Federalist paper fully engaged: that the choice was not between the present Constitution and dissolution into thirteen separate states, but might include the formation of two or three regional confederacies — a northern commercial confederacy and a southern agricultural one — each small enough to be genuinely republican and large enough to provide collective security. Jay addressed this possibility only to dismiss it; he did not engage the affirmative argument for its merits.

---

## FEDERALIST No. 6 — Hamilton: Dangers from Dissensions Between States

### Direct Response

**"Federal Farmer" No. 4** challenged Hamilton's historical catalogue of wars among republics. Hamilton was demonstrating that commercial republics were not naturally peaceful — but Federal Farmer argued that the lesson of that history was not that republics needed a powerful consolidated government, but that *small* republics had been destroyed by the machinations of powerful neighbors and by the absence of democratic accountability. The remedy for the pathologies Hamilton described was deeper republicanism at the state level, not consolidation into a large national republic.

**"Agrippa" No. 6** specifically contested Hamilton's argument that commercial rivalry between states would produce war. He noted that the most commercially intensive relationships among the states — New England and the mid-Atlantic — were also the most peaceful, suggesting that commercial interdependence produced cooperation rather than conflict.

### Independent Arguments

**Commerce and Peace**: Several Anti-Federalists drew on a strand of Enlightenment thought that Hamilton conspicuously omitted: the argument (associated with Montesquieu, Hume, and later Kant) that commercial relations tend to produce peace rather than war because trading partners have mutual interests in each other's prosperity. Hamilton in No. 6 attacked the "visionary" idea that commercial republics are naturally peaceful — but the Anti-Federalists noted that the actual record of relations among the American states, which were already commercially integrated, supported the pacific commerce thesis rather than Hamilton's Realpolitik.

---

## FEDERALIST No. 7 — Hamilton: Sources of Interstate Conflict

### Direct Response

**"Brutus" No. 1** (Oct. 18, 1787) anticipated and directly engaged Hamilton's enumeration of potential interstate conflicts by noting that the Constitution's solution — consolidated national authority — created a more serious problem than the interstate conflicts it was designed to prevent. Hamilton listed territorial disputes, commercial rivalries, and debt apportionment as potential sources of conflict. Brutus responded: these were all manageable through treaty-like negotiations among sovereign states, as the history of European diplomacy demonstrated. The alternative — subordinating all these disputes to a single national sovereign — solved the problem by eliminating state sovereignty entirely, which was a cure far worse than the disease.

### Independent Arguments

**Western Lands and Sectional Interest**: Luther Martin (*Genuine Information*) argued that Hamilton's treatment of western lands as a potential source of interstate conflict revealed a sectional bias: the Constitution's provisions for western territories would serve the interests of large eastern land-speculators at the expense of both existing western settlers and future small landowners. The conflict over western lands was real, but the Constitution resolved it in favor of the wealthy and well-connected.

---

## FEDERALIST No. 8 — Hamilton: Consequences of Hostilities Between States

### Direct Response

**"Centinel" No. 2** (Oct. 24, 1787) turned Hamilton's garrison-state argument on its head. Hamilton argued that disunion would produce standing armies and military government. Centinel argued that it was the Constitution *itself* — by creating a powerful central government with unlimited military authority — that produced the garrison state. The very scenario Hamilton described as the consequence of disunion was, Centinel contended, the design of the proposed Constitution.

**Patrick Henry** (Virginia Convention, June 5, 1788): "A standing army we shall have, also, to execute the laws at the point of the bayonet — a terrible mode of legislation." Henry made precisely the inverse of Hamilton's argument: it was the consolidated national government of the Constitution, not disunion, that required and authorized a standing military force that would be turned against domestic dissenters.

### Independent Arguments

**The English Civil War as Counterexample**: Several Anti-Federalists noted that England — a consolidated unitary state — had experienced a devastating civil war in the seventeenth century, demonstrating that consolidation did not prevent military government. Hamilton's argument implied that political union produced peace; the historical record showed that internal civil wars were at least as common in consolidated states as in confederate ones.

---

## FEDERALIST No. 9 — Hamilton: The Union as Safeguard Against Faction

### Direct Response

**"Brutus" No. 1** engaged Hamilton's invocation of Montesquieu directly and at length. Hamilton had argued that Montesquieu's endorsement of confederate republics for larger territories could accommodate the proposed Constitution. Brutus responded that Hamilton had misread Montesquieu: what Montesquieu endorsed was a *confederation* of small republics — each internally self-governing, each genuinely small-scale — not a *consolidated* national republic dressed in federal language. The proposed Constitution created the latter, not the former.

Brutus also contested Hamilton's claim that the "science of politics" had advanced sufficiently to overcome Montesquieu's size objection. The improvements Hamilton listed — separation of powers, legislative checks, independent courts, elected representation — were all known to Montesquieu and did not address his central concern: that large republics would be ungovernable because their representatives could not maintain genuine connection with distant constituents.

**"Federal Farmer" No. 2**: The proposed system was not a confederate republic in Montesquieu's sense — it was a national government with some federal features retained as concessions to the states. Calling it a "confederate republic" was a rhetorical maneuver that obscured the degree of consolidation actually proposed.

### Independent Arguments

**The Confederation as Genuinely Federal**: Yates and Lansing (*Reasons for Refusing to Sign*, Dec. 1787): The proper response to the acknowledged defects of the Articles was reform of the federal structure — strengthening the confederation's capacity to act — not its replacement by a national government. The choice Hamilton presented — present Articles or proposed Constitution — omitted the actually available middle path.

## FEDERALIST No. 10 — Madison: The Extended Republic and Faction

### Direct Response

This is the Anti-Federalist response that most concerns modern scholars, because Madison's No. 10 is now regarded as the constitutional theorist's lodestar.

**"Brutus" No. 1** is the most sustained and prescient direct engagement. Brutus made four arguments against Madison's extended-republic theory:

*First*, the filtering argument fails in practice. Madison claimed that large electoral districts would filter out demagogues and elevate statesmen. Brutus responded that large districts would instead filter out *ordinary citizens* and elevate those with the resources, name recognition, and political networks to campaign across large constituencies — the wealthy, the legally prominent, the well-connected. The extended republic would produce not a natural aristocracy of wisdom but a political aristocracy of wealth.

*Second*, multiplying factions is not the same as protecting rights. Madison argued that no single faction could form a majority in a large republic. Brutus responded: what prevents *minority* tyranny? If no majority can form, a well-organized minority with concentrated interests — precisely the propertied classes Hamilton and Madison themselves represented — can capture the legislative agenda. The multiplicity of factions protects against popular majorities but not against elite minorities.

*Third*, the extended republic destroys the conditions for republican self-government. In a large republic, citizens cannot know their representatives, representatives cannot know their constituents, and the social basis for trust and accountability dissolves. Republican government requires not just formal electoral mechanisms but a living social relationship between governors and governed — which only small-scale polities can sustain.

*Fourth*, Brutus — with remarkable prescience — predicted that the extended republic would develop not into the cacophony of competing interests Madison described, but into a two-faction system: "There will be a constant clashing of opinions; and the representatives of one part will be continually striving against those of the other." The extended republic would produce not Madisonian pluralism but sectional or class polarization.

**"Federal Farmer" No. 3**: Madison's theory required that the multiplication of factions produce genuine political competition. But factions are not equal — some have far more resources, organization, and access than others. A system in which tobacco planters, textile merchants, and frontier farmers all compete will not produce outcomes reflecting all their interests equally. It will produce outcomes reflecting the interests of those with the greatest capacity for sustained political organization. Madison's model systematically underestimated the role of structural economic power in shaping factional competition.

**Melancton Smith** (New York Convention, June 21, 1788): Directly engaged Madison's No. 10 by arguing that the filtering of representatives in large electoral districts would produce a legislature dominated by "the natural aristocracy" — men of "birth, education, talents, and wealth." Smith did not object to wisdom in government but insisted that a legislature that did not *resemble* the people in social composition could not faithfully represent them. The extended republic's electoral filtration was not a feature; it was a fatal flaw.

### Independent Arguments

**The Social Basis of Representation**: "Federal Farmer" Nos. 7–8 developed the representation argument at length: genuine representation requires that the body of representatives resemble the body of the represented — not just that members be elected, but that they reflect the diversity of occupations, social positions, and economic interests of the citizenry. A House of 65 members chosen by hundreds of thousands could not achieve this. The *numbers* of representation were so inadequate that genuine representation was impossible regardless of electoral mechanisms.

**Faction as Community, Not Just Interest**: Several Anti-Federalists implicitly challenged Madison's definition of faction as interest-based. For writers like "Agrippa," the relevant political communities were not competing material interests but *communities of shared life* — the towns, counties, and regions whose members knew each other, worshipped together, and governed their common affairs through shared institutions. These communities were not the factions Madison feared; they were the substance of republican self-government. Dissolving them into a continental republic did not tame faction; it destroyed the social infrastructure of political life.

## FEDERALIST No. 11 — Hamilton: Commercial Benefits of Union

### Direct Response

**"Agrippa" Nos. 8–9** directly contested Hamilton's argument that a unified commercial policy under a powerful national government would benefit American commerce. Agrippa argued from New England's experience: Massachusetts and the other northern commercial states had developed their economies without federal commercial regulation and had done so successfully. Hamilton's vision of a unified commercial republic would subordinate the specific commercial interests of each region to a single national policy — which in practice meant commercial policy designed by and for the dominant economic interests in the national legislature, which would inevitably be the large landed and mercantile interests of the mid-Atlantic and southern states.

**"Centinel" No. 5**: Hamilton's vision of American commercial supremacy required a powerful navy, which required taxation, which required a stronger federal government. But the argument had not been made that the commercial benefits of naval supremacy would be distributed equitably across all regions and classes. Hamilton's commercial republic would enrich merchants and large landowners at the expense of small farmers and artisans.

### Independent Arguments

**Sectional Commercial Interests**: Luther Martin argued that Hamilton's commercial vision was structurally biased toward the commercial states. The southern states' economy was agricultural and export-oriented — they needed free access to foreign markets, not the protective commercial regime Hamilton envisioned. A national commercial policy that served northern manufacturing and shipping would actively damage southern agriculture.

## FEDERALIST No. 12 — Hamilton: Revenue and Union

### Direct Response

**"Brutus" No. 5** (Nov. 30, 1787) directly engaged Hamilton's revenue argument. Hamilton had claimed that commercial prosperity generated by union would provide sufficient federal tax revenue through import duties, minimizing the need for direct taxation. Brutus contested this: import duties were already levied by states for their own revenue. Federal duties on top of state duties would either duplicate the tax burden or require the elimination of state revenue sources — which would make states financially dependent on federal grants or compelled to rely on more burdensome forms of direct taxation.

**"Centinel" No. 4**: Hamilton's claim that commercial union would reduce the need for direct taxation was internally inconsistent with his argument in No. 23 that the federal government needed unlimited power to tax. If import duties were sufficient, why was unlimited taxing power necessary?

### Independent Arguments

**Who Bears the Tax Burden**: "An Old Whig" No. 5 made the distributional argument that Hamilton systematically avoided: import duties fall most heavily on consumers of imported goods, who are disproportionately the poor and middling classes. Hamilton's revenue program, however efficient in the aggregate, was regressive in its incidence — it would fund a national government primarily through taxes that fell hardest on those least able to pay.

---

## FEDERALIST No. 13 — Hamilton: Economy of Government

### Direct Response

**"Federal Farmer" No. 3**: Hamilton's claim that a single national government would be cheaper than multiple confederacies was plausible for military expenditure but false for governance generally. The administrative apparatus required to govern a continent — the courts, the revenue officers, the postal network, the land offices — would necessarily be larger, more expensive, and more removed from the people than comparable state-level institutions. Economies of scale in military matters did not translate to economies of scale in civil administration; the opposite was more likely.

### Independent Arguments

**Administrative Costs of Distance**: "Brutus" No. 3 argued that the cost of governance is not merely financial but includes the costs of compliance, access, and participation. Citizens who must travel to distant federal courts to obtain justice, pay federal tax collectors without recourse to local institutions, and participate in elections for representatives they cannot know pay costs that Hamilton's financial analysis ignored entirely.

## FEDERALIST No. 14 — Madison: The Extended Republic Answered

### Direct Response

**"Brutus" No. 1** had anticipated and rejected Madison's distinction between *democracy* (direct, requiring small territory) and *republic* (representative, compatible with large territory). Brutus argued that the distinction was formally valid but practically misleading. Representative democracy in a large territory would fail not because citizens could not physically travel to vote but because:

1. Representatives cannot maintain genuine accountability to distant constituents
2. Citizens cannot acquire the information necessary to evaluate distant representatives
3. The social conditions for civic engagement — community knowledge, reputational accountability, shared fate — dissolve with distance

The formal machinery of elections survives the scaling to continental size; the substance of republican self-government does not.

**"Federal Farmer" No. 2**: Madison's appeal to American patriotic exceptionalism — "rear the fabrics of governments which have no model on the face of the globe" — was not an argument but a rhetorical gesture. The lack of historical precedent for a large-scale republic was not evidence that such a republic would succeed; if anything, it was evidence for caution.

### Independent Arguments

**Communication as a Republican Requirement**: Several Anti-Federalists made an argument about communication infrastructure that Madison did not address: republican self-government requires that citizens be informed about their government's actions, that they be able to petition and organize, and that their voices reach their representatives. The communication technology of the 1780s — correspondence by post, travel by horse — made genuine informational connection between a continental legislature and its constituents practically impossible. Madison's republic would govern citizens who had no real knowledge of its proceedings.

## FEDERALIST No. 15 — Hamilton: Insufficiency of the Articles

### Direct Response

**Melancton Smith** (New York Convention) accepted Hamilton's diagnosis of the Articles' defects more readily than most Anti-Federalists, but contested the cure. The Articles failed because they lacked revenue-raising capacity and could not enforce federal obligations on states. The cure for these specific defects was targeted: give Congress the power to levy import duties and to enforce its requisitions. The Constitution's cure — replacing the entire federal structure with a consolidated national government — was disproportionate to the diagnosed disease.

**"Federal Farmer" No. 3**: Hamilton's critique of the Articles conflated structural defects with contingent failures. The Articles failed partly because of their structural design (requisition-based finance, unanimity requirement for amendment) and partly because of contingent political failures (Rhode Island's blocking of reform, state unwillingness to pay requisitions during the postwar depression). The structural defects could be addressed without wholesale constitutional replacement.

### Independent Arguments

**The Adequacy of Confederation Reform**: Yates and Lansing argued that the Convention had been given authority to reform the confederation, not replace it — and that reforms adequate to address Hamilton's specific list of defects (revenue, commerce, treaty enforcement) could have been accomplished without abandoning the federal principle that sovereignty resided in the states.

**The Role of State Non-Compliance**: Several Anti-Federalists pointed out that the states' failure to comply with congressional requisitions was itself a symptom of a deeper problem that the Constitution did not address: the absence of public confidence in and identification with the national government. A government that citizens do not regard as legitimate will face non-compliance regardless of its formal powers. The Constitution gave the national government enforcement tools but did not address the legitimacy deficit that caused the compliance problem.

## FEDERALIST No. 16 — Hamilton: The Coercion of States

### Direct Response

**"Brutus" No. 1**: Hamilton argued that a government operating on states must ultimately use military force against them — which is indistinguishable from war. Therefore government must operate directly on individuals. Brutus accepted the logic but rejected Hamilton's conclusion. The alternative to military coercion of states was not federal coercion of individuals — it was a federal government with powers genuinely limited to matters of national concern (defense, foreign relations, interstate commerce), leaving all other governance to states. Hamilton's binary — coerce states militarily or coerce individuals through federal law — omitted the option of genuinely limited federal power.

### Independent Arguments

**The Democratic Deficit of Federal Enforcement**: "An Old Whig" No. 5 argued that federal enforcement against individuals — the proposed alternative to coercing states — required federal courts, federal revenue officers, and federal marshals operating throughout the country. These federal enforcement agents would be appointed by the national government, not elected, and would be accountable to Washington rather than to local communities. Replacing state non-compliance with federal enforcement was not obviously an improvement in republican governance.

## FEDERALIST No. 17 — Hamilton: States Retain Dominant Loyalty

### Direct Response

**"Brutus" No. 3**: Hamilton argued that state governments would always retain greater popular loyalty than the distant national government because citizens' daily lives were governed by state institutions. Brutus agreed with this empirical observation but drew from it the opposite policy conclusion: if state governments command greater loyalty, then concentrating power at the national level is fundamentally anti-republican — it places decisive authority in the hands of the least-trusted, least-understood, most-remote institution. The Federalist argument proved too much: if states naturally command more loyalty, the national government needs more coercive authority to overcome that loyalty — a self-defeating dynamic.

**"Federal Farmer" No. 3**: Hamilton's prediction that the federal government would be content with limited powers — because state loyalty would naturally limit its reach — required assuming that federal officials would not pursue the expansion of federal power that their institutional interests incentivized. This was naive. Every government tends toward expansion; without structural limitations, the federal government would systematically use its superior resources and authority to overcome state resistance.

### Independent Arguments

**Prophecy of Federal Expansion**: Anti-Federalists across multiple essays predicted, with remarkable accuracy, that the national government's powers under the Necessary and Proper Clause, the Supremacy Clause, and the Commerce Clause would expand over time to absorb most significant governance authority, regardless of the original intent to preserve state autonomy. Hamilton's reassurance that state loyalty would check federal expansion relied on a static model of institutional behavior that ignored dynamic incentives toward power maximization.

## FEDERALIST No. 18 — Madison and Hamilton: The Amphictyonic League

### Direct Response

**"Federal Farmer" No. 6**: The historical analogy to ancient confederacies was methodologically suspect. The Amphictyonic League and similar confederacies failed for reasons specific to their contexts — military technology, geographic circumstances, economic organization — that did not straightforwardly apply to the American situation. Hamilton and Madison used historical analogies selectively, choosing examples that supported their argument and ignoring contrary evidence (the Swiss Confederation, which managed a multi-lingual, multi-religious confederation with considerable success for centuries).

### Independent Arguments

**History Cuts Both Ways**: "Agrippa" No. 11 argued that if the history of ancient confederacies demonstrated the dangers of confederal weakness, it equally demonstrated the dangers of consolidated empire. Rome — the endpoint of Mediterranean political consolidation — did not represent a model of republican success; it represented the trajectory from republic to military dictatorship to empire. If the lesson of No. 18 was that confederacies fail, the lesson of Roman history was that consolidated empires destroy liberty.

---

## FEDERALIST No. 19 — Madison and Hamilton: The German Empire

### Direct Response

**"Agrippa" No. 11**: The German Empire and the Polish republic were extreme cases specifically chosen to illustrate confederal failure. The Swiss Confederation — which Hamilton and Madison did not examine comparably — was a successful confederal system that had survived for centuries precisely by developing institutions for managing diversity without consolidated central authority. The selective use of historical cases undermined the analytical claim to be deriving lessons from history.

### Independent Arguments

**The American Experience**: Anti-Federalists consistently pointed to the American colonial experience itself as a better historical guide than ancient or European precedents. The colonies had governed themselves effectively with minimal central authority for 150 years. The suggestion that Americans were constitutionally incapable of self-governance at the state level — which Hamilton's historical examples implied — contradicted their own demonstrated capacity.

---

## FEDERALIST No. 20 — Madison and Hamilton: The Dutch Republic

### Direct Response

**"Centinel" No. 5**: The Dutch Republic's difficulties were partly structural (the confederal design) and partly historical (the specific dynamics of Dutch domestic politics and the Wars of the Spanish and Austrian Successions). Neither analysis supported the conclusion that the American states required the same degree of centralization that the Constitution proposed. The Dutch Republic's commercial success — which Hamilton had cited in No. 11 as a model — was achieved despite its confederal structure, not because it was replaced.

---

## FEDERALIST No. 21 — Hamilton: Additional Defects of the Articles

### Direct Response

**"Federal Farmer" No. 5**: Hamilton's third defect — no regulation of commerce — was real but not as serious as he claimed. The states had been regulating interstate commerce through reciprocal arrangements since 1776, with imperfect but not catastrophic results. The Navigation Acts, not the Articles' commercial provisions, were the main source of commercial inequality — and the Constitution did nothing to address foreign commercial discrimination, which remained at the mercy of executive treaty-making.

**"Brutus" No. 6** (Dec. 27, 1787): Hamilton's list of defects was accurate but his proposed cure created problems as large as those it addressed. The absence of a sanction under the Articles was real; the proposed sanction — federal coercion of individuals through courts, marshals, and ultimately the military — created an apparatus of federal enforcement that was incompatible with genuine republican self-government.

---

## FEDERALIST No. 22 — Hamilton: More Defects of the Articles

### Direct Response

**"Federal Farmer" No. 4**: Hamilton's criticism of equal state representation in Congress as producing voting inequity was only valid from a purely nationalist perspective. The equal state vote reflected the federal principle that the Union was an alliance among sovereign states. If the states were equal sovereigns, equal voting was appropriate. The Federalist objection to equal state representation was simultaneously an argument *against federalism itself* — against the principle that the states were the primary political communities, with the national government their agent.

**"Brutus" No. 4**: Hamilton's appeal to "the pure, original fountain of legitimate authority" — the people themselves, ratifying the Constitution through state conventions rather than state legislatures — was rhetorically powerful but concealed a significant sleight of hand. The people were voting separately in state conventions; they were not voting as a single national people. The Constitution's ratification process was not a single national democratic act but thirteen separate state acts, each of which could be interpreted as the state people ratifying an agreement with the other states. This was more consistent with a federal compact than with a national constitution.

---

## FEDERALIST No. 23 — Hamilton: Adequacy of Federal Power

### Direct Response

**"Brutus" No. 8** (Jan. 10, 1788): Hamilton's argument that the means must be adequate to the ends was formally valid but materially dangerous. If the ends include "common defence" and "general welfare," and if Congress is the judge of what is necessary to achieve these ends, then there are no effective limits on federal power. The argument — that the powers must match the ends — could justify any expansion of federal authority that Congress chose to rationalize as necessary. The Constitution had no mechanism to prevent this rationalization from proceeding without limit.

**"Centinel" No. 6**: "Adequate power" was indistinguishable from unlimited power if the body possessing the power was also the judge of its adequacy. Hamilton's principle — the means must be proportionate to the ends — required some external standard for measuring adequacy. The Constitution provided no such standard; it left adequacy determination to Congress, supervised by a Supreme Court appointed by the national government.

### Independent Arguments

**Enumeration as the Constitutional Principle**: Anti-Federalists consistently argued that the proper response to the inadequacy argument was to enumerate specifically what additional powers the federal government needed, not to grant open-ended authority to determine its own powers. The Articles could have been amended to grant specifically the revenue, commerce, and treaty-enforcement powers that Hamilton identified as lacking. The leap from "these specific powers are inadequate" to "therefore grant unlimited power" was a logical non sequitur dressed in constitutional argument.

---

## FEDERALIST No. 24 — Hamilton: Peacetime Standing Armies

### Direct Response

**"Centinel" No. 7**: Hamilton's defense of the absence of a constitutional prohibition on peacetime standing armies rested partly on the claim that only two state constitutions had such prohibitions. Centinel disputed this reading of state constitutional law and argued that the practice of state constitutions reflected the principle — inherited from the English Bill of Rights — that standing armies in peace required legislative authorization, not merely the absence of prohibition.

**"An Old Whig" No. 6**: Hamilton argued that the two-year appropriation limit on army funding was an adequate substitute for a categorical prohibition on peacetime standing armies. Old Whig responded: a legislature that controls military appropriations can easily rationalize any standing force it chooses to maintain, particularly when the executive controls the formulation of military needs and the assessment of threats. The two-year limit was a procedural requirement without substantive content — it required Congress to vote on appropriations regularly, not to limit the size of the army.

### Independent Arguments

**English Constitutional History**: The Anti-Federalist argument against standing armies drew on a century of English constitutional history that Hamilton's essays systematically underweighted. The Petition of Right (1628), the debates of the Interregnum, and the English Bill of Rights (1689) all reflected hard-won constitutional learning: standing armies are the instrument of executive tyranny, and their absence or limitation is a precondition of civil liberty. Hamilton's invocation of "modern" military requirements to override this constitutional tradition was exactly the kind of argument that each generation of would-be tyrants had made to justify military power.

---

## FEDERALIST No. 25 — Hamilton: Federal Military Power

### Direct Response

**Patrick Henry** (Virginia Convention, June 9, 1788): Hamilton argued that the alternative to federal military power was state militaries that would be turned against each other. Henry argued that the real danger was opposite: federal military power would be turned against the states themselves — against state governments attempting to exercise their reserved powers, against state populations resisting federal taxation or federal law. Henry's vision of federal military suppression of state resistance was, within a century, largely realized in the Civil War and Reconstruction.

### Independent Arguments

**The Militia as Alternative**: Anti-Federalists consistently proposed the well-regulated state militia as the institutional alternative to both federal standing armies and state standing armies. A citizen militia — armed, organized, and trained by the states under congressional standards — could provide national defense without creating the permanent professional military establishment that was the historic instrument of domestic tyranny. Hamilton's dismissal of militia effectiveness in Nos. 24–29 was empirically disputed by the performance of state militias in the Revolutionary War.

---

## FEDERALIST No. 26 — Hamilton: Legislative Control of Military

### Direct Response

**"Brutus" No. 9**: Hamilton argued that legislative oversight — specifically the two-year appropriation limit — was more reliable than constitutional prohibitions in checking military power. Brutus responded that legislative oversight was only as reliable as the legislature itself, and that a legislature elected from large districts under the influence of a powerful executive could be expected to defer to executive military judgments, not to check them. The history of the English Parliament's relationship with the Crown demonstrated that legislatures routinely surrendered military oversight under the pressure of claimed emergency.

**"Philadelphiensis" No. 7**: The argument that Congress would effectively check executive military power assumed that Congress would maintain genuine independence from the executive — but the Constitution's provisions on appointments, executive influence over legislation through the veto, and the President's role as commander-in-chief all created structural dependencies that would prevent effective congressional oversight of military affairs.

---

## FEDERALIST No. 27 — Hamilton: Federal Attachment and State Officers

### Direct Response

**"Brutus" No. 5**: Hamilton argued that citizens would develop loyalty to the federal government through experience with its institutions, and that state officials swearing oaths to the Constitution effectively became federal agents. Brutus treated the second point as the most alarming part of Hamilton's argument: if state officials are agents of the federal system, then the states themselves become administrative subdivisions of the national government rather than independent sovereigns. This was precisely the consolidation that Madison had denied was intended — and Hamilton was admitting it openly.

**"Federal Farmer" No. 5**: The theory that federal loyalty would develop through experience assumed that federal institutions would be experienced as beneficial rather than burdensome. But the federal tax collector, the federal court imposing judgments at a distance, and the federal marshal enforcing alien and sedition laws would all be experienced as hostile impositions, not as sources of attachment. Experience with federal power was as likely to produce alienation as loyalty.

---

## FEDERALIST No. 28 — Hamilton: States as Counterweight to Federal Tyranny

### Direct Response

**"Brutus" No. 7** (Jan. 3, 1788): Hamilton's ultimate safeguard against federal tyranny was state resistance — and ultimately popular revolution. Brutus found this both alarming and inadequate. Alarming because Hamilton was openly acknowledging that the Constitution itself did not prevent tyranny — it only made tyranny resistible after the fact. Inadequate because by the time federal tyranny had developed to the point of requiring armed resistance, the federal government would control the military forces necessary to suppress that resistance. Hamilton's safety valve was not a constitutional safeguard but a counsel of revolution — an odd foundation for the rule of law.

### Independent Arguments

**Tyranny Prevention vs. Tyranny Resistance**: Anti-Federalists drew a crucial distinction that Hamilton collapsed: between *preventing* tyranny through constitutional design and *resisting* tyranny after it has developed. The entire point of constitutional government was to make tyranny structurally difficult, not merely to preserve the right of revolution as a last resort. Hamilton's argument in No. 28 was essentially an admission that the Constitution did not adequately prevent tyranny and relied instead on the people's ability to resist it — which was the Anti-Federalist argument for stronger constitutional constraints on federal power.

---

## FEDERALIST No. 29 — Hamilton: The Militia

### Direct Response

**"Brutus" No. 9**: Hamilton argued for a select, well-trained militia corps rather than either a mass citizen militia or a large standing army. Brutus responded that a select militia — chosen and trained by the federal government — was functionally indistinguishable from a standing army. It would be a professional force loyal to the federal government, not a citizen force rooted in local community. The militia's constitutional value was as an alternative to standing armies; Hamilton's proposal undermined that alternative.

**George Mason** (Virginia Convention): Mason had pushed for a stronger militia guarantee at Philadelphia and lost. He argued in the ratification debates that Congress's power to "govern" the militia, combined with its power to "call forth" the militia for federal purposes, effectively gave the federal government control of the states' principal defense institutions, leaving states militarily dependent on federal discretion.

---

## FEDERALIST No. 30 — Hamilton: Federal Taxation Power

### Direct Response

**"Brutus" No. 5**: Hamilton argued for an "unqualified" federal power to tax. Brutus identified this as perhaps the most dangerous of all the constitutional provisions — because unlimited taxing power, combined with unlimited spending power for the "general welfare," gave Congress effectively unlimited authority over the entire economy. The history of taxation as an instrument of oppression — from Stuart forced loans to parliamentary taxation of the colonies — made unlimited federal taxing power the most immediate threat to popular liberty.

**"Centinel" No. 8**: Hamilton's argument that concurrent state and federal taxation would operate as a mutual check was naive. When federal tax needs expanded (as they inevitably would, especially in wartime), federal taxation would displace state taxation — leaving states without independent revenue sources and therefore without the fiscal basis for genuine independence.

### Independent Arguments

**Taxation Without Genuine Representation**: "Federal Farmer" No. 8 developed at length the argument that the federal House of Representatives was too small and too socially unrepresentative to legitimately exercise the taxing power. Taxation by an unrepresentative body — one that did not reflect the diversity of occupations, classes, and regions that would bear the tax burden — was not constitutionally different in principle from taxation by an external power. The American Revolution had been fought on this principle; it applied as readily to federal taxation by a distant, socially unrepresentative Congress as to parliamentary taxation by Westminster.

---

## FEDERALIST Nos. 31–36 — Hamilton: Taxation Continued

### Direct Responses (consolidated)

The taxation essays prompted sustained responses from multiple Anti-Federalists:

**"Cincinnatus" Nos. 1–6** (Arthur Lee, Nov.–Dec. 1787): Lee wrote specifically to rebut Hamilton's taxation arguments, arguing that: (1) the concurrent taxing power was not a check but a license for double taxation; (2) Hamilton's economic projections for revenue from commerce were speculative and optimistic; (3) the claim that federal revenue needs would be modest and mainly satisfied by import duties was inconsistent with the argument elsewhere that the federal government needed unlimited military and administrative capacity.

**"Federal Farmer" No. 9**: Hamilton's argument that merchants and lawyers would represent all economic interests in Congress (No. 35) was class-based reasoning masquerading as functional analysis. Merchants represented merchant interests; lawyers represented paying clients. The farmer and artisan had no reliable advocate in a Congress dominated by commercial and legal elites.

**"Brutus" No. 6**: The argument that states could protect their citizens from federal taxation through political resistance (No. 36) contradicted the Supremacy Clause. If federal law was supreme, states had no legal mechanism to protect citizens from federal tax impositions that exceeded what Hamilton promised would be modest. The political check Hamilton described was not a constitutional limitation but a political hope.

### Independent Arguments

**The Direct Tax Problem**: Anti-Federalists raised a concern about the apportionment requirement for direct taxes (capitation and property taxes must be apportioned by population) that Hamilton did not adequately address: apportionment by population meant that states with the most people paid the most in direct taxes, regardless of their wealth. This created perverse incentives and would be experienced as deeply unfair by less populous but wealthier states — or by more populous but poorer states, depending on the specific tax design.

---

## FEDERALIST No. 37 — Madison: Difficulties of the Convention

### Direct Response

**"Federal Farmer" No. 4**: Madison's plea for charitable interpretation of the Convention's work — acknowledging that the framers faced genuine difficulties and produced an imperfect but reasonable result — was self-serving. The argument "this is difficult, therefore give us the benefit of the doubt" was not a reason to ratify a specific constitution; it was a reason to continue deliberating until the specific defects were corrected. The Convention's acknowledged difficulty was an argument for amendment before ratification, not for accepting the imperfect instrument.

**"Centinel" No. 9**: Madison's argument that language is inherently imprecise, making constitutional text unavoidably ambiguous, was correct — but it supported the Anti-Federalist position more than the Federalist one. If constitutional text is inherently ambiguous, then the broad grants of power in the Constitution — to levy taxes for the "general welfare," to make laws "necessary and proper," to regulate "commerce" — would be interpreted expansively by the federal government that benefited from such interpretation. Acknowledged textual ambiguity was an argument for more specific enumeration, not for accepting ambiguous text.

---

## FEDERALIST No. 38 — Madison: Anti-Federalist Contradictions

### Direct Response

**"Federal Farmer" No. 6**: Madison argued that Anti-Federalists contradicted each other — some wanting more federal power, others less, some objecting to the Senate's strength, others to its weakness. Federal Farmer responded: this diversity of objection was not incoherence but evidence of the Constitution's broad defectiveness. A constitution that was simultaneously too strong and too weak in different respects was not a well-designed instrument; different critics identified different flaws in a fundamentally flawed document.

**"Brutus" No. 14** (Feb. 28, 1788): Madison's comparison of the Constitution's critics to critics of Solon and Lycurgus was inapt. Those ancient lawgivers were criticized for specific imperfections in generally sound constitutions. The Anti-Federalist objections were to fundamental structural features — the unlimited taxing power, the lack of a bill of rights, the unlimited military power, the judiciary's potential to swallow state courts — not to peripheral details. Comparing the two was a rhetorical diminution.

---

## FEDERALIST No. 39 — Madison: Republican Principles

### Direct Response

**"Brutus" No. 1**: Before No. 39 was published, Brutus had challenged the classification of the proposed government as republican. Madison in No. 39 offered a careful definition of republic — government deriving from the mass of the people, administered by elected representatives, serving for limited terms. Brutus's implicit response (his essays predate No. 39 but address the same question): Madison's definition of republic was so minimal — any government that holds elections qualifies — as to be meaningless. The substance of republican government required genuine representation, accountability, and the connection between governors and governed that the Constitution's scale and structure made impossible.

**"Centinel" No. 1**: The question was not whether the proposed government was formally republican but whether it would function as a republic in practice. A legislature of 65 representatives governing millions, chosen in elections dominated by wealth and social position, was formally republican and substantively oligarchic. The label "republic" obscured rather than described what the Constitution actually created.

### Independent Arguments

**The Mixed Nature as Concealment**: Luther Martin argued that Madison's admission that the Constitution was partly national and partly federal — the "mixed" character celebrated in No. 39 — was not a virtue but a concealment. The Constitution was presented to the states as a federal compact but contained national features that would progressively overwhelm its federal ones. The mixing was not a thoughtful compromise but an attempt to sell a national government to a population that would not have accepted one openly.

---

## FEDERALIST No. 40 — Madison: Convention's Authority

### Direct Response

**Yates and Lansing** (*Reasons for Refusing to Sign*, Dec. 1787): This was the most direct response to the question Madison addressed in No. 40 — the Convention's authority. Yates and Lansing had left the Convention early and refused to sign the Constitution precisely because they believed the Convention had exceeded its mandate. They argued:

1. The states sent delegates to amend the Articles, not to draft a new constitution
2. The product of the Convention was therefore unauthorized, regardless of its merits
3. Submitting the unauthorized product to state conventions for ratification did not retroactively authorize what the Convention had done — it was a unilateral alteration of the amendment procedure specified in the Articles (unanimity) to a different procedure (nine of thirteen states)

Madison's response — that the general mandate to "render the federal constitution adequate to the exigencies of government" was broad enough to encompass a new constitution — was, in Yates and Lansing's view, an after-the-fact rationalization. The delegates had been given specific, limited authority; they exceeded it; the product was constitutionally void.

### Independent Arguments

**The Secrecy of the Convention**: "Centinel" No. 2 argued that the Convention's secrecy — delegates voted to keep all proceedings confidential during deliberations — was itself a constitutional violation and an affront to republican principles. The people, whose sovereign authority was being invoked to legitimate the Convention's product, had no knowledge of what was being proposed until the finished document was presented to them. This was not the transparent deliberation that republican theory required.

---

## FEDERALIST No. 41 — Madison: Powers of Congress Surveyed

### Direct Response

**"Brutus" No. 5**: Madison's argument that "general welfare" in the preamble and Article I was merely a description of the purposes of the enumerated powers — not an independent grant — was contested by Anti-Federalists who read the text differently. "Brutus" No. 5 argued that the phrase "to lay and collect taxes... to pay the debts and provide for the common defence and general welfare" was grammatically ambiguous: it could be read as authorizing taxes for any purpose falling under "general welfare" — which was Hamilton's reading and which had the better of the textual argument.

**"Federal Farmer" No. 8**: The war powers provisions — declare war, raise armies, provide a navy, call forth the militia — gave Congress essentially unlimited military authority with no effective checks other than the procedural requirement to appropriate money every two years. Madison's argument that these were necessary powers ignored the question of what institutional mechanisms would prevent their abuse.

---

## FEDERALIST Nos. 42–44 — Madison: Federal Powers Detailed

### Direct Response (consolidated)

**"Brutus" No. 6**: The Necessary and Proper Clause, which Madison defended in No. 44 as merely declaratory of implied powers, was described by Brutus as effectively unlimited:

> This clause may be said to be the most important one in the constitution: for it gives to congress the power to pass any law whatever, under the plausible pretense of necessity and propriety... the congress have power to enact any law which they may think necessary and proper to carry any of the powers given them into effect.

Brutus argued — correctly anticipating the subsequent history of constitutional interpretation — that the Necessary and Proper Clause would be construed to permit any legislation that Congress could rationalize as useful to any enumerated power. Madison's reassurance that Congress would exercise only the powers "necessary" in a strict sense was belied by the constitutional text, which said "necessary and proper" — a conjunction that substantially softened the necessity requirement.

**"Brutus" No. 7**: The restrictions on states in Article I, Section 10 — prohibiting states from issuing paper money, passing bills of attainder, or impairing contract obligations — represented federal interference in matters properly belonging to state domestic governance. The contract clause, in particular, was a direct constitutional prohibition on state legislative power over private debt relations — a provision that would be systematically used to protect creditors against debtor-protection legislation at the state level.

---

## FEDERALIST No. 45 — Madison: Federal vs. State Power

### Direct Response

**"Brutus" No. 1**: Madison's claim that federal powers would be "few and defined" while state powers would be "numerous and indefinite" was contested as a mischaracterization of the constitutional text. "Few and defined" and "numerous and indefinite" were aspirational descriptions, not legal categories. The Necessary and Proper Clause, the General Welfare Clause, and the Commerce Clause were, in their actual textual scope, the opposite of "few and defined" — they were deliberately open-ended. Madison's description would be accurate only if those clauses were interpreted narrowly, but nothing in the Constitution compelled narrow interpretation.

**Patrick Henry** (Virginia Convention, June 5, 1788): Madison's prediction that the states would remain the dominant governing authorities was not a constitutional guarantee but a political prediction — and Henry believed it was a wrong prediction. The national government's superior resources (unlimited taxing power, control of commerce, military authority) would progressively draw all significant governance authority to the center. The history of every consolidated government in history supported this prediction.

### Independent Arguments

**Structural Incentives for Centralization**: "Federal Farmer" No. 4 made the structural argument that Madison's reassurance about state dominance ignored institutional incentives. Federal officials were paid by the federal treasury, appointed by the federal government, and advanced through federal institutions. Their interests lay in expanding federal authority, not in preserving state autonomy. State officials could resist federal encroachment, but the resources and institutional advantages favored the federal side in any sustained conflict.

---

## FEDERALIST No. 46 — Madison: State Resistance to Federal Tyranny

### Direct Response

**"Brutus" No. 7**: Madison argued that states could organize coordinated resistance to federal tyranny, including armed resistance if necessary. Brutus accepted Madison's empirical argument — states *could* organize such resistance — but noted that this was a description of revolution, not of constitutional government. The Constitution was supposed to prevent tyranny through structural design, not leave citizens to resist it after the fact through insurrection. If the last resort against federal tyranny was state-organized armed resistance, then the Constitution had failed at its most basic task.

**Patrick Henry** (Virginia Convention): Henry was more direct: the Constitution gave the federal government the military power to suppress state resistance before it could be organized. Federal control of the army, the navy, and the power to call up the state militia for federal service meant that by the time states concluded resistance was necessary, the means of resistance would be in federal hands.

---

## FEDERALIST No. 47 — Madison: Separation of Powers

### Direct Response

**"Brutus" No. 9**: Madison's survey of state constitutions to show that separation of powers was never complete — that all governments mixed powers to some degree — was technically correct but missed the point. Anti-Federalists were not arguing for perfect separation; they were arguing that the particular mixing in the Constitution went too far. The combination of the President's veto, treaty-making, and appointment powers with the Senate's role in advice and consent, impeachment, and treaty ratification created a mutual dependency between the executive and the Senate that resembled the "aristocratic branch" more than either pure executive or pure legislative power.

**"Centinel" No. 1**: The mixing of powers in the Constitution was specifically designed to prevent the democratic accountability that genuine separation required. The Senate — chosen by state legislatures for six-year terms, participating in treaty-making and appointments — was insulated from popular control in ways that made it structurally aristocratic regardless of its formal republican character.

---

## FEDERALIST No. 48 — Madison: Parchment Barriers Insufficient

### Direct Response

**"Federal Farmer" No. 9**: Madison argued that parchment barriers alone were insufficient — that structural institutional mechanisms were necessary to maintain separation. Anti-Federalists agreed with the critique of parchment barriers but noted that the Constitution's structural mechanisms for preventing legislative dominance were equally inadequate against executive dominance. The President's appointment power, treaty power, and command of the military created structural leverage that no legislative parchment barrier could effectively check.

### Independent Arguments

**Executive Rather Than Legislative Danger**: Several Anti-Federalists disputed Madison's identification of the legislature as the primary threat in a republic. The American experience — particularly the experience of Virginia under legislative dominance during the Revolution, which Madison cited in No. 48 — was specific to a moment of crisis governance. In normal operation, the danger came from the executive: from the tendency of a single, energetic, permanent executive to expand authority, create dependent client networks through appointments, and use military force to suppress opposition. Madison's legislative-centric threat assessment understated the executive danger that subsequent American history confirmed.

---

## FEDERALIST No. 49 — Madison: Against Frequent Constitutional Conventions

### Direct Response

**"Federal Farmer" No. 10**: Madison argued against Jefferson's proposal for periodic popular conventions to resolve constitutional disputes, on the grounds that frequent revision would undermine constitutional stability and legislative dominance. Federal Farmer accepted the concern about stability but argued that Madison had proven too much: if constitutional revision must be avoided for stability reasons, then the Constitution's amendment process (requiring two-thirds of both houses and three-fourths of states) made constitutional correction so difficult as to be practically impossible. The choice between Jefferson's too-easy revision and Madison's too-hard amendment process was a false binary; a more accessible amendment process was available.

---

## FEDERALIST No. 50 — Madison: Periodic Appeals to the People

### Direct Response

**"Brutus" No. 15** (Mar. 20, 1788): Madison's use of the Pennsylvania Council of Censors as evidence against periodic popular review was selective. The Council's failure was partly due to the partisan conflicts Madison described, but partly because it was attempting to review the actions of a government that had been in operation for less than seven years. Long-established institutions develop constituencies and vested interests that make popular review politically difficult regardless of their constitutional design. Madison's lesson — periodic review fails — was less established than he claimed.

---

## FEDERALIST No. 51 — Madison: Checks and Balances

### Direct Response

**"Brutus" No. 15**: Madison's theory that "ambition must counteract ambition" would produce stable constitutional equilibrium depended on each branch having both the means and the motivation to resist encroachment. But the Constitution created structural asymmetries that would predictably produce imbalance:

- The President controls appointments, creating dependent relationships across the executive branch
- The Senate participates in appointments and treaties, aligning its interests with executive success
- The judiciary is appointed by the President with Senate consent, creating executive/senatorial influence over the supposedly independent branch

These structural dependencies would produce not balanced competition among branches but an executive-senatorial alignment that would dominate the House — precisely the aristocratic consolidation that Anti-Federalists feared.

**"Centinel" No. 1**: Madison's theory assumed that each branch's officials would act primarily to protect institutional prerogatives against other branches. But officials in all branches would be drawn from the same social and economic elite — the "natural aristocracy" that would dominate elections in large electoral districts. An elite sharing economic interests and social connections would not faithfully execute the inter-branch competition Madison's theory required.

**"Federal Farmer" No. 9**: The "double security" Madison described — federal-state competition and inter-branch competition — required both levels to function simultaneously. But the Supremacy Clause eliminated states as genuine co-equal competitors with the federal government, and the federal branches' structural dependencies undermined genuine inter-branch competition. The double security was a theoretical construct unsupported by the Constitution's actual institutional design.

### Independent Arguments

**The Coordination Problem**: Anti-Federalists across multiple essays implicitly challenged the assumption that branch competition would prevent tyranny without preventing governance. The practical experience of governmental gridlock — branches checking each other into paralysis — was a real cost of the Madisonian design that *Publius* systematically underweighted. The choice was not between efficient consolidated power and balanced liberty but between effective governance and constitutional liberty — a genuine tradeoff that required more careful treatment than No. 51 provided.

---

## FEDERALIST Nos. 52–58 — Madison: The House of Representatives

### Direct Responses (consolidated)

**Melancton Smith** (New York Convention, June 1788): Smith's sustained engagement with the House of Representatives is the most analytically rigorous Anti-Federalist argument on representation. His specific contributions:

*On biennial elections* (vs. No. 52–53): Annual elections were not merely a preference but a structural mechanism for keeping representatives accountable. Two-year terms created a 24-month window during which representatives could accumulate experience in deceiving constituents without facing electoral accountability. Annual elections maintained the connection between governors and governed that republican theory required.

*On House size* (vs. No. 55–57): A House of 65 members — rising eventually to 200 or so — for a population of millions was grossly insufficient for genuine representation. Smith calculated that each representative would govern approximately 30,000 people — a constituency so large that no representative could know his constituents or they him. Genuine representation required that representatives "resemble" those they represented — reflect their diversity of occupation, wealth, and social position. A House dominated by lawyers, merchants, and large landowners could not represent farmers, artisans, and small traders regardless of electoral mechanisms.

**"Brutus" No. 4**: The biennial election provision, combined with the House's small size and large constituencies, created a legislature that would be experienced as alien and distant by most citizens. The connection between representatives and constituents that gave democratic legitimacy to legislative authority would be broken by the structural features of the House.

**"Federal Farmer" No. 7**: The initial House size of 65 was not a temporary expedient pending reapportionment — it was a constitutional minimum that Congress had no obligation to exceed. Given the vested interest of existing representatives in not diluting their own power by increasing the House, the constitutional minimum was likely to become the actual size in practice, or to grow only slowly and insufficient to maintain representative ratios.

### Independent Arguments

**The Three-Fifths Compromise** (independent of Madison's No. 54): Anti-Federalists across the North — particularly in Massachusetts and New York — argued that the Three-Fifths Clause was a constitutional endorsement of slavery and a fundamental violation of republican principles. It gave slaveholders extra political representation for their enslaved property — people they denied all political rights — at the expense of free citizens in non-slaveholding states. "Agrippa" (James Winthrop) argued that the Three-Fifths Clause made New England's citizens constitutionally inferior to Virginia's slaveholders: a Massachusetts farmer counted for one vote, but a Virginia planter's enslaved workers were counted at three-fifths apiece in apportioning the representation that protected slavery's political power.

---

## FEDERALIST Nos. 59–61 — Hamilton: Congressional Election Regulation

### Direct Responses (consolidated)

**"Brutus" No. 6**: Congressional control of the time, place, and manner of its own elections was — as Hamilton admitted — a potential instrument of self-serving manipulation. Hamilton argued that diverse congressional interests would prevent such abuse. Brutus argued: the one interest that all members of Congress shared, regardless of their other differences, was self-perpetuation. The power to regulate their own elections would inevitably be used, in subtle and cumulative ways, to disadvantage challengers and entrench incumbents.

**"Centinel" No. 10**: Hamilton's claim that no majority coalition could form to manipulate elections was contradicted by the existence of the Constitution itself — a document produced by a specific majority coalition at Philadelphia that served the interests of that coalition at the expense of others. The assumption that no durable governing coalition could form in a large republic was empirically falsified by the Convention's own proceedings.

---

## FEDERALIST Nos. 62–63 — Madison: The Senate

### Direct Responses (consolidated)

**"Federal Farmer" No. 3**: Madison defended equal state representation in the Senate as a necessary compromise. Federal Farmer accepted the political necessity but contested the constitutional theory: equal state representation was not a principled federal arrangement but a concession to small-state self-interest that would systematically distort democratic representation. New Hampshire (population ~140,000) had the same Senate voice as Virginia (population ~750,000 including enslaved people at three-fifths). This was not federalism; it was political inequality elevated to constitutional principle.

**"Centinel" No. 11**: Madison's defense of six-year Senate terms as providing stability and institutional memory was accurate — but stability and institutional memory in a body that was structurally aristocratic (indirect election, long terms, limited numbers) was the stability of aristocracy, not the stability of republican deliberation. The Senate's institutional qualities — experience, continuity, insulation from electoral pressure — were exactly the qualities of a hereditary upper chamber, regardless of its formal republican mode of selection.

**"Brutus" No. 16** (Apr. 10, 1788): The Senate's combination of executive and legislative functions — participating in appointments, treaties, and impeachments while also serving as one half of the legislature — violated separation of powers more severely than any other constitutional provision. The Senate was the institutional center of the aristocratic tendency in the Constitution: long-serving, indirectly elected, participating across multiple branches, and beyond effective popular control.

**"An Old Whig" No. 7**: Madison's argument that the Senate would serve as a stable, temperate counterweight to the passions of the House assumed that the Senate's institutional qualities — experience and insulation from electoral pressure — would produce wisdom rather than self-interest. The history of aristocratic upper chambers — the House of Lords, the Roman Senate in its late republican phase — suggested that insulation from popular accountability produced not wisdom but entrenchment of elite privilege.

### Independent Arguments

**The Senate as Aristocratic Body**: Patrick Henry (Virginia Convention, June 9, 1788) made the structural argument directly: a Senate chosen by state legislatures, serving six-year terms, with a two-thirds vote required for any treaty, constituted a governing council that would exercise decisive authority over the most important matters of state while remaining effectively beyond popular control. This was not the "temperate and respectable body" Madison described; it was a constitutionally entrenched oligarchy.

---

## FEDERALIST Nos. 64–66 — Jay and Hamilton: The Senate and Treaties/Impeachment

### Direct Responses (consolidated)

**"Brutus" No. 12** (Feb. 7, 1788) and **No. 13** (Feb. 21, 1788): Jay's defense of Senate treaty ratification emphasized expertise, deliberation, and secrecy. Brutus responded: treaty-making was precisely the power most susceptible to foreign corruption, and the Constitution's provisions — requiring only a two-thirds Senate vote and providing no House participation — concentrated this corruptible power in the branch farthest from popular accountability. The Senate's six-year terms and indirect election made it the easiest target for systematic foreign bribery and influence.

**"Centinel" No. 12**: Hamilton's defense of Senate impeachment was undermined by the constitutional text he was defending. The Senate trying impeachments of officers it had helped confirm, in proceedings presided over by the Chief Justice it had confirmed, was not an independent check but an institutional conflict of interest elevated to constitutional status. The Senate had participated in creating the officers it was supposed to judge impartially.

### Independent Arguments

**Jay Treaty Anticipation**: Several Anti-Federalists — with remarkable prescience — predicted that the Senate's treaty power would be used to negotiate commercial agreements that served northeastern mercantile interests at the expense of southern agriculture. The Jay Treaty of 1794 — negotiated in secret, ratified by the Senate over fierce popular opposition, and widely seen as sacrificing southern commercial interests to British mercantile demands — confirmed this concern within a decade of ratification.

---

## FEDERALIST Nos. 67–69 — Hamilton: The Executive

### Direct Responses (consolidated)

**"Centinel" No. 11**: Hamilton's comparison of the President to a constitutional governor rather than a king was technical rather than substantive. The President served four years with reeligibility (effectively unlimited terms), commanded the military, made appointments, negotiated treaties, and possessed a conditional veto. The formal differences from a hereditary monarch were less important than the structural similarities in power and independence from popular control.

**"An Old Whig" No. 8**: Hamilton's careful comparison of the President to the British king and New York governor obscured the crucial difference: the British king was constrained by centuries of constitutional convention, parliamentary precedent, and aristocratic resistance. The American President faced no such historical constraints. The office was new; its constitutional boundaries were contested; and the first several holders would effectively define its scope through practice. Hamilton's minimizing description of the presidency was a description of the office as limited by text; what mattered was the office as it would develop in practice.

**"Philadelphiensis" No. 11**: The President was a king in all but name. The four-year term with reeligibility was effectively a lifetime position, since a popular President would be reelected indefinitely. The appointment power — covering all federal judges, all military officers, all ambassadors, and all significant administrative positions — created a clientele network of unprecedented scope. The combination of military command, appointment power, treaty negotiation, and veto authority made the President the most powerful executive in any republic in history.

### Independent Arguments

**The Electoral College**: Anti-Federalists were skeptical of Hamilton's defense of the Electoral College as a deliberative body that would select the wisest available executive. "Federal Farmer" No. 13 predicted — accurately — that the Electoral College would not function as deliberate selection but as a ratifying mechanism for nominations made through other political processes. The college would not independently choose but would translate popular support (or party organization) into electoral votes. Hamilton's vision of wise electors deliberating independently was already being overtaken by the party nominating system developing in 1787–88.

---

## FEDERALIST No. 70 — Hamilton: Executive Energy and Unity

### Direct Response

**"Centinel" No. 12**: Hamilton's argument for executive "energy" — the concentration of executive power in a single person for decisive action — was precisely the argument that defenders of monarchy had always made. Energy, dispatch, secrecy, and unity were virtues in an executive when the executive's interests aligned with the people's; they were instruments of tyranny when they did not. Hamilton's case for an energetic executive provided no mechanism for ensuring alignment between executive energy and popular interest.

**"Philadelphiensis" Nos. 8–10**: Hamilton argued against an executive council on grounds of accountability — with multiple executives, no one could be blamed for bad decisions. Philadelphiensis responded: Hamilton's argument for accountability through unity could equally support monarchy. A king is the most accountable executive imaginable under Hamilton's theory — there is no one else to blame. The argument proved too much.

**Patrick Henry** (Virginia Convention, June 7, 1788): "A firm, energetic government" was the Federalists' constitutional argument. But Henry asked: energy for what purpose? An energetic government that enforced unjust laws was not a benefit but a tyranny. The founders of liberty had not fought the Revolution to substitute a distant American despot for a distant British one.

### Independent Arguments

**The Council of State**: George Mason (*Objections*) proposed — and had proposed at the Convention — a privy council or executive council that would advise the President and share executive responsibility. This would provide the accountability Hamilton valued without concentrating all executive authority in a single person. Hamilton dismissed executive councils without engaging Mason's specific proposal, which had deep roots in both English constitutional practice and colonial American governance.

---

## FEDERALIST Nos. 71–73 — Hamilton: Executive Duration, Re-eligibility, and Veto

### Direct Responses (consolidated)

**"Brutus" No. 16**: Hamilton's defense of presidential reeligibility — arguing that the prospect of reelection gave presidents an incentive to govern well — relied on a model of incentives that broke down in practice. A president in his second term faced no further electoral accountability (if term limits were not constitutionally imposed) and a president seeking reelection had incentives to govern *popularly* rather than *well* — to pursue short-term popular measures at the expense of long-term sound policy. The alignment between electoral incentive and good governance was not as reliable as Hamilton assumed.

**"An Old Whig" No. 8**: The presidential veto — requiring a two-thirds override — gave the President an effective legislative veto over most legislation. Combined with the appointment power, the treaty power, and the military command, the veto made the President the dominant branch of government in any sustained conflict with Congress. Hamilton's claim that the veto was merely defensive — protecting the executive from legislative encroachment — understated its affirmative policy power.

### Independent Arguments

**Term Limits**: "Federal Farmer" No. 14 and multiple other Anti-Federalists argued for constitutionally mandated presidential term limits — a single six-year term, or two terms of four years, with permanent ineligibility thereafter. The argument was simple: unlimited reeligibility, combined with the power of incumbency (appointments, patronage, military command, policy control), made the President practically unbeatable after an initial term. Term limits were the only structural mechanism for ensuring that the office did not become a life appointment in practice if not in form.

---

## FEDERALIST Nos. 74–77 — Hamilton: Executive Powers (Command, Pardon, Treaty, Appointments)

### Direct Responses (consolidated)

**"Brutus" No. 11** (Jan. 31, 1788): The combination of powers in Hamilton's essays on executive authority — command of the military, pardon power, treaty negotiation, appointment of all significant federal officers — constituted a concentration of authority unprecedented in republican history. Each power, examined separately, could be defended. Examined together, they described an executive who controlled the military (commander-in-chief), controlled who sat in judgement over him (appointment of judges), could forgive his own subordinates' crimes (pardon power), and could make binding law without Congress through treaties.

**"Centinel" No. 13**: Hamilton's defense of the pardon power — that it allowed mercy in cases of rebellion — was precisely backwards as a matter of constitutional design. A president who could pardon rebels and conspirators had the power to protect his own co-conspirators from punishment. Hamilton argued that the pardon power could be used to end insurrection peacefully; Centinel argued that it could be used to shield participants in coups and political crimes.

**On Appointments (No. 76)**: Patrick Henry (Virginia Convention) argued that presidential nomination with Senate confirmation was not the accountability mechanism Hamilton claimed. The President would nominate his political allies and social peers; the Senate — which shared the President's social class — would confirm them. The result would be an administrative state staffed by the friends and clients of the governing elite, regardless of the formal confirmation requirement.

**On Removals (No. 77)**: Hamilton's argument that Senate consent was required for removals was the constitutionally sound position — it constrained executive power most consistently with the checked, balanced design. Anti-Federalists noted with interest that this position was subsequently abandoned by the Federalists themselves in the Decision of 1789, when Congress voted that the President could remove executive officers without Senate consent. The Federalists claimed to favor checked executive power; their first legislative action was to expand it.

---

## FEDERALIST Nos. 78–83 — Hamilton: The Judiciary

### Direct Response

These papers prompted the most sustained and prescient Anti-Federalist engagement of the entire ratification debate. **"Brutus" Nos. 11–15** constitute a systematic response to Hamilton's judiciary essays that many scholars regard as superior in analytical rigor.

**"Brutus" No. 11** (Jan. 31, 1788) — On Judicial Review:

Hamilton argued in No. 78 that courts must give effect to the Constitution as superior law and therefore could void acts of Congress. Brutus accepted the logic but identified its terrifying implication: the Supreme Court would become the supreme governing authority in America.

> The supreme court then have a right, independent of the legislature, to give a construction to the constitution and every part of it, and there is no power provided in this system to correct their construction or controul their adjudications. From this court there is no appeal... They will give the sense of every article of the constitution that may from time to time come before them. And in their decisions they will not confine themselves to any fixed or established rules, but will determine, according to what appears to them, the reason and spirit of the constitution.

Brutus identified three consequences Hamilton had not addressed:

1. The Supreme Court's interpretations of the Constitution would be final and unreviewable — more powerful than any legislative act, including constitutional amendments if the Court chose to give them a narrow reading.

2. Federal judges — appointed by the President, confirmed by the Senate, serving for life, immune from removal except by impeachment — would exercise this unlimited authority with effectively no democratic accountability.

3. The Court's power to interpret the "spirit" of the Constitution (as Hamilton's No. 78 contemplated) rather than merely its text would give judges essentially legislative authority — the power to extend constitutional meaning to cases and circumstances the text did not cover.

**"Brutus" No. 12** (Feb. 7, 1788): Hamilton had argued that the Court's lack of sword and purse made it the least dangerous branch. Brutus responded: the Court's power was not its ability to act directly but its ability to authorize others to act. A court that could declare any legislative restriction on executive power unconstitutional effectively controlled both the executive and legislative branches through its interpretive authority. The Court was not weak; it was the most powerful branch precisely because its power was invisible — exercised through interpretation rather than command.

**"Brutus" No. 14** (Feb. 28, 1788): The Supreme Court's appellate jurisdiction over both law and fact in all federal cases created a mechanism for eliminating jury trials in civil cases on appeal. Hamilton's claim in No. 81 that Congress could regulate appellate jurisdiction to protect jury rights was technically true but ignored the political reality: a Congress and an executive with interests in adjudicating cases favorably would have no incentive to maintain jury trial rights against federal authority.

**"Brutus" No. 15** (Mar. 20, 1788): The most prescient essay in the Anti-Federalist literature. Brutus predicted — with extraordinary accuracy for 1788 — the entire trajectory of American constitutional history:

> The supreme court... will be authorized to decide upon the meaning of the constitution, and... they will be inclined to draw all power under their cognizance. This power in the judges... will enable them to mould the government, into almost any shape they please... The judicial power of the United States, will lean strongly in favour of the general government, and will give such an explanation to the constitution, as will favour an extension of its jurisdiction... They will, therefore, always be inclined to enlarge the exercise of their power.

This passage is a description, published in 1788, of *McCulloch v. Maryland* (1819), *Gibbons v. Ogden* (1824), the Commerce Clause jurisprudence of the New Deal, and the nationalization of constitutional rights under the Fourteenth Amendment. Brutus was essentially correct about what judicial review would produce, and Hamilton's reassurances in No. 78 about the judiciary's weakness were essentially wrong.

### Independent Arguments

**The Circuit Riding Problem**: Anti-Federalists from interior states argued that federal court jurisdiction over commercial disputes, diversity cases, and federal questions would require citizens to travel long distances to federal courts located in coastal cities. This was a structural access-to-justice problem that Hamilton's essays did not address: federal courts would be cheap for merchants in Philadelphia and New York, prohibitively expensive for farmers in western Massachusetts or frontier Virginia.

**Jury Trial in Civil Cases**: George Mason's *Objections* led with the absence of civil jury trial protection in the Constitution's original text. This was the Anti-Federalist argument that gained the most popular traction — every citizen understood what a civil jury trial was and why it mattered. The Seventh Amendment was the direct constitutional response to this objection.

## FEDERALIST No. 84 — Hamilton: Against a Bill of Rights

### Direct Response

This essay produced the most comprehensive and ultimately successful Anti-Federalist response of the entire ratification debate. Hamilton's argument — that a bill of rights was not only unnecessary but dangerous — was rejected by the ratifying public, and the promise of a bill of rights was essential to securing ratification in Massachusetts, Virginia, and New York.

**"Federal Farmer" No. 16**: Hamilton argued that a bill of rights was inappropriate because the Constitution granted only enumerated powers, leaving all other authority with the people. Federal Farmer responded:

1. Hamilton's premise was false: the Necessary and Proper Clause, the General Welfare Clause, and the Commerce Clause collectively gave Congress authority that was not "enumerated" in any meaningful sense.

2. The absence of express prohibitions was not a protection but a gap. A government with broad implied powers could restrict speech, press, assembly, and religion without any explicit constitutional authority for the restriction — simply by claiming the restriction was "necessary and proper" for some authorized end.

3. The English Magna Carta and Bill of Rights, which Hamilton dismissed as unnecessary concessions from kings, were actually the source of the specific liberties Americans most valued. The suggestion that these rights needed no enumeration was an invitation to forget them.

**"An Old Whig" No. 5**: The argument that a bill of rights was dangerous — because listing rights implied that unlisted rights were not retained — could be answered by a declaration specifically providing that the enumeration of rights did not exhaust the people's retained rights. (This is exactly what the Ninth Amendment accomplished.)

**George Mason** (Virginia Convention): Mason had refused to sign the Constitution precisely because it lacked a bill of rights. His *Objections* began with this point: without a bill of rights, there was no constitutional protection for freedom of the press, no protection against unreasonable searches and seizures, no guarantee of jury trial in criminal cases, no protection against cruel punishment. Mason's objections were the most politically effective Anti-Federalist arguments, precisely because they were concrete and comprehensible to ordinary citizens.

**Patrick Henry** (Virginia Convention, June 9, 1788): Henry's speeches on the bill of rights were the most rhetorically powerful arguments in any ratification convention. He challenged the Federalists to identify the constitutional provision that protected freedom of conscience, the press, or trial by jury in criminal cases (Article III provided jury trial in criminal cases, but not in the terms Henry found adequate). The Federalists could not provide satisfactory answers, and the Virginia ratification was conditioned on the promise of amendments.

### Independent Arguments

**The Bill of Rights as Democratic Achievement**: Anti-Federalists argued that bills of rights were not merely limitations on government but affirmative declarations of popular sovereignty. A people that enumerated its rights was a people that had reflected on the nature of its liberty and committed itself to its protection through law. The Federalist argument that rights could be adequately protected by structural mechanisms (separation of powers, enumerated federal powers) reflected an elite constitutionalism that distrusted popular declarations of rights — precisely the kind of paternalistic governance that the Revolution had been fought to end.

---

## FEDERALIST No. 85 — Hamilton: Conclusion

### Direct Response

**"Federal Farmer" No. 18** (Jan. 25, 1788): Hamilton's concluding appeal — that the Constitution was better than the Articles and could be improved through the amendment process — made the amendment process do far too much work. Hamilton had argued throughout the *Federalist* that the Constitution's provisions were necessary and carefully calibrated. Now, at the conclusion, he conceded that many Anti-Federalist objections were valid but could be addressed after ratification. Federal Farmer responded: if significant defects existed and were acknowledged, the proper course was to correct them before ratification — when the states had maximum leverage — rather than after, when the new federal government would be the gatekeeper for proposed amendments.

**Melancton Smith** (New York Convention, July 1788): Hamilton argued that New York's ratification was necessary to prevent the state from being isolated outside a union formed without it. Smith's response was the most sophisticated: New York should ratify conditionally — with a list of amendments to be considered within a specified period, and with the right to withdraw ratification if the amendments were not adopted. This was not merely a tactical position; it reflected the genuine Anti-Federalist theory that the Constitution was a compact that states ratified with specific understandings and could withdraw from if those understandings were violated.

**"Centinel" No. 18**: Hamilton's citation of Hume — that one must evaluate constitutions against realistic alternatives, not ideal ones — was methodologically sound but applied selectively. Hume's standard required evaluating the Constitution against a reformed confederation, not just against the existing Articles. Hamilton had throughout the *Federalist* refused to seriously engage the reformed confederation option; he could not fairly invoke Hume's comparative realism at the conclusion while having avoided it throughout.

### Independent Arguments

**The Anti-Federalist Legacy**: The most important independent argument at the end of the ratification debate was about process rather than substance. Anti-Federalists consistently argued that the most consequential constitutional decisions should not be rushed — that the pace of ratification, compressed into a period of months, was itself a threat to democratic deliberation. The specific provisions they objected to were important, but the underlying principle was more important: constitutional government derived its legitimacy from the deliberate, informed consent of the governed, and any process that short-circuited that deliberation produced a constitution that lacked genuine popular authorization, whatever its formal electoral legitimacy.

This argument — that the *process* of constitutional formation matters as much as the *product* — is perhaps the Anti-Federalists' deepest contribution to constitutional theory, and the one that contemporary constitutional scholars (from Bruce Ackerman on "constitutional moments" to Cass Sunstein on constitutional incompleteness) have found most generative.

# THEMATIC SYNTHESIS: THE ANTI-FEDERALIST CONSTITUTIONAL VISION

The Anti-Federalist arguments, taken together, constitute a coherent alternative constitutional vision rather than a collection of objections to specific provisions:

**Small-scale republican government** as the precondition of genuine self-governance, not merely its form. Brutus's extended republic critique, Federal Farmer's representation argument, and Smith's descriptive representation theory all converge on a single point: republican government is not simply electoral government but government in which citizens and governors stand in a genuine relationship of mutual knowledge and accountability — a relationship that only small-scale polities can sustain.

**Federal structure as principled, not expedient**. The Anti-Federalists were not simply defenders of state rights as such; they believed in the federal principle — that political authority should be lodged at the level of government closest to the people it governs — as a substantive constitutional value, not merely a concession to political necessity.

**Bills of rights as democratic achievement**. The demand for explicit protection of rights was not a lawyerly technicality but an expression of the principle that free governments must acknowledge their limits — that the people, in constituting a government, reserve to themselves the rights that define their freedom.

**Skepticism of concentrated power**, particularly executive power, as the primary threat to republican government. The Anti-Federalists were not wrong about this. The expansion of executive power through war, emergency, and administrative delegation has been the central constitutional development of American history since 1789, and it has proceeded in exactly the direction they feared — overcoming the structural checks Madison and Hamilton described as adequate safeguards.

**Representation as resemblance**, not merely accountability. The Anti-Federalist argument that representatives must reflect the social diversity of those they represent — not merely be elected by them — anticipates modern debates about descriptive representation, majority-minority districts, and the relationship between elite governance and democratic legitimacy that remain unresolved in American constitutional life.

---

## A Note on the Anti-Federalists' Ultimate Success

The Anti-Federalists lost the ratification debate. But they won the constitutional argument in several crucial respects:

- The **Bill of Rights** (Amendments I–X) was their achievement, not the Federalists'
- The **Seventeenth Amendment** (direct election of Senators) addressed their critique of Senate aristocracy
- The **Twenty-Second Amendment** (presidential term limits) addressed their concern about executive perpetuation
- The **Voting Rights Act** and subsequent expansion of suffrage addressed their concern about narrow representation
- The **Administrative Procedure Act** and subsequent regulatory law partially addressed their concern about unaccountable executive administration

The Anti-Federalist critique, in other words, was not defeated by history but slowly incorporated into the constitutional order through exactly the amendment process that Madison in No. 85 promised would be available — a promise made under duress, which took two centuries and enormous political conflict to partially redeem.
