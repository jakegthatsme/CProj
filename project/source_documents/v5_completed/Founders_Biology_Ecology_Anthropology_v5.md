## Preface — The Inquiry Under the Fifth-Pass Architecture

This document is the Part II diagnostic foundation of the fifth-pass constitutional project. The project rests on a load-bearing triad: biocultural governance as constitutional ontology, eco-mimetic policy-making as operational method, disturbance-adapted implementation as temporal orientation. The Indigenous and Eastern traditions are the principle's primary articulators; the Western complex-adaptive-systems, ecology, biomimicry, and disturbance-ecology canons are recent and partial recovery. "Grown, not built" is the colloquial summary phrase.

Under the fifth-pass framing, the document's central analytical move is to re-read the founders' anthropological assumptions — the Lockean possessive individualism, the social-contract framing, the bounded-rational-actor model, the property-as-natural-right premise — not merely as empirically incomplete on the modern evolutionary, cognitive, and anthropological evidence but as ontologically mistaken about what kind of formation the polity is. The polity is a biocultural formation cultivated across nested scales as a complex adaptive system, with biological substrate and cultural plurality coupled; the founders' anthropological assumptions are design-imposition framings that mistake what kind of formation is in front of them and therefore mis-specify the architectural problem the implementation must solve.

The document is engaged in continuity with the existing project record. The substantive evolutionary, cognitive, and anthropological analysis the prior pass developed is preserved through the targeted re-framing the fifth-pass commits to: the cognitive-bias and behavioral-economics literature on the rational-actor assumption; the evolutionary-anthropological record's substantive engagement with the social-contract premise; the ecology-and-carrying-capacity material on the founders' ecological blindness; the evolutionary-biology material; the human-behavioral-ecology material on status, tribalism, and political attitudes; the closing synthesis on the Constitution's biological blindspots. The substantive analysis relocated to Document L is preserved in its move; the founding-era critique focused on the four design-imposition framings remains in Document 4 as Part II diagnostic substrate.

The founders are engaged on their own analytical terms — not as moral failures to be condemned, not as subjects of presentist correction. They were eighteenth-century thinkers working with the analytical resources their period made available. The Lockean possessive-individualism premise was the dominant English political philosophy they had inherited; the social-contract framing was the prevailing analytical mode through which European political philosophy specified the polity's foundations; the bounded-rational-actor assumption tracked the cognitive theory of their period; the property-as-natural-right premise was constitutive of the political-economic order they were attempting to legitimate. The substantive critique developed here is not that they should have known better in moral or psychological registers but that the analytical resources they worked with were inadequate to the kind of formation the polity is, and the implementation they produced operationalized that inadequacy. Modern evolutionary, cognitive, and anthropological evidence does not show the founders to have been confused; it shows them to have been operating within design-imposition framings the substantive evidence cannot accommodate.

The document positions itself analytically as Part II diagnostic foundation against which the five disturbance-regime diagnostics (Documents B, S, T, U, V) and the cross-regime interactions diagnostic (Document W) are read, because each disturbance regime specifies how the biocultural formation the founders mistook for an aggregation of pre-social possessive individuals is threatened, captured, or eroded along the regime-specific dimensions the diagnostic documents articulate.

## Part One — The Rational Actor and the Behavioral Revolution: Bounded-Rational-Actor as Design-Imposition Framing

The founders' political theory rested on a model of the human as bounded but rational actor: capable of deliberation in matters of public concern; susceptible to passion, faction, and self-interest but corrigible through institutional design; substantially uniform across the categories the model recognized (the propertied white male citizen the polity took as its operational subject). The model was sophisticated for its period. *Federalist 10*'s analysis of faction, *Federalist 51*'s analysis of separation of powers, the broader engagement with the conditions under which rational deliberation might produce republican outcomes — these articulate substantive engagement with the cognitive and motivational conditions of governance. The substantive critique developed here is not that the founders failed to anticipate modern cognitive science; it is that the bounded-rational-actor framing the model operates within is a design-imposition framing that mistakes the cognitive substrate at the organism-and-organ-system scale of the nested-scales reference frame.

### The Core Assumption and Its Failure on the Cognitive-Bias Evidence

The substantive cognitive-bias literature developed under Daniel Kahneman and Amos Tversky's heuristics-and-biases program — initiated through "Judgment under Uncertainty: Heuristics and Biases" (Tversky and Kahneman, *Science*, 1974), substantively elaborated through Kahneman's *Thinking, Fast and Slow* (2011), and extended through the broader behavioral-economics tradition — establishes that human cognition operates through systematic departures from the rational-actor model: availability heuristics that overweight vivid recent instances; representativeness heuristics that mistake similarity for probability; anchoring effects that bind judgment to arbitrary starting points; loss aversion that asymmetrically weights losses over gains; present bias that systematically discounts the future; framing effects that produce different decisions on logically equivalent presentations. The substantive evidence is robust across decades of experimental and observational research and across multiple methodological approaches.

The behavioral-economics extension through Richard Thaler and Cass Sunstein's *Nudge: Improving Decisions about Health, Wealth, and Happiness* (2008) attempted to translate the cognitive-bias evidence into design recommendations for institutional architecture. The substantive engagement with the *Nudge* program through Thaler's *Misbehaving* (2015), through Sunstein's broader regulatory-state corpus, and through the critical engagement of George Loewenstein, Cass Sunstein, and the broader behavioral-public-policy literature provides the contemporary engagement. The methodological caveats raised against parts of the cognitive-bias literature — the replication crisis in social psychology that has reduced confidence in some heuristics-and-biases findings, the methodological critiques through Gerd Gigerenzer's *Rationality for Mortals: How People Cope with Uncertainty* (2008) and Gigerenzer's broader ecological-rationality program, the substantive engagement with bounded rationality through Herbert Simon's foundational work and the contemporary engagement through Vernon Smith — must be engaged substantively rather than dismissed.

The cognitive-bias literature does not show the rational-actor model to have been wrong in the simple sense that human cognition is "irrational"; it shows the rational-actor model to be inadequate to the cognitive substrate at the organism-and-organ-system scale of the nested-scales reference frame. Human cognition operates with substantial variability across persons, contexts, and conditions; the variability is structural, not noise. The substantive critique of the bounded-rational-actor framing is that it mistakes the cognitive substrate for a uniform input to deliberative architecture when the substrate is plural, contextual, and varying.

### The Bounded-Rational-Actor Framing as Design-Imposition

What the bounded-rational-actor framing mistook: the polity as aggregation of substantially uniform deliberative subjects whose cognitive substrate could be specified in advance of institutional design. The model presumes the deliberative subject as an abstract category — the citizen, the voter, the juror, the legislator — without engagement with the substantive cognitive plurality the substrate actually carries. The institutional architecture is then designed against the abstract category, with the operational reality of cognitive plurality producing systematic mismatches between the designed institutions and the people they are meant to serve. The designed assumption that the polity's deliberative capacity is uniform across persons, contexts, and conditions does not survive engagement with the substrate.

What the biocultural-governance framing puts in its place: the cognitive substrate at the organism-and-organ-system scale of the nested-scales reference frame is plural; the polity is the biocultural formation cultivated through the cognitive substrate's variability rather than through its abstraction. The deliberative capacity of the polity is not a property of abstract subjects; it is a cultivation outcome of the conditions under which embodied cognitive plurality engages substantive deliberation across nested scales. The implementation's failure to register the cognitive plurality is not a design defect to be patched; it is the ontological mistake the design-imposition framing produced. Documents F (Part XII — e-democracy as structural public check), J (Part XII — neurodivergent polis), and U (Part II — cognitive-and-informational-disturbance regime) operationalize the cognitive-substrate engagement downstream.

The contemporary failure modes the cognitive-bias and cognitive-plurality literatures together specify — the susceptibility of deliberation to manipulation through framing, the systematic disenfranchisement of cognitively plural persons through accommodation failures, the broader epistemic-injustice architecture documented through Miranda Fricker's *Epistemic Injustice: Power and the Ethics of Knowing* (2007), through José Medina's *The Epistemology of Resistance* (2013), through Kristie Dotson on epistemic oppression, and through Rebecca Tsosie on Indigenous epistemic injustice — are not anomalies of the implementation; they are the operational signature of the implementation's ontological mistake about the cognitive substrate.

## Part Two — Anthropology and the Social-Contract Framing as Design-Imposition

The founders' political theory rested on the social-contract framing: the analytical claim that the polity's foundations can be derived from a hypothetical pre-political state of nature in which substantially individual subjects, capable of recognizing their own interests, consent to political association on terms specifiable in advance. The Hobbesian, Lockean, and Rousseauian variants of the framing differed substantively, but each operated under the shared assumption that the social-contract derivation is the appropriate analytical mode for specifying the polity's foundations. The substantive critique developed here is not that the founders failed to anticipate modern evolutionary anthropology; it is that the social-contract framing is a design-imposition framing that mistakes the empirical-historical formation of polities for an analytical-conceptual derivation from a hypothetical state-of-nature.

### Hobbes, Locke, and the Pre-Political State

Thomas Hobbes's *Leviathan* (1651) articulated the foundational early-modern social-contract framing through the analytical claim that absent government the human condition is "solitary, poor, nasty, brutish, and short" — and that political association is rationally chosen by individuals who would otherwise face the war of all against all. John Locke's *Two Treatises of Government* (1689) articulated the framing the founders most directly inherited: substantial natural rights to life, liberty, and property are inherent in individuals prior to political association; political association is contracted to secure those rights; the legitimacy of government derives from the consent of the governed; the right to alter or abolish government when it fails its purposes is preserved. Jean-Jacques Rousseau's *Of the Social Contract* (1762) articulated a variant in which the social contract produces the general will (*volonté générale*) as foundational political category.

The founders engaged the social-contract framing as analytical mode through which they specified the colonial revolutionary claim. The Declaration of Independence's articulation of life, liberty, and the pursuit of happiness as unalienable rights endowed by the Creator, of governments instituted to secure those rights deriving their just powers from the consent of the governed, and of the right of the people to alter or abolish a government that fails its purposes — this is the Lockean framing rendered as foundational political claim. The Federalist's substantive engagement with the conditions under which the social-contract framing could be operationalized through specific institutional architecture provides the design-imposition extension.

### The Evolutionary-Anthropological Record

The substantive evolutionary-anthropological record establishes that the social-contract framing's hypothetical state-of-nature is not a description of how polities actually form. The empirical-historical record of human political organization across deep time, across cultures, and across modes of subsistence shows wide variability in political formations — none of which is reducible to the social-contract derivation. The substantive scholarly engagement runs through Christopher Boehm's *Hierarchy in the Forest: The Evolution of Egalitarian Behavior* (1999) and *Moral Origins: The Evolution of Virtue, Altruism, and Shame* (2012) on the empirical record of hunter-gatherer egalitarianism maintained through reverse-dominance hierarchies; Polly Wiessner's long-term ethnographic and archaeological work on the Ju/'hoansi; David Wengrow and David Graeber's *The Dawn of Everything: A New History of Humanity* (2021) on the empirical variability of political organization across the deep human past; Marshall Sahlins's *Stone Age Economics* (1972) including "The Original Affluent Society"; Pierre Clastres's *Society Against the State* (1974) on stateless societies' active resistance to state-formation; James C. Scott's *Against the Grain: A Deep History of the Earliest States* (2017) and *The Art of Not Being Governed: An Anarchist History of Upland Southeast Asia* (2009).

The deep historical record establishes that human political organization across the Pleistocene and Holocene operated through multiple distinct political modalities — egalitarian band, larger-scale lineage-based polity, horticultural village, the agrarian state, the trade-and-tribute polity — none of which is well-described as derived from a hypothetical state-of-nature inhabited by abstract individuals. The Indigenous foundational record engaged at primary-articulator standing in Document A specifies the empirical-historical alternative: the Haudenosaunee Great Law as historically situated cultivation-condition specification across a confederacy of nations rather than as derivation from a hypothetical state-of-nature; the comparative archive of Indigenous polity-formation across the Americas, Africa, the Pacific, and Eurasia as empirical record of polities cultivated through specifiable conditions across deep time. The cooperative-cognitive-primate evidence through Frans de Waal's *Good Natured: The Origins of Right and Wrong in Humans and Other Animals* (1996) and *The Age of Empathy* (2009); through Michael Tomasello's *A Natural History of Human Thinking* (2014), *A Natural History of Human Morality* (2016), and *Becoming Human: A Theory of Ontogeny* (2019) on shared intentionality and joint commitment as the cognitive-evolutionary substrate of human cooperation; through Sarah Blaffer Hrdy's *Mothers and Others: The Evolutionary Origins of Mutual Understanding* (2009) on cooperative breeding as the evolutionary-cognitive substrate of distributed responsibility — establishes that the cooperative substrate is not an outcome of contract but a substrate the contract framing presupposes.

The substantive engagement with the social-contract framing through C. B. Macpherson's *The Political Theory of Possessive Individualism: Hobbes to Locke* (1962) — Macpherson's foundational critique of the Lockean-Hobbesian framing as historically specific to seventeenth-century English political-economic conditions rather than as universal-anthropological derivation — anchored the contemporary critical engagement that the substantive evolutionary-anthropological record has subsequently extended.

### The Lockean Possessive-Individualism Premise Re-Read

The Lockean possessive-individualism premise — the analytical claim that the human is in the first instance a proprietor of his own person and capacities, owing nothing to society for them, with political association entered into to secure those proprietary interests — operationalizes the social-contract framing through a specific anthropological commitment that the substantive record cannot accommodate. The cooperative-cognitive-primate evidence establishes that the human is in the first instance a cooperative-cognitive being whose individual capacities are constituted through joint intentionality, shared commitment, and cooperative breeding rather than being prior properties of an abstract self. Michael Tomasello's shared-intentionality framework — articulated across the *Natural History* sequence and substantively summarized in *A Natural History of Human Morality* — establishes that the species-defining cognitive innovation distinguishing human social cognition from that of other great apes is shared intentionality: the capacity to form joint goals, to coordinate around joint commitments, to engage in genuinely collaborative activities structured by mutual awareness of the shared project. Joseph Henrich's *The Secret of Our Success: How Culture Is Driving Human Evolution, Domesticating Our Species, and Making Us Smarter* (2015) and *The WEIRDest People in the World: How the West Became Psychologically Peculiar and Particularly Prosperous* (2020) extends the engagement with cultural learning as the species-defining capacity through which human cognition is constituted as relational rather than as individually proprietary.

The Indigenous and Eastern primary articulators stand at primary-articulator standing on the cooperative-cognitive substrate. Robin Wall Kimmerer's *Braiding Sweetgrass: Indigenous Wisdom, Scientific Knowledge, and the Teachings of Plants* (2013) on the honorable harvest as primary articulation of relational human-and-more-than-human relations; Tyson Yunkaporta's *Sand Talk: How Indigenous Thinking Can Save the World* (2019) on relational cognition; the African Ubuntu specification of personhood-through-relation through Mogobe Ramose's *African Philosophy through Ubuntu* (1999) — "I am because we are" as foundational ontological commitment that the possessive-individualism premise inverts; the Confucian relational-ontology tradition through Tu Weiming, Roger Ames and David Hall, and the broader engagement; the Buddhist dependent-origination tradition through Nagarjuna and Garfield; the Daoist relational ontology through the *Daodejing* and *Zhuangzi*; the Andean *ayni* reciprocity through PRATEC and Estermann; the Pacific Islander *Vā* relational ontology through Wendt and Vaai. Each tradition stands as primary articulator of the cooperative-cognitive substrate the possessive-individualism premise cannot accommodate.

What the possessive-individualism premise mistook: possessive individualism as natural baseline; the polity as aggregation of pre-social possessive individuals. What the biocultural-governance framing puts in its place: cooperative cognition as evolutionary-cognitive substrate; the polity as biocultural formation cultivated through the cooperative substrate. The Indigenous and Eastern primary articulators carry the cooperative-cognitive substrate at primary-articulator standing; the Western cooperative-cognitive-primate canon (de Waal, Tomasello, Hrdy, Henrich) is recent and partial recovery of insights the primary articulators have carried as substantive engagement.

### The Social-Contract Framing as Design-Imposition

The social-contract framing operates as design-imposition not in its substantive normative commitments — life, liberty, the pursuit of happiness, consent of the governed, the right to alter or abolish — but in its analytical mode. The framing derives the polity from a hypothetical pre-political state through abstract individuals' rational consent, and then specifies the institutional architecture appropriate to the derivation. The substantive normative commitments may survive the critique (they survive in this document and across the fifth-pass project at primary-articulator standing in Document A's foundational-texts engagement); the analytical mode does not. The polity is not derived; the polity is cultivated. The conditions under which the polity is cultivated are not specified in advance through a contract; the conditions emerge through the relational, cooperative-cognitive, and cultural-evolutionary substrate the polity grows through across deep time.

The cultivation framing does not abandon the normative commitments of the social-contract tradition. It re-specifies their analytical status: the commitments are cultivation conditions rather than contractual derivations. The right to alter or abolish is the cultivation-condition specification that any governmental form remains revisable in response to its failure to cultivate the conditions it was instituted to maintain — a commitment substantively articulated across the multi-traditional canonical inheritance Document A engages at primary-articulator standing.

## Part Three — Ecology, Carrying Capacity, and the Founders' Ecological Blindness

The founders had no ecology in the contemporary scientific sense. The Linnaean taxonomic project was underway during their lifetimes; Alexander von Humboldt's *Cosmos* (1845–1862) and the substantive development of ecology as discipline through the late nineteenth century and the twentieth (Frederic Clements, Henry Cowles, Arthur Tansley's coining of "ecosystem" in 1935; the substantive elaboration through Eugene Odum's *Fundamentals of Ecology*, 1953; the contemporary CAS-and-disturbance-ecology canon engaged in Document K) were not yet developed. The founders' substantive engagement with the physical-environmental and ecological substrate was conducted through agricultural-practical knowledge, through eighteenth-century natural-philosophical engagement (Buffon's *Histoire Naturelle*, 1749–1788; Linnaeus's *Systema Naturae*, 1735 onward), and through the substantive but pre-ecological commercial-and-agricultural-improvement tradition. The substantive critique developed here is not that the founders should have anticipated twentieth-and-twenty-first-century ecological science; it is that the absence of ecological substrate from their political theory left the implementation structurally unprepared to register the carrying-capacity, ecological-feedback, and disturbance-regime conditions the polity actually operates within.

### Carrying Capacity and Republican Virtue

The Jeffersonian agrarian-republican vision — articulated through the *Notes on the State of Virginia* (1785), the ward-republic correspondence with John Taylor of Caroline (1816), Joseph Cabell (1816), and Samuel Kercheval (1816), and the broader agrarian-republican tradition through Drew McCoy's *The Elusive Republic: Political Economy in Jeffersonian America* (1980), Lance Banning's *The Jeffersonian Persuasion: Evolution of a Party Ideology* (1978), and Joyce Appleby's *Capitalism and a New Social Order: The Republican Vision of the 1790s* (1984) — engaged substantively with the relationship between the polity's agricultural-ecological substrate and its republican-political character. The agrarian-republican vision held that republican virtue depended on broad distribution of landed independence, and that the polity's political-economic conditions therefore required ecological substrate adequate to sustain widespread freeholding. The vision was not naive about ecological conditions in the local sense; the agrarian-republican literature engaged substantively with soil quality, agricultural practice, and the relationship between agriculture and the polity's political-economic structure.

The substantive failure of the agrarian-republican vision under modern ecological evidence is not in its engagement with local agricultural conditions but in its inability to scale to planetary-boundary and biogeochemical-cycle conditions the substantive Anthropocene scholarship through Will Steffen and colleagues' planetary-boundaries framework (Stockholm Resilience Centre publications from 2009 forward), through Johan Rockström and colleagues' substantive engagement, through the broader Anthropocene-and-Earth-system-science literature, has subsequently established. The agrarian-republican vision could engage county-and-state-scale ecological substrate; it could not engage biosphere-and-ecozone-scale ecological substrate, because the substrate at that scale was not yet known and would not be substantively engaged until the twentieth and twenty-first centuries.

### The Commons Problem and the Property-as-Natural-Right Premise

The founders' engagement with property was substantively Lockean: property as natural right derivable from the mixing of labor with previously unowned natural substrate, with the polity instituted to secure the property thereby constituted. The Lockean labor-mixing argument in the *Second Treatise* chapter 5 articulated the foundational analytical claim; the founders' engagement operationalized the claim as foundational political-economic commitment. The substantive critique developed here is not that the labor-mixing argument is internally inconsistent but that the property-as-natural-right premise mistakes one historically specific property regime for the natural baseline of human economic life.

The substantive evidence against the natural-right framing of property runs across multiple scholarly literatures. Marcel Mauss's *The Gift: The Form and Reason for Exchange in Archaic Societies* (1925, Essai sur le don in the original French) established the foundational ethnographic engagement with gift-economy regimes as substantive alternatives to commodity-property regimes. David Graeber's *Debt: The First 5,000 Years* (2011) and *Toward an Anthropological Theory of Value* (2001) provide the substantive contemporary engagement with the historical-anthropological record of property and exchange. Annette Weiner's *Inalienable Possessions: The Paradox of Keeping-While-Giving* (1992) articulates the substantive engagement with the keeping-while-giving paradox in gift economies. Marshall Sahlins's *Stone Age Economics* (1972) engages substantively with the alternative property regimes operative in foraging and horticultural societies.

The commons-management archive through Elinor Ostrom's *Governing the Commons: The Evolution of Institutions for Collective Action* (1990) and the broader Ostrom corpus — extended through the Vincent and Elinor Ostrom polycentric-governance scholarship, through Bonnie McCay and James Acheson's *The Question of the Commons: The Culture and Ecology of Communal Resources* (1987), through Charlotte Hess and Elinor Ostrom's *Understanding Knowledge as a Commons: From Theory to Practice* (2007), and through the contemporary commons literature — establishes that commons-management institutions sustaining shared resources without private appropriation or central administration are substantive operational reality across cultures, time periods, and resource types. The "tragedy of the commons" articulated through Garrett Hardin's *Science* paper of 1968 was substantively wrong as anthropological generalization (though substantively correct as a description of one particular failure mode under specific institutional conditions); Ostrom's substantive engagement established the operational reality of commons-management institutions and specified the design principles under which they sustain themselves.

The Indigenous gift-economy and commons traditions stand at primary-articulator standing on the alternative-property substrate. Kimmerer's honorable harvest as primary articulation of relational property regime in which the takers are bound by reciprocal obligations to the more-than-human world from which the take is taken; the Andean *ayni* reciprocity tradition through PRATEC and Estermann's *Filosofía Andina* (1998) on substantive reciprocity as foundational economic-ethical category; the African Ubuntu specification of mutual economic obligation through Ramose; the Pacific Islander *Vā* relational ontology and the *ahupuaʻa* land-management tradition in Hawaiʻi through Davianna McGregor's *Nā Kuaʻāina: Living Hawaiian Culture* (2007); the broader Indigenous land-relational traditions engaged in Document A at primary-articulator standing.

The Islamic *waqf* tradition (the perpetual charitable trust under which property is endowed for charitable or public purposes in perpetuity with the principal remaining inviolate) and the Islamic *zakat* obligation (the foundational requirement of wealth redistribution to the poor) — engaged in Document A at primary-articulator standing through Hallaq's *The Impossible State* (2013), Çizakça's *A History of Philanthropic Foundations* (2000) — articulates the substantive anti-concentration economic-constitutional commitment that the property-as-natural-right premise cannot accommodate. The substantive engagement with the property regime that operates under *waqf* commitments is substantively distinct from the commodity-property regime the Lockean framing operationalizes.

### Future Generations and the Founders' Temporal Blindness

The founders engaged substantively with intergenerational continuity at the level of the polity's institutional persistence — the substantive question of how the Constitution might be designed to operate across generations is engaged across the Federalist Papers and the broader founding-period record. The substantive failure is not in the engagement with institutional persistence but in the absence of substantive engagement with intergenerational ecological obligation. The Haudenosaunee seventh-generation criterion — engaged at primary-articulator standing in Document A and operationalized in Document H (Part XII — Intergenerational Rectification as Cultivation Work) — articulates the substantive intergenerational ecological commitment that the founders did not develop. The Andean *Pachakuti* cyclical temporal architecture, the Buddhist *anicca* tradition on impermanence, the Daoist temporal-flow tradition, the Hopi temporal cosmology through Ekkehart Malotki's *Hopi Time* (1983), the Aboriginal Dreamtime tradition — each stands at primary-articulator standing on the intergenerational temporal substrate the polity actually operates within.

The contemporary Western engagement with intergenerational ethics through Edith Brown Weiss's *In Fairness to Future Generations: International Law, Common Patrimony, and Intergenerational Equity* (1989), through Stephen Gardiner's *A Perfect Moral Storm: The Ethical Tragedy of Climate Change* (2011), through the Long Now Foundation corpus (Stewart Brand's *The Clock of the Long Now*, 1999), through Rob Nixon's *Slow Violence and the Environmentalism of the Poor* (2011) — extends recent and partial recovery of insights the Indigenous and Eastern primary articulators have carried as substantive constitutional commitments.

### Property-as-Natural-Right as Design-Imposition

What the property-as-natural-right premise mistook: the polity as ground of fee-simple absolute property under Lockean labor-mixing premises; property as natural right derivable from pre-political theory. What the biocultural-governance framing puts in its place: property as one cultural-historical specification of cultivation-condition material relations among persons and between persons and the more-than-human world; the polity as biocultural formation cultivated through specifiable property-and-commons regimes appropriate to nested scales. Documents Q (Part VI — Cradle-to-Cradle and Circular Material Flows), E (Part XII — Currency Architecture), and D-3 (Part XII — Provisioning Decision Rights) operationalize the cultivation-condition property regime downstream.

## Part Four — Evolutionary Biology, Political Institutions, and the Limits of the Founders' Anthropology

The founders' engagement with human nature operated under the pre-Darwinian anthropology of the late eighteenth century. The substantive evolutionary biology subsequently developed through Charles Darwin's *On the Origin of Species* (1859) and *The Descent of Man, and Selection in Relation to Sex* (1871), and substantively extended through twentieth-and-twenty-first-century evolutionary-biological scholarship, was not available to the founders. The substantive critique developed here is not that the founders should have anticipated Darwinian and post-Darwinian evolutionary biology; it is that the eighteenth-century anthropology they worked within mistook the evolutionary substrate for a stable natural property of an abstract human nature, with substantive implications for the institutional architecture they specified. The substantial cultural-evolutionary scaling-mechanisms and multilevel-selection material that earlier passes engaged in this Part relocates to Document L (Part IV — Deep Substrate) per the holding file at `project/relocations/Document_4_relocations.md`. Document L carries the substantive treatment of the Sober-and-Wilson, Boyd-and-Richerson, Henrich, Tomasello cultural-evolutionary and cooperative-cognitive substrate; the present document retains the founding-era critique focused on the design-imposition framings the founders operated under.

### Evolution Did Not Optimize for Democracy

The substantive evolutionary record establishes that human cognition, motivation, and social organization were shaped by selective pressures across the Pleistocene that operated in small-band conditions substantially different from the large-scale-polity conditions any modern constitutional architecture must engage. The evolved psychology of political behavior — the substantive engagement with which runs through the broader evolutionary-psychology and behavioral-political-science literature, engaged with critical attention to the methodological caveats the contemporary scholarship has raised — establishes that political behavior at the organism-and-organ-system scale operates through cognitive-motivational substrates that were not selected for the conditions of large-scale democratic governance. The substantive implication is not that democracy is impossible (the empirical record establishes that democratic institutions operate substantively across cultures, time periods, and scales); the substantive implication is that the cognitive substrate's adequacy to large-scale democratic governance is a cultivation outcome rather than a natural property.

The dominance-hierarchy substrate that the substantive primatological and evolutionary-anthropological record establishes — including the Boehm engagement with reverse dominance hierarchies in *Hierarchy in the Forest* engaged in Part Two above — bears on the persistence of aristocracy, the substantive vulnerability of democratic institutions to authoritarian capture, and the broader political-psychological substrate the polity operates through. The founders engaged substantively with the persistence of aristocracy as political-historical reality — the Federalist Papers' analytical engagement with faction, with the substantive risks of executive concentration, with the conditions under which republican government might be sustained against the substantive pressures toward consolidation — but operated under the assumption that institutional design could neutralize the substrate. ## Part Five — Human Behavioral Ecology and the Founders' Substantive Engagement with Political Sustainability

The founders engaged substantively with the conditions under which republican government might be sustained. ### Tribalism, Out-Group Hostility, and the Costs of Faction Theory

## Part Six — Synthesis: The Constitution's Biological Blindspots and the Polity-as-Biocultural-Formation Thesis

The four founding-era anthropological assumptions — Lockean possessive individualism, the social-contract framing, the bounded-rational-actor model, the property-as-natural-right premise — operate together as the integrated anthropological substrate of the founding-era design-imposition framing of the polity. Each assumption is a particular analytical mode through which the founders engaged the polity's foundations; together they constitute a coherent (though flawed) anthropological-political theory. The substantive analytical move developed here is to specify the four assumptions as integrated design-imposition framings and to articulate the polity-as-biocultural-formation thesis as the analytical answer.

### The Four Design-Imposition Framings Read Together

The Lockean possessive-individualism premise mistook the human in the first instance as a proprietor of the human's own person and capacities. The cooperative-cognitive-primate evidence and the Indigenous and Eastern primary-articulator engagement with relational personhood establish that the human is in the first instance a cooperative-cognitive being constituted through joint intentionality, cultural learning, and relational substrate. The substantive analytical correction is foundational; the human is not in the first instance an individual.

The social-contract framing mistook the polity as an analytical-conceptual derivation from a hypothetical state-of-nature inhabited by abstract individuals. The substantive evolutionary-anthropological and Indigenous-foundational record establishes that the polity is empirically-historically formed through specifiable conditions across deep time; no hypothetical state-of-nature is required for the analytical engagement. The substantive analytical correction is foundational; the polity is not derived from a contract.

The bounded-rational-actor model mistook the cognitive substrate at the organism-and-organ-system scale as substantially uniform across persons, contexts, and conditions. The cognitive-bias and cognitive-plurality literatures establish that the substrate is plural, contextual, and varying. The substantive analytical correction is foundational; the deliberative subject is not abstract.

The property-as-natural-right premise mistook one historically specific commodity-property regime for the natural baseline of human economic life. The gift-economy and commons-management archives, the Indigenous and Eastern primary-articulator engagement with alternative property regimes, and the Islamic *waqf-and-zakat* tradition establish that the substantive operational reality of human property-and-economy is plural, with commodity-property as one historically specific regime among many. The substantive analytical correction is foundational; property is not naturally constituted prior to its cultural-historical articulation.

The four assumptions together constitute the integrated anthropological substrate of the design-imposition framing the founders operated under. The implementation they produced operationalized that substrate: the polity as aggregation of pre-social possessive individuals contracting under specifiable terms with rational-deliberative capacities and natural-right property in their own labor and its products. The substantive failure of the implementation under modern evidence is not a failure of any one assumption; it is the failure of the integrated design-imposition framing the four assumptions together specify.

### The Polity-as-Biocultural-Formation Thesis

The substantive analytical answer the fifth-pass framing develops is the polity-as-biocultural-formation thesis: the polity is a complex adaptive system cultivated across nested spatial and temporal scales as a biocultural formation, with biological substrate and cultural plurality coupled. The biological substrate is cooperative-cognitive-primate evolutionary inheritance shaped by deep-time historical specifications the comparative-anthropology archive records; the cultural substrate is the multi-traditional canonical inheritance Document A engages at primary-articulator standing; the cultivation conditions are specifiable at each scale of the nested-scales reference frame Document K (Part V) articulates in operational detail; the temporal orientation is intergenerational across the disturbance landscape Part II diagnostically specifies.

The polity-as-biocultural-formation thesis is foundational analytical move at substantive scholarly depth. The substantive engagement with the thesis runs through Document M (Part III — Biocultural Governance: Constitutional Ontology), which takes up the thesis as substantive scholarly analytical work. The substantive engagement with the deep substrate of the thesis runs through Document L (Part IV — Deep Substrate), which carries the multilevel-selection, hunter-gatherer cognitive-baseline, cultural-evolutionary scaling-mechanisms, comparative-anthropology-of-governance archive, and sub-species evolutionary backdrop material. The substantive engagement with the systems-science substrate of the thesis runs through Document K (Part V — CAS, Emergence, Disturbance Ecology, Adaptive Governance), which carries the complex-adaptive-systems and disturbance-ecology canon. The substantive engagement with the methodological discipline of the thesis runs through Document I (Part VII — Multi-Eyed Seeing as Constitutional Method), which carries the methodological commitment to multi-traditional canonical engagement at primary-articulator standing.

What the founders' anthropological assumptions cannot register: the polity as biocultural formation cultivated across nested scales; cooperative cognition as evolutionary-cognitive substrate; the polity's empirical-historical formation across deep time through specifiable conditions; cognitive plurality at the organism-and-organ-system scale; alternative property regimes operative across the multi-traditional canonical inheritance; the disturbance landscape the polity actually operates within. The implementation's failure to register these conditions is not a design defect to be patched; it is the ontological mistake the design-imposition framing produced.

### What the Science Implies and What It Does Not

The substantive evidence engaged in this document implies that the founders' anthropological assumptions are inadequate to the kind of formation the polity is, and that the implementation operationalized that inadequacy. The substantive evidence does not imply that human nature is fixed by biological substrate alone, that political institutions are reducible to cognitive-evolutionary substrates, or that the substantive normative commitments the foundational normative inheritance carries (engaged in Document A at substantive depth) are extinguished by the critique of the design-imposition framing. The substantive normative commitments survive the critique; the design-imposition framing does not.

The substantive engagement with what the evidence does not imply is foundational to the analytical work. Modern evolutionary, cognitive, and anthropological evidence does not establish biological determinism, does not establish that political institutions are reducible to evolved cognition, does not establish that the substantive normative commitments of the foundational inheritance are mere products of cultural evolution to be revised on biological grounds. The cultivation framing of the fifth-pass project's commitment is that the substantive normative commitments are foundational and that the design-imposition framing through which the founders operationalized those commitments is what requires revision under modern evidence.
