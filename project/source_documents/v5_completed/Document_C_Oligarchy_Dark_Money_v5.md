## Part One — The Founding-Era Anti-Corruption Tradition Reconstructed and Re-Framed Under Cultivation

The founding-era anti-corruption tradition is substantively engaged through Zephyr Teachout's *Corruption in America: From Benjamin Franklin's Snuff Box to Citizens United* (2014), which provides the foundational reconstruction of the substantive anti-corruption constitutional commitment the founders held. Teachout's substantive analytical claim is that the founders understood corruption as a substantive constitutional category whose contemporary judicial narrowing (engaged in Part Two) has substantially reduced the constitutional vocabulary available to address concentrated-wealth capture of political institutions. The founders engaged anti-corruption commitments at multiple registers: through the Federalist Papers' analytical engagement with faction, particularly *Federalist 10*'s analytical engagement with concentrated factional interests and the conditions under which republican government might be sustained against them; through *Federalist 51*'s analytical engagement with separation-of-powers as anti-concentration architecture; through *Federalist 57*'s analytical engagement with the substantive conditions under which representatives might remain accountable to their constituents; through *Federalist 76* on appointments and the substantive engagement with the conditions under which appointment power might be exercised without corruption. The Anti-Federalist anti-concentration tradition — engaged substantively in Document 2 through the Brutus essays, the Federal Farmer essays, and the Centinel essays — articulated parallel substantive concerns about concentrated power and the conditions under which republican government might be sustained.

The Emoluments Clauses of the U.S. Constitution (Article I, Section 9, Clause 8 prohibiting foreign emoluments; Article II, Section 1, Clause 7 prohibiting domestic emoluments to the President) constitute the founding-era constitutional architecture of anti-corruption operationalized through specific institutional provisions. The substantive engagement with the contemporary application of the Emoluments Clauses through the litigation surrounding the Trump administration (2017–2021) provides the contemporary substantive engagement with the founding-era architecture's contemporary operation.

The longer anti-corruption tradition extends through the Progressive Era anti-trust corpus — Louis Brandeis's *Other People's Money and How the Bankers Use It* (1914) on the substantive engagement with concentrated financial power, Theodore Roosevelt's New Nationalism address of 1910 on the substantive engagement with concentrated wealth as constitutional problem, the substantive engagement with the broader Progressive Era anti-trust legislation and adjudication — and through the New Deal and Great Society anti-concentration architecture. The substantive campaign-finance regulatory architecture of 1971 through 1974 (the Federal Election Campaign Act of 1971; the substantial 1974 amendments following Watergate; the establishment of the Federal Election Commission) constitutes the most recent substantial federal anti-corruption architecture before the post-1976 judicial dismantling Part Two engages.

### Cultivation-Condition Re-Frame

Under the cultivation framing of the fifth-pass project, the founding-era anti-corruption tradition is re-read as cultivation-condition specification: anti-corruption commitments are not engineered safeguards specifiable in advance of cultivation but cultivation conditions for the operation of republican decision rights. The cultivation conditions are sustained or eroded across generations through substantive political and institutional work; the conditions are not properties of designed architecture alone but of the ongoing political-institutional-cultural cultivation through which the architecture operates. The Anti-Federalist scale-and-locality tradition engaged in Document 2 articulates the cultivation-condition substrate at substantive depth: feedback conditions, scale-matching of decision rights to the substrate they operate over, the cultivation of civic capacity, the conditions of consent.

The substantive cultivation-framing engagement with the founding-era anti-corruption tradition is that the substantive normative commitments survive the cultivation re-frame — the substantive commitments to anti-corruption, anti-concentration, fiduciary public-trust government, and feedback-and-accountability architecture are foundational cultivation conditions; what does not survive is the design-imposition framing under which the founders sometimes operated in specifying the institutional architecture that would secure those commitments.

### The Confucian and Mencian Tradition

The Confucian and Mencian tradition on concentrated power is engaged through the *Mencius* on *renzheng* (humane rule) and the legitimacy of overthrow when the polity's substantive conditions of life are abandoned. The substantive engagement runs through Bryan W. Van Norden's translation of *Mengzi: With Selections from Traditional Commentaries* (2008); through Wm. Theodore de Bary's *The Trouble with Confucianism* (1991) and *Asian Values and Human Rights: A Confucian Communitarian Perspective* (1998); through Tu Weiming's *Confucian Thought: Selfhood as Creative Transformation* (1985) and *Centrality and Commonality: An Essay on Confucian Religiousness* (1989). The Mencian doctrine articulated at Mengzi 1A.7, 1B.8, and 4A.9 establishes the substantive constitutional commitment that the ruler's legitimacy depends on the cultivation of virtue and on the maintenance of the conditions under which the people flourish; when the ruler fails — when the people are without their constant means of livelihood (*zhi chan*), when concentrated wealth at the top denies the people their material foundation — the legitimacy is forfeit, and overthrow becomes legitimate. The substantive engagement at Mengzi 1B.8 is foundational: when asked whether the regicide of King Zhou by King Wu was legitimate, Mencius responded that what was killed was not a king but "a mere fellow" — a ruler who had so thoroughly abandoned the cultivation of virtue that he had ceased to be a ruler in the substantive sense.

The Confucian remonstrance tradition (*jian*) — institutionalized through the Imperial Censorate systems of the Han, Tang, Song, Ming, and Qing dynasties as substantive constitutional architecture of public-check against sovereign authority — articulates substantive anti-concentration commitments at the level of institutional architecture. The substantive engagement with the contemporary relevance through the broader Confucian-democracy engagement (de Bary, Tu Weiming, Joseph Chan's *Confucian Perfectionism*, 2014, Daniel Bell's *The China Model* of 2015 engaged with substantive critical caveats) provides the contemporary substantive engagement.

### The Daoist Tradition

The Daoist tradition on concentrated power is engaged through the *Daodejing*'s substantive engagement with the violence of hoarded plenty and the wisdom of substantive economic moderation. The *Daodejing*'s chapters 53, 75, and 77 (engaged through Philip J. Ivanhoe's *The Daodejing of Laozi*, 2002, and Brook Ziporyn's *Zhuangzi: The Essential Writings*, 2009) articulate substantive critique of concentrated wealth as violence against the polity's substantive conditions. The Daoist tradition's substantive commitment is that concentrated wealth produces substantive social and political harm not as collateral effect but as constitutive feature; the cultivation framing of the polity under Daoist commitments requires substantive structural commitment against accumulation.

### The Classical Indian Arthashastra Tradition

Kautilya's *Arthashastra* (engaged through Patrick Olivelle's *King, Governance, and Law in Ancient India: Kauṭilya's Arthaśāstra*, 2013) is foundational political-economic-constitutional text comparable in scope and analytical depth to the Aristotelian *Politics* but with longer continuous engagement. The Arthashastra's substantive engagement with the corruption of revenue administration — its detailed engagement with the conditions under which revenue officials defraud the sovereign and the public, with the institutional architecture necessary to constrain such corruption, with the substantive accountability mechanisms — articulates foundational substantive engagement with anti-corruption commitments at constitutional standing. The substantive engagement with the dharmashastra tradition on substantive constraints on unrestricted accumulation, with the Asokan dharma-edicts on right rule (engaged through Romila Thapar's *Aśoka and the Decline of the Mauryas*, 1961, revised editions through 2012), extends the engagement.

The dalit-Ambedkarite re-reading of the classical Indian tradition is foundational counter-tradition at primary-articulator standing. B. R. Ambedkar's *Annihilation of Caste* (1936) and the broader Ambedkarite corpus articulates substantive critique of concentrated caste-and-capital power as constitutive architecture of structural oppression that the classical Indian tradition has historically maintained. The substantive engagement preserves both the classical Indian and the dalit-Ambedkarite traditions at primary-articulator standing in their substantive contention.

## Part Three — The Post-1976 Doctrinal Sequence as Constitutional Architecture of Capture

The post-1976 doctrinal sequence — *Buckley v. Valeo* (1976), *Federal Election Commission v. Massachusetts Citizens for Life* (1986), *Austin v. Michigan Chamber of Commerce* (1990), *McConnell v. Federal Election Commission* (2003), *Federal Election Commission v. Wisconsin Right to Life* (2007), *Citizens United v. Federal Election Commission* (2010), *SpeechNow.org v. Federal Election Commission* (2010, D.C. Circuit), *McCutcheon v. Federal Election Commission* (2014), *Americans for Prosperity Foundation v. Bonta* (2021), *Janus v. American Federation of State, County, and Municipal Employees* (2018) — constitutes the constitutional architecture under which concentrated-wealth political participation has been progressively expanded and the post-Watergate campaign-finance regulatory architecture has been progressively dismantled.

### Buckley and the Money-as-Speech Equivalence

*Buckley v. Valeo*, 424 U.S. 1 (1976), established the foundational money-as-speech equivalence the subsequent doctrinal sequence has elaborated. The per curiam opinion upheld the Federal Election Campaign Act's contribution limits as substantively constitutional under the substantive interest in preventing corruption and the appearance of corruption, while striking down independent expenditure limits as substantively burdening political speech. The decision's substantive analytical move — that the expenditure of money in political communication is substantively expressive speech protected by the First Amendment, with regulation of that expenditure subject to the most demanding constitutional scrutiny — established the doctrinal foundation under which the subsequent expansion of concentrated-wealth political participation has operated. The substantive critique of *Buckley*'s analytical move runs through Lawrence Lessig's *Republic, Lost: How Money Corrupts Congress — and a Plan to Stop It* (2011), through Zephyr Teachout's *Corruption in America* (2014), through Richard L. Hasen's *Plutocrats United: Campaign Money, the Supreme Court, and the Distortion of American Elections* (2016) and *Cheap Speech: How Disinformation Poisons Our Politics — and How to Cure It* (2022).

### The Austin-McConnell-Citizens United Sequence

*Austin v. Michigan Chamber of Commerce*, 494 U.S. 652 (1990), upheld Michigan's substantive prohibition on corporate independent expenditures in candidate elections, articulating the anti-distortion rationale that the substantive concentration of corporate wealth creates substantive distortion in political processes that the state has substantive interest in preventing. *McConnell v. FEC*, 540 U.S. 93 (2003), upheld the substantial provisions of the Bipartisan Campaign Reform Act (BCRA) of 2002, including the substantive restrictions on soft-money contributions to political parties and the substantive electioneering-communications restrictions on corporate and union expenditures within specified windows before federal elections. *Federal Election Commission v. Wisconsin Right to Life*, 551 U.S. 449 (2007), began the substantive narrowing of *McConnell* through its substantive engagement with as-applied challenges to the electioneering-communications provisions.

*Citizens United v. FEC*, 558 U.S. 310 (2010), substantially overruled *Austin* and the relevant portions of *McConnell*, holding that the First Amendment substantively prohibits restrictions on independent corporate political expenditures in candidate elections. The substantive opinion (Justice Kennedy for the majority) articulated the analytical claim that corporations and unions hold substantive First Amendment expression rights coextensive with those of individuals in the political-speech context, and that the substantive anti-distortion rationale of *Austin* did not survive substantive First Amendment analysis. Justice Stevens's substantive dissent (joined by Justices Ginsburg, Breyer, and Sotomayor) articulated the substantive critique: that corporations are substantive legal-person entities created under specific legal frameworks for specific purposes, not natural persons holding inherent First Amendment rights; that the substantive anti-distortion rationale and the substantive interest in preventing corruption and the appearance of corruption survive substantive constitutional analysis; that the substantive consequences for democratic governance of the majority's holding are substantively severe. The substantive scholarly engagement with *Citizens United* — through Robert C. Post's *Citizens Divided: Campaign Finance Reform and the Constitution* (2014), through Adam Winkler's *We the Corporations: How American Businesses Won Their Civil Rights* (2018), through Lessig (2011), Hasen (2016, 2022), and Teachout (2014) — provides the substantive doctrinal-historical engagement.

### SpeechNow, McCutcheon, AFP v. Bonta, and Janus

*SpeechNow.org v. FEC*, 599 F.3d 686 (D.C. Cir. 2010), held that independent-expenditure-only committees (the Super-PAC architecture) cannot be subject to contribution limits, effectively eliminating the contribution-limit architecture for political action committees that operate independently of candidate campaigns. *McCutcheon v. FEC*, 572 U.S. 185 (2014), struck down aggregate-contribution limits on individual donors to federal candidates, committees, and parties, substantially expanding the substantive volume of contributions any single donor can make in a single election cycle. *Americans for Prosperity Foundation v. Bonta*, 594 U.S. ___ (2021), held that California's substantive requirement of donor disclosure for tax-exempt charitable organizations operating in the state violated the First Amendment's substantive associational-rights protection, substantially restricting the substantive disclosure architecture for charitable organizations engaged in political-advocacy activity.

*Janus v. American Federation of State, County, and Municipal Employees, Council 31*, 585 U.S. ___ (2018), is engaged here as part of the broader money-in-politics asymmetry. The decision held that public-sector unions cannot collect agency fees from non-member employees, substantially reducing the financial resources of public-sector unions and substantially altering the political-economy balance between concentrated-wealth political-advocacy entities and labor unions. The substantive engagement is not that *Janus* is directly a campaign-finance decision (it is not) but that the broader money-in-politics asymmetry the post-1976 doctrinal sequence has produced is substantively reinforced by the substantive constraints *Janus* has placed on labor-union political resources.

### The Doctrinal Sequence as Capture Architecture

Under the fifth-pass framing, the post-1976 doctrinal sequence is read not as a sequence of separate constitutional decisions but as the substantive constitutional architecture under which concentrated-wealth capture of the political-economic conditions of cultural formation has been licensed and expanded. The substantive analytical claim is that the cumulative architectural effect of the sequence — the expansion of corporate political-speech rights, the elimination of independent-expenditure limits, the elimination of aggregate-contribution limits, the substantial reduction of donor-disclosure architecture, the constraints on union political resources — constitutes substantive constitutional architecture of capture that operates through formally First Amendment-protected channels. The substantive critique through Lessig, Hasen, Teachout, Post, Winkler, and the broader contemporary campaign-finance scholarship provides the substantive analytical engagement.

The substantive engagement with the dissents — Stevens in *Citizens United*; Breyer in *McCutcheon* and the related opinions; the broader liberal-bloc dissents across the sequence — articulates the substantive constitutional alternative the majority sequence has displaced. The substantive engagement does not collapse the analytical work into polemic; the substantive doctrinal-historical engagement attends to the substantive constitutional arguments on each side of the majority-dissent disagreement.

The multi-traditional canonical inheritance engaged in Part Two articulates the substantive anti-concentration cultivation conditions across more than two millennia of substantive constitutional-philosophical engagement: the Confucian-Mencian *renzheng* commitment, the Daoist anti-accumulation tradition, the classical Indian Arthashastra engagement with revenue-administration corruption, the Islamic anti-*riba* and *zakat-and-waqf* architecture, the dalit-Ambedkarite anti-concentrated-power tradition, the Andean *ayni* reciprocity, the African Ubuntu economic critique. The Anglo-American founding-era anti-corruption tradition engaged in Part One contributes substantively to this inheritance while operating within particular historical-political-philosophical assumptions that the multi-traditional inheritance both extends and contests.

The contemporary American capture architecture violates the cultivation conditions the multi-traditional canonical inheritance specifies. The substantive analytical task of the rest of the arc is to specify the cultivation conditions under which the polity might be cultivated against the capture architecture's operation — through the constitutional-ontology articulation of Document M (Part III), through the systems-science substrate of Document K (Part V), through the methodological discipline of Document I (Part VII), through the material-substrate cultivation of Documents N–Q (Part VI), through the structural-commitments architecture of Part XII Documents E, F, H, J, D-1 through D-4, R, and through the cultivation-protocol synthesis of Document D-Synthesis (Part XIII) and the arc-wide capstone of Document G (Part XIV).

The substantive constitutional question is not whether the implementation can be renovated to address the capture architecture (the renovation framing the project's earlier passes carried) but how the polity can be cultivated to honor the multi-traditional anti-concentration foundational inheritance the implementation has not adequately engaged at all. The cultivation framing's analytical answer is that the substantive normative commitments survive — the substantive anti-corruption, anti-concentration, fiduciary, and feedback-and-accountability commitments are foundational cultivation conditions across the multi-traditional inheritance — and the design-imposition framing through which the founders sometimes operationalized those commitments does not survive substantive engagement with the multi-traditional inheritance and with the empirical evidence of how concentrated wealth has captured the institutional architecture across the post-1976 doctrinal sequence.

The capture architecture's diagnostic engagement specifies the empirical landscape; the cultivation-condition response operates downstream across the project's positive constitutional proposal.
