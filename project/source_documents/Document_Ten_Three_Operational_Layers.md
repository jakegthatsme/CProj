# Document Ten — Three Operational Layers of the Multi-Decade Project: State Innovation, International and Indigenous Governance, and Cognitive-Aware Mobilization

Document Nine closed the strategic phase of this project's argument by locating the contemporary moment in a panarchy cycle whose visible legislative phase has not yet arrived, and by prescribing what the conditions actually permit: patient, multi-scale, multi-decade infrastructure-building under hostile federal conditions, oriented toward the eventual reorganization phase that the panarchy framework specifies follows the release phase the inherited American constitutional system has now entered. That prescription remained at a level of generality. The three forward threads it sketched — state-level legislative innovation, international and Indigenous-governance engagement, and cognitive-aware political mobilization — were named as components without being specified as a strategy. This document specifies them, and it specifies them together. The three are not parallel essays glued at the binding. They are three operational layers of the same project, operating at distinct spatial and temporal scales — sub-national, international and cross-civilizational, cognitive and individual — but constituting one coherent panarchy-cycle infrastructure. State innovation builds the demonstration cases, the regulatory capacity, and the political constituencies that any eventual federal transformation will require. International and Indigenous frameworks supply the institutional vocabulary that the inherited American constitutional structure cannot generate from within itself: the seven-generation decision rule, legal personhood for natural systems, free, prior and informed consent, the doughnut, the relational ontologies of buen vivir and Ubuntu. Cognitive-aware mobilization closes the political-force gap — the gap between empirical crisis severity and political pressure — that Document Nine identified as the binding constraint on transformative action and that no quantity of institutional innovation can substitute for. The integration thesis of this document is that the three layers do not merely coexist but reinforce one another along specific causal pathways, and that the discipline of holding them together as one strategy is itself the strategic question the contemporary moment poses. The remainder of the arc — the document on constitutional architecture as positive proposal that should follow this one — depends on the operational layers' being specified first, because the constitutional architecture the project will eventually propose is not a blueprint to be drafted in advance of the political conditions that would receive it but a horizon toward which the operational work assembles the conditions.

## PART ONE — STATE-LEVEL LEGISLATIVE INNOVATION AS PANARCHY INFRASTRUCTURE

The state-level layer of the multi-decade project is its primary venue under contemporary federal conditions, and the framing of that claim matters. State innovation is not the project's substitute for federal transformation; it is the infrastructure that makes federal transformation possible when the political conditions for it eventually arrive. The framing matters because the alternative framings are widely available and have specific failure modes. The framing of state innovation as a federalist alternative to federal action — the position associated in different forms with libertarian "exit" theorists, with progressive devolutionists, and with state-level political consultants — accepts the inherited federalism as a settled rather than contested institutional architecture, and it treats the absence of federal transformation as a permanent rather than cyclical condition. The framing of state innovation as policy laboratory — Justice Louis Brandeis's 1932 dissent in *New State Ice Co. v. Liebmann* invoked the figure of states as "laboratories" that "may, if its citizens choose, serve as a laboratory; and try novel social and economic experiments without risk to the rest of the country" — captures something real about the experimental capacity of state-level institutions, but it has been deployed in service of a great variety of programs, including programs whose effect has been to constrain rather than enable substantive innovation. The project's framing differs from both. State innovation is panarchy infrastructure: the lower-scale, faster-cycle institutional adaptation that, in Lance Holling's panarchy framework, accumulates the structural capacity that eventual reorganization at higher scales draws upon. This framing requires that state innovation be assessed not by its immediate policy yield but by its contribution to the longer-cycle institutional capacity-building that the project specifies as its goal.

### The federalism question, revisited

The project has engaged the federalism question in pieces from Document Two forward, where the Federalist Papers' theory of compound republic was examined alongside the Anti-Federalist concern that a national government would absorb the states into administrative subordination. The contemporary federalism literature does not return to that founding-era debate as a settled matter. It reopens it from the other direction — not whether national power threatens the states, but whether state-level structures enable or obstruct the kinds of governance the contemporary moment requires. Heather Gerken's articulation of progressive federalism, developed across her 2012 essay "A New Progressive Federalism" in *Democracy: A Journal of Ideas* and through her subsequent scholarship including *Federalism All the Way Down*, makes the case that federalism's value for racial, religious, and ideological minorities is not a contingent feature dependent on which group happens to hold national power, but a structural feature of a polity in which different communities can constitute different majorities at different scales. Gerken's argument turns on what she calls the "minority's day in the sun" — the capacity of a numerical minority at the national scale to be a working majority in a state, county, or city, and to govern there in ways that build the political capacity, the demonstration cases, and the institutional infrastructure that national-scale change eventually requires. Jessica Bulman-Pozen and Gerken's joint essay "Uncooperative Federalism," published in the *Yale Law Journal* in 2009, develops a complementary argument: that the states' capacity to refuse cooperation with federal programs, or to implement them in ways that reshape their meaning, is a constitutional resource for democratic contestation rather than a defect to be cured. The Brandeisian "laboratories of democracy" frame is the older and looser form of these arguments; the Gerken-Bulman-Pozen frame is more theoretically precise about the conditions under which the laboratory metaphor describes anything real.

The critique of progressive federalism is equally important to engage and is not adequately addressed by the laboratories metaphor. Erin Ryan's *Federalism and the Tug of War Within* and her subsequent work on the "many faces of federalism" emphasize that federalism is an instrument as readily turned to exclusion as to inclusion, and that the same structural features that enable California's climate policy enable Texas's voter suppression, Florida's attacks on academic freedom and on transgender persons, and the systematic preemption of municipal innovation that has accelerated across the contemporary period. Bridget Fahey's recent work on federalism's structural incoherence — including her *Yale Law Journal* article "Federalism by Contract" — pushes further, arguing that the contemporary practice of federalism is not the polycentric structure of Madisonian theory but a series of ad hoc bargains between federal agencies and individual states that produces governance whose democratic legitimacy is far less defensible than either federalist or nationalist accounts would suggest. The honest answer to whether federalism enables or constrains the polycentric governance that Elinor Ostrom's framework prescribes is that it does both, and that which effect predominates depends on the substantive policy domain, the partisan configuration, and the institutional design choices made within the federalist structure. The project's framework does not require that federalism be defended in the abstract; it requires that the design features of state-level institutions be assessed for whether they produce the long-cycle capacity-building that the panarchy framework identifies as the work of the contemporary phase.

A further dimension of the federalism question that the project's framework brings into focus is the relationship between formal constitutional federalism and the polycentric governance that Ostrom's *Governing the Commons* (1990) and her 2009 *Science* paper "A General Framework for Analyzing Sustainability of Social-Ecological Systems" specify as the institutional form best suited to managing common-pool resources at multiple scales. Polycentricity in Ostrom's sense is not identical with American federalism, and conflating the two has produced confusion in both directions. Ostrom's polycentricity refers to multiple, overlapping centers of governance authority that operate at different scales, communicate with one another, and adapt their boundaries and rules to the resource systems they govern. American federalism is one possible institutional realization of polycentricity, but it is a peculiar realization: its scales are fixed by constitutional text rather than adapted to resource systems, its communication channels among levels are mediated by partisan and judicial institutions whose dynamics are not optimized for resource governance, and its authority allocations are products of historical contingency rather than design. The project's framework suggests that state-level innovation is most generative when it explicitly builds polycentric structures that align governance scales with the social-ecological systems being governed — watershed councils, regional climate compacts, multi-state grid governance, tribal-state-federal co-management arrangements — even when these structures fit awkwardly into the formal federalism categories. Carl Folke's work on social-ecological systems at regional scales, particularly his synthesis essays for the Stockholm Resilience Centre, specifies what kinds of multi-scale institutional arrangements have actually performed under the kinds of cross-scale stresses the contemporary period imposes.

### The empirical record of state-level innovation in consequential domains

Within the domains the project has identified as most consequential, state-level innovation in the contemporary period has produced a record substantial enough to assess. The record is not uniform. Some innovation has built durable institutional capacity that survives partisan transitions and propagates across jurisdictions. Some has depended on specific political alignments and dissipated when those alignments changed. The project's framework requires distinguishing these two categories, because only the first contributes the long-cycle capacity-building that the strategic project depends on.

Climate policy is the domain where the empirical record is most extensive and the institutional learning curve most legible. California's cap-and-trade program, established under Assembly Bill 32 (2006) and operationalized through the Air Resources Board's regulations in 2012, has now run for more than a decade and has produced a body of regulatory experience, technical staff capacity, and design lessons that the project's framework can assess. The program's record is mixed in ways that are themselves informative. The cap has bound, allowance prices have generally tracked policy intent, and the program has linked with Quebec's system to create a multi-jurisdictional carbon market. The program has also encountered the design pathologies that the cap-and-trade literature anticipated — over-allocation of allowances in early phases, the offsets controversy involving forest projects whose additionality is disputed, the environmental-justice critique that aggregate emission reductions can coexist with local pollution concentrations in disadvantaged communities, the political vulnerability of the program to industry lobbying for allowance set-asides. California's Senate Bill 100 (2018), which established the 100-percent zero-carbon electricity target by 2045, builds on a different regulatory architecture — the renewable portfolio standard — and operates through utility planning rather than market pricing. The Regional Greenhouse Gas Initiative, the multi-state cap-and-trade compact for the power sector that began operations in 2009 with ten Northeastern and Mid-Atlantic states, has produced documented emission reductions in the covered sector, has survived the temporary New Jersey withdrawal under Governor Christie and the subsequent rejoining under Governor Murphy, has expanded to include Virginia (which subsequently withdrew under Governor Youngkin and rejoined the policy conversation in altered form), and has demonstrated that interstate climate compacts can sustain themselves across partisan transitions when their design includes credible exit costs. Renewable portfolio standards now operate in approximately thirty states with significant variation in stringency, eligible technologies, and compliance mechanisms; the empirical literature including work by Galen Barbose at Lawrence Berkeley and Sanya Carley at Indiana University has produced reasonable consensus that these standards have driven substantial renewable deployment, with effects partially offsetting in adjacent states without standards.

The New York Climate Leadership and Community Protection Act of 2019 deserves particular attention as a more recent and more architecturally ambitious instance. The Act establishes binding statutory targets for greenhouse gas emissions (a forty percent reduction below 1990 levels by 2030 and an eighty-five percent reduction by 2050), requires the Department of Environmental Conservation to promulgate regulations to achieve those targets, establishes a Climate Action Council to develop a scoping plan with statutory standing, and includes a Climate Justice Working Group with explicit authority over the criteria for identifying disadvantaged communities and a thirty-five-percent investment-benefit floor for those communities. The Act's design includes features the project's framework specifies as important: binding statutory targets that constrain agency discretion, explicit equity provisions that are not advisory, a deliberative council structure that includes both expert and community representation, and feedback mechanisms that require periodic plan updates. The Act's implementation has been contested in ways that test whether the design features hold under political pressure: the cap-and-invest regulation has been delayed, the gas-system transition planning has encountered the predictable utility-industry opposition, and the workforce-transition provisions have lagged behind the emission-reduction provisions. The relevant question for the project's framework is not whether the Act has succeeded on its own terms — that question is premature — but whether the institutional capacity it has built (the agency staff, the technical models, the deliberative council, the community organizations now operating within the Act's framework) constitutes durable infrastructure that survives political transition and propagates beyond New York. The early indications are mixed. The disadvantaged-communities criteria methodology has been borrowed by other states. The Climate Action Council structure has been studied as a model. The agency capacity has been built in ways that would be difficult for a future hostile administration to dismantle quickly. The political-economy weakness of relying on a single state's continued majority commitment remains real.

Building electrification ordinances in cities including Berkeley (whose 2019 ordinance was eventually struck down by the Ninth Circuit in *California Restaurant Association v. City of Berkeley* on preemption grounds in 2023), New York City, Seattle, Washington, and Denver have produced a litigation record that is itself instructive about the constraints on state and municipal innovation. The Ninth Circuit's Berkeley decision, which held that the city's ordinance was preempted by the federal Energy Policy and Conservation Act, illustrates how federal preemption doctrines can constrain municipal innovation in ways that progressive federalism scholarship has tracked. The institutional response — building-code and air-quality-regulation pathways that achieve similar outcomes through different legal authority — illustrates the adaptive capacity that durable institutional infrastructure makes possible.

Voting rights and democratic reform constitute a second domain with a substantial state-level record. Automatic voter registration, first enacted in Oregon in 2015 and now operating in approximately twenty-five states and the District of Columbia, has produced a documented increase in registration rates and has begun to produce empirical literature on whether the registration increase translates into voter participation increase (the answer appears to be modestly yes, with effects concentrated in low-information voters who would not have registered through prior processes). Same-day registration, operating in approximately twenty states, has similarly produced empirical evidence of participation effects. Ranked-choice voting has been adopted at the state level in Maine for federal elections in 2016, in Alaska for state and federal elections in 2020, and at the municipal level in approximately fifty jurisdictions including New York City, Minneapolis, San Francisco, and Salt Lake City. The empirical literature on ranked-choice voting's effects — work by Jack Santucci, Lee Drutman, and others — suggests modest effects on candidate diversity and on negative campaigning, with the institutional learning curve still in progress. The Michigan Independent Citizens Redistricting Commission, established by ballot initiative in 2018 and operationalized for the 2020 redistricting cycle, represents one of the most institutionally ambitious instances of structural democratic reform: a commission of randomly selected citizens (with statutory criteria for selection that approximate sortition principles), with binding redistricting authority constitutionally protected from legislative interference, producing maps that have been litigated and largely upheld. The Commission's design includes features that the deliberative-democracy literature specifies as important — citizen selection by lot, support staff with technical capacity, transparent deliberation, and binding output. Its performance through one cycle is not yet a verdict, but the institutional capacity built — staff, procedures, public legitimacy, demonstration that citizen redistricting can produce technically credible maps — constitutes durable infrastructure of the kind the project's framework values. The New York City citizen assemblies, the Oregon Citizens' Initiative Review (operating since 2010 with statutory authorization since 2011), and the various climate assemblies at city and state levels constitute a smaller but expanding set of institutional experiments in deliberative democracy that Part Three will engage in more detail.

Labor policy at the state level has produced a record that the project's framework should engage carefully because the political economy of labor regulation differs from the political economy of climate or democratic-reform policy in ways that affect what kinds of state innovation propagate. California's Assembly Bill 5 (2019), which codified the *Dynamex Operations West v. Superior Court* (2018) standard for distinguishing employees from independent contractors, and the subsequent Proposition 22 (2020) that exempted gig-economy companies from AB 5, illustrate both the potential for substantial state-level labor reform and the political-economy vulnerability of such reform when concentrated industry interests can mobilize ballot-initiative resources. The California Private Attorneys General Act (PAGA, 2003), which authorizes employees to sue employers for Labor Code violations on behalf of the state and to recover civil penalties, has produced a substantial body of enforcement that operates parallel to and partially independent of the state Labor Commissioner's enforcement capacity. State minimum wage laws above the federal floor now operate in approximately thirty states with significant variation in level and indexing; the empirical literature including work by Arindrajit Dube, Michael Reich, and others has produced reasonable consensus that minimum-wage increases at the levels enacted have produced employment effects substantially smaller than the predictions of competitive-market models would suggest. Public-sector bargaining frameworks, varying substantially across states, have proved both creative (the Hawaii and Oregon expansions to public-sector workers) and vulnerable (Wisconsin's Act 10 of 2011 effectively eliminated public-sector bargaining for most workers; Indiana, Michigan, and other states have enacted similar measures). The Washington State long-term care insurance program, the Massachusetts Fair Share Amendment (2022) that established a state surtax on incomes above one million dollars dedicated to education and transportation, and the various state-level paid-family-leave programs constitute additional instances of substantial state-level labor and social-insurance innovation.

Rights of Nature and ecological constitutionalism at the sub-federal level have produced a more limited but instructive record, one whose strategic significance is best understood in conjunction with the international Rights of Nature jurisprudence that Part Two engages in detail. The Lake Erie Bill of Rights, adopted by Toledo voters in 2019 and struck down by federal district court in *Drewes Farms Partnership v. City of Toledo* in 2020 on vagueness and preemption grounds, illustrates both the political possibility of municipal Rights of Nature ordinances and the legal vulnerability of such ordinances under existing federal jurisprudence. The White Earth Band of Ojibwe's adoption of the Rights of Manoomin (wild rice) in 2018, and the subsequent litigation including the tribal court proceedings against the Minnesota Department of Natural Resources, represents a different institutional pathway — tribal sovereignty-based Rights of Nature recognition that operates within a different legal framework than municipal ordinances and may be more durable for that reason. The rights-of-nature ordinances adopted in Pittsburgh (2010), Santa Monica (2013), and other municipalities under the framework developed by the Community Environmental Legal Defense Fund constitute a corpus that has not yet been substantially tested in litigation but that has built political constituencies and institutional vocabulary. Tamaqua Borough's 2006 ordinance, often cited as the first municipal Rights of Nature provision in the United States, addressed the specific concern of sewage sludge disposal and demonstrates that the ordinance form can be deployed against specific extractive threats with local political viability.

Doughnut-economics and post-growth experiments at sub-federal scales include Amsterdam's adoption of the doughnut framework in 2020 as the official basis for municipal planning under the framework developed by Kate Raworth and operationalized through the Doughnut Economics Action Lab. Portland Oregon's well-being budgeting initiatives, the New Mexico legislation requiring a Genuine Progress Indicator alongside Gross State Product reporting, the Hawaii Aloha+ Challenge sustainability framework with constitutional grounding, and various municipal experiments with alternative indicators constitute a smaller record but one whose institutional significance lies in establishing that alternatives to GDP-based decision-making are operationally feasible at sub-national scales. The project's framework values these experiments not for their immediate policy effects, which are modest, but for their construction of institutional vocabulary and demonstration cases that larger-scale adoption would require.

Indigenous co-governance arrangements constitute the final and in many ways most institutionally substantive category of state-level innovation that the project's framework engages. The Bears Ears Inter-Tribal Coalition — comprising the Hopi Tribe, the Navajo Nation, the Ute Mountain Ute Tribe, the Ute Indian Tribe, and the Pueblo of Zuni — and the management framework established under President Obama's 2016 designation, partially rescinded under President Trump in 2017, restored under President Biden in 2021, and contested again under the current administration, illustrates both the potential for substantive co-governance between tribal and federal authorities and the political vulnerability of administrative co-governance arrangements that lack statutory or constitutional grounding. The Klamath Basin restoration, including the dam removal completed in 2024 — the largest dam removal project in American history — demonstrates how decades-long coordination among tribal nations (the Klamath Tribes, the Yurok Tribe, the Karuk Tribe, the Hoopa Valley Tribe), federal agencies, state governments, irrigators, and environmental organizations can produce substantive ecological restoration even under conditions where the political coalition is fragile and the legal architecture is contested. The Bad River Band of Lake Superior Chippewa's defeat of the Enbridge Line 5 reroute through tribal sovereignty assertion, treaty-rights litigation, and Wisconsin state coordination demonstrates the strategic possibilities of tribal-state coordination on infrastructure questions that operate at scales the federalism literature has not adequately theorized. Tribal water settlements — the Navajo-Gallup Water Supply Project, the Gila River Indian Community settlement, the Yakama Nation settlement, among many others — constitute a body of institutional design experience in tribal-federal-state co-governance of water resources that the project's framework should engage as some of the most substantively polycentric arrangements that operate in contemporary American governance.

### What the biomimicry and adaptive-management framework specifies

Document Five established the framework's analytical purchase on policy questions through the work of Janine Benyus, J.B. Ruhl, Carl Folke, and the panarchy theorists. Applied to state-level innovation, that framework specifies a distinction the immediate-policy assessment does not capture: the distinction between innovation that builds durable institutional capacity and innovation that depends on specific political alignments to operate at all. The distinction is not between formal and informal institutions, nor between regulatory and legislative innovation, nor between executive and legislative authority. It is between institutional designs that include the feedback mechanisms, technical-capacity foundations, and adaptive-rule-revision procedures that adaptive management identifies as constitutive of resilient governance, and institutional designs that operate as static rule-sets vulnerable to political reversal.

The California Air Resources Board exemplifies the first category. Its statutory authority predates the contemporary climate policies, its technical staff capacity has been built over decades and is not easily reconstituted by political appointees, its rulemaking procedures include feedback mechanisms that incorporate compliance data and emerging science, and its regulatory output has propagated through Section 177 of the Clean Air Act to other states that have adopted California's vehicle standards. A future hostile California administration could rewrite the Board's rules, but the institutional capacity — the staff, the procedures, the data systems, the regulated-community expertise that has formed around the Board's standards — would persist as latent infrastructure that could be reactivated. The New York Department of Environmental Conservation, the Massachusetts Department of Energy Resources, the Washington Department of Ecology, and a small set of other state agencies operate at similar institutional levels. The Michigan Independent Citizens Redistricting Commission, although it has operated for only one cycle, has built procedural infrastructure with constitutional grounding that would be substantially harder to dismantle than legislative redistricting authority.

The first category has the property that the panarchy framework identifies as essential to lower-scale capacity-building: the institutional capacity persists across cycles of political release and reorganization, accumulating over time and becoming available as resources for higher-scale reorganization when conditions permit. The second category does not have this property. A renewable portfolio standard enacted by simple legislative majority, without an associated regulatory agency with built capacity, can be repealed by simple legislative majority. A climate target without statutory standing, an executive order without legislative ratification, an administrative co-governance arrangement without congressional or constitutional grounding — all of these can be reversed in a political cycle. They contribute to immediate policy yield, but they do not contribute to long-cycle capacity-building.

The strategic implication is that the project's framework prioritizes a specific subset of state-level innovation: the construction of regulatory agencies with technical staff capacity that exceeds political-appointee turnover; the establishment of standing deliberative bodies, particularly those grounded in sortition or constitutional protection, with adaptive procedures and feedback mechanisms; the negotiation of legally protected co-management structures, particularly those grounded in tribal sovereignty or interstate compact, that operate across political cycles; and the institutionalization of decision-quality procedures, particularly adaptive-management-derived rule-revision protocols, that can absorb new information and revise rules without requiring new legislation. J.B. Ruhl and Robin Craig, in their joint work including their 2014 *Vanderbilt Law Review* article "Designing Administrative Law for Adaptive Management," have specified what the legal-architectural requirements for adaptive management actually are; their analysis suggests that most state-level adaptive-management efforts have implemented partial versions that lack the full architectural support adaptive management requires, and that the project's framework should distinguish state innovations that include the architectural elements (standing rule-revision authority, technical capacity, structured uncertainty-management procedures, transparency requirements) from those that adopt the adaptive-management vocabulary without the underlying architecture.

The panarchy framework adds a further consideration: the propagation question. Holling's panarchy specifies that lower-scale innovation propagates to higher-scale reorganization through specific causal pathways — accumulation of demonstrated alternatives, training of personnel, formation of constituencies that can mobilize at higher scales, generation of institutional vocabulary that higher-scale actors can adopt. Some state-level innovations propagate well — California's vehicle emission standards through Section 177, renewable portfolio standards through interstate emulation, the Affordable Care Act's draw on Massachusetts's Romneycare. Some do not — Wisconsin's Act 10 has propagated but in directions hostile to the project's framework, while progressive labor reforms have generally failed to propagate beyond their originating states. The propagation patterns are not random. They depend on the institutional features of the originating innovation (whether its design includes elements that other states can readily borrow), on the existence of professional networks across states (the National Conference of State Legislatures, regional governors' associations, state-attorneys-general networks), on the political coalitions that mobilize for emulation, and on the federal regulatory environment that may permit, encourage, or obstruct propagation. The project's framework prioritizes innovations whose institutional design facilitates propagation, even when the immediate political conditions for propagation are not yet present.

### The structural pathologies of state-level innovation

The project's framework cannot honestly assess state-level innovation as panarchy infrastructure without engaging the structural pathologies that the same federalist architecture enabling progressive innovation also enables. The framing of state innovation as project-relevant infrastructure does not require that all state innovation be valorized; it requires honest assessment of which state innovations contribute to the project's framework and which obstruct it.

The federalism that enables California's climate policy enables Texas's voter suppression and its abortion bounty regime, Florida's "Don't Say Gay" legislation and its attacks on academic freedom and on transgender persons, the Tennessee legislature's expulsion of duly elected legislators and its preemption of Memphis and Nashville's local authority, the proliferation of book bans and curriculum restrictions across Republican-controlled states, and the systematic state-level legislation directed at restricting the substantive citizenship of LGBTQ+ persons, immigrants, racial minorities, and women. These are not aberrations within a generally progressive federalism; they are operations of the same institutional structure that enables the progressive innovations the project's framework values. A federalism evaluation honest enough to be useful must hold both sides of this ledger together.

State preemption laws — state legislation that constrains or prohibits municipal authority on specific policy questions — have accelerated dramatically across the contemporary period. The Local Solutions Support Center has documented the proliferation of preemption laws across multiple policy domains: minimum wage preemption, sick-leave preemption, anti-discrimination preemption, environmental-regulation preemption, paid-leave preemption, plastic-bag-regulation preemption, sanctuary-city preemption, gun-regulation preemption, broadband-municipalization preemption. The asymmetry is structural: state legislatures, particularly under Republican control in states whose political geography concentrates Democratic voters in urban areas, have systematically preempted municipal innovation when that innovation has run ahead of state-level political consensus. The Supreme Court's expansion of federal preemption doctrine, including its preemption of California's vehicle emission rule under specific provisions of the Clean Air Act in *Engine Manufacturers Association v. South Coast Air Quality Management District* (2004) and the Berkeley building-electrification preemption in 2023, has compounded the constraint on innovation at sub-state scales. Richard Schragger's work, including his *City Power: Urban Governance in a Global Era* (2016), has developed the theoretical and historical case that the city is a constitutional actor whose authority has been systematically constrained by state preemption in ways that the federalism literature has under-engaged. Schragger's analysis suggests that the contemporary state-level innovation conversation cannot be conducted without explicit attention to the asymmetric preemption pattern: progressive states permit substantial municipal innovation, while conservative states preempt it, and the aggregate effect is to constrain municipal innovation more in jurisdictions where its value to historically marginalized communities would be greatest.

The political-geography literature, including work by Jonathan Rodden in *Why Cities Lose: The Deep Roots of the Urban-Rural Divide* (2019), Lily Geismer on the suburban-progressive coalition, and the broader literature on the geographic concentration of partisan preferences, specifies how the federalism structure interacts with electoral geography to produce asymmetric outcomes. The geographic concentration of Democratic voters in urban areas, combined with single-member-district legislative representation, produces durable Republican majorities in state legislatures even where state-level partisan preference is roughly balanced. The result is that the federalism structure functions less as a polycentric system of multiple majorities and more as a specific asymmetric mechanism that empowers rural and exurban majorities to constrain urban and suburban majorities. The project's framework does not require resolving this geographic asymmetry — that is a structural feature of the inherited constitutional architecture that any reorganization phase will need to address — but it does require honest reckoning with how the asymmetry constrains the strategic possibility of state-level innovation.

The interstate compact mechanism, often invoked as a vehicle for cooperative innovation among progressive states, has produced both the Regional Greenhouse Gas Initiative and other progressive instruments and the Compact for a Balanced Budget and other instruments hostile to the project's framework. The Article V interstate-compact mechanism for proposing constitutional amendments, the National Popular Vote Interstate Compact (which has been adopted by states totaling 209 electoral votes as of recent counts and is conditioned on reaching 270), and the various compacts on water, education, and infrastructure constitute a body of institutional architecture that the project's framework can engage as both opportunity and risk. The strategic implication is that the compact mechanism is a multivalent institutional resource whose progressive use requires explicit attention to its conservative use by counterpart actors.

### What decision-quality literature specifies for state legislative process design

The decision-science literature engaged across Documents Four and Seven provides the project's framework with specific design prescriptions for state legislative processes that have not yet been fully internalized in state-level innovation. The decision-quality framework, developed in Ronald Howard and James Matheson's *The Principles and Applications of Decision Analysis* (1984) and subsequent work, specifies six elements of decision quality: appropriate framing, useful alternatives, meaningful information, clear values and trade-offs, logically correct reasoning, and commitment to action. State legislative processes systematically underperform on most of these elements. Framing is dominated by partisan framings inherited from national politics rather than developed for the substantive issue. Alternatives are typically restricted to the two or three options that have been politically pre-vetted, with the substantive option-space inadequately explored. Information is filtered through partisan staff structures and lobbyist channels rather than through structured information-elicitation procedures. Values and trade-offs are usually implicit rather than explicit, with the consequence that decisions are made without clarity about which values they advance and which they sacrifice. Logical reasoning is constrained by the cognitive limitations identified in the Kahneman, Tversky, and Gigerenzer literature. Commitment to action is variable, with much state legislation amounting to symbolic position-taking rather than implementation-grounded policy.

Bent Flyvbjerg's work on optimism bias in megaproject planning, particularly his *How Big Things Get Done* (2023) with Dan Gardner, applies directly to state-level infrastructure and policy planning. Flyvbjerg's empirical record across thousands of large projects shows that planners systematically underestimate costs, overestimate benefits, and underestimate completion times, with the magnitude of the bias substantial enough to invert the cost-benefit analyses that justified the projects. The reference-class forecasting methodology Flyvbjerg has developed — comparing a planned project's parameters to the realized parameters of comparable completed projects rather than relying on bottom-up estimation — has been adopted in some jurisdictions including the United Kingdom's Treasury Green Book guidance and is beginning to be adopted in some American state-level processes, but its adoption is far from systematic. The project's framework specifies that state-level climate planning, infrastructure investment, and economic-development planning should incorporate reference-class forecasting and structured uncertainty management, and that the state innovations that include these procedures contribute disproportionately to long-cycle capacity-building.

Robert Lempert and the RAND Pardee Center's work on Robust Decision Making, including the methodology developed in *Shaping the Next One Hundred Years* (2003) and applied to climate adaptation planning across multiple state and regional jurisdictions, specifies a different but complementary set of design prescriptions. RDM begins not from probability assessments — which the cognitive-bias literature suggests are systematically unreliable for novel and consequential decisions — but from identifying decision strategies that perform reasonably well across a wide range of plausible futures, and then identifying the conditions under which strategies fail. RDM has been deployed in California's climate adaptation planning, in Louisiana's coastal master plan, in the Colorado River Basin study, and in various municipal water-management contexts. The state-level innovations that incorporate RDM-style approaches are distinguishable from those that rely on point-estimate cost-benefit analysis, and the project's framework values the former more highly because they produce decisions whose validity does not depend on forecasts that the cognitive-bias literature suggests we cannot reliably make.

The Citizens' Initiative Review, established in Oregon in 2010 and now operating in several states with varying institutional support, exemplifies a different decision-quality intervention: the use of randomly selected citizens, supported by trained moderators and balanced expert testimony, to evaluate ballot measures and produce statements that appear in the voters' pamphlet. The empirical literature on the Citizens' Initiative Review, including work by John Gastil and Katherine Knobloch, suggests that the process produces voter information products that are more accurate, more comprehensive, and more usable by voters than the partisan-produced alternatives. The institutional investment required is modest, and the procedural innovation is replicable; the project's framework values the Citizens' Initiative Review as an instance of the kind of decision-quality infrastructure that can be built incrementally at state scales and that propagates well because its design is portable. James Fishkin's Deliberative Polling methodology, which Part Three engages in more detail, has been deployed at state and regional scales in California, Connecticut, Texas, and elsewhere, with results that suggest the methodology can produce reliable indicators of what citizen preferences would be under conditions of structured deliberation.

### The Part One synthesis: which state innovations are highest-leverage

The strategic implication of the analysis above is that not all state-level innovation contributes equally to the multi-decade infrastructure project the prior document prescribed, and that the project's framework can specify which categories contribute most. The highest-leverage state-level innovations share four properties. They build durable regulatory or technical-agency capacity that exceeds political-appointee turnover. They establish standing deliberative or decision-quality procedures, particularly those grounded in sortition or constitutional protection, with adaptive-revision authority. They produce institutional vocabulary and design templates that propagate to other states. They generate constituencies and personnel networks that build the political capacity for higher-scale reorganization when conditions permit.

By these criteria, the highest-leverage state-level innovations of the contemporary period include: the institutional architecture of the New York Climate Leadership and Community Protection Act, particularly its Climate Justice Working Group and disadvantaged-communities methodology; the California Air Resources Board's regulatory infrastructure, including its expansion under SB 100 and its incorporation of environmental-justice criteria; the Michigan Independent Citizens Redistricting Commission, as a sortition-grounded constitutionally protected deliberative body with binding output; the tribal-state co-management arrangements, particularly those grounded in treaty rights and tribal sovereignty rather than in administrative discretion, including the Klamath restoration and the Bears Ears coalition under conditions of statutory grounding; the Citizens' Initiative Review and analogous deliberative-democracy procedures with statutory support; and the multi-state regulatory compacts including the Regional Greenhouse Gas Initiative and the National Popular Vote Interstate Compact, as instances of inter-jurisdictional coordination architecture.

By the same criteria, lower-leverage innovations despite immediate political appeal include: legislation without associated regulatory-capacity building; executive orders without legislative ratification; symbolic resolutions and target-setting without implementation infrastructure; programs whose funding depends on annual appropriation rather than dedicated revenue or trust-fund structures; advisory bodies without binding authority; and pilot programs whose institutional integration into permanent structures is not specified at the outset. These innovations have policy value, and the project's framework does not dismiss them, but they do not produce the long-cycle capacity-building that the strategic project depends on.

A further specification is required. The state-level layer of the multi-decade project is not separable from the layers Parts Two and Three develop, and the highest-leverage state innovations are those whose design facilitates integration with the international and Indigenous-governance vocabularies that Part Two engages and the cognitive-aware mobilization methodologies that Part Three engages. State innovations that build institutional vocabulary translatable across the international Rights of Nature jurisprudence, that institutionalize co-governance arrangements with tribal nations consistent with the United Nations Declaration on the Rights of Indigenous Peoples and free, prior and informed consent norms, and that generate concrete demonstration cases usable by cognitive-aware mobilization methodology constitute the integration target. The state-level layer is most generative when it operates not as a self-contained policy program but as the sub-national instantiation of the multi-scale project the next two parts continue to specify.

## PART TWO — INTERNATIONAL AND INDIGENOUS GOVERNANCE: INSTITUTIONAL VOCABULARIES OUTSIDE THE INHERITED FRAMEWORK

The international and Indigenous-governance layer of the multi-decade project has been engaged across prior documents in pieces — the Rights of Nature jurisprudence in Document Five, the Indigenous knowledge traditions in Documents Four and Six, the planetary boundaries framework in Documents Seven and Eight — but has not been synthesized as a single operational layer of the strategic project. This Part synthesizes it. The analytical thesis is that international venues and Indigenous governance traditions supply institutional vocabulary, normative authority, demonstration cases, and ontological frames that the inherited American constitutional structure cannot generate from within itself, and that engagement with these venues is therefore not optional supplementation to a domestically focused project but a necessary component of the multi-decade infrastructure-building that the panarchy framework specifies as the work of the contemporary phase. The American constitutional structure as engaged in Documents One through Three was designed before the planetary-boundaries scientific framework existed, before the Universal Declaration of Human Rights existed, before the United Nations Declaration on the Rights of Indigenous Peoples existed, before the legal personhood of natural systems was an active jurisprudential question, before Indigenous governance traditions were treated as live institutional resources rather than as historical curiosities or as legitimacy claims to be settled through extinguishment. The vocabulary the inherited structure provides is not adequate to the institutional questions the contemporary moment poses. Part Two specifies where the additional vocabulary comes from, what it consists of, and how engagement with it integrates with the state-level innovation Part One developed.

### The international climate and biodiversity governance frameworks

The international climate and biodiversity governance frameworks operate at a scale and through institutional logics that American constitutional doctrine has historically treated with suspicion. The Senate's repeated refusal to ratify environmental treaties — the United Nations Convention on the Law of the Sea, the Convention on Biological Diversity, the Kyoto Protocol — and the formal withdrawal mechanisms that allow successive administrations to enter and exit international frameworks have produced a pattern in which American participation in international environmental governance is inconsistent across political cycles and in which the United States has often been the principal obstacle to ambition rather than its principal supporter. The pattern has consequences for American politics that the project's framework should engage rather than ignore.

The Paris Agreement of 2015 represents the contemporary architecture of international climate governance. Its principal features include the long-term temperature goal of holding global warming "well below 2°C above pre-industrial levels and pursuing efforts to limit the temperature increase to 1.5°C above pre-industrial levels" (Article 2(1)(a)), the nationally determined contributions architecture under which states submit and update emission-reduction commitments (Articles 3 and 4), the global stocktake every five years (Article 14), the financial provisions for climate finance from developed to developing countries (Article 9), and the loss and damage provisions whose institutional content has been substantially developed since the Agreement's adoption. The architectural design choice of nationally determined contributions — bottom-up commitment rather than top-down quotas — was made in response to the failure of the Kyoto Protocol's top-down architecture to produce American participation, and it has produced near-universal participation but at the cost of aggregate ambition that falls substantially short of the Agreement's own temperature goals. The 2023 Synthesis Report of the United Nations Framework Convention on Climate Change found that the aggregated nationally determined contributions, if fully implemented, would produce a global temperature increase of approximately 2.5 to 2.9°C, with the gap to 1.5°C compatible pathways requiring roughly a forty-three percent reduction from 2019 emission levels by 2030 that the current contributions do not achieve.

The Loss and Damage Fund, established by decision at the 27th Conference of the Parties (COP27) in Sharm El-Sheikh in 2022 and operationalized at COP28 in Dubai in 2023 with the World Bank as interim host, represents an institutional development whose significance the project's framework should specify. The Fund addresses the climate-justice claim that vulnerable countries — particularly small island developing states, least developed countries, and African states — have suffered and will suffer climate impacts disproportionate to their historical emissions, and that the developed-country emitters who produced the bulk of cumulative atmospheric carbon owe compensation for the resulting losses and damages. The negotiation of the Fund, led by the Alliance of Small Island States and supported by the broader G77 and China negotiating bloc, represents one of the most significant institutional victories of climate-justice mobilization in three decades of UNFCCC negotiations. The initial pledged capitalization remains modest in relation to the estimated losses — the developed-country pledges at COP28 totaled approximately seven hundred million dollars, against estimates of climate-related losses in developing countries running to hundreds of billions annually — and the operational rules continue to be negotiated. The institutional precedent matters more than the initial capitalization. The Fund establishes that climate finance is not exclusively about mitigation and adaptation but extends to losses and damages, and it establishes that the developed-developing distinction in obligation persists despite the long-running American negotiating effort to dissolve it.

The Kunming-Montreal Global Biodiversity Framework, adopted at the 15th Conference of the Parties to the Convention on Biological Diversity in December 2022, establishes targets for global biodiversity governance through 2030. Its headline target — the protection of thirty percent of terrestrial and marine areas by 2030, the so-called 30 by 30 target — has propagated rapidly into national and sub-national policy commitments, including the United States's commitment to a domestic 30 by 30 target through the America the Beautiful initiative announced by the Biden administration in 2021. The Framework also includes targets on the reduction of pollution, the restoration of degraded ecosystems, the integration of biodiversity values into economic decision-making, the recognition of Indigenous and traditional knowledge, and the financial mobilization for biodiversity conservation including a target of at least two hundred billion dollars annually by 2030. Like the Paris Agreement, the Framework lacks binding compliance mechanisms; like the Paris Agreement, it has propagated normative commitments rapidly into domestic and sub-national policy spaces. The strategic significance for the project's framework is that the Framework supplies institutional vocabulary — particularly the 30 by 30 target and the recognition of Indigenous-led conservation — that state-level innovation can reference and that domestic political mobilization can deploy.

The High Seas Treaty, formally the Agreement on Marine Biodiversity of Areas Beyond National Jurisdiction, adopted in March 2023 after nearly two decades of negotiation, addresses the governance gap in marine areas beyond national jurisdiction that constitute approximately two-thirds of the ocean. The Treaty establishes procedures for marine protected areas in international waters, environmental impact assessments for activities in those waters, capacity-building and technology transfer to developing countries, and the equitable sharing of benefits from marine genetic resources. The Treaty's significance for the project's framework lies less in its specific provisions — which are weaker than the negotiating parties' initial proposals and contain substantial conditional language — than in its institutional precedent: the recognition that planetary commons require governance architectures that operate beyond the inherited Westphalian state-system, and the establishment of an institutional vocabulary for that recognition.

The planetary boundaries framework, developed by Johan Rockström, Will Steffen, and colleagues at the Stockholm Resilience Centre and articulated initially in their 2009 *Nature* paper "A Safe Operating Space for Humanity" and subsequently revised in the 2015 *Science* paper "Planetary Boundaries: Guiding Human Development on a Changing Planet" and in the 2023 update extending the framework to cover all nine boundaries, has not yet been operationalized into formal international governance architecture comparable to the Paris Agreement or the Convention on Biological Diversity. The framework's institutional traction operates through its incorporation into the Sustainable Development Goals' analytical framework, through Kate Raworth's adaptation in *Doughnut Economics: Seven Ways to Think Like a 21st-Century Economist* (2017) into the doughnut framework that Amsterdam and other jurisdictions have adopted, and through its uptake in scientific and advocacy communities. The 2023 update's finding that humanity has now transgressed six of the nine planetary boundaries — climate change, biosphere integrity, biogeochemical flows, land-system change, freshwater change, and novel entities — establishes an empirical baseline against which the inadequacy of existing international governance architecture can be assessed. The project's framework values the planetary boundaries framework as an institutional vocabulary in formation: the framework's authority within scientific communities supports its eventual incorporation into governance architectures even before formal incorporation has occurred, and its use in domestic political deliberation can deploy that scientific authority in ways that contest the inherited cost-benefit framings of climate and biodiversity policy.

The honest critique of these international frameworks, which the project's framework must engage rather than dismiss, includes both realist and climate-justice strands. Joseph Nye's analysis in *The Future of Power* (2011) and subsequent work on the limits of soft power in addressing collective-action problems specifies the structural reasons international environmental governance has produced commitments that exceed enforcement: the absence of central enforcement authority, the asymmetric distribution of costs and benefits across states, the susceptibility of long-term commitments to short-term political reversal. The realist critique is not that international environmental governance is meaningless but that its causal pathways operate through normative pressure, reputational consequences, and domestic political mobilization rather than through enforcement, and that overstating its capacity produces strategic miscalculation. The climate-justice critique, articulated by Mary Robinson in *Climate Justice: Hope, Resilience, and the Fight for a Sustainable Future* (2018) and by the Global South negotiating bloc across decades of UNFCCC negotiations, is that the architecture of international climate governance has been shaped by the negotiating leverage of historical emitters in ways that systematically inadequately address the just-transition obligations the empirical record entails. The failure-rate critique, articulated empirically by the United Nations Environment Programme's Emissions Gap reports and by the Climate Action Tracker, is that the actual emissions trajectory has not been substantially altered by the Paris architecture: emissions continued to rise after 2015 and only began to decline in some developed economies in the most recent years, while developing-economy emissions continued to rise. These critiques are accurate. They do not refute the project's framework's claim that international governance is a venue for institutional-vocabulary construction; they specify what international governance can and cannot do and require honest assessment of which of the multi-decade project's needs international engagement actually serves.

What international climate and biodiversity governance can do for the project's framework: generate normative resources whose authority within domestic American politics exceeds what the inherited constitutional architecture can generate; produce demonstration cases of institutional design (the Loss and Damage Fund, the Te Awa Tupua structure that Part Two engages below, the Whanganui co-governance arrangement) that domestic actors can reference; create the evidentiary record on which domestic litigation, legislation, and political mobilization can draw; influence the soft-power consequences for American international standing in ways that affect domestic political calculations. What international governance cannot do: substitute for domestic political mobilization; bind the United States to obligations a domestic political coalition does not support; produce institutional change inside the United States without domestic actors translating international norms into domestic political and legal action.

### Rights of Nature jurisprudence as comparative legal frontier

The Rights of Nature jurisprudence developed across multiple jurisdictions over the last two decades constitutes the most institutionally substantive comparative legal frontier the project's framework can engage. The jurisprudence is not unitary. Its ontological premises, legal architectures, implementing institutions, and empirical performance vary substantially across jurisdictions. Engaging it as a single body of law conflates importantly different institutional designs. The project's framework should engage the jurisdictions in their specificity, identifying what each contributes to the institutional vocabulary the multi-decade project requires.

Ecuador's 2008 Constitution, adopted under the constitutional process initiated by President Rafael Correa and shaped by the Pachakutik political movement and the broader Indigenous and ecological coalitions of the period, established Rights of Nature in Articles 71 through 74. Article 71 provides that "Nature, or Pacha Mama, where life is reproduced and exists, has the right to integral respect for its existence and for the maintenance and regeneration of its life cycles, structure, functions and evolutionary processes." Article 72 establishes the right to restoration of nature, distinct from human compensation for damage. Article 73 requires the State to apply precautionary and restrictive measures to activities that can lead to species extinction, ecosystem destruction, or permanent natural-cycle alteration. Article 74 recognizes the right of communities to benefit from environmental services in ways that the State will regulate. The constitutional language is the work of multiple authors and political traditions, with Indigenous-cosmology references (Pacha Mama as Andean cosmological figure) integrated with Western legal-rights vocabulary. The implementing jurisprudence has developed across several major cases. The Vilcabamba River case (*Wheeler v. Provincial Government of Loja*, Provincial Court of Loja, 2011), the first successful Rights of Nature litigation under the new constitutional provisions, ruled in favor of the river against road-construction activities that had altered its course and damaged its banks. The Yasuní-ITT case, while not strictly a Rights of Nature decision, addressed the protection of Yasuní National Park against oil development and culminated in the 2023 referendum in which Ecuadorian voters approved the indefinite suspension of oil extraction in Block 43-ITT. The Los Cedros Forest case (Constitutional Court of Ecuador Case No. 1149-19-JP/21, decided 2021) established the most substantial constitutional jurisprudence on Rights of Nature to date, with the Court ruling that mining concessions in the Los Cedros protected forest violated the rights of nature and ordered their cancellation. The Court's opinion engaged in extended analysis of how Rights of Nature operate as constitutional rights, what relationship they bear to other constitutional rights including economic-development rights, and what implementation procedures they require.

The Mihnea Tănăsescu literature, including *Understanding the Rights of Nature: A Critical Introduction* (2022), has specified the legal-architectural questions Rights of Nature jurisprudence raises and not yet fully answered: whether nature is a rights-bearer comparable to a person or a different kind of legal entity; what entities (rivers, forests, ecosystems, species, individual non-human animals) hold what rights; who has standing to litigate on behalf of nature; what remedies follow from rights violations; how Rights of Nature interact with property rights, with economic-development imperatives, with Indigenous sovereignty, and with state authority. Tănăsescu's analysis is sympathetic but rigorous: he argues that Rights of Nature is best understood not as a unified legal doctrine but as a family of related institutional experiments, and that its evaluation should be conducted experiment by experiment rather than in the abstract. Craig Kauffman and Pamela Martin's empirical work, particularly *The Politics of Rights of Nature: Strategies for Building a More Sustainable Future* (2021), has documented the implementation gap between constitutional and statutory adoption of Rights of Nature and on-the-ground ecological outcomes: in many jurisdictions, including parts of Ecuadorian and Bolivian implementation, the formal legal recognition of Rights of Nature has not translated into changed outcomes on extractive activity, sometimes because of lack of judicial enforcement, sometimes because of conflicting development imperatives, sometimes because of the political-economy power of extractive industries.

Bolivia's Law of the Rights of Mother Earth (Ley de Derechos de la Madre Tierra), adopted in 2010 and supplemented by the Framework Law of Mother Earth and Integral Development for Living Well (Ley Marco de la Madre Tierra y Desarrollo Integral para Vivir Bien) in 2012, establishes a different architectural design. The 2010 Law recognizes Mother Earth as a collective subject of public interest and establishes specific rights including the right to life, the right to the diversity of life, the right to water, the right to clean air, the right to equilibrium, the right to restoration, and the right to live free from pollution. The 2012 Framework Law operationalizes the rights through the broader concept of vivir bien (living well, drawn from the Andean concept of suma qamaña in Aymara and sumak kawsay in Quechua) and establishes implementing institutions including the Plurinational Mother Earth Authority. The Bolivian implementation has encountered the tension between the legal recognition of Mother Earth's rights and the actual continuation of extractive activities — particularly lithium and natural gas extraction — that the Morales and subsequent administrations relied upon for state revenue. The implementation gap is substantial and has been the principal critique directed at the Bolivian framework. The constitutional and legal architecture nonetheless supplies institutional vocabulary that other jurisdictions have engaged: the recognition of Earth as a collective subject of public interest, the integration of Indigenous cosmological language (vivir bien, suma qamaña, sumak kawsay) into formal legal frameworks, the establishment of dedicated implementing institutions.

New Zealand's Te Awa Tupua (Whanganui River Claims Settlement) Act 2017 represents an institutional design distinct from the Ecuadorian and Bolivian constitutional frameworks. The Act recognizes the Whanganui River as Te Awa Tupua, a legal person with all the corresponding rights, duties, and liabilities of a legal person. The Act establishes Te Pou Tupua as the human face of Te Awa Tupua, comprising two persons appointed jointly by the Crown and by Whanganui Iwi (the Maori tribal nation whose traditional territory includes the river). The Act's design grounds the legal personhood not in an abstract rights-of-nature framework but in the Treaty of Waitangi settlement process and in the specific tikanga (Maori law and practice) of the Whanganui Iwi, including the foundational principle that "Ko au te awa, ko te awa ko au" — I am the river, the river is me. The institutional design includes the Te Pou Tupua structure for representing the river, the Te Karewao advisory group, the Te Heke Ngahuru strategy group, and the Te Kōpuka management plan structure. The Te Urewera Act 2014, addressing the former Te Urewera National Park, establishes a similar but distinct framework: the legal personhood of Te Urewera as a former-national-park area, with Te Urewera Board appointed by Tūhoe (the iwi whose territory includes Te Urewera) and the Crown jointly. The Te Urewera and Te Awa Tupua arrangements are products of the Treaty of Waitangi settlement process, and they cannot be understood apart from that larger institutional context. The strategic significance for the project's framework is that the New Zealand experiments demonstrate the legal personhood of natural systems can be grounded in Indigenous law and treaty relationships rather than in abstract Rights of Nature constitutional language, and that the resulting institutional designs have specific co-governance properties that the broader Rights of Nature jurisprudence has not generally achieved.

Colombia's Constitutional Court Decision T-622 of 2016, addressing the Atrato River, recognized the river as a "subject of rights" entitled to "protection, conservation, maintenance, and restoration," and established a guardianship structure for the river's representation. The decision was followed by Decision STC-4360 of 2018 from the Supreme Court of Justice's Civil Cassation Chamber, which recognized the Colombian Amazon as a "subject of rights" and ordered the executive branch to develop and implement a plan to address deforestation. Subsequent decisions have addressed the Magdalena River, the Cauca River, and other ecosystems. Daniel Bonilla Maldonado's work on Colombian constitutional law, particularly his analysis of the activist Constitutional Court's role in the development of social and environmental rights, situates the Rights of Nature jurisprudence within the broader Colombian constitutional tradition of progressive jurisprudence. The Colombian implementation has encountered the political-economy and security challenges that constrain Colombian environmental governance generally — the assassination of environmental defenders, the persistence of armed-group operations in resource-extraction zones, the political-economy power of mining and agribusiness interests — but the constitutional jurisprudence itself has continued to develop and has influenced comparative jurisprudence in other Latin American jurisdictions.

The Indian jurisprudence on the Ganges and Yamuna rivers, developed initially by the Uttarakhand High Court in 2017 in *Mohd. Salim v. State of Uttarakhand* and subsequent decisions, recognized the rivers as legal persons with the corresponding rights. The decisions were stayed by the Supreme Court of India shortly thereafter on the ground that the legal-personhood designation produced practical difficulties including the question of who would be liable for damage caused by river flooding. The Indian jurisprudence is therefore an instance of Rights of Nature recognition that has not stabilized institutionally, and it serves the project's framework as a cautionary case about the design questions that legal-personhood frameworks must resolve to operate durably.

The White Earth Band of Ojibwe's Rights of Manoomin, adopted by tribal council resolution in 2018, recognizes the rights of wild rice (manoomin in Anishinaabemowin) within the territory of the White Earth Band's reservation. The litigation that followed — the White Earth Band's tribal-court action against the Minnesota Department of Natural Resources over the issuance of water-rights permits to the Enbridge Line 3 pipeline that the Band claimed would harm wild rice — represents one of the first instances of tribal-court Rights of Nature jurisprudence in the United States. The Minnesota Department of Natural Resources challenged tribal-court jurisdiction over a state agency, and the federal litigation that followed has not yet conclusively resolved the jurisdictional question. The strategic significance of the White Earth experiment for the project's framework is its demonstration that tribal sovereignty constitutes a legal authority distinct from state and federal authority, that tribal law can recognize ecological subjects in ways that state and federal law generally do not, and that the jurisdictional questions raised at the boundaries between tribal and state authority are themselves a frontier of constitutional development. The Yurok Tribe's Rights of the Klamath River, adopted in 2019, represents a similar institutional development: the recognition by tribal sovereignty of the river as a legal person with rights to existence, to a healthy ecosystem, and to restoration. The Yurok Tribe's involvement in the Klamath dam-removal coalition, culminating in the 2024 dam removal, demonstrates the operational capacity of tribal Rights of Nature recognition when integrated with broader environmental coalitions and with state and federal counterparts willing to engage tribal authority as a co-equal jurisdiction.

The skeptical literature on whether Rights of Nature legal recognition actually changes ecological outcomes deserves engagement on its strongest terms. The skeptical case rests on several propositions. First, the empirical record across jurisdictions shows substantial gaps between formal legal recognition and ecological outcomes; in Bolivia and Ecuador, extractive activities have continued at substantial rates after Rights of Nature constitutional adoption, and in Colombia the Atrato and Amazon decisions have not reversed the deforestation trajectory. Second, the institutional design of Rights of Nature frameworks often relies on existing courts whose enforcement capacity is constrained by the same political-economy forces that produced the ecological harms in the first place; legal recognition without enforcement capacity is performative rather than substantive. Third, the Rights of Nature framing risks displacing more direct political mobilization for ecological protection, by channeling energy into legal-recognition campaigns whose connection to actual ecological outcomes is mediated by judicial implementation. Fourth, the ontological claims of Rights of Nature — that nature is a subject of rights, that rivers and ecosystems have legal personhood — face cultural and conceptual resistance that limits the framework's political traction in many jurisdictions, including the United States.

The response to the skeptical case must be honest. The empirical record is mixed; legal recognition without enforcement is not adequate; the political-economy forces that produced ecological harm continue to operate after legal recognition; the ontological claims face resistance. The project's framework does not value Rights of Nature jurisprudence because it has produced uniformly successful ecological outcomes — it has not — but because it constitutes an institutional vocabulary in active development, generating institutional designs (the Te Awa Tupua co-governance structure, the Atrato guardianship, the Yurok-Klamath integration) that the multi-decade project requires as resources for the eventual reorganization phase. The framework's value to the project is as vocabulary-construction, demonstration-case generation, and ontological-frame development, not as a uniformly effective ecological-protection mechanism. The honest assessment is that the framework is a frontier of institutional experimentation whose results are mixed, that the most institutionally substantive instances are those grounded in Indigenous law and treaty relationships rather than in abstract constitutional rights-language, and that the strategic engagement should be selective and informed by attention to which institutional designs have actually produced operational changes.

### The United Nations Declaration on the Rights of Indigenous Peoples and free, prior and informed consent

The United Nations Declaration on the Rights of Indigenous Peoples, adopted by the General Assembly in September 2007 with 144 states in favor, four against (the United States, Canada, Australia, and New Zealand), and eleven abstentions, establishes the framework of international Indigenous-rights law that the contemporary period has continued to develop. The four states that initially opposed the Declaration have subsequently endorsed it in modified form: Australia in 2009, New Zealand in 2010, Canada in 2010 (and through legislation in 2021), and the United States in 2010. The endorsement is not the same as ratification or domestic-law incorporation, and the United States's endorsement was accompanied by interpretive statements that substantially constrained its domestic effect.

The Declaration's substantive content includes the right of Indigenous peoples to self-determination (Article 3), the right to autonomy or self-government in matters relating to internal and local affairs (Article 4), the right to maintain and strengthen distinct political, legal, economic, social, and cultural institutions (Article 5), and a series of more specific rights including the right to free, prior and informed consent (FPIC) for relocation (Article 10), for the use of cultural property (Article 11), for legislative or administrative measures that may affect them (Article 19), and for projects affecting their lands or territories and other resources (Article 32). The FPIC framework operates as a procedural right whose substantive content has been developed across two decades of jurisprudence, treaty-body interpretation, and practical implementation.

S. James Anaya, who served as the United Nations Special Rapporteur on the Rights of Indigenous Peoples from 2008 to 2014 and whose work including *International Human Rights and Indigenous Peoples* (2009) constitutes one of the most authoritative legal analyses of the Declaration's content, has specified what FPIC requires: that consent be free of coercion, intimidation, or manipulation; that it be obtained prior to authorization or commencement of activities; that it be informed, with adequate information about the project's nature and impacts; and that consent be the substantive standard, with specific guidance on when consent is required versus when consultation may suffice. The Inter-American Court of Human Rights has applied the FPIC framework in cases including *Saramaka People v. Suriname* (2007), *Kichwa Indigenous People of Sarayaku v. Ecuador* (2012), and *Kaliña and Lokono Peoples v. Suriname* (2015), establishing a body of regional jurisprudence that operates as comparative law for jurisdictions including the United States.

The contrast between Canadian and American implementation of UNDRIP norms is instructive for the project's framework. Canada's United Nations Declaration on the Rights of Indigenous Peoples Act, adopted in June 2021, requires the federal government to take all measures necessary to ensure that the laws of Canada are consistent with the Declaration, to develop and implement an action plan to achieve the Declaration's objectives, and to report annually on progress. The Act does not directly incorporate the Declaration as binding domestic law, and its implementation has been contested in ways that have produced both substantive progress and substantial limitations. The Canadian implementation nonetheless represents a more substantive operationalization of UNDRIP norms than the American non-implementation, and the comparative material has been used in American advocacy and litigation.

The strategic litigation that has used UNDRIP norms in domestic American courts constitutes a body of practice that the project's framework should engage. The Standing Rock Sioux Tribe's litigation over the Dakota Access Pipeline, including *Standing Rock Sioux Tribe v. U.S. Army Corps of Engineers* and the subsequent litigation through 2020, drew on UNDRIP norms in advocacy and amicus briefing although the controlling legal framework was domestic. The Mauna Kea protests against the Thirty Meter Telescope, the Bears Ears litigation over the boundaries of the national monument, and various pipeline and extractive-industry challenges have similarly drawn on UNDRIP norms. The American legal system has not generally adopted UNDRIP norms as controlling domestic law, but the norms have operated as interpretive resources, as advocacy framings, and as soft-law constraints on administrative discretion. The project's framework values these uses for what they actually accomplish: the construction of a body of precedent in which UNDRIP norms have been deployed in domestic American legal contexts, the development of advocacy infrastructure that can mobilize UNDRIP norms in future cases, and the gradual normalization of FPIC standards in administrative and corporate decision-making that the formal absence of binding domestic incorporation would otherwise prevent.

Kyle Whyte's work, particularly his essays on Indigenous environmental justice and on what he calls the "intergenerational injustice" of climate change, including "Indigenous Climate Change Studies: Indigenizing Futures, Decolonizing the Anthropocene" (2017) and his subsequent work, articulates the theoretical framework within which UNDRIP norms operate as more than procedural protections: as the institutional architecture for the recognition of Indigenous peoples as polities with their own governance traditions, decision procedures, and ecological relationships. Whyte's analysis specifies that FPIC is not merely a consultation requirement but a recognition of the political authority of Indigenous nations to make decisions about their territories and futures, and that the institutional implications extend beyond the procedural to the structural. Glen Coulthard's *Red Skin, White Masks: Rejecting the Colonial Politics of Recognition* (2014) articulates a critique of the recognition framework itself, arguing that institutional recognition by colonial states can substitute for the substantive return of land and political authority that Indigenous self-determination actually requires. The project's framework engages both Whyte's institutional account and Coulthard's structural critique without resolving the tension between them, because the tension is itself a feature of the institutional terrain that the multi-decade project must navigate.

### Indigenous governance traditions as load-bearing institutional vocabulary

The project's framework specifies that Indigenous governance traditions are not optional supplementation to the institutional vocabulary the multi-decade project requires but load-bearing components of that vocabulary. The framing matters because the alternatives are widely available and have specific failure modes. Treating Indigenous traditions as historical curiosities, as repositories of "wisdom" detached from operational governance, as ornament added to a Western institutional framework, or as one example among many of "alternative" approaches all underestimate what the traditions actually are: continuing, operational, intellectually substantive governance institutions whose principles, decision procedures, and ontological frames the project's framework must engage on their own terms. The engagement requires citing Indigenous scholars and primary sources directly, not exclusively through Western mediators, and treating the institutional vocabulary as available resources for the construction of alternative architectures rather than as anthropological data about how non-Western peoples think.

The Haudenosaunee Great Law of Peace (Kaianere'kó:wa, also rendered Gayanashagowa) constitutes one of the longest-continuously-operating governance institutions in the world. The Confederacy of the Five Nations (Mohawk, Oneida, Onondaga, Cayuga, Seneca) — joined by the Tuscarora in the early eighteenth century to form the Six Nations — operates the Great Law as a constitutional framework that has been sustained through Indigenous governance institutions despite centuries of colonial disruption. The Great Law's institutional design includes features of substantial relevance to the project's framework: the seven-generation decision rule, requiring that decisions consider their effects on the seventh generation hence; the consensus-with-clan-deliberation procedures, requiring that proposals proceed through specific deliberative sequences within and across clans before reaching Confederacy decisions; the Clan Mother veto, structurally locating women in the selection and removal of male chiefs; the explicit provision for the removal of chiefs who fail to act consistently with the Great Law's principles; the dedication of decision procedures to "peace, power, and righteousness" (skennen, kasastensera, kariwiio) as substantive standards rather than procedural ones.

Engaging the Great Law on its own terms requires citing Haudenosaunee scholars and traditional teachers as primary sources. Oren Lyons (Onondaga, Faithkeeper of the Turtle Clan) has articulated the Great Law's principles across decades of teaching, writing, and international advocacy, including his work in the United Nations on Indigenous rights. His co-authored *Exiled in the Land of the Free: Democracy, Indian Nations, and the U.S. Constitution* (1992) with John Mohawk and others establishes the historical case for the Great Law's influence on the American constitutional founders and articulates the principles that distinguish the Great Law from the constitutional architecture the founders eventually adopted. John Mohawk (Seneca) elaborated these arguments across his teaching and writing including *Utopian Legacies: A History of Conquest and Oppression in the Western World* (1999) and his subsequent work on Iroquois corn agriculture and ecological governance. Doug George-Kanentiio's *Iroquois on Fire: A Voice from the Mohawk Nation* (2006) and his subsequent journalism and writing constitute another body of primary-source material on contemporary Haudenosaunee governance. The Onondaga Nation's land-rights litigation, particularly the *Onondaga Nation v. State of New York* litigation that proceeded through the federal courts in the 2000s and 2010s before being dismissed on laches grounds, represents a body of legal practice in which the Great Law's principles have been articulated within American litigation.

The seven-generation decision rule deserves particular attention as institutional vocabulary the multi-decade project requires. The rule operates not as an exhortation but as a procedural standard: decisions are required to consider their effects on those whose faces are not yet visible, those who will live seven generations hence. The procedural implication is that decision processes must include explicit assessment of long-term effects, that the long-term effects must be substantively rather than discountedly weighted, and that decisions whose long-term effects are inconsistent with the principles the Great Law specifies must be rejected. The contrast with the discount-rate procedures that dominate Western cost-benefit analysis is structural rather than incremental. Standard cost-benefit analysis applies discount rates that systematically reduce the weight of future effects: a future cost or benefit fifty years out is weighted at perhaps twenty percent of an equivalent present cost or benefit, and a future effect a hundred and fifty years out — within the seven-generation horizon — is weighted at less than five percent. The seven-generation rule does not apply this discounting. It treats the welfare of those seven generations hence as substantively comparable to present welfare, with implications for how natural-resource decisions, infrastructure investments, and constitutional commitments are evaluated. The rule has been adapted in various forms in non-Indigenous governance contexts — Sweden's seven-generation principle in environmental policy, the Welsh Well-being of Future Generations Act 2015, various municipal long-term planning frameworks — and the project's framework values these adaptations while specifying that the rule's authority and substantive content derive from the Haudenosaunee tradition rather than from these adaptations.

The Andean buen vivir or sumak kawsay tradition, articulated in Quechua as sumak kawsay and in Aymara as suma qamaña, constitutes the second body of Indigenous governance vocabulary the project's framework treats as load-bearing. The tradition's principles include living well rather than living better, the centrality of reciprocity (ayni) in social and ecological relationships, the recognition of Pacha Mama as a subject of relationship rather than as an object of use, the integration of human and ecological flourishing rather than their separation, and the rejection of the development model that frames progress as accumulation. Eduardo Gudynas's extensive work on buen vivir, including his essays in *Development* (2011) and his book *Derechos de la Naturaleza: Ética biocéntrica y políticas ambientales* (2014), specifies what the tradition contributes to contemporary institutional design: a normative framework for evaluating development policies, an institutional architecture for plurinational state structures (as elaborated in the Bolivian and Ecuadorian constitutions), a vocabulary for the recognition of nature as a relationship-partner rather than an object. Catherine Walsh's work on interculturality and decolonial thought, including her co-authored work with Walter Mignolo *On Decoloniality: Concepts, Analytics, Praxis* (2018), specifies the broader theoretical framework within which buen vivir operates as an alternative to the Western development paradigm. Marisol de la Cadena's *Earth Beings: Ecologies of Practice across Andean Worlds* (2015), based on extended ethnographic work with Mariano and Nazario Turpo of the Pacchanta region of Peru, articulates the ontological dimensions of Andean governance traditions: the recognition of mountains as Earth-beings (tirakuna) with whom political and economic relationships are conducted, the integration of human politics with ecological relationships in ways that the inherited Western political vocabulary cannot accommodate.

The Ubuntu philosophical tradition, articulated principally in Southern African political philosophy and operationalized in South African post-apartheid jurisprudence, constitutes the third body of Indigenous governance vocabulary the project's framework engages. The tradition's central principle, captured in the Zulu and Xhosa expression "umuntu ngumuntu ngabantu" (a person is a person through other persons), articulates a relational ontology in which personhood is constituted through relationship rather than through prior individual existence. Mogobe Ramose's *African Philosophy through Ubuntu* (1999) constitutes the philosophical articulation of the tradition's principles, with particular attention to the implications for governance institutions. The tradition has been operationalized in South African jurisprudence through the Constitutional Court's invocation of Ubuntu in *S v Makwanyane* (1995), the case that abolished the death penalty in post-apartheid South Africa, and in subsequent decisions including *Port Elizabeth Municipality v. Various Occupiers* (2004) and *Dikoko v. Mokhatla* (2006). Drucilla Cornell's work on Ubuntu and dignity, including *uBuntu and the Law: African Ideals and Postapartheid Jurisprudence* (2011) co-edited with Kenneth Muvangua, articulates how Ubuntu operates as a substantive jurisprudential principle rather than as a rhetorical ornament. The tradition's contribution to the project's framework is the relational ontology it articulates: an institutional vocabulary in which legal and political subjects are not pre-political individuals whose rights are negotiated but persons constituted through relationship whose institutional architecture must reflect that constitution.

Albert Marshall's articulation of Two-Eyed Seeing (Etuaptmumk in Mi'kmaw) constitutes the fourth body of Indigenous institutional vocabulary the project's framework engages. The principle, developed by Marshall (Mi'kmaw Elder, member of Eskasoni First Nation) in collaboration with Cheryl Bartlett at Cape Breton University and articulated in their joint publications including the 2012 *Journal of Environmental Studies and Sciences* article "Two-Eyed Seeing and Other Lessons Learned within a Co-Learning Journey of Bringing Together Indigenous and Mainstream Knowledges and Ways of Knowing," specifies a methodological approach to integrating Indigenous and Western knowledge systems. The principle holds that one should learn to see from one eye with the strengths of Indigenous knowledge and ways of knowing, and from the other eye with the strengths of mainstream knowledge and ways of knowing, and to use both eyes together for the benefit of all. The methodological substance of Two-Eyed Seeing has been operationalized in Canadian environmental governance, particularly through the Bras d'Or Lake biosphere reserve management and through various university research partnerships including the integrative science program at Cape Breton University. The contribution to the project's framework is methodological: a specific approach to integrating Indigenous and Western knowledge systems that does not collapse one into the other or treat one as supplementary to the other, and that has produced operational governance arrangements that the project can engage as demonstration cases.

Robin Wall Kimmerer's *Braiding Sweetgrass: Indigenous Wisdom, Scientific Knowledge, and the Teachings of Plants* (2013) and her subsequent work, including *Gathering Moss: A Natural and Cultural History of Mosses* (2003) and her recent essays on the grammar of animacy, articulate an ontological alternative to the subject-object distinction that the inherited American legal system has institutionalized. Kimmerer's work, grounded in her standing as a member of the Citizen Potawatomi Nation and as a botanist trained in the Western scientific tradition, specifies what the ontological alternative actually consists of: a grammar of animacy in which other-than-human beings are addressed and referred to in ways that the English subject-object grammar systematically prevents; a relational ethic in which the harvesting of plants is conducted through procedures of asking, gratitude, and reciprocity; an institutional vocabulary in which the "Honorable Harvest" specifies substantive standards for the human-plant relationship. The contribution to the project's framework is ontological: Kimmerer specifies what an institutional architecture grounded in relational rather than transactional ontology would actually require, and her work supplies vocabulary that the multi-decade project can deploy in domestic political mobilization where the ontological frame is itself the strategic issue.

Vine Deloria Jr.'s body of work, including *Custer Died for Your Sins: An Indian Manifesto* (1969), *God Is Red: A Native View of Religion* (1973), and *Red Earth, White Lies: Native Americans and the Myth of Scientific Fact* (1995), constitutes a foundational scholarly engagement with the structural relationship between American constitutional law and Indigenous nations that the project's framework must engage. Deloria's analysis specifies how the American constitutional architecture has systematically displaced Indigenous governance authority, how the legal-trust relationship has functioned as a mechanism of dispossession rather than as protection, and how the recognition framework can substitute for substantive return of land and authority. Nick Estes's *Our History Is the Future: Standing Rock Versus the Dakota Access Pipeline, and the Long Tradition of Indigenous Resistance* (2019) articulates how the contemporary Indigenous resistance against extractive infrastructure operates as a continuation of the long tradition of Indigenous nation-building and constitutes both political action and constitutional argument. Leanne Betasamosake Simpson's *As We Have Always Done: Indigenous Freedom through Radical Resistance* (2017) articulates the framework of resurgence as the grounded normativity from which Indigenous governance can be reconstituted. Daniel Wildcat's *Red Alert! Saving the Planet with Indigenous Knowledge* (2009) specifies how Indigenous knowledge systems can contribute substantively to the climate-response architecture the contemporary period requires. Vandana Shiva's body of work on biodiversity, agriculture, and ecological governance, including *Staying Alive: Women, Ecology and Development* (1988) and *Earth Democracy: Justice, Sustainability, and Peace* (2005), articulates how Indigenous and traditional ecological knowledge intersects with contemporary ecological governance in ways that the project's framework must engage. Winona LaDuke's *All Our Relations: Native Struggles for Land and Life* (1999) and her subsequent work through Honor the Earth and the broader Indigenous environmental movement specifies how the institutional vocabulary the project requires has been articulated through specific cases of Indigenous environmental defense.

### The strategic implications: integration with the multi-decade project

The strategic implication of Part Two for the multi-decade project is that international and Indigenous-governance engagement is not optional supplementation to a domestically focused project but a necessary component of the institutional vocabulary-construction the contemporary phase requires. The international layer generates normative resources, demonstration cases, and political pressures that influence American politics through specific causal pathways: comparative-law citation in domestic constitutional argument, treaty and customary international law obligations that constrain American action in some domains, soft-power consequences for American international standing, and the construction of evidentiary records that American actors cannot produce within the American legal system alone. The Indigenous-governance layer supplies institutional vocabulary that the inherited American constitutional structure cannot generate from within itself: the seven-generation decision rule, the legal personhood of natural systems, the Clan Mother structure, the ayni reciprocity principle, Ubuntu's relational ontology, Two-Eyed Seeing's integrative methodology, the grammar of animacy.

The integration with state-level innovation is operationally specific. The state-level innovations engaged in Part One can borrow institutional vocabulary from the international and Indigenous-governance frameworks: the New York Climate Leadership and Community Protection Act's disadvantaged-communities methodology echoes climate-justice principles developed in international negotiations; the Klamath restoration's tribal-state-federal co-governance arrangement operates within UNDRIP-consistent principles even where it does not formally cite the Declaration; the Michigan Independent Citizens Redistricting Commission's sortition design echoes deliberative-democracy principles developed in international experiments. The integration is most generative when it is explicit rather than implicit, with state innovations citing the international and Indigenous frameworks they draw on, building demonstration cases that international actors can reference, and constructing co-governance arrangements with tribal nations that operationalize UNDRIP norms within the American constitutional framework.

The strategic implication for the eventual constitutional reorganization phase is that the institutional vocabulary the reorganization will require is being developed in international and Indigenous venues during the contemporary infrastructure-building phase. The Whanganui River legal-personhood framework, the Ecuadorian Rights of Nature constitutional architecture, the Haudenosaunee seven-generation decision rule, the Andean buen vivir framework, the Ubuntu jurisprudence, the FPIC procedural framework — these are not historical curiosities or comparative-law tourism. They are the institutional resources that any eventual American constitutional reorganization aimed at ecological survival, polycentric governance, and decision quality will draw on, and the multi-decade infrastructure-building phase requires that these resources be engaged, developed, and translated during the contemporary period rather than left for the reorganization phase to discover. The strategic discipline is to treat the international and Indigenous engagement as construction work on the institutional vocabulary that domestic transformation will eventually deploy, not as foreign-policy adjacency or as comparative-law tourism, and to integrate the engagement with the state-level innovation and the cognitive-aware mobilization that the next part develops.

## PART THREE — COGNITIVE-AWARE POLITICAL MOBILIZATION: CLOSING THE POLITICAL-FORCE GAP

Parts One and Two specified the institutional venues and vocabularies the multi-decade project requires. The state-level layer builds demonstration cases, regulatory capacity, and political constituencies under contemporary conditions; the international and Indigenous-governance layer supplies institutional vocabulary that the inherited American constitutional architecture cannot generate from within itself. Neither layer produces political force without translation. The translation is the work of the third layer this Part addresses: the cognitive and informational conditions through which institutional resources at lower scales generate the political pressure that scales them up. Document Nine identified the political-force gap — the gap between empirical crisis severity and the political pressure actually mobilized in response — as the binding constraint on transformative action under contemporary conditions. The gap is not produced by ignorance, by indifference, or by the moral failures the moralizing literature has often invoked. It is produced by the systematic interaction between the empirical features of the contemporary crises and the cognitive architecture human reasoners actually have, with the interaction mediated by the political and informational institutions through which crises are processed into political pressure or fail to be. Closing the gap is not a problem of better facts or louder messaging. It is a problem of designing communication, organizing, and deliberative institutions consistent with what the cognitive science actually specifies about how the relevant decisions get made.

### What the cognitive science specifies about why standard messaging underperforms

The cognitive-science literature engaged across Documents Four and Seven provides specific resources for diagnosing why environmental, democratic-reform, and economic-justice messaging has systematically underperformed relative to its empirical basis. The diagnosis is not unitary; multiple cognitive features interact to produce the underperformance, and effective interventions must address the specific features rather than generic "better communication."

Daniel Kahneman's analysis in *Thinking, Fast and Slow* (2011), synthesizing decades of work with Amos Tversky and others, specifies that human reasoning operates through two systems with substantially different properties. System 1 operates fast, automatic, intuitive, low-effort; System 2 operates slow, controlled, analytical, high-effort. The interaction matters for the political-force question because System 1 substantially shapes political reasoning even when System 2 is engaged: judgments of evidence quality, of source credibility, of risk magnitude, and of action priorities are heavily influenced by System 1 features that operate before System 2 deliberation. The availability heuristic — the substitution of "how easily can I bring an instance to mind" for "how frequent is this" — systematically distorts risk perception in ways relevant to climate, public-health, and democratic-erosion communication. The substitution heuristic — the substitution of an easier question for the harder question actually asked — produces patterns in which voters answer "do I trust this candidate" instead of "what are this candidate's policy positions," "do I like this politician's tribe" instead of "is this politician's claim supported by evidence." The anchoring effect and various framing effects shape judgments in directions that the framers of communication often have not analyzed. The cognitive features Kahneman specifies are not bugs to be removed; they are features of human cognition that have been adaptive across the long arc of human evolutionary history and that operate by default in political reasoning. Effective communication does not pretend to bypass them but works with them.

Yaacov Trope and Nira Liberman's construal-level theory, developed in their joint work including the 2010 *Psychological Review* article "Construal-Level Theory of Psychological Distance" and subsequent work, specifies a cognitive feature directly relevant to climate, democratic-decline, and other long-term-stakes communication. The theory holds that psychological distance — temporal, spatial, social, and hypothetical — systematically affects how people construe events. Events that are psychologically distant are construed in abstract, decontextualized, high-level terms; events that are psychologically near are construed in concrete, contextualized, low-level terms. The systematic implication is that the empirical features of climate communication that have been most prominent — temporally distant effects (impacts decades hence), spatially distant effects (impacts in other regions), socially distant effects (impacts on out-groups), hypothetically distant effects (probabilistic projections rather than confirmed observations) — produce abstract construal that systematically reduces the urgency and concreteness with which the issue is processed. The construal-level finding has been operationalized in climate-communication research showing that messaging emphasizing local, concrete, present-tense effects produces stronger engagement than messaging emphasizing global, abstract, future effects, even when the empirical content is held constant.

Dan Kahan and the Cultural Cognition Project at Yale Law School, developing across more than a decade of empirical work including Kahan's foundational *Yale Law Journal* article "Cultural Cognition and Public Policy" (2006) and the subsequent empirical literature, specify a cognitive feature whose implications the political-communication community has been slower to absorb than its empirical robustness warrants. Cultural cognition operates through identity-protective cognition: people process information about empirically contested questions in ways that protect the identity of the cultural-political group with which they identify. The protection is not a bug; it is adaptive to the social landscape in which most people make most decisions, where alignment with the group is more consequential for daily life than getting empirical questions right. Kahan's empirical work has demonstrated cultural-cognition effects across a wide range of empirical questions — climate change, gun policy, vaccine safety, fracking risk, nuclear waste — with the magnitude of the effect substantial enough to invert the relationship between scientific literacy and accurate empirical judgment for individuals strongly identified with cultures whose received view conflicts with the scientific consensus. The implication is that informational-deficit communication strategies — strategies premised on the assumption that providing more or better information will change the empirical judgments of those whose current judgments are inconsistent with the evidence — systematically fail when the empirical questions are entangled with cultural-identity questions. The implication is not that empirical communication is impossible; it is that effective empirical communication requires institutional and discursive structures that decouple empirical questions from cultural-identity threats.

Hugo Mercier and Dan Sperber's *The Enigma of Reason: A New Theory of Human Understanding* (2017) develops the argumentative theory of reason that significantly revises the standard account of how human reasoning actually operates. The theory holds that reason is best understood as evolved primarily for argumentation — for producing reasons that justify positions to others and for evaluating reasons others give — rather than for unbiased individual truth-seeking. The empirical pattern of human reasoning, including the systematic operation of confirmation bias, the asymmetry between argument production and argument evaluation, and the substantial improvement in reasoning that occurs in deliberative discussion, is best explained by this argumentative theory. The implication for political communication is substantial: communication strategies premised on individual reasoners independently weighing evidence misunderstand the cognitive architecture, while strategies that engage reasoning in social and deliberative contexts work with the architecture. The further implication, developed in Mercier's *Not Born Yesterday: The Science of Who We Trust and What We Believe* (2020), is that humans are not as gullible as the disinformation-panic literature often suggests; people are reasonably careful about which sources they trust, but their care operates through specific heuristics that are vulnerable to specific manipulations.

George Lakoff's cognitive-frames work, articulated in *Don't Think of an Elephant!* (2004) and *The All New Don't Think of an Elephant* (2014), specifies how the framing of political issues systematically shapes their cognitive processing. Lakoff's analysis, drawing on the cognitive-linguistics tradition, holds that political issues are processed through cognitive frames — coherent structured representations of situations and their participants — and that the choice of frame substantially determines what counts as a relevant fact, who counts as a relevant actor, and what counts as an appropriate response. Lakoff's central political claim — that conservative communication has more systematically operated through frame-construction while progressive communication has more often operated through fact-listing — has been widely engaged in progressive communication work and has produced both substantive uptake (the Race-Class Narrative project, the More in Common research, deep canvassing) and substantial controversy (about the empirical strength of his specific claims, about whether his strategic prescriptions actually work). The strategic implication that the project's framework retains from Lakoff's work, separate from the contested specific claims, is that effective political communication operates at the level of frame-construction rather than at the level of fact-presentation, and that the multi-decade project's communication infrastructure must include explicit attention to the frames within which its substantive content is processed.

Joshua Kalla and David Broockman's empirical work on deep canvassing and persuasion, developed across multiple field experiments and articulated in their 2020 *American Political Science Review* article "Reducing Exclusionary Attitudes through Interpersonal Conversation: Evidence from Three Field Experiments" and subsequent work, has produced what is currently the most empirically robust finding about durable attitude change in political contexts. The deep-canvassing methodology, developed in practice by the Leadership LAB at the Los Angeles LGBT Center and tested empirically through multiple randomized controlled trials, produces durable attitude change on contested social issues including transgender rights, immigration, and racial justice through extended one-on-one conversations that include perspective-taking exercises, narrative exchange, and explicit engagement with the canvassed person's own experience. The finding is significant in two respects. First, the durability of the effect — measured at intervals up to several months after the conversation — substantially exceeds what most political-persuasion interventions produce, where effects typically decay rapidly. Second, the methodology's mechanism appears to operate through cognitive features the standard political-communication models do not target: the activation of the canvassed person's own related experiences, the construction of common ground through narrative exchange, the engagement of perspective-taking processes that the cognitive-empathy literature identifies as substantively powerful. The implication for the multi-decade project is that the methodology of cognitive-aware mobilization is not a matter of crafting better messages but of designing interactional architectures within which durable attitude change is actually possible.

The diagnostic synthesis of Part Three's first section is that the political-force gap is produced by the interaction of multiple cognitive features — the System 1 / System 2 architecture, construal-level effects, cultural cognition, the argumentative structure of reasoning, frame effects — with the empirical features of the contemporary crises that systematically activate the cognitive features unfavorably. The standard environmental, democratic-reform, and economic-justice messaging has underperformed because it has frequently presented temporally distant, spatially distant, statistically framed, identity-threatening content through individual-reasoner channels, in ways that activate availability and construal-level effects unfavorably, threaten cultural-identity processing, and rely on individual evidence-weighing rather than deliberative-argumentation processes. Closing the gap requires methodologies that work with rather than against the cognitive architecture.

### Methodologies that cognitive-aware practitioners have developed

The practitioner literature on cognitive-aware mobilization has developed substantially across the contemporary period, with several methodologies producing measurable durable attitude change and several others producing only short-term mobilization. The project's framework distinguishes these categories.

Marshall Ganz's public narrative methodology, developed at the Harvard Kennedy School and articulated in his teaching, training materials, and writing including *Why David Sometimes Wins: Leadership, Organization, and Strategy in the California Farm Worker Movement* (2009), specifies a structured approach to political narrative that integrates personal, collective, and immediate dimensions. The story of self addresses the speaker's own formation and motivations, articulating the values and experiences that connect the speaker to the issue. The story of us addresses the collective identity of the audience and movement, articulating shared values and shared experience. The story of now addresses the urgent moment of choice, articulating what the audience and movement are called to do in the present. The methodology was deployed with substantial impact in the Obama 2008 campaign's Camp Obama trainings, in the subsequent organizing infrastructure of the New Organizing Institute and successor organizations, and in the international training programs that Ganz and his colleagues have conducted across multiple countries and movements. The methodology's contribution to the project's framework is that it operationalizes several of the cognitive-science findings simultaneously: the personal narrative addresses construal-level distance by making issues concrete and present-tense, the collective narrative engages cultural-identity processing in service of mobilization rather than against it, and the call to action addresses the action-mobilization rather than only the cognition-modification component of communication. The empirical record on the methodology's effects, as distinct from the broader effects of campaigns within which it has been deployed, is more limited than its widespread adoption would suggest, but the methodological framework supplies a structured approach to communication that the multi-decade project can deploy and refine.

Hahrie Han's *How Organizations Develop Activists: Civic Associations and Leadership in the 21st Century* (2014) and her subsequent work including *Prisms of the People: Power and Organizing in Twenty-First-Century America* (2021, with Elizabeth McKenna and Michelle Oyakawa), specify a distinction central to the project's framework: the distinction between organizing and mobilizing. Mobilizing operates by aggregating individuals to act on positions they already hold, through channels (mass communication, online petition, digital ad-targeting) that minimize the friction of participation. Organizing operates by building relationships among people through which positions, identities, and political capacities are developed over time, through channels (face-to-face conversation, membership organizations, collective action) that require substantial friction to participate. Han's empirical work, comparing high-performing and lower-performing chapters of the same national organizations, demonstrates that the organizations that produce durable political power and durable issue-shift are those that engage in genuine organizing rather than relying on mobilizing infrastructure. The contemporary funder ecosystem, the digital-organizing technology stack, and the consultant infrastructure have all systematically biased toward mobilizing — which is more measurable, more scalable, and more legible to funders — at the cost of the organizing that Han's empirical work identifies as the mechanism for durable power-building. The strategic implication for the project's framework is substantial: the multi-decade infrastructure-building requires organizing rather than only mobilizing, and the institutional designs that channel resources into organizing rather than into mobilizing produce the long-cycle capacity-building the panarchy framework identifies as the work of the contemporary phase.

Jane McAlevey's *No Shortcuts: Organizing for Power in the New Gilded Age* (2016) constitutes the labor-side complement to Han's work. McAlevey's analysis, drawing on her experience as a union organizer and on her empirical work on contemporary labor organizing, specifies what she calls "deep organizing" — the methodology of organic-leader identification, structure-building through workplace mapping, and supermajority strike capacity-building — as distinct from the "advocacy" and "mobilizing" approaches that have dominated American labor since the mid-twentieth-century retrenchment. Deep organizing's central claim, supported by McAlevey's case studies of successful organizing campaigns at the Catholic Healthcare West-Smithtown, Smithfield Foods Tar Heel, and other sites, is that durable union power requires the patient, structured, methodologically rigorous work of building genuine majorities through one-on-one conversations conducted by organic workplace leaders. The methodology operationalizes the cognitive-science findings in similar ways to Ganz's public narrative work: face-to-face conversation engages the deliberative-argumentation processes that the Mercier-Sperber literature identifies as essential to durable reasoning shifts; organic-leader identification leverages cultural-cognition processing in service of mobilization; structure-building through workplace mapping addresses the collective-action problems that the standard mobilizing approaches systematically underperform on. The contemporary labor revival — the Amazon Labor Union victory at the JFK8 facility in 2022, the Starbucks Workers United organizing across hundreds of stores, the United Auto Workers Stand Up Strike of 2023 that produced substantial wage gains and organizing precedents, the Writers Guild of America and SAG-AFTRA strikes of 2023 — has substantially deployed deep-organizing methodologies and provides a contemporary empirical record that the project's framework can engage.

The More in Common research program, established in 2017 with the *Hidden Tribes* report and continuing through subsequent reports including *The Perception Gap* (2019) and *Two Stories of Distrust in America* (2021), has developed a body of empirical research on political polarization and on the methodologies that work across cultural-cognition divides. The Hidden Tribes typology — organizing the American electorate into seven segments (Progressive Activists, Traditional Liberals, Passive Liberals, Politically Disengaged, Moderates, Traditional Conservatives, Devoted Conservatives) — specifies that the active-political segments at the ideological extremes constitute approximately a third of the population, while the "exhausted majority" comprising the remaining segments is substantially less ideologically engaged and substantially more open to cross-cutting communication than the active-segment messaging tends to assume. The Perception Gap research documents the systematic overestimation of out-group extremism by both progressive and conservative active segments, with the perception gap particularly large for the most engaged consumers of partisan media. The methodological implications, articulated in More in Common's communication-strategy work and in their pilot programs in the United Kingdom, Germany, France, and the United States, are specific: communication strategies that engage shared values rather than amplifying out-group threats produce more durable cross-cutting effects than the threat-amplification strategies that have dominated digital-organizing approaches. The strategic implication for the project's framework is that the multi-decade project's communication infrastructure should incorporate the More in Common typology and methodology as resources for thinking about which segments of the electorate the project's communication is designed to reach and how.

Anat Shenker-Osorio's race-class narrative work, developed with the Race-Class Narrative Project beginning in 2017 and elaborated in her communications consulting and her writing including *Don't Buy It: The Trouble with Talking Nonsense About the Economy* (2012), specifies an approach to integrated economic and racial messaging that the empirical record suggests outperforms either component messaging. The race-class narrative methodology operates by explicitly naming both the economic interests and the racial divisions through which those interests are pursued, connecting the audience's lived economic experience to the broader political-economic structure, and articulating a multiracial coalition narrative that integrates rather than separates economic and racial concerns. The empirical testing of the methodology, including the experimental work conducted by Demos and the Race-Class Academy training program, has produced evidence that the integrated narrative outperforms both pure economic messaging (which leaves racial-resentment processing intact and unaddressed) and pure racial-justice messaging (which can activate cultural-cognition resistance among voters whose racial identification is in tension with the messaging) in producing durable cross-segment support for substantively progressive policies. The methodological contribution to the project's framework is that integration of substantive concerns is not a weakness to be avoided but a strategic resource that the cognitive-science framework specifies as more powerful than separation.

George Marshall's *Don't Even Think About It: Why Our Brains Are Wired to Ignore Climate Change* (2014) constitutes the most systematic application of cognitive-science findings to climate communication specifically. Marshall's analysis specifies how the empirical features of climate change — temporally distant, statistically framed, cumulative rather than punctual, lacking visible enemies, requiring sacrifice — interact with cognitive features the project has engaged across this Part to produce systematic underperformance of standard climate communication. The Yale Program on Climate Change Communication, directed by Anthony Leiserowitz, has developed the empirical research program that operationalizes these findings, including the Six Americas typology of climate-attitude segments and the regular tracking of climate-attitude shifts across segments. The Center for Climate Change Communication at George Mason University, directed by Edward Maibach, has developed the practitioner-facing research program that translates the empirical findings into communication-strategy resources. The strategic implication is that climate communication that has incorporated the cognitive-science findings systematically — emphasizing local and present-tense effects, naming specific actors and accountabilities, connecting climate to economic and social-justice concerns through integrated narratives, engaging cultural identities in service of climate action rather than against it — has produced measurable improvements in attitude tracking that more conventional climate communication has not. The improvement is not transformative, but it is real, and the multi-decade project's communication infrastructure should incorporate it as a working resource.

The Sunrise Movement, established in 2017 and operating through the contemporary period with substantial impact on the Green New Deal framing, the youth climate movement, and the politics of climate-policy ambition, exemplifies a contemporary mobilization methodology that has integrated cognitive-aware communication, organizing rather than only mobilizing, and aggressive political pressure. The Sunrise methodology — articulated in their training materials, their public communications, and Varshini Prakash and Guido Girgenti's edited collection *Winning the Green New Deal: Why We Must, How We Can* (2020) — combines the public-narrative methodology drawn from Ganz, the organizing methodology drawn from Han and the broader organizing tradition, and the disruptive-tactics methodology drawn from civil-rights and labor movements. The combination produced the November 2018 Pelosi-office occupation that launched the Green New Deal as a national policy framework, the subsequent legislative and electoral pressure that secured the Green New Deal's prominence in the 2020 Democratic primary, and the institutional pressure that contributed to the climate provisions of the Inflation Reduction Act of 2022. The empirical record of the Sunrise Movement's contribution, as distinct from the broader political dynamics of the period, is contested in the political-organizing literature, but the methodology constitutes a contemporary instance of cognitive-aware mobilization that the project's framework can engage as both inspiration and case study.

### Deliberative-democracy methodologies as institutional rather than communicative

The methodologies engaged in the previous section operate principally as communication and organizing methodologies — methodologies for shaping the discourse and movement infrastructure within which political pressure is produced. The deliberative-democracy methodologies engaged in this section operate as institutional methodologies — methodologies for designing the institutions within which political decisions are made, with the institutional design itself contributing to decision quality and political legitimacy. The distinction matters because the project's framework does not view communication and institutional design as separable strategic elements but as integrated components of the multi-decade infrastructure.

James Fishkin's Deliberative Polling methodology, developed across more than three decades of empirical research and articulated in *Democracy When the People Are Thinking: Revitalizing Our Politics through Public Deliberation* (2018) and earlier works including *The Voice of the People: Public Opinion and Democracy* (1997), specifies an institutional methodology with substantial empirical record. The methodology operates by recruiting a representative sample of the public — through random selection — and convening them for structured deliberation that includes balanced briefing materials, small-group discussion with trained moderators, plenary sessions with experts, and surveys before and after the deliberation that measure attitude change and learning. The methodology has been deployed in approximately one hundred Deliberative Polls across approximately thirty countries, with documented effects including substantial attitude change toward better-informed positions, substantial increases in policy-knowledge, increased political efficacy of participants, and the production of policy preferences that more accurately reflect what citizens would think under conditions of structured deliberation than do uninformed-public surveys. The methodology has been used in policy contexts including European Union constitutional questions, energy policy in Texas and California, foreign policy questions in multiple jurisdictions, and constitutional questions in Mongolia and elsewhere. The strategic significance for the project's framework is that the methodology supplies an institutional mechanism for producing decision-quality input that the standard polling and electoral mechanisms systematically fail to produce, and that its deployment at sub-national and sub-state scales has demonstrated feasibility within American institutional constraints.

Hélène Landemore's *Open Democracy: Reinventing Popular Rule for the Twenty-First Century* (2020) articulates a more architecturally ambitious institutional theory in which the deliberative-democracy methodologies are integrated into a comprehensive institutional architecture — what Landemore calls "open democracy" — that combines lottocratic representation (selection by lot rather than only by election), enhanced accessibility of decision-making, transparency, and inclusion. Landemore's empirical work draws on the Iceland constitutional process of 2010-2013, the Irish Citizens' Assemblies, the Convention Citoyenne pour le Climat in France, and other deliberative-democracy experiments, articulating both what these experiments demonstrate as institutionally feasible and what their limits suggest about the architectural questions that more comprehensive deliberative-democracy adoption would require to resolve. Landemore's analysis specifies that deliberative-democracy methodologies are not supplementary to electoral democracy but constitute alternative institutional architectures that could substantially or partially replace electoral mechanisms in specific decision domains. The strategic implication for the project's framework is that the institutional vocabulary the multi-decade project will eventually deploy includes lottocratic and deliberative architectures more substantial than the supplementary deliberative-polling methodology, and that the experimentation with such architectures during the contemporary period builds the institutional capacity that the eventual reorganization phase will require.

David Van Reybrouck's *Against Elections: The Case for Democracy* (2016, English translation 2016) articulates the historical and theoretical case for sortition as an alternative to electoral democracy. Van Reybrouck's analysis traces the history of sortition from Athenian democracy through medieval Italian city-states through the contemporary deliberative-democracy revival, articulating how the displacement of sortition by electoral democracy in the eighteenth and nineteenth centuries was a contingent rather than necessary development. The argument has substantial implications for the project's framework: the specific cognitive and structural pathologies of electoral democracy — including the systematic biases of campaign finance, the cognitive demands of voter information processing, the principal-agent problems of representative selection — are not necessary features of democracy but specific features of the electoral architecture, and alternative architectures including sortition can address some of the pathologies while introducing different ones. The argument is not that sortition should replace electoral democracy in the multi-decade project's vision but that sortition is a substantial institutional resource whose deployment in specific decision domains can address specific pathologies that electoral democracy systematically produces.

The practical record of Citizens' Assemblies provides empirical material for assessing what sortition-based deliberative architectures can and cannot do in contemporary conditions. The Irish Citizens' Assemblies on the Eighth Amendment (abortion), conducted in 2016-2017 and producing recommendations that the subsequent referendum substantially adopted, represent one of the most consequential deployments of sortition-based deliberation in contemporary politics. The Irish methodology — random selection of approximately one hundred citizens, structured deliberation with expert input across multiple weekend sessions, production of substantive recommendations on a politically charged constitutional question, and the subsequent legitimacy of the referendum result — demonstrated that sortition-based deliberation can produce substantive policy guidance on issues that the standard parliamentary and electoral mechanisms had failed to resolve. The Irish Citizens' Assembly on Climate, conducted in 2022, produced similarly substantive recommendations that have continued to shape Irish climate policy. The French Convention Citoyenne pour le Climat, conducted from October 2019 through June 2020, convened 150 randomly selected citizens to produce recommendations on climate policy that would reduce French emissions by forty percent below 1990 levels by 2030 in a manner consistent with social justice. The Convention produced 149 recommendations, of which the Macron government formally rejected three and committed to advancing the others; the subsequent legislative implementation was substantially weaker than the Convention's recommendations had specified, producing the political controversy and the broader deliberative-democracy literature critique that the institutional design did not include adequate guarantees that the recommendations would be implemented. The UK Climate Assembly, the Oregon Citizens' Initiative Review (operating since 2010), and approximately six hundred deliberative processes catalogued in the OECD's *Innovative Citizen Participation and New Democratic Institutions: Catching the Deliberative Wave* (2020) constitute a substantial body of institutional experimentation whose empirical lessons the project's framework can draw upon.

The critique of the deliberative-democracy program, articulated in the academic literature including the work of Cristina Lafont (*Democracy without Shortcuts*, 2020) and the broader political-theory engagement, addresses several substantial concerns. The scaling concern — whether deliberative methodologies can operate at the scale of national decision-making without losing the quality features that make them work at smaller scales — has been addressed in part by the Deliberative Polling methodology and by the multi-stage citizens' assembly designs but is not fully resolved. The capture concern — whether the institutional designers (the conveners, the experts, the briefing materials authors) can be prevented from systematically biasing the deliberation toward predetermined conclusions — has been addressed in part through transparency requirements, diverse expert panels, and procedural guarantees but remains a structural feature of the methodology that the practical record demonstrates can be managed but cannot be fully eliminated. The legitimacy concern — whether deliberative-assembly outputs carry the political legitimacy that electoral outputs are presumed to carry — has been addressed in part through the integration of assembly recommendations with referenda or with formal legislative consideration but is contested. The project's framework engages these critiques honestly, recognizing that the deliberative-democracy methodologies are an institutional frontier in active development whose institutional designs are not yet settled.

### Movement infrastructure-building methodologies

The cognitive-aware mobilization layer of the multi-decade project requires not only communication and deliberative-institutional methodologies but also the movement infrastructure-building methodologies through which political force is sustained over the multi-decade time horizon the panarchy framework specifies. The infrastructure question is the question Document Nine identified as binding: the contemporary period has accumulated substantial intellectual infrastructure but has not yet assembled the political coalition or institutional opportunity structure that transformative legislation comparable to the historical waves would require. Movement infrastructure-building is the work that produces those conditions.

Saul Alinsky's *Rules for Radicals: A Pragmatic Primer for Realistic Radicals* (1971) constitutes the foundational text of the modern American community-organizing tradition. Alinsky's methodology — articulated through his work with the Industrial Areas Foundation, the Back of the Yards Neighborhood Council in Chicago, and the broader network of community organizations he built and inspired — specifies a pragmatic approach to building local power through institutional confrontation, leadership development among ordinary people, and the construction of organizations whose membership is rooted in existing institutions (churches, unions, neighborhood associations) rather than constructed from individual recruitment. The Alinsky approach has been substantially revised across the subsequent half-century by practitioners including Ed Chambers, who succeeded Alinsky at the Industrial Areas Foundation and articulated the revisions in *Roots for Radicals: Organizing for Power, Action, and Justice* (2003), and by the broader organizing tradition that has developed across the IAF, PICO/Faith in Action, the Direct Action and Research Training (DART) Center, the Center for Popular Democracy, and the Working Families Party. The contemporary critique of Alinsky's methodology, developed by Marshall Ganz, Hahrie Han, Jane McAlevey, and others, addresses Alinsky's relative under-attention to issue-strategic ambition (his framework prioritized winnable local fights over transformative ambition), to ideological coherence (his framework was deliberately non-ideological in ways that produced organizations whose substantive direction was contestable), and to the construction of trans-local infrastructure (his framework produced substantial local capacity but limited trans-local capacity). The contemporary organizing methodology synthesizes the Alinsky approach with the public-narrative methodology, the deep-organizing methodology, and the explicit ideological framework-construction that the contemporary period requires.

The state-level and regional organizing infrastructures of the contemporary period constitute the institutional architecture within which movement-infrastructure work actually operates. The Industrial Areas Foundation, with its network of organizations across approximately fifty cities, continues the Alinsky tradition with substantial accumulated capacity. PICO/Faith in Action operates through faith-based community organizations with roughly comparable scale. The Center for Popular Democracy, established in 2012, operates as the umbrella organization for a network of state-level community-organizing affiliates. The Working Families Party, established in 1998 and operating in approximately twenty states, combines electoral and community-organizing work in a fusion-voting framework where state law permits. People's Action, established in 2016 from the merger of National People's Action and other organizations, operates a network of state-level affiliates with substantial accumulated capacity in housing, healthcare, and economic-justice organizing. The Movement for Black Lives, established in 2014 in the wake of the Ferguson uprising, operates as a coalition of approximately fifty Black-led organizations with substantial movement-infrastructure capacity. These institutional infrastructures constitute the existing organizational base from which the multi-decade project's organizing dimension would operate, and the project's framework values the assessment of which infrastructure components are highest-leverage for the long-cycle capacity-building.

The labor-organizing revival of the contemporary period, exemplified by the Amazon Labor Union, Starbucks Workers United, the United Auto Workers Stand Up Strike, the Writers Guild of America strike, and SAG-AFTRA strike, demonstrates that contemporary mobilization methodologies are working in specific institutional contexts. The Amazon Labor Union victory at JFK8 in April 2022 — establishing the first union representation election victory at an Amazon facility in the United States — was substantially the product of a small organizing committee using methodologies that combined deep organizing with creative communication and digital mobilization, on a substantially smaller resource base than the established labor unions had brought to prior unsuccessful Amazon campaigns. The Starbucks Workers United campaign, beginning at the Elmwood Avenue Starbucks in Buffalo in late 2021 and propagating to several hundred stores by 2024, demonstrated that contemporary methodologies could produce a propagating wave of organizing victories in an industry that established labor had largely treated as unreachable. The UAW Stand Up Strike of 2023, under the leadership of Shawn Fain, produced substantial wage gains, the recovery of cost-of-living adjustments lost in earlier negotiations, and organizing precedents that have continued to operate. The Writers Guild and SAG-AFTRA strikes of 2023 produced contractual gains around artificial intelligence, residuals, and minimum compensation that have established negotiating precedents the broader entertainment industry will continue to operate within. The strategic implication for the project's framework is that movement-infrastructure-building is not a hypothetical future activity but an active contemporary practice whose specific successful instances supply both methodological learning and organizational capacity that the multi-decade project can engage.

### Digital and information-environment intervention

The contemporary period requires methodologies for digital and information-environment intervention that the cognitive-aware mobilization framework specifies as integral to the multi-decade project. The information environment within which political deliberation operates has been substantially restructured by the platform architecture of the contemporary period in ways that the institutional designs of prior periods did not anticipate.

The literature on platform algorithm dynamics and democratic deliberation has developed substantially across the contemporary period. Renée DiResta's work, including her writing through the Stanford Internet Observatory and her recent book *Invisible Rulers: The People Who Turn Lies into Reality* (2024), specifies how the contemporary platform architecture has produced specific dynamics — algorithmic amplification of high-engagement content, the construction of niche audiences through targeted-advertising infrastructure, the propagation of coordinated inauthentic behavior, the substitution of platform-native epistemic communities for institutional epistemic authority — that affect democratic deliberation in ways the standard theoretical frameworks did not anticipate. Yochai Benkler's *Network Propaganda: Manipulation, Disinformation, and Radicalization in American Politics* (2018, with Robert Faris and Hal Roberts) provides empirical analysis of how the right-wing media ecosystem operates as a structurally distinct epistemic community whose dynamics are not symmetric with mainstream media dynamics, and specifies the implications for democratic deliberation in a structurally asymmetric information environment. Joan Donovan's work, including her co-authored *Meme Wars: The Untold Story of the Online Battles Upending Democracy in America* (2022), articulates the specific tactical methodologies through which the contemporary information environment is contested and the implications for democratic resilience.

The empirical work on what specifically combats the cognitive features that disinformation exploits has produced several methodologies with documented effectiveness. Sander van der Linden's prebunking research, conducted at the Cambridge Social Decision-Making Lab and articulated in *Foolproof: Why Misinformation Infects Our Minds and How to Build Immunity* (2023), specifies that pre-exposure to weakened forms of manipulation techniques (the inoculation methodology) produces measurable resistance to subsequent manipulation attempts. The methodology has been operationalized in interventions including the Bad News game, the Harmony Square game, and the prebunking videos developed in collaboration with Google's Jigsaw unit, with empirical evidence of effects that persist over weeks and that propagate across related but unencountered manipulation attempts. The Sam Wineburg-led work on lateral reading at the Stanford History Education Group, articulated in *Verified: How to Think Straight, Get Duped Less, and Make Better Decisions about What to Believe Online* (2022, by Wineburg, Joel Breakstone, and Sarah McGrew), specifies the specific reading methodologies that distinguish effective from ineffective approaches to evaluating online sources. The empirical work shows that the methodologies professional fact-checkers use — leaving the source under evaluation immediately to evaluate its reputation through external sources, reading laterally rather than attempting deeper engagement with the source itself — substantially outperform both the methodologies that students typically use and the methodologies that traditional information-literacy curricula have emphasized.

The platform-regulatory frameworks that have begun to operationalize specific responses to the platform-algorithm dynamics include the European Union's Digital Services Act, which entered into force in 2022 and became fully applicable in February 2024, establishing tiered obligations on platform operators including transparency requirements, user-control provisions, and risk-management requirements for the largest platforms. The Digital Services Act's approach — focused on procedural requirements and risk management rather than on content-based regulation — provides one institutional model for democratic-deliberation-protective platform regulation. The Brazilian Marco Civil da Internet, the Australian News Media Bargaining Code, the various American state-level legislation including California's age-appropriate design code (parts of which have been enjoined by federal courts on First Amendment grounds), and the federal-level proposals including the Platform Accountability and Transparency Act and the Algorithmic Accountability Act, constitute a body of regulatory experimentation whose institutional lessons the project's framework should engage. The American constitutional architecture, particularly the First Amendment and the Section 230 immunity framework of the Communications Decency Act, constrains the regulatory architecture available within American jurisdictional control more substantially than the European architecture, and the project's framework specifies that the constitutional architecture itself is part of what the multi-decade project would eventually need to address.

The honest assessment of methodologies for digital and information-environment intervention is that the empirical evidence is more limited than the urgency of the problem would warrant, that several interventions with conceptual appeal have not produced robust empirical evidence of effectiveness, and that the project's framework should distinguish methodologies with documented effects (prebunking, lateral reading, structured deliberation including the Citizens' Initiative Review's information products) from methodologies that are conceptually appealing but lack robust evidence. The further honest assessment is that the structural features of the contemporary platform architecture — the attention-economy incentives, the algorithmic amplification of high-engagement content, the structural asymmetries between organized disinformation production and individual reasoner defense — are not fully amenable to individual-level interventions, and that institutional and regulatory responses are necessary components of any adequate response.

### How cognitive-aware mobilization integrates with the prior layers

The integration of the cognitive-aware mobilization layer with the state-level innovation and international/Indigenous-governance layers is the central strategic claim of this document, and Part Three's synthesis must specify the integration explicitly. The integration operates along specific causal pathways that the prior parts have established and that this Part now connects.

The state-level innovations engaged in Part One produce demonstration cases that cognitive-aware mobilization can deploy as concrete narrative material. The construal-level theory engaged earlier in this Part specifies that abstract messaging about climate, democratic erosion, and economic injustice systematically activates psychological-distance processing that reduces engagement. Concrete demonstration cases — the Klamath dam removal, the New York Climate Leadership and Community Protection Act's environmental-justice provisions, the Michigan citizen redistricting commission's maps, the Bears Ears coalition's co-management arrangement — supply the concrete, specific, present-tense narrative material that cognitive-aware communication can deploy in ways that the abstract framing systematically cannot. The state-level innovations also supply the "us" component of public narrative in Marshall Ganz's framework: the demonstration that "this is what we are already doing in our state, our region, our community," which engages cultural-cognition processing in service of mobilization rather than against it. The strategic implication is that state-level innovation and cognitive-aware mobilization are not separate strategic activities but mutually constitutive: state-level innovation that lacks the communication infrastructure to translate it into political narrative produces less political force than its empirical merit warrants, while cognitive-aware mobilization that lacks concrete demonstration cases to translate produces less durable engagement than its methodological capacity could deliver.

The international and Indigenous-governance frameworks engaged in Part Two supply institutional vocabulary that cognitive-aware mobilization methodology can translate into culturally accessible frames. The seven-generation decision rule, the legal personhood of natural systems, the doughnut framework, the buen vivir principle, the Ubuntu relational ontology, the Two-Eyed Seeing methodology — these institutional vocabularies operate as frames in the Lakoff sense, organizing cognitive processing of substantive issues in ways that the inherited Western vocabularies systematically constrain. The strategic implication is that cognitive-aware mobilization methodology can deploy these vocabularies as resources for frame-construction, and that the engagement is most generative when the vocabularies are deployed in ways that operate culturally rather than as foreign-policy adjacency. The seven-generation rule operates as a frame for long-term thinking that has propagated substantially into non-Indigenous American discourse; the doughnut framework operates as a frame for ecological-economic thinking that has propagated similarly; the Ubuntu principle operates as a frame for relational thinking that has propagated less far but with substantial latent capacity. The cognitive-aware mobilization layer's strategic work is to translate these vocabularies into communication and organizing methodologies that engage American cultural-cognition processing in service of the project's framework.

The cognitive-aware mobilization layer in turn closes the political-force gap that allows state innovation to scale beyond demonstration-case status and that allows international norms to penetrate domestic political deliberation. State innovations remain demonstration cases until political pressure produces their adoption at higher scales. International norms remain comparative-law citations until domestic political mobilization translates them into domestic political force. The closing of these gaps is the work of cognitive-aware mobilization, operating through the communication, organizing, and deliberative-institutional methodologies this Part has specified. The integration is therefore not optional but constitutive: each layer requires the others to operate at the strategic scale the multi-decade project requires.

## CLOSING INTEGRATION — THE THREE LAYERS AS ONE STRATEGY

The integration thesis of this document is that the three operational layers — state-level legislative innovation, international and Indigenous-governance engagement, cognitive-aware political mobilization — constitute one panarchy-cycle infrastructure project rather than three separable initiatives, and that the discipline of holding them together as one strategy is itself the strategic question the contemporary moment poses. This closing section articulates the integration explicitly, addresses the failure modes the integration thesis must guard against, and links the document to the prior arc and to the constitutional-architecture work that should follow.

### The integration thesis articulated

The three layers operate at distinct spatial and temporal scales — sub-national, international and cross-civilizational, cognitive and individual — but the panarchy framework of Holling and the broader resilience tradition specifies that distinct scales are not separable systems but components of one cross-scale dynamic. The lower scales accumulate the structural capacity, demonstration cases, and personnel networks that higher-scale reorganization draws upon. The higher scales generate the normative authority, institutional vocabulary, and political pressure that constrain and shape what is possible at lower scales. The cognitive and individual scale operates as the medium through which the structural and normative resources at other scales translate into political force, and through which political force translates back into structural and normative change. The architecture is not a hierarchy with one scale dominant and the others derivative; it is a cross-scale system in which each scale operates simultaneously as cause and effect of the others.

The causal pathways by which each layer reinforces the others are operationally specific. State-level innovation produces demonstration cases that international frameworks can normative-launder into the American conversation: California's cap-and-trade program is not merely a state policy but a contribution to the institutional record that international actors can cite as evidence that climate policy is feasible at scale; New York's environmental-justice provisions are not merely state law but a translation of climate-justice norms developed in international negotiation into operational American legal architecture; the Klamath dam removal is not merely an ecological restoration project but a demonstration that the Free, Prior and Informed Consent norms developed in international Indigenous-rights jurisprudence can operate within American constitutional structure. The state-level innovations also supply the concrete narrative material that cognitive-aware mobilization methodology requires to operate effectively: the demonstration cases address the construal-level distance that the cognitive-science literature identifies as a principal barrier to political engagement, and the demonstration that "this is being done by us, in our state, our community" engages cultural-cognition processing in service of the project's framework.

International and Indigenous frameworks supply institutional vocabulary that state-level innovation can borrow and adapt within the constraints of the inherited federalism. The Te Awa Tupua co-governance structure provides architectural vocabulary that the Klamath restoration's tribal-state-federal coordination has implicitly drawn on and could explicitly reference. The Ecuadorian and Bolivian Rights of Nature frameworks provide architectural vocabulary that the White Earth Rights of Manoomin and Yurok Rights of the Klamath River have implicitly drawn on and could explicitly reference. The Free, Prior and Informed Consent framework provides procedural vocabulary that the Bears Ears Inter-Tribal Coalition's co-management arrangement has implicitly approximated and could explicitly operationalize. The Andean buen vivir framework, the Ubuntu jurisprudence, the seven-generation decision rule, and the doughnut framework supply normative vocabularies that state-level innovation can deploy to articulate substantive standards that the inherited American constitutional vocabulary cannot articulate. The international and Indigenous frameworks also supply the cognitive frames that mobilization methodology can translate into culturally accessible communication, with the strategic discipline being to deploy the frames in ways that operate culturally within American discourse rather than as foreign-policy adjacency or as anthropological exoticism.

Cognitive-aware mobilization closes the political-force gap that allows state innovation to scale beyond demonstration-case status and that allows international norms to penetrate domestic political deliberation. State innovations remain isolated demonstrations until political pressure produces their adoption at higher scales and in additional jurisdictions. International norms remain available but unused vocabulary until domestic political mobilization translates them into domestic political force. The closing of these gaps is the work of cognitive-aware mobilization through its communication, organizing, and deliberative-institutional methodologies. The closing operates on a multi-decade time horizon that matches the panarchy framework's specification of how long the lower-scale capacity-accumulation phase actually takes. The contemporary moment is in that phase; the political force the project's framework requires is being constructed during the contemporary period and will become available for higher-scale deployment in the reorganization phase the panarchy framework specifies as following the release phase the inherited constitutional system has now entered.

### The failure modes the integration must guard against

The integration thesis is contestable, and several specific failure modes can produce the appearance of integration without its substantive operation. Naming these failure modes is part of the strategic discipline the project requires.

The three layers can degenerate into three separable initiatives, each with its own funder networks, conferences, journals, and professionalized infrastructure, with no operational integration across layers. The state-level innovation conversation can proceed within its own institutional infrastructure — the National Conference of State Legislatures, the State Innovation Exchange, the various regional governors' associations, the state-level policy advocacy organizations — without engaging the international and Indigenous-governance vocabulary that this document has specified as integral to its strategic significance. The international engagement can proceed within its own institutional infrastructure — the United Nations process, the international NGO sector, the academic comparative-constitutional-law conversation — without engaging the state-level innovation that this document has specified as the principal venue for its operational deployment. The cognitive-aware mobilization conversation can proceed within its own institutional infrastructure — the organizing-training programs, the communications consultancies, the academic political-science research community — without engaging the state-level innovation that supplies its concrete narrative material or the international and Indigenous-governance vocabularies that supply its frame-construction resources. The pattern of degeneration produces the appearance of substantial activity in each layer with limited cross-scale propagation, and it is the dominant pattern across most of the institutional infrastructure that currently operates within each layer. The strategic discipline that prevents the degeneration is the institutional and personnel integration across layers: the funders who explicitly fund cross-layer integration, the convenings that bring together actors from each layer, the personnel pipelines that move actors across layers, the publications and intellectual infrastructure that sustain cross-layer conversation.

State-level innovation can degenerate into a substitute for federal action rather than infrastructure for it. The framing of state innovation as federalist alternative has specific failure modes: it accepts the inherited federalism as a permanent rather than cyclical constitutional structure, it underweights the federal-action requirements that climate, democratic-reform, and economic-justice transformation actually require, and it produces strategic complacency about the federal-political work that the multi-decade project does not relinquish. The strategic discipline is to frame state innovation explicitly as panarchy infrastructure for eventual federal transformation, to specify the federal-political work that state innovation is not a substitute for, and to maintain the institutional and political-coalition work at federal scale even when contemporary conditions make federal action infeasible.

International engagement can degenerate into foreign-policy adjacency rather than institutional-vocabulary construction. The pattern of degeneration produces engagement with international processes that resembles diplomatic activity — attending conferences, building international networks, developing comparative expertise — without producing the institutional translation into domestic American politics that the project's framework specifies as the strategic work. The strategic discipline is to frame international engagement explicitly as institutional-vocabulary construction whose operational deployment is in domestic American politics, to maintain the personnel pipelines that translate international institutional vocabulary into domestic political and legal practice, and to resist the cosmopolitan professional-class trajectory that produces accomplished international experts whose domestic-political deployment of their expertise is limited.

Cognitive-aware mobilization can degenerate into messaging consultancy rather than infrastructure-building. The pattern of degeneration produces a market for communications expertise — the proliferation of consulting firms, the conference circuit on political communication, the academic-practitioner conversation that operates substantially within professional rather than movement-political contexts — without producing the organizing infrastructure that Han, McAlevey, and the broader organizing-tradition literature specify as the actual mechanism of durable power-building. The strategic discipline is to frame cognitive-aware mobilization explicitly as movement-infrastructure-building whose communication and deliberative-institutional methodologies are means to the infrastructure-building rather than ends in themselves, to maintain the institutional resources and personnel pipelines for the organizing component that funder structures and consultant economies systematically underweight, and to resist the substitution of measurable mobilization indicators (digital reach, social-media engagement, fundraising metrics) for the harder-to-measure but substantively essential indicators of durable power-building.

The cumulative pattern across all three failure modes is the substitution of professionalized activity within each layer for the strategic-political work of cross-layer integration. The professionalization of each layer has been substantial across the contemporary period and has produced both substantive accumulated capacity and the structural conditions for the failure modes the integration thesis must guard against. The strategic discipline is the explicit construction of cross-layer integration mechanisms — funder strategies, convenings, personnel pipelines, publications, training programs — that channel the accumulated capacity within each layer toward cross-layer strategic deployment.

### Linking back and forward

The arc of this project as it now stands has a structure the closing integration should make explicit. The diagnostic phase — Documents One through Six — specified what is wrong with the inherited constitutional architecture. Document One assembled the constitutional text and amendments as the textual foundation. Document Two analyzed the Federalist Papers for what they reveal about the founders' assumptions and the internal tensions within the founders' theory. Document Three engaged the founders' post-ratification reconsiderations for what they reveal about how the designers themselves understood the limits of their work. Document Four developed the biological and anthropological critique of the founders' assumptions about human nature, social organization, and human-environment relations. Document Five surveyed the biomimicry, polycentric governance, and ecological-economics scholarship that offers a positive design vocabulary for governance. Document Six synthesized the diagnostic phase into a unified critique. The diagnostic phase established that the inherited constitutional architecture is not adequate to the institutional questions the contemporary period poses, and it specified the empirical and theoretical resources that any adequate alternative architecture must engage.

The extension phase — Documents Seven and Eight — deepened the diagnosis. Document Seven engaged the cognitive, macroeconomic, and incommensurability dimensions of governance failure, integrating the decision-science, sacred-values, and ecological-economics literatures into a unified analytical framework. Document Eight engaged the specific structural failures of the constitutional architecture as a climate-response system and proposed the categories of constitutional redesign that would address the failures. The extension phase moved the project from diagnostic to prescriptive in specific domains, while the broader prescriptive question of what positive constitutional architecture the project's framework would propose remained at a level of generality.

The strategic phase — Document Nine — located the contemporary moment in the panarchy cycle and prescribed multi-decade infrastructure-building under hostile federal conditions. Document Nine identified that the historical waves of transformative legislation were the visible legislative phases of much longer panarchy cycles of movement infrastructure-building, intellectual reorientation, litigation strategy, and institutional preparation; that the cycles cannot be reproduced through legislation alone; and that the contemporary period has accumulated substantial assets but has not yet assembled the political coalition or institutional opportunity structure that transformative legislation comparable to the historical waves would require. The strategic prescription was patient, multi-scale, multi-decade infrastructure-building, oriented toward the eventual reorganization phase that the panarchy framework specifies follows the release phase the inherited constitutional system has now entered.

This Document Ten has specified the three operational layers of that infrastructure-building. The state-level layer builds the demonstration cases, the regulatory and technical-agency capacity, and the political constituencies that any eventual federal transformation will require. The international and Indigenous-governance layer supplies the institutional vocabulary that the inherited American constitutional structure cannot generate from within itself. The cognitive-aware mobilization layer closes the political-force gap that no quantity of institutional innovation can substitute for. The integration of the three layers as one strategy is the strategic question the contemporary moment poses, and the discipline of holding them together as one project is itself the strategic work.

The remaining documents in the arc must address the constitutional architecture question directly. The diagnostic phase specified what is wrong with the inherited architecture. The extension phase deepened the diagnosis in specific domains. The strategic phase located the contemporary moment in the panarchy cycle. This document specified the operational layers of contemporary work. The prescriptive constitutional question — what specific amendments, what specific institutional architecture, what specific changes in the constitutional structure would the project's framework prescribe as the eventual goal of the work that this document specifies — is the natural next question and the appropriate subject of Document Eleven.

Document Eleven, accordingly, should be the constitutional design document. Drawing on the diagnostic work that established the inadequacy of the inherited architecture, on the extension work that specified the cognitive and ecological-economic dimensions of the inadequacy, on the strategic work that located the contemporary moment in the panarchy cycle, and on the operational-layers work this document has specified, Document Eleven should articulate what a constitutional architecture grounded in ecological science, decision quality, polycentric governance, and Indigenous governance traditions would actually look like as a positive proposal. The proposal need not be a single comprehensive blueprint; the project's framework specifies that constitutional architecture is a multi-decade construction project rather than a one-time design exercise. But Document Eleven should specify the architectural elements the project's framework supports — the structural features (including but not limited to the design questions raised in Document Eight: temporal architecture, spatial-scale architecture, decision-quality architecture, polycentric-governance architecture, Indigenous co-governance architecture), the procedural features, the substantive provisions, and the integration with the operational layers this document has specified — in sufficient specificity to constitute a positive proposal that the multi-decade project is working toward.

The constitutional design document should not be drafted in isolation from the political conditions that would receive it. The strategic work that this document has specified is part of the construction of the political conditions under which the constitutional design would become operationally available. The architecture and the political conditions are not separable strategic elements but mutually constitutive components of the multi-decade project. Document Eleven, accordingly, should specify the architecture in the context of the operational layers, and should articulate how the architectural elements connect to the operational work that this document has specified as the contemporary phase.

The arc of the project as it will then stand will have moved from constitutional text and founding-era theory through biological and ecological critique through positive design vocabulary through synthesis through extension through strategy through operational specification through positive constitutional proposal. The project will have specified what is wrong with the inherited architecture, what positive architecture the project's framework supports, what the contemporary work toward that architecture consists of, and how the architecture and the contemporary work integrate. The project will not have completed the multi-decade work it has specified — the work itself extends across decades that exceed any single intellectual project's scope. But the project will have articulated the framework, the diagnosis, the strategy, the operational layers, and the positive constitutional proposal in sufficient specificity to constitute a substantive contribution to the multi-decade infrastructure-building it has specified as the work the contemporary period requires.
