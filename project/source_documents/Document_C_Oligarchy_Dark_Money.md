# Oligarchy, Dark Money, and Constitutional Capture: The Doctrinal-Historical Case

## Opening

This document is the third installment in the sequence of new works extending the project under the Declaration-as-foundation reframe. Document A established the Declaration of Independence and the Virginia Declaration of Rights as the foundational normative texts of the colonial founding period and the 1787 Constitution as one implementation of those texts' commitments to be tested for fidelity. Document B developed the diagnostic spine — a five-axis analysis of structural subordination identifying caste, oligarchy, the media-information environment, structural racism, and embodiment as the axes along which the implementation has failed the founding-era commitments. The present document develops the case-study extension of the oligarchy axis. Where Document B established the structural-analytical apparatus through Jeffrey Winters's structural theory, the Gilens-Page-Bartels empirical literature, the Lessig-Teachout constitutional analysis, the Mayer-MacLean network and ideological documentation, and the racial-capitalism corrective developed by Cedric Robinson, W. E. B. Du Bois, Manning Marable, and Heather McGhee, the present document develops the doctrinal-historical case for what Document B's structural argument identifies. The doctrinal-historical case proceeds along six analytical lines: the founding-era anti-corruption tradition reconstructed; the constitutional architecture of oligarchy as developed through the post-1976 doctrinal sequence; the principal money-in-politics case studies that document the specific institutional and ideological infrastructure through which the oligarchic configuration has been built; the information-and-platform capture that the contemporary scholarship on media, surveillance capitalism, algorithmic governance, and artificial intelligence governance has developed; the Indigenous and underrepresented engagement that the multi-eyed seeing commitment requires as load-bearing rather than supplementary; and the architectural response that subsequent documents — D on constitutional architecture, E on currency architecture, F on e-democracy as structural public check — will develop in detail.

The methodological commitment of this document is the same multi-eyed seeing commitment that organizes the project arc and that Document I, *Multi-Eyed Seeing as Constitutional Method*, develops at length. The commitment extends Albert Marshall (Mi'kmaq) and Murdena Marshall's (Mi'kmaq) Two-Eyed Seeing (Etuaptmumk) — the bilateral practice articulated in Cheryl Bartlett, Murdena Marshall, and Albert Marshall's *Two-Eyed Seeing and Other Lessons Learned within a Co-Learning Journey of Bringing Together Indigenous and Mainstream Knowledges and Ways of Knowing* (2012) — into the multilateral architecture the project's equal-weight commitment requires, deployed with explicit care to honor the Marshall framework as a learning process rather than a perfected outcome. The case-study development of the oligarchy axis the present document advances is co-produced from the simultaneous engagement of the founding-era constitutional-historical scholarship, the post-1970s constitutional and administrative law scholarship that has documented the doctrinal reconstitution of the architecture in directions favorable to oligarchic interests, the investigative-journalistic and political-science scholarship on the dark-money networks and their ideological architects, the Indigenous political-economic tradition's analysis of resource extraction and treaty-rights violation as part of the oligarchic configuration, the Black-radical political-economy tradition's analysis of how the oligarchic configuration has been racialized in its construction and is racially structured in its operation, the dalit tradition's analysis of caste-capital concentration as a structural form of oligarchic configuration the comparative analysis must engage, the Latinx tradition's analysis of the labor and immigration architectures through which the oligarchic configuration has operated, and the disability-justice tradition's analysis of how the oligarchic configuration has constructed and maintained the structural exclusion of disabled persons from full participation in the political-economic architecture. The integrative analytical move the document advances is the demonstration that the contemporary American oligarchic configuration is not the failure of an otherwise functioning constitutional architecture; it is the predictable consequence of the architectural choices the founding-era settlement embedded, the doctrinal reconstitutions the post-1970s architecture has developed, and the institutional and ideological infrastructure the dark-money networks have constructed across the past five decades. The architectural response the project advances in subsequent documents is the response the case the present document develops supports.

The comparative engagement with cases from outside the United States — Karen Dawisha on Russia's transition to a kleptocratic political-economic configuration, Bálint Magyar on Hungary's articulation as a "post-communist mafia state," James Crabtree on India's "billionaire raj" and the broader contemporary Indian political economy — is engaged as comparative-structural diagnosis rather than as claim of equivalence. The structural features the comparative literature has identified are present in the United States case in some forms and not in others, and the diagnostic the present document develops engages the comparative literature for the analytical apparatus it supplies and for the warning it issues. The United States is not in the structural condition Magyar's analysis specifies for Hungary; the structural mechanisms that have produced the Hungarian configuration have analogs in the United States case that the diagnostic must specify carefully and without overclaiming. The honest version of the comparative engagement requires both the recognition that the United States case has not reached the configurations the comparative cases document and the recognition that several of the structural mechanisms the comparative cases document are present in the United States case in identifiable, particular forms.

## Part One — The Founding-Era Anti-Corruption Tradition Reconstructed

### The Doctrinal-Historical Question

The first analytical line of the present document is the reconstruction of the founding-era anti-corruption tradition that the contemporary doctrinal architecture has progressively narrowed. The reconstruction is doctrinal-historical rather than originalist in the methodological sense the project rejects: the question is not what the founders' specific intentions were on questions they did not anticipate, but what conception of corruption the founding generation worked with, what constitutional commitments that conception generated, and how the contemporary doctrinal architecture has departed from the conception. The reconstruction is not deployed in service of binding contemporary constitutional interpretation to founding-era expectations; it is deployed in service of demonstrating that even on the most conservative interpretive premises, the contemporary doctrinal architecture is constitutionally indefensible — and that the architectural reconstruction the project advances is consistent with rather than departing from the substantive commitments the founding-era tradition articulated.

Zephyr Teachout's *Corruption in America: From Benjamin Franklin's Snuff Box to Citizens United* (2014), engaged briefly in Document B's Part Two, is the foundational scholarly work for the present argument and is engaged here in greater detail. Teachout's analytical contribution is the demonstration that American constitutional law from the founding period to roughly the 1970s recognized a robust anti-corruption principle whose substantive content was substantially broader than the contemporary quid-pro-quo standard the post-*Buckley* doctrinal architecture has constructed. The broader conception of corruption was not idiosyncratic to a few founders or to a particular faction; it was a shared analytical-political vocabulary that organized constitutional discourse from the founding through the long anti-corruption tradition that ran from the founders through the Jacksonian period, the Reconstruction era, the Progressive Era, and the early-to-mid twentieth century. The doctrinal narrowing the post-1976 architecture has produced is therefore not the recovery of an original constitutional understanding; it is a recent doctrinal innovation, and the contemporary doctrinal architecture is the departure rather than the baseline.

The argument's force depends on the specific founding-era texts and on the specific doctrinal sequence the post-1976 architecture has produced. The texts include the *Federalist* papers themselves, particularly the discussions of corruption in *Federalist* 10, 49, 51, 57, 60, 61, and 75; the Anti-Federalist responses, particularly the discussions of corruption in Brutus, the Federal Farmer, and Centinel; the founding-era constitutional debates in the Philadelphia convention of 1787 and in the ratification conventions; the post-ratification correspondence of Madison, Hamilton, Jefferson, Washington, Adams, Franklin, and Mason; and the broader founding-era political-philosophical discourse that informed the constitutional debates. The doctrinal sequence includes *Buckley v. Valeo* (1976) and the cases that have developed its doctrinal architecture in subsequent decades, taken up in detail in Part Two.

### The Founders' Conception of Corruption

The founding-era conception of corruption operated with substantial breadth. It included what the contemporary architecture would recognize as corruption in the narrow sense — bribery, extortion, the personal enrichment of officials through the use of public office for private gain — but it was not limited to these. It also included what Teachout calls "structural" or "systemic" corruption: the conditions under which officials become structurally dependent on private interests to such an extent that their official judgment is oriented toward those interests rather than toward the public good, regardless of whether any specific quid-pro-quo exchange has occurred. The broader conception was articulated through a vocabulary that included terms like "dependence," "influence," "interest," "gratitude," "obligation," and "private interest" as specifications of the structural conditions under which corruption operates, and through a conceptual architecture that treated corruption as a constitutional rather than merely criminal-law concept.

James Madison's *Federalist* 10 articulates the structural-political analysis of factions that supplies one foundational element of the founding-era anti-corruption architecture. Madison's argument is that the propensity to faction is sown in human nature, that the latent causes of faction cannot be removed without removing liberty itself, and that the constitutional architecture must accordingly be designed to control the effects of faction. The argument's force depends on the recognition that faction operates not principally through individual corruption but through the systematic alignment of political institutions with the interests of particular groups whose interests diverge from the public good. The structural-political analysis Madison develops in *Federalist* 10 is therefore a structural-political analysis of corruption in the broad sense, and the constitutional architecture *Federalist* 10 defends — the extended republic, the indirect election of senators, the multiple veto points designed to obstruct the consolidation of factional power — is articulated as response to the structural-political problem rather than principally to the individual-corruption problem.

*Federalist* 51, also Madison's, develops the structural-political analysis further with the famous formulation that "ambition must be made to counteract ambition" and that the constitutional architecture must produce the conditions under which the multiple ambitions of multiple actors check one another in service of the public good. The formulation presupposes that ambition operates structurally — that the structural conditions under which officials act shape the trajectory of their ambitions — and that the constitutional architecture's task is to engineer the structural conditions rather than to rely on individual virtue alone. The analysis is consistent with the structural-political analysis of *Federalist* 10, and it supplies the structural-political vocabulary that the broader founding-era anti-corruption tradition deploys.

*Federalist* 57, also Madison's, develops the analysis of the relationship between representatives and constituents and the structural conditions under which representatives can be expected to legislate in service of the public good rather than in service of private interest. The argument turns on the structural mechanisms — frequent elections, the limited duration of legislative terms, the requirement that representatives live among their constituents and share the consequences of the legislation they enact — through which the structural conditions are sustained. The analysis is, again, structural-political rather than individual-character-based; the founding-era conception of representation is a conception of structural relationship, and the corruption the conception identifies is the failure of the structural relationship rather than the moral failure of individual representatives.

Alexander Hamilton's *Federalist* 75, on the treaty power, articulates the structural-political analysis of the conditions under which the executive can be expected to negotiate treaties faithfully on behalf of the United States rather than under the influence of foreign powers. Hamilton's argument is that the structural conditions of executive office — the limited duration of the presidential term, the requirement of Senate concurrence in treaty ratification, the constitutional prohibition on executive acceptance of presents from foreign powers (the Foreign Emoluments Clause of Article I, Section 9, Clause 8) — are designed to address the structural-political problem of foreign influence rather than the individual-corruption problem of foreign bribery alone. The analysis is consistent with the broader structural-political vocabulary the founding-era anti-corruption tradition deploys, and it supplies one of the principal textual foundations for the contemporary scholarly engagement with the foreign-influence dimension of corruption that the post-2016 American political-historical period has made urgent.

The Foreign Emoluments Clause itself is a textual foundation for the founding-era conception of structural corruption that the contemporary doctrinal architecture has insufficiently engaged. The clause, prohibiting any person holding any office of profit or trust under the United States from accepting any present, emolument, office, or title of any kind whatever from any king, prince, or foreign state without the consent of Congress, articulates a categorical commitment whose structural function is the prevention of foreign influence on American officeholders. The categorical character of the commitment — the prohibition is not balanced against competing considerations; it is categorical — is the structural feature the analysis Teachout develops engages, and it is the structural feature the contemporary doctrinal architecture has under-developed in its treatment of analogous structural-corruption questions.

Benjamin Franklin's snuff box, which gives Teachout's book its subtitle, is the historical episode that supplies the most evocative founding-era illustration of the structural-corruption analysis. In 1785, at the conclusion of his diplomatic service in France, Franklin was given a diamond-encrusted miniature portrait of King Louis XVI as a parting gift. Franklin sought congressional consent under the Foreign Emoluments Clause to accept the gift, and the question generated substantial debate over whether and on what conditions the consent should be granted. The episode illustrates several features of the founding-era conception. The Foreign Emoluments Clause was understood as operating at the structural rather than the individual level — Franklin's personal integrity was not in question, and yet the clause's application to him was treated as a serious constitutional question requiring congressional engagement. The categorical character of the prohibition was understood as essential to its structural function — the question was not whether Franklin would be corrupted by accepting the gift, but whether the systematic acceptance of such gifts would produce structural conditions under which the constitutional commitment to non-foreign-influence could not be sustained. The episode is one of many founding-era instances Teachout documents in which the broader conception of corruption was operative in actual constitutional practice.

### The Anti-Federalist Articulation

The Anti-Federalist tradition supplies a parallel and complementary articulation of the structural-corruption analysis that the *Federalist* papers themselves engage. The Anti-Federalists were not the exponents of unconstrained majoritarian democracy that some twentieth-century scholarship has portrayed them as; they were exponents of a particular structural-political analysis whose substantive commitments included the preservation of the small-republic conditions under which structural corruption could be avoided. The *Federalist* tradition's argument for the extended republic was, on the Anti-Federalist analysis, the argument for an architectural configuration in which structural corruption would be more difficult to address rather than less, because the distance between representatives and constituents, the size of the legislative bodies, and the complexity of the federal architecture would diminish the structural conditions under which structural corruption could be detected and constrained. The Anti-Federalist analysis was, in important respects, the more pessimistic and the more analytically sharp on the structural-corruption question.

Brutus, in his series of essays published in the *New York Journal* between October 1787 and April 1788, articulates the structural-political analysis of how the proposed federal architecture would generate conditions favorable to structural corruption. Brutus's argument is that the federal legislative bodies would be too small to represent the diverse interests of the population, that the federal officeholders would be too distant from their constituents to be effectively held accountable by them, that the federal taxation power would generate the conditions under which federal officeholders could enrich themselves and their associates at the expense of the public, and that the federal judiciary's life tenure and broad jurisdiction would generate the conditions under which the judicial power could be turned against the public interest. The analysis is structural-political throughout, and its substantive commitments are continuous with the broader founding-era anti-corruption tradition.

The Federal Farmer's series of letters, published in 1787 and 1788, develops a parallel structural-political analysis with particular attention to the relationship between representation and corruption. The Federal Farmer's argument is that adequate representation requires substantial similarity between representatives and constituents in interests, condition, and outlook, and that the proposed federal architecture would generate representatives whose interests, condition, and outlook would diverge systematically from those of their constituents. The structural-political analysis is, again, an analysis of structural corruption: the divergence the Federal Farmer identifies is precisely the structural-political condition under which official judgment is oriented toward interests other than those of the public, and the constitutional response the Federal Farmer advocates is structural rather than individual.

Centinel's series, written principally by Samuel Bryan and published in the *Independent Gazetteer* of Philadelphia between October 1787 and April 1788, develops the structural-political analysis with particular force in connection with the architecture of the Senate. Centinel's argument is that the Senate's structural features — the indirect election by state legislatures, the six-year terms, the small size, the equal representation of states regardless of population — would generate a body whose structural conditions would favor the accumulation of wealth and power in a small political class whose interests would diverge systematically from those of the broader population. The analysis is consistent with the broader structural-political analysis the Anti-Federalist tradition develops, and it supplies one of the principal textual foundations for the contemporary scholarly engagement with the structural-political analysis of the Senate that the project's prior documents have engaged.

George Mason's *Objections to the Constitution of Government Formed by the Convention*, written in October 1787 immediately after the conclusion of the Philadelphia convention, articulates Mason's specific objections to the proposed Constitution. The objections include the absence of a bill of rights, the architecture of the Senate, the composition of the federal judiciary, and the specific constitutional accommodations of slavery the document embedded. The substantive content of Mason's objections is structural-political throughout: each objection identifies a specific structural feature of the proposed Constitution that, on Mason's analysis, would generate the conditions under which the Constitution's substantive commitments could not be sustained. The analysis is consistent with the broader founding-era anti-corruption tradition and supplies one of the principal contemporary texts on which the structural-political reading of the founding-era settlement depends.

Mercy Otis Warren's *Observations on the New Constitution and the Federal and State Conventions*, published anonymously in February 1788 under the pseudonym "A Columbian Patriot," develops the structural-political analysis with particular attention to the constitutional risks of consolidated power and to the Constitution's accommodations of slavery. Warren's *History of the Rise, Progress, and Termination of the American Revolution*, published in three volumes in 1805, develops the analysis at length and supplies one of the principal contemporary historical-analytical engagements with the founding period. The analysis Warren develops is structural-political throughout, with sustained attention to the specific constitutional features that, on her analysis, would generate the structural conditions under which the founding-era commitments could not be sustained. The contemporary scholarly engagement with Warren's *Observations* and *History* — particularly through the work of Linda K. Kerber, Mary Beth Norton, and the contemporary scholarship on women in the founding period — has recovered the analytical depth of Warren's contribution and supplies the principal source for the project's understanding of the structural-political analysis the broader founding-era anti-corruption tradition contains.

### The Long Anti-Corruption Tradition

The founding-era anti-corruption tradition was extended through the Jacksonian period, the Reconstruction era, the Progressive Era, and the early-to-mid twentieth century in a continuous tradition that the contemporary doctrinal architecture has progressively narrowed. The Jacksonian period's anti-corruption rhetoric, articulated principally by Andrew Jackson in his veto message of the Bank of the United States rechartering bill in 1832, deployed the structural-political vocabulary of the founding-era tradition with particular force. Jackson's argument was that the Bank's concentration of financial and political power produced structural conditions favorable to corruption in the broad sense, and that the constitutional response was the structural dismantling of the institutional configuration that produced the conditions. The analysis was substantively continuous with the founding-era tradition, and its rhetorical force depended on the public's familiarity with the structural-political vocabulary the founding-era tradition had supplied.

The Reconstruction-era constitutional and legislative architecture, engaged at length in Document B's Part Four on structural racism, included substantive anti-corruption provisions whose structural-political content was continuous with the founding-era tradition. The Fourteenth Amendment's Section 3, disqualifying from federal or state office any person who, having previously taken an oath to support the Constitution, engaged in insurrection or rebellion against it or gave aid or comfort to its enemies, articulates an anti-corruption principle whose structural function is the protection of the constitutional architecture against the structural threat that disloyal officeholders pose. The provision's contemporary application — the litigation around Section 3's application to former officeholders involved in the events of January 6, 2021, including the Supreme Court's decision in *Trump v. Anderson* (2024) holding that Section 3 cannot be enforced against federal candidates by states without congressional implementing legislation — has been a contemporary site of the doctrinal-historical question the present document engages.

The Progressive Era's anti-corruption movement, articulated through the work of Louis Brandeis, Robert La Follette, Theodore Roosevelt, the muckraking journalism of Ida Tarbell, Lincoln Steffens, and Upton Sinclair, and the legislative architecture of the Sherman Antitrust Act of 1890, the Clayton Act of 1914, the Federal Reserve Act of 1913, and the Federal Trade Commission Act of 1914, deployed the structural-political vocabulary of the founding-era tradition in connection with the structural-political analysis of corporate concentration and its political consequences. Brandeis's *Other People's Money and How the Bankers Use It* (1914) is the foundational Progressive-era articulation of the structural-political analysis of how concentrated economic power generates structural-political conditions favorable to corruption in the broad sense. The analysis Brandeis develops is substantively continuous with the founding-era tradition, and its analytical apparatus is the principal contemporary precursor of the structural-corruption analysis Teachout develops and that the present document engages.

The Tillman Act of 1907, prohibiting corporations from making direct contributions to federal political campaigns, articulates the structural-corruption commitment in legislative form. The Federal Corrupt Practices Act of 1910 (reenacted in 1925), the Hatch Act of 1939, the Federal Election Campaign Act of 1971, and the Federal Election Campaign Act Amendments of 1974, each in different ways, extended the legislative architecture of campaign finance regulation in directions consistent with the founding-era anti-corruption tradition. The doctrinal architecture that emerged from *Buckley v. Valeo* (1976) and its progeny, taken up in detail in Part Two, has progressively dismantled this legislative architecture by progressively narrowing the conception of corruption that constitutional law would recognize as authorizing the regulation.

### The Cross-Traditional Engagement

The multi-eyed seeing commitment requires that the load-bearing analytical authority on the question of structural corruption be drawn from multiple traditions in roughly co-equal weight. The founding-era anti-corruption tradition reconstructed in the preceding subsections is the principal Western analytical contribution; the cross-traditional engagement supplies analytical contributions that the standard founding-era reconstruction does not produce.

The dalit constitutional tradition, engaged at length in Document B's Part One on caste, supplies the analytical apparatus for understanding what may be called caste-capital concentration as a structural form of oligarchic configuration. B. R. Ambedkar's analysis in *Annihilation of Caste* (1936) and *States and Minorities* (1947) articulates the structural-political mechanisms through which the concentration of capital, land, and economic opportunity in dominant-caste hands has been sustained across generations through the structural mechanisms of caste subordination and through the architecture of property law, family law, and inheritance law that has codified the concentration. Sukhadeo Thorat's empirical work on caste discrimination in contemporary India documents the structural persistence of the concentration across the formal-equality commitments the post-independence Indian constitutional architecture has articulated. The analytical contribution for the present argument is the demonstration that the structural-corruption analysis the founding-era tradition supplied is one specification of a broader structural-political analysis whose specific structural mechanisms vary across cultural and political-economic contexts but whose analytical core is recognizable across traditions. The corruption the contemporary American architecture exhibits is, on the cross-traditional analysis, one specification of the broader structural-political pattern the dalit tradition has analyzed in its own context, and the analytical apparatus for diagnosing the American case is enriched by the cross-traditional engagement.

The Indigenous political-economic tradition, engaged at length in Document B's Part Two on oligarchy and across multiple project documents, supplies the analytical apparatus for understanding the structural-political mechanisms through which oligarchic accumulation has operated through the dispossession of Indigenous lands and resources. Glen Coulthard's (Yellowknives Dene) *Red Skin, White Masks* (2014), Nick Estes's (Lower Brule Sioux) *Our History Is the Future* (2019), and Winona LaDuke's (Ojibwe) *All Our Relations* (1999) develop the analysis of how the wealth concentration the structural-oligarchy theory analyzes has been produced through ongoing dispossession of Indigenous lands and resources, and how the structural-political mechanisms through which the dispossession has been sustained — the federal-Indian-law architecture engaged in Document B's Part Four, the resource-extraction architecture engaged in the contemporary Indigenous political-economic scholarship, the treaty-rights violation architecture engaged across the entire historical and contemporary record — are part of the broader oligarchic configuration. The analytical contribution for the present argument is the demonstration that the structural-corruption analysis cannot be conducted without reference to the structural mechanisms of dispossession the Indigenous political-economic tradition documents, and that the architectural response the project advances must engage the dispossession architecture at the constitutional level rather than treating it as a separate legal-doctrinal area.

The Black-radical political-economy tradition, engaged at length in Document B's Part Two and Part Four and across multiple project documents, supplies the analytical apparatus for understanding the structural-political mechanisms through which oligarchic accumulation has operated through racial expropriation. Cedric Robinson's *Black Marxism* (1983), W. E. B. Du Bois's *Black Reconstruction* (1935), Manning Marable's *How Capitalism Underdeveloped Black America* (1983), and Heather McGhee's *The Sum of Us* (2021) develop the analysis of how the wealth concentration has been produced through chattel slavery, sharecropping, convict leasing, the racial structuring of New Deal labor and housing protections, and the contemporary architecture of structural racism that Document B Part Four engaged. The analytical contribution for the present argument is the demonstration that the structural-corruption analysis is incomplete if it does not engage the racial mechanisms through which the structural-political configuration has been historically constructed and is currently maintained, and that the architectural response must engage the racial mechanisms at the constitutional level.

The Latinx political-economic tradition, engaged in Document B's Part Four and elsewhere, supplies the analytical apparatus for understanding the structural-political mechanisms through which oligarchic accumulation has operated through the labor and immigration architectures. Zaragosa Vargas's *Labor Rights Are Civil Rights: Mexican American Workers in Twentieth-Century America* (2005) develops the analysis of how the agricultural-labor and industrial-labor architectures of the twentieth century operated as mechanisms of structural-political configuration whose effect was the systematic underpayment and political-economic subordination of Mexican-American workers. Mae Ngai's *Impossible Subjects* (2004) develops the analysis of how the legal-political category of "illegal alien" has operated as part of the labor-architectural configuration that has subsidized the agricultural and industrial economies through the structural exclusion of undocumented workers from the legal protections that would otherwise constrain their exploitation. The analytical contribution for the present argument is the demonstration that the structural-corruption analysis must engage the labor-architectural and immigration-architectural mechanisms through which the contemporary oligarchic configuration is sustained, and that the architectural response must engage these mechanisms at the constitutional level.

The disability-rights political-economy tradition supplies the analytical apparatus for understanding how the structural-architectural configuration has produced and maintained the structural exclusion of disabled persons from full participation in the political-economic architecture, with consequences for the wealth-concentration analysis the structural-oligarchy theory advances. The disability-justice tradition's analysis of how the architecture of work, of public services, of educational institutions, and of the broader social architecture has been built around design assumptions that exclude disabled persons from full participation, engaged at length in Document B's Part Five, identifies the structural-architectural mechanism through which the wealth-concentration in the contemporary American political economy has been articulated as wealth concentration in the able-bodied and neurotypical population disproportionately. The analytical contribution for the present argument is the demonstration that the structural-corruption analysis cannot be conducted without reference to the design-assumption mechanisms the disability-justice tradition documents, and that the architectural response must engage these mechanisms at the constitutional level.

### The Doctrinal-Historical Conclusion of Part One

The reconstruction of the founding-era anti-corruption tradition the preceding subsections develop establishes the doctrinal-historical baseline against which the post-1976 doctrinal sequence must be assessed. The founding-era tradition operated with a substantially broader conception of corruption than the contemporary architecture recognizes; the broader conception was shared across the *Federalist* and Anti-Federalist traditions, was extended through the long anti-corruption tradition that ran from the founders through the Jacksonian period, the Reconstruction era, the Progressive Era, and the early-to-mid twentieth century, and was substantively continuous with cross-traditional analytical apparatus the dalit, Indigenous, Black-radical, Latinx, and disability-justice traditions supply. The contemporary doctrinal architecture has departed from this baseline in directions the founding-era tradition would not have authorized, and the architectural reconstruction the project advances is consistent with rather than departing from the substantive commitments the founding-era tradition articulated.

The doctrinal-historical case the preceding subsections develop is sufficient to establish that even on the most conservative interpretive premises — premises the project does not endorse but that are deployed here to demonstrate the analytical force of the case — the contemporary doctrinal architecture is constitutionally indefensible. The case is stronger when the cross-traditional analytical apparatus is engaged, because the structural-corruption analysis the cross-traditional traditions supply identifies structural mechanisms of contemporary oligarchic configuration that the standard founding-era reconstruction does not adequately specify. Part Two takes up the post-1976 doctrinal sequence in detail and develops the analytical case for the doctrinal reconstitution Part One has identified at the level of constitutional discourse.

## Part Two — The Constitutional Architecture of Oligarchy: The Post-1976 Doctrinal Sequence

### The Buckley Framework and Its Premises

The doctrinal architecture of contemporary American campaign finance and money-in-politics jurisprudence begins, for the present argument, with *Buckley v. Valeo*, 424 U.S. 1, decided by the Supreme Court in a per curiam opinion on January 30, 1976. The case arose from constitutional challenges to the Federal Election Campaign Act Amendments of 1974, the most ambitious legislative effort to regulate money in federal politics that the post-Watergate Congress could produce. The Act limited contributions to federal candidates, limited expenditures by candidates and by independent actors, established a system of public financing for presidential campaigns, and required substantial disclosure of contributions and expenditures. The plaintiffs — a coalition that included the conservative Senator James L. Buckley of New York, former Senator Eugene McCarthy, the New York Civil Liberties Union, and several allied organizations — challenged the Act on First Amendment grounds, arguing that the regulation of political money constituted regulation of political speech.

The Court's per curiam opinion produced the analytical-doctrinal architecture that has organized the subsequent doctrinal sequence. The architecture has three principal components, each consequential for the analysis the present document develops. First, the Court held that political money constitutes "speech" for First Amendment purposes — that the expenditure of money to communicate political messages is itself protected expression, with the consequence that legislative restrictions on political money are subject to First Amendment scrutiny. Second, the Court drew the doctrinal distinction between contributions (money given to candidates and political committees) and expenditures (money spent independently to communicate political messages), holding that contributions could be regulated to prevent corruption and the appearance of corruption while expenditures could not be regulated for that purpose because expenditures, in the Court's analysis, do not pose comparable corruption risks. Third, the Court specified the conception of corruption that would be recognized as constitutional justification for the contribution regulation: corruption, in the *Buckley* framework, refers to "quid pro quo" arrangements in which contributors receive specific governmental favors in exchange for their contributions.

Each component of the *Buckley* framework has been the subject of substantial scholarly criticism, and the criticism is foundational for the diagnostic the present document advances. The first component — the equation of money with speech — has been criticized on the analytical ground that money is a resource whose distribution reflects the underlying distribution of wealth, with the consequence that the *Buckley* framework's First Amendment architecture systematically privileges those with greater resources to express their political views over those with fewer resources. Lawrence Lessig's analytical contribution in *Republic, Lost* (2011, revised as *Republic, Lost: Version 2.0* in 2015), engaged in Document B Part Two, develops this criticism in connection with the structural-dependence-corruption analysis. The criticism is consistent with the cross-traditional analytical apparatus the multi-eyed seeing commitment requires: the Indigenous, dalit, Black-radical, and disability-justice traditions all engage the analytical question of how the formal-equality architecture of constitutional law has been compatible with substantive structural inequality, and the *Buckley* framework's equation of money with speech is one specification of the structural pattern these traditions have analyzed.

The second component — the contribution-expenditure distinction — has been criticized on the analytical ground that the distinction does not track the empirical reality of how political money operates. Independent expenditures coordinated with candidates and parties through structural-political networks operate functionally as contributions to those candidates and parties, and the formal-doctrinal distinction does not capture the structural-political reality. The contemporary doctrinal architecture has progressively eroded the distinction by extending the constitutional protections for expenditures into domains the *Buckley* Court treated as legitimate subjects of contribution regulation, with the consequence that the structural-political effect of the doctrinal architecture has been the progressive expansion of constitutional protections for political money rather than the maintenance of the distinction *Buckley* drew.

The third component — the narrow conception of corruption — is the principal subject of the present argument and is the doctrinal departure from the founding-era anti-corruption tradition Part One reconstructed. The narrow conception treats corruption as a matter of specific quid-pro-quo exchanges between contributors and officeholders, and treats the absence of specific exchanges as the absence of corruption. The structural-corruption analysis the founding-era tradition supplied — the analysis of how structural conditions of dependence, influence, interest, gratitude, and obligation produce corruption regardless of any specific exchange — is not recognized as constitutional justification for regulation under the *Buckley* framework. The narrow conception has progressively narrowed further in the subsequent doctrinal sequence, with the consequence that the contemporary constitutional architecture has progressively withdrawn the constitutional grounds for legislative anti-corruption regulation.

The *Buckley* framework's structural-political effects have been substantial. The legislative architecture of campaign finance regulation that Congress had constructed across the post-Watergate period was substantially constrained by the framework's doctrinal commitments, and the subsequent legislative attempts to extend the architecture — the McCain-Feingold Bipartisan Campaign Reform Act of 2002, the various state-level efforts at campaign finance reform, the contemporary federal proposals — have operated within the doctrinal constraints the framework specified. The structural-political effect has been the progressive expansion of the role of money in American politics across the period from 1976 to the present, with the empirical evidence the political-science literature documents (Document B Part Two engages the Gilens-Page-Bartels findings) consistent with the analysis that the structural-political effect of the doctrinal framework has been to enable the structural-political configuration the project's diagnostic identifies.

### The Corporate-Personhood Line: Bellotti, Citizens United

The corporate-personhood line of cases constitutes the second principal component of the doctrinal architecture the present part engages. *First National Bank of Boston v. Bellotti*, 435 U.S. 765, decided April 26, 1978, was the first case in the line, holding that the First Amendment protected the right of business corporations to expend funds in connection with referenda on issues that did not materially affect their business interests. The Massachusetts statute the case challenged had prohibited corporate expenditures on referenda except where the issues materially affected the corporation's property, business, or assets, and the Court held the statute unconstitutional. The case extended First Amendment protections originally developed in connection with natural persons to corporate entities in the political-speech domain, and it supplied the doctrinal foundation for the subsequent expansion of corporate-personhood doctrine in the political-money domain.

Justice William Rehnquist's dissent in *Bellotti* articulated the principal contemporary objection to the doctrinal move the case made. Rehnquist's argument was that corporations are entities created by state law for specific purposes, that the constitutional protections originally developed for natural persons should not be assumed to extend to entities whose existence depends on the legislative grant, and that the structural-political consequence of extending the protections would be the constitutional empowerment of concentrated economic enterprises against democratic regulation. The argument is structurally consistent with the founding-era anti-corruption tradition Part One reconstructed, and Rehnquist's articulation of the structural-political analysis is one of the principal contemporary doctrinal-historical sources for the present argument's analytical case.

*Citizens United v. Federal Election Commission*, 558 U.S. 310, decided January 21, 2010, is the contemporary apex of the corporate-personhood line. The case arose from a challenge by Citizens United, a non-profit corporation, to the application of section 203 of the Bipartisan Campaign Reform Act of 2002 — the so-called electioneering-communications provision, prohibiting corporations and unions from making independent expenditures from their general treasury funds for express advocacy or its functional equivalent in connection with federal candidate elections within thirty days of a primary or sixty days of a general election — to its release of a film critical of then-Senator Hillary Clinton during the 2008 presidential primary cycle. The Court, in a 5-4 decision authored by Justice Anthony Kennedy, held the section unconstitutional as applied to independent expenditures by corporations and unions.

The decision's analytical-doctrinal architecture has been the subject of substantial scholarly criticism whose analytical force the present argument engages. The decision held that the government could not restrict independent expenditures by corporations on the basis of corporate identity; that independent expenditures, by definition, do not give rise to corruption or the appearance of corruption in the *Buckley* sense; that the disclosure and disclaimer requirements the BCRA imposed were not unconstitutional; and that the line of cases that had authorized restrictions on corporate political expenditures, including *Austin v. Michigan Chamber of Commerce* (1990), should be overruled. The decision's structural-political effect has been the elimination of substantial portions of the legislative architecture that had constrained corporate political expenditures across the post-Watergate period and the constitutional empowerment of corporate political spending at scales the prior legislative architecture had constrained.

The dissent, authored by Justice John Paul Stevens and joined by Justices Ruth Bader Ginsburg, Stephen Breyer, and Sonia Sotomayor, articulated the principal contemporary objection to the decision. Stevens's dissent — at ninety pages, the longest of his career — developed the structural-political analysis of how the majority's doctrinal commitments would generate the conditions under which the constitutional architecture's commitment to the public interest could not be sustained. The dissent's analysis is substantively continuous with the founding-era anti-corruption tradition Part One reconstructed, and its substantive depth has supplied one of the principal contemporary scholarly sources for the present argument.

The structural-political effects of *Citizens United* have been documented across the subsequent decade and a half by a substantial scholarly literature. The decision's combination with the lower-court decision in *SpeechNow.org v. FEC*, 599 F.3d 686 (D.C. Cir. 2010), which held that contribution limits to independent-expenditure-only committees were unconstitutional, produced the architecture of "Super PACs" that have organized substantial portions of contemporary American political fundraising and expenditure. The decision's combination with the existing tax-exempt-organization architecture produced the architecture of "dark money" — political expenditures by entities not required to disclose their donors — that has organized substantial portions of the contemporary political-money configuration. The empirical evidence on dark-money expenditures across the post-2010 period is engaged in detail in Part Three.

### The Aggregate-Limits and Individual-Contribution Lines: McCutcheon

*McCutcheon v. Federal Election Commission*, 572 U.S. 185, decided April 2, 2014, extended the doctrinal-architectural pattern of *Buckley* and *Citizens United* to the aggregate limits on individual contributions. The case arose from a challenge by Shaun McCutcheon, an Alabama businessman, to the federal aggregate limits — limits on the total amount an individual could contribute to all federal candidates and political committees combined in a two-year cycle — that the Federal Election Campaign Act and its amendments had imposed. The Court, in a plurality opinion authored by Chief Justice John Roberts and joined in operative effect by Justices Antonin Scalia, Samuel Alito, and Anthony Kennedy with Justice Clarence Thomas concurring in the judgment, held the aggregate limits unconstitutional.

The decision's analytical-doctrinal architecture is consequential for the present argument. The plurality opinion explicitly engaged and substantially narrowed the conception of corruption that the *Buckley* framework had articulated. The opinion held that the only conception of corruption the First Amendment would permit Congress to regulate was the quid-pro-quo corruption of *Buckley* and that broader conceptions — including the structural-corruption conception the founding-era tradition supplied — were not constitutionally cognizable. The opinion rejected the government's argument that the aggregate limits served a constitutional anti-corruption interest by preventing the circumvention of the per-candidate and per-committee limits, holding that the circumvention concerns the government raised did not constitute the quid-pro-quo corruption the *Buckley* framework recognized. The decision's effect has been the elimination of one of the principal architectural mechanisms through which the structural-political concentration of political money had been constrained.

Justice Stephen Breyer's dissent in *McCutcheon*, joined by Justices Ginsburg, Sotomayor, and Elena Kagan, articulated the principal contemporary objection to the doctrinal narrowing the plurality opinion advanced. Breyer's dissent developed the structural-political analysis of how the elimination of the aggregate limits would generate the conditions under which the constitutional architecture's commitment to non-corruption could not be sustained, and his articulation of the broader conception of corruption the founding-era tradition supplied is substantively continuous with the analytical case Part One reconstructed. The dissent is one of the principal contemporary doctrinal sources for the analytical case the present document advances.

The post-*McCutcheon* contribution architecture has been organized around the per-candidate and per-committee limits, with the elimination of the aggregate limits substantially expanding the role of high-contribution donors in federal political fundraising. The architecture has been further reorganized by the proliferation of Super PACs and dark-money organizations whose fundraising and expenditure operations are not subject to the contribution limits the per-candidate and per-committee architecture preserves, with the consequence that the post-*McCutcheon* contribution architecture has operated within a broader political-money configuration in which the contribution limits constitute one of several regulatory architectures whose interaction shapes the structural-political effect.

### The Narrowing of Bribery: McDonnell

*McDonnell v. United States*, 579 U.S. 550, decided June 27, 2016, narrowed the conception of "official act" under the federal bribery statute (18 U.S.C. § 201) in ways consequential for the present argument. The case arose from the federal prosecution of former Virginia Governor Robert F. McDonnell for honest-services fraud, Hobbs Act extortion, and conspiracy to commit honest-services fraud and Hobbs Act extortion in connection with $175,000 in loans, gifts, and other benefits he and his wife had received from Jonnie Williams, the chief executive officer of a Virginia-based dietary-supplement company, allegedly in exchange for the McDonnells' assistance in promoting Williams's company and its products. The Court, in a unanimous opinion authored by Chief Justice Roberts, vacated the conviction on the ground that the jury instructions had defined "official act" too broadly.

The decision's substantive content is consequential for the present argument. The Court held that "official act" under the bribery statute requires more than the kind of access, attention, and informal assistance that a public official customarily provides to those who request it; it requires a specific decision or action on a "question, matter, cause, suit, proceeding or controversy" that is or may be pending before the public official. The decision narrowed the federal anti-bribery architecture in ways that made the statutory architecture less capable of addressing the structural-corruption configuration the present argument identifies, and it operated as the criminal-law counterpart to the constitutional-law narrowing of the corruption conception the *Buckley*-through-*McCutcheon* line had developed.

The post-*McDonnell* federal anti-bribery architecture has been the subject of substantial scholarly criticism whose analytical force the present argument engages. The decision's effect has been the substantial constraint on federal prosecutions of public-corruption cases that had previously operated under the broader conception of "official act," and the legislative architecture has not been amended to restore the broader conception. The Indigenous, Black-radical, dalit, Latinx, and disability-justice traditions' cross-traditional analytical apparatus identifies the structural-political mechanism the *McDonnell* decision protects: the structural-political configuration in which officeholders provide systematic access, attention, and informal assistance to wealthy contributors without engaging in specific quid-pro-quo exchanges is the configuration the founding-era anti-corruption tradition recognized as structural corruption, and the *McDonnell* decision's narrowing of the federal anti-bribery architecture has eliminated one of the principal legal-architectural mechanisms through which the structural configuration could be constrained.

### The Standing Doctrine: Lujan

*Lujan v. Defenders of Wildlife*, 504 U.S. 555, decided June 12, 1992, is engaged in detail in Document 8 in the climate context and is engaged here in the broader oligarchy context the present document develops. The case held that environmental plaintiffs lacked Article III standing to challenge a federal regulation interpreting the Endangered Species Act because they had not demonstrated specific, particularized injury traceable to the agency action and redressable by the court. The decision's analytical-doctrinal architecture, articulated principally in Justice Scalia's majority opinion, narrowed the standing doctrine in ways that have constrained the access of citizens and citizen organizations to federal courts for the enforcement of public rights against concentrated economic interests.

The decision's structural-political effect for the present argument is the demonstration that the doctrinal narrowing has operated across multiple constitutional-doctrinal areas in a coordinated rather than incidental pattern. The narrowing of corruption in the *Buckley*-through-*McCutcheon* line, the narrowing of "official act" in *McDonnell*, the narrowing of standing in *Lujan* and its progeny, and the doctrinal developments engaged in the subsequent subsections of this part have shared analytical-doctrinal commitments and have operated in directions consistent with the structural-political effect of insulating the structural-political configuration from constitutional and legal-doctrinal constraint. The pattern is not the random aggregation of doctrinal developments; it is the coordinated reconstitution of the doctrinal architecture in directions favorable to the structural-political configuration the project's diagnostic identifies.

### The Major Questions Doctrine: West Virginia v. EPA

*West Virginia v. Environmental Protection Agency*, 597 U.S. 697, decided June 30, 2022, articulated the "major questions doctrine" as a substantial doctrinal development whose analytical-doctrinal architecture is consequential for the present argument. The case arose from a challenge to the EPA's authority under section 111(d) of the Clean Air Act to regulate greenhouse gas emissions from existing power plants through generation-shifting requirements. The Court, in a 6-3 opinion authored by Chief Justice Roberts, held that the EPA lacked the authority the regulation claimed because the regulation addressed a "major question" of "vast economic and political significance" and Congress had not "clearly authorized" the agency to address the question.

The decision's analytical-doctrinal architecture is consequential for the structural-political analysis the present document develops. The major questions doctrine articulated in *West Virginia v. EPA* and developed in subsequent cases (notably *Biden v. Nebraska*, 600 U.S. 477, decided June 30, 2023, which held that the Department of Education lacked authority under the HEROES Act to forgive substantial portions of federal student-loan debt) operates as a substantive constraint on the regulatory authority of administrative agencies in domains where the political-economic stakes are large. The doctrine's structural-political effect is the constitutional empowerment of the legislative-political process — including the legislative-political process the dark-money networks engaged in Part Three have substantially captured — to determine the regulatory architecture in domains where the existing legislative architecture has authorized agency regulation under broader statutory grants. The doctrine operates, on the structural-political analysis, in concert with the constitutional architecture of campaign finance and the post-Chevron architecture engaged in the next subsection to produce the configuration in which the structural-political effects of dark-money political spending are progressively expanded.

The dissent in *West Virginia v. EPA*, authored by Justice Elena Kagan and joined by Justices Breyer and Sotomayor, articulated the principal contemporary objection to the doctrine. Kagan's dissent developed the analytical case that the major questions doctrine operates as an extra-textual interpretive device that selectively constrains the regulatory authority of administrative agencies in directions favorable to the regulated industries and that the doctrine's application has been systematically asymmetric, with the doctrinal apparatus deployed against environmental, public-health, consumer-protection, and similar regulations more reliably than against regulations that serve concentrated economic interests. The structural-political analysis Kagan's dissent develops is substantively consistent with the analytical case the present document advances.

### Loper Bright and the Post-Chevron Architecture

*Loper Bright Enterprises v. Raimondo*, 603 U.S. ___, decided June 28, 2024 (and consolidated with *Relentless, Inc. v. Department of Commerce*), overruled the Chevron framework, the doctrinal architecture that had organized judicial review of agency interpretations of ambiguous statutes since *Chevron U.S.A., Inc. v. Natural Resources Defense Council*, 467 U.S. 837 (1984). The Court, in a 6-2 opinion authored by Chief Justice Roberts and joined by Justices Thomas, Alito, Gorsuch, Kavanaugh, and Barrett, with Justice Jackson recused and Justice Kagan dissenting joined by Justice Sotomayor, held that "courts must exercise their independent judgment in deciding whether an agency has acted within its statutory authority" and that the *Chevron* framework's deferential standard violated section 706 of the Administrative Procedure Act.

The decision's analytical-doctrinal architecture is the most consequential administrative-law development of the past four decades, and its structural-political implications for the present argument are direct. Under the *Chevron* framework, courts had been required to defer to agency interpretations of ambiguous statutory provisions if the interpretation was "reasonable" or "permissible," with the consequence that the agency's expertise and institutional position supplied the substantive content of regulatory architecture in domains where the legislative architecture had not specified the content explicitly. Under the *Loper Bright* framework, courts must independently determine the meaning of the statutory provision, with the consequence that the substantive content of regulatory architecture is determined by judicial rather than agency interpretation. The structural-political effect of the doctrinal shift is the transfer of regulatory-architectural authority from agencies — institutional bodies whose composition reflects executive-branch political configurations and whose substantive expertise is institutional — to federal courts whose composition reflects the longer-term political configuration of the appointing presidents and Senates and whose substantive expertise on regulatory questions is institutional in different and more constrained ways.

The structural-political effect of *Loper Bright* operates in concert with the contemporary federal-judicial composition that the Federalist Society judicial pipeline (engaged in Part Three) has substantially produced. The federal judiciary as currently composed has been substantially shaped by the Federalist Society's judicial-selection apparatus, with the consequence that the doctrinal-architectural authority *Loper Bright* shifted from agencies to courts has been transferred to a judicial corps whose ideological orientation has been substantially shaped by the structural-political configuration the present document analyzes. The doctrinal sequence is therefore not a neutral redistribution of authority among institutional actors; it is a redistribution that has empirically favored the structural-political configuration the project's diagnostic identifies.

The Kagan dissent in *Loper Bright* articulated the principal contemporary objection. The dissent's analytical case is that the *Chevron* framework had operated for forty years as the organizing doctrinal architecture for federal administrative law, that the framework had been substantively congruent with the structural-political features of the modern administrative state (the technical complexity of regulatory questions, the institutional expertise of agencies, the executive-branch accountability of agencies through the appointment and removal apparatus), and that the doctrinal shift the *Loper Bright* majority effected would generate substantial uncertainty and would systematically favor the regulated industries whose institutional resources permit sustained engagement with the new doctrinal architecture. The structural-political analysis the dissent develops is substantively consistent with the analytical case the present document advances, and the *Loper Bright* dissent is one of the principal contemporary doctrinal sources for the analytical apparatus the present part deploys.

### Trump v. United States and Presidential Immunity

*Trump v. United States*, 603 U.S. ___, decided July 1, 2024, is the most consequential constitutional development of the contemporary period for the analysis the present document advances on the executive-branch dimension of the structural-political configuration. The case arose from the federal indictment of former President Donald J. Trump for alleged conduct relating to the 2020 presidential election and its aftermath, including the events of January 6, 2021. The Court, in a 6-3 opinion authored by Chief Justice Roberts and joined by Justices Thomas, Alito, Gorsuch, Kavanaugh, and Barrett, with Justices Sotomayor, Kagan, and Jackson dissenting, held that former Presidents have absolute immunity from criminal prosecution for actions within their "core" constitutional authority and presumptive immunity for actions within the "outer perimeter" of their official authority.

The decision's analytical-doctrinal architecture has been the subject of substantial scholarly criticism whose analytical force the present argument engages. The decision's effect is the substantial expansion of the constitutional immunity of the executive office in directions the founding-era constitutional architecture and the long American constitutional tradition had not authorized. The Sotomayor dissent, joined by Kagan and Jackson, articulated the principal contemporary objection in language whose analytical force has been substantial: the dissent argued that the decision's doctrinal commitments would generate the conditions under which the constitutional architecture's commitment to the rule of law and to the equal application of criminal law could not be sustained, and that the decision's effect would be the constitutional empowerment of the executive office in directions the founding-era anti-corruption tradition Part One reconstructed would not have authorized.

The decision's structural-political analysis for the present argument is the demonstration that the constitutional doctrinal architecture has been progressively reconstituted across multiple doctrinal areas in ways consistent with the structural-political configuration the project's diagnostic identifies. The narrowing of corruption in the campaign-finance line; the narrowing of "official act" in *McDonnell*; the narrowing of standing in *Lujan* and its progeny; the major questions doctrine articulated in *West Virginia v. EPA* and developed in *Biden v. Nebraska*; the post-*Chevron* architecture established by *Loper Bright*; the executive-immunity architecture established by *Trump v. The pattern is not the random aggregation of doctrinal developments; it is the coordinated reconstitution of the doctrinal architecture in directions whose substantive effect has been the constitutional empowerment of concentrated economic and political power against the substantive commitments the founding-era anti-corruption tradition articulated.

### Trump v. Anderson and Section 3

*Trump v. Anderson*, 601 U.S. 100, decided March 4, 2024, addressed the application of Section 3 of the Fourteenth Amendment to former officeholders allegedly involved in the events of January 6, 2021. ### Regulatory Capture: The Structural Analysis

The analytical literature on regulatory capture, developed principally through the work of George Stigler, Daniel Carpenter, and the broader scholarly tradition, supplies the analytical apparatus for understanding the structural-political mechanism through which the legislative-deregulation track and the doctrinal sequence have operated. George Stigler's foundational article, "The Theory of Economic Regulation," published in *The Bell Journal of Economics and Management Science* in 1971, articulated the analytical framework for understanding regulatory capture as the systematic alignment of regulatory operations with the interests of regulated industries rather than with the public interest. Stigler's analysis identified the structural-political conditions under which the alignment occurs: the concentrated benefits of favorable regulation to regulated industries combined with the dispersed costs to the broader public, the institutional advantages of regulated industries in supplying the technical and institutional expertise that regulatory agencies require, the systematic patterns of post-government employment that produce structural-political conditions favorable to the regulated industries, and the broader political-economic configuration that channels regulatory operations in directions favorable to regulated industries.

Daniel Carpenter's subsequent work on regulatory capture, including his collaboration with David A. Moss on *Preventing Regulatory Capture: Special Interest Influence and How to Limit It* (Cambridge University Press, 2014), has developed the analytical framework with substantial empirical and analytical refinement. Carpenter's analysis distinguishes among multiple forms of capture — material capture, cultural capture, cognitive capture — and identifies the structural-political conditions under which each form operates. The analysis is essential for the diagnostic the present document advances because it identifies the specific structural-political mechanisms through which the regulatory architecture has been progressively reconstituted in directions favorable to the structural-political configuration the project's diagnostic identifies.

The cross-traditional engagement on the regulatory-capture question is consequential. The Indigenous political-economic tradition's analysis of how the federal-Indian-law architecture has operated as a regulatory architecture whose substantive commitments have been to the resource-extraction industries rather than to the Indigenous nations whose lands and territories the regulatory architecture purports to govern is one specification of the regulatory-capture analysis in a domain the standard regulatory-capture literature has not adequately engaged. The Black-radical political-economic tradition's analysis of how the financial regulatory architecture has operated as an architecture whose substantive commitments have been to the financial-services industries rather than to the communities whose financial conditions the regulatory architecture purports to govern is another specification. The dalit tradition's analysis of caste-capital concentration and its relationship to regulatory architectures, the Latinx tradition's analysis of immigration enforcement as a regulatory architecture whose substantive commitments have been to the agricultural and industrial labor markets that depend on undocumented labor, the disability-justice tradition's analysis of the regulatory architectures that have governed accessibility and the structural-political conditions of disabled persons — each supplies analytical contributions to the regulatory-capture analysis that the standard literature does not produce, and the cross-traditional engagement enriches the analytical apparatus the present argument deploys.

## Part Three — The Money-in-Politics Case Studies

### The Powell Memo and the Inception of the Contemporary Configuration

The contemporary structural-political configuration the project's diagnostic identifies has identifiable institutional and ideological origins, and the principal originating document the contemporary scholarly literature has converged on as analytical reference point is the memorandum Lewis F. Powell Jr. wrote on August 23, 1971, for the United States Chamber of Commerce. Powell, then a corporate attorney in Richmond, Virginia, who would be nominated to the Supreme Court two months later (he was confirmed in December 1971 and served until his retirement in 1987), wrote the memorandum at the request of his neighbor, Eugene Sydnor Jr., chair of the Chamber's Education Committee. The memorandum was titled "Attack on American Free Enterprise System" and articulated a comprehensive analysis of what Powell perceived as a systematic attack on the American business community by intellectuals, the media, the academy, the legal profession, and the political-economic culture more broadly. The memorandum was not initially public; it was distributed within the Chamber's network and subsequently published by syndicated columnist Jack Anderson in September 1972 after the Powell nomination had been confirmed. The memorandum has been engaged in the contemporary scholarly literature as the principal originating document of the institutional and ideological infrastructure that the present part develops.

The Powell memorandum's substantive content includes both a diagnostic and an architectural recommendation. The diagnostic analyzed what Powell described as the structural-political position of the business community as having been weakened by the structural-political configuration of the late 1960s and early 1970s — the consumer-protection movement, the environmental movement, the civil-rights movement, the anti-war movement, the broader cultural configuration that had produced the regulatory architecture of the period. The diagnostic identified the universities, the media, the legal profession, the courts, and the broader cultural-intellectual apparatus as the structural-political mechanisms through which the configuration had been produced and was being sustained, and it argued that the business community's political-economic position required a coordinated institutional and ideological response. The architectural recommendation called for substantial business-community investment in the institutional and ideological infrastructure that would produce the structural-political conditions favorable to the business community's interests: investment in academic and intellectual institutions, in legal-architectural institutions, in media institutions, in political-organizational institutions, and in the broader institutional configuration through which structural-political effects are produced.

The Powell memorandum's structural-political effect has been the subject of substantial scholarly analysis whose interpretive force the present argument engages. The memorandum was not, in itself, the cause of the institutional and ideological infrastructure that emerged across the subsequent decades; the structural-political configuration that produced the infrastructure included multiple actors, multiple resources, multiple institutional projects, and multiple ideological commitments operating in coordinated rather than centrally directed relationship. The memorandum's significance is as articulation rather than cause: it articulated the structural-political analysis and the architectural recommendation that subsequently became the operating framework of the institutional and ideological infrastructure, and it supplied the rhetorical-analytical apparatus through which the actors who built the infrastructure understood their project. The memorandum has accordingly been engaged in the contemporary scholarly literature as the principal originating document for understanding the institutional and ideological infrastructure that the present part develops, and as the principal source for the structural-political analysis the present argument advances.

### The Foundation Infrastructure: Olin, Bradley, Scaife

The foundation infrastructure that emerged across the 1970s, 1980s, and 1990s in response to the structural-political analysis the Powell memorandum articulated has been documented in detail by the contemporary scholarly literature. The principal foundations include the John M. Olin Foundation, established in 1953 and directed by William E. Simon (former Secretary of the Treasury under Presidents Nixon and Ford) from 1977 until its planned dissolution in 2005; the Lynde and Harry Bradley Foundation, established in 1942 and reorganized in the 1980s under the leadership of Michael Joyce; the Sarah Scaife Foundation and other foundations associated with the Scaife family, particularly Richard Mellon Scaife; and the network of related foundations that emerged in coordinated relationship across the period. The foundations operated as the institutional vehicles through which substantial financial resources were directed to the academic, intellectual, legal-architectural, media, and political-organizational institutions that the structural-political analysis the Powell memorandum articulated had identified as priorities.

John J. Miller's *A Gift of Freedom: How the John M. Olin Foundation Changed America* (2006) supplies one principal account of the Olin Foundation's operations from the inside, written by a contributor whose substantive commitments are aligned with the foundation's project. Jane Mayer's *Dark Money: The Hidden History of the Billionaires Behind the Rise of the Radical Right* (2016), engaged in Document B Part Two and engaged at length later in this part, supplies the principal investigative-journalistic account of the foundation infrastructure's operations from the outside, written by a journalist whose substantive commitments are critical of the project. The two accounts converge on the substantial empirical record and diverge on the substantive evaluation, with the convergence on the empirical record establishing the structural-political significance the present argument engages.

The foundations' operations across the period included substantial funding for the establishment and operation of conservative academic centers and law-and-economics programs at major American universities, including the law-and-economics movement's institutional foundations at the University of Chicago Law School, the Olin Center for Law, Economics, and Public Policy at Yale Law School, the Olin Center for Law, Economics, and Business at Harvard Law School, and the broader network of law-and-economics programs at law schools across the country; substantial funding for the establishment and operation of conservative think tanks, including the American Enterprise Institute (founded 1938, substantially reoriented in the 1970s and 1980s), the Heritage Foundation (founded 1973), the Cato Institute (founded 1977), the Manhattan Institute (founded 1978), and the broader network of policy-research institutions that the foundations funded; substantial funding for the establishment and operation of conservative legal-architectural institutions, including the Federalist Society (founded 1982 and engaged in detail in a subsequent subsection), the Pacific Legal Foundation (founded 1973), the Mountain States Legal Foundation (founded 1977), and the broader network of public-interest legal organizations that the foundations funded; and substantial funding for the establishment and operation of conservative media institutions, journals, and intellectual publications.

The structural-political effect of the foundation infrastructure has been the substantial reconstitution of the institutional and ideological apparatus across the period. The reconstitution has not been the work of the foundations alone; it has been the work of the broader structural-political configuration the Powell memorandum analyzed and the foundations facilitated. The foundations supplied the financial resources, the institutional vehicles, and the strategic coordination that made the broader project possible, and they operated in coordinated relationship with the corporate political-spending architecture, the political-organizational architecture, and the broader institutional and ideological apparatus the contemporary scholarly literature has documented.

### ALEC and the Legislative-Architectural Infrastructure
