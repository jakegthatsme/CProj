# The Federalist Papers: Complete Summaries and Scholarly Context

## A Guide to All 85 Essays, Their Intellectual Sources, and Their Argument Against the Articles of Confederation

---

# PART ONE: SCHOLARLY AND HISTORICAL CONTEXT

## Origins and Composition

The Federalist Papers were published between October 27, 1787, and May 28, 1788, in New York newspapers — primarily the *Independent Journal* and the *New York Packet* — under the pseudonym "Publius," a name drawn from Publius Valerius Publicola, one of the founders of the Roman Republic. The essays were written by Alexander Hamilton (51 essays), James Madison (29 essays), and John Jay (5 essays), with authorship of a handful of papers disputed between Hamilton and Madison.

Their immediate purpose was to persuade the citizens of New York — a state deeply skeptical of ratification, governed by the powerful Anti-Federalist Governor George Clinton — to support the new Constitution. But they quickly transcended that polemical context. Collected in book form in 1788, they became the most sustained and intellectually rigorous defense of the constitutional design ever produced.

Thomas Jefferson called them "the best commentary on the principles of government which ever was written." Justice Joseph Story drew on them heavily in his *Commentaries on the Constitution* (1833). The Supreme Court began citing them as authoritative sources in the early nineteenth century, a practice that has only accelerated. Yet scholars since the mid-twentieth century have been careful to note that the Papers are advocacy documents — they present the Constitution in its best light, occasionally distorting or omitting tensions in the actual document.

---

## The Articles of Confederation: The Negative Case

To understand *Publius*, one must understand what he was arguing against. The Articles of Confederation, ratified in 1781, embodied a set of governing principles nearly the opposite of what the Constitution proposed:

**Sovereignty resided in the states.** Article II declared that "each state retains its sovereignty, freedom, and independence." The Confederation Congress was not a government of the people but a compact among sovereign states.

**Congress had no power to tax.** It could only requisition funds from states, which routinely ignored the requisitions. By 1786–87, the federal government was effectively bankrupt.

**Congress had no power to regulate commerce.** States enacted competing tariffs against each other, creating economic chaos that historians like John Fiske (writing in *The Critical Period of American History*, 1888) dramatized, though later scholars like Merrill Jensen (*The Articles of Confederation*, 1940) argued Fiske overstated the crisis.

**There was no executive and no judiciary.** Congress exercised all federal functions, making it both legislator and executor — a design Madison in *Federalist No. 47* would associate with tyranny.

**Amendments required unanimity.** Every state had to ratify any change, making reform nearly impossible. Rhode Island blocked multiple reform efforts.

**Congress acted on states, not individuals.** It could not compel compliance by individuals, only by states — and it had no army with which to enforce even that.

The Philadelphia Convention of 1787 was officially called only to revise the Articles. Instead, it produced an entirely new instrument of government. Anti-Federalists immediately attacked this as an usurpation, and much of *The Federalist* is a response to that charge.

---

## The Anti-Federalist Challenge

The Anti-Federalists — writing as "Brutus," "Centinel," "Federal Farmer," "A Landholder," and others — raised objections that *Publius* had to answer directly:

- The republic was too large to be governed on republican principles (drawing on Montesquieu's *Spirit of the Laws*)
- The Constitution lacked a bill of rights
- The Senate was aristocratic
- The President resembled a king
- The federal judiciary would swallow state courts
- The necessary and proper clause and supremacy clause gave Congress unlimited power
- Standing armies in peacetime were dangerous to liberty

Scholars including Herbert Storing (*What the Anti-Federalists Were For*, 1981) have argued that the Anti-Federalists' concerns were not merely reactionary but represented a coherent alternative vision — a genuinely republican, small-scale, participatory democracy rooted in civic virtue rather than institutional mechanics. *The Federalist* is, in part, a direct engagement with these anxieties.

---

## Intellectual Sources and Philosophical Background

*Publius* drew on a rich tradition of political philosophy and historical scholarship. Key sources include:

**John Locke** — *Two Treatises of Government* (1689): The social contract, natural rights, the right of revolution, and the idea that government derives its just powers from the consent of the governed. The Lockean framework underlies much of the constitutional structure's legitimacy arguments.

**Montesquieu** — *The Spirit of the Laws* (1748): The theory of separation of powers, the argument for checks and balances, and — crucially — the claim that republics could only survive in small territories. *Publius* had to directly refute this last point.

**David Hume** — *Essays, Moral, Political, and Literary* (1741–42): Madison's argument in *Federalist No. 10* about the advantages of an extended republic is heavily indebted to Hume's essay "Idea of a Perfect Commonwealth," which turned the Montesquieu argument on its head, suggesting that a large republic might actually be *more* stable because it would contain more competing factions. Douglass Adair's landmark 1943 article "That Politics May Be Reduced to a Science: David Hume, James Madison, and the Tenth Federalist" first established this connection rigorously.

**William Blackstone** — *Commentaries on the Laws of England* (1765–69): The background legal framework for the constitutional structure, especially regarding common law, property rights, and the relationship between courts and statutes.

**The Classical Republics** — *Publius* draws constantly on Greek and Roman history, invoking the Amphictyonic League, the Achaean League, the Lycian Confederacy, Sparta, Athens, Carthage, and Rome as historical case studies. The use of "Publius" as a pseudonym deliberately invoked Roman republican heritage.

**English Parliamentary History** — The constitutional struggles between Crown and Parliament, Magna Carta, the Glorious Revolution of 1688, and the Bill of Rights of 1689 all inform the Papers' understanding of limited government and separated powers.

**The Swiss and Dutch Confederacies** — Cited as cautionary tales of confederal weakness, particularly their inability to act effectively in wartime or resolve internal disputes.

---

## The Scholarly Debate Over Originalism

The Federalist Papers have become a central battleground in the debate over constitutional interpretation. Originalists cite them as evidence of the "original public meaning" or "original intent" of constitutional provisions. This use has been criticized on several grounds:

**The Papers are advocacy, not interpretation.** As Jack Rakove (*Original Meanings*, Pulitzer Prize, 1996) demonstrates, the Papers were written to persuade, not to provide a neutral account of constitutional meaning. Hamilton and Madison sometimes argued for broader federal powers than their opponents feared, precisely to reassure skeptics.

**Hamilton and Madison disagreed with each other.** By the 1790s, Hamilton (as Treasury Secretary) and Madison (leading the congressional opposition) had sharply divergent views of what the Constitution meant — particularly regarding implied powers and the power to create a national bank. If the authors of the Papers could not agree, using them as a unified "intent" is problematic.

**Madison himself rejected originalism.** In letters late in his life, Madison wrote that the meaning of the Constitution should be fixed not by the intent of the framers but by the practice and interpretation of the people over time. He resisted attempts to use his own Notes from the Convention as a definitive guide, and he asked that they not be published until after his death.

**The Papers do not represent the full ratification debate.** As Pauline Maier (*Ratification*, 2010) shows, the actual ratification debates in the state conventions were messy, locally specific, and often turned on concerns quite different from those addressed in the Papers.

---

## The Unitary Executive Debate

The Papers are frequently cited in support of the unitary executive theory — the idea that the President has sole, hierarchical, unremovable control over the entire executive branch. This reading is contested:

**The textual basis is thin.** Article II's vesting clause ("The executive Power shall be vested in a President") is notably briefer than Article I's ("All legislative Powers herein granted shall be vested"). It does not enumerate those powers in the same way.

**Hamilton's *Federalist No. 70*** — which argues for executive "energy" and "unity" — is frequently cited. But Hamilton was arguing for a *single* executive (rather than a council) and for adequate power to act in emergencies. He was not arguing for presidential control of all administrative officers free from congressional oversight.

**Madison's design was about checks, not executive supremacy.** *Federalist No. 51* describes a system where each branch is given the "necessary constitutional means and personal motives to resist the encroachments of the others." Congress has vast power to structure the executive branch through legislation.

**The Decision of 1789** — Congress's first debate over whether the President could remove the Secretary of State without Senate approval — is frequently cited as establishing the unitary executive. But as Saikrishna Prakash (*Imperial from the Beginning*, 2015) acknowledges and as opponents like Lawrence Lessig and Cass Sunstein ("The President and the Administration," *Columbia Law Review*, 1994) argue at length, the debate was ambiguous and does not clearly establish a constitutional principle.

# PART TWO: THE FEDERALIST PAPERS — COMPLETE SUMMARIES

---

## No. 1 — Hamilton
### *General Introduction*

Hamilton opens with a direct challenge to the reader: the question before the American people is whether good government can be established "from reflection and choice" or whether humanity is forever condemned to receive its political institutions "from accident and force." The stakes of ratification, he argues, are therefore not merely local but universal — America's example will either vindicate or discredit republican self-government for all time.

He introduces the pseudonym "Publius," briefly surveys the essays to come, and warns against the rhetorical devices of opponents who will appeal to passion rather than reason. He hints at the Anti-Federalist motivations — some oppose the Constitution out of ambition, some out of fear, some out of honest mistake — and asks for an open and candid hearing.

*Response to the Articles:* The implicit premise is that the Articles have failed the test of "reflection and choice" — they produced a government that cannot sustain itself.

---

## No. 2 — Jay
### *Concerning Dangers from Foreign Force and Influence*

Jay makes the providential and geographic argument for union: America occupies a connected territory, its people share a common language, ancestry, laws, religion, and customs, and they fought together in a common revolution. This natural unity argues for political unity. A divided America — multiple confederacies, as some proposed — would be vulnerable to foreign manipulation, as European powers would play the confederacies against each other.

He introduces the idea that the framers were wise and experienced men, deserving of trust — a somewhat elitist argument that Anti-Federalists would contest.

*Response to the Articles:* The Articles created a union that foreign powers did not respect, as shown by British retention of western forts in violation of the 1783 peace treaty and Spanish closure of the Mississippi.

---

## No. 3 — Jay
### *The Same Subject Continued: Concerning Dangers from Foreign Force and Influence*

Jay argues that a strong national government, administered by the best men selected from the entire country, will be more likely to make wise and consistent foreign policy and to adhere to treaties than thirteen separate state governments, each subject to local passions and parochial interests. State governments are more likely to be swept into unjust wars by local grievances, insults to commerce, or border disputes.

*Response to the Articles:* Under the Articles, there was no effective federal treaty enforcement mechanism, and states repeatedly violated the 1783 peace treaty with Britain.

---

## No. 4 — Jay
### *The Same Subject Continued: Concerning Dangers from Foreign Force and Influence*

Jay shifts from just to unjust wars — arguing that strong nations are less likely to be attacked, because potential aggressors calculate the costs of conflict. A unified America would maintain a stronger navy and present a more formidable defense; a fragmented America would be picked apart.

He anticipates the argument that a confederacy might achieve the same — and responds that confederacies historically have proven unable to coordinate effective military responses.

---

## No. 5 — Jay
### *The Same Subject Continued: Concerning Dangers from Foreign Force and Influence*

If disunion occurs, Jay argues, the resulting confederacies would not be peaceful neighbors but rivals. Northern and southern confederacies would have competing commercial interests, competing alliances with foreign powers, and competing territorial ambitions. He invokes the history of England and Scotland before the Act of Union (1707) as an example of how neighboring nations sharing a land border tend toward rivalry and war — and their unification as an example of the benefits of consolidation.

---

## No. 6 — Hamilton
### *Concerning Dangers from Dissensions Between the States*

Hamilton now makes the historical argument in detail, challenging the "visionary" notion that commercial republics are naturally peaceful. He catalogs wars among ancient city-states, medieval Italian republics, and modern European commercial powers (England, Holland) to demonstrate that republics are just as prone to war as monarchies. The causes of conflict — territorial disputes, commercial competition, the ambitions of leaders — are not eliminated by republican forms of government.

This is an implicit critique of the Anti-Federalist confidence in the peace-producing effects of small republics, and also of certain strands of liberal internationalism (echoing arguments later associated with Kant, though written before *Perpetual Peace*).

---

## No. 7 — Hamilton
### *The Same Subject Continued: Concerning Dangers from Dissensions Between the States*

Hamilton enumerates the specific sources of interstate conflict that would follow disunion: territorial disputes (the western lands), commercial rivalries (competing tariffs and navigation laws), apportionment of the public debt (who would owe what portion of the Revolutionary War debt), and the likely development of state militias that would fuel regional arms races.

He specifically predicts that if the Union dissolved, states with good harbors would tax interior states' commerce, generating the exact kind of grievances that produce wars.

---

## No. 8 — Hamilton
### *The Consequences of Hostilities Between the States*

This is one of the most sobering essays in the collection. Hamilton traces out the full logic of disunion: if the states separate and fall into conflict, they will need standing armies; standing armies create internal security threats; to manage those threats, governments centralize power and suspend civil liberties; the end result is that disunited Americans will live under European-style militarized states, surrendering the freedoms they fought to secure.

The Union, paradoxically, is the guarantee of liberty — not its enemy. America's geographic security as a unified nation allows it to maintain only a modest military and preserve civilian government.

*Scholarly note:* This essay reveals *Publius*'s sophisticated security-politics analysis, essentially anticipating what Harold Lasswell in the 1940s would call the "garrison state" hypothesis.

---

## No. 9 — Hamilton
### *The Union as a Safeguard Against Domestic Faction and Insurrection*

Hamilton takes on Montesquieu's famous argument that republics can only survive in small territories (drawn from Book VIII of *The Spirit of the Laws*). He argues that Montesquieu was describing the city-states of antiquity, not modern states, and that in any event Montesquieu himself endorsed confederate republics for larger territories. Hamilton then argues that the science of politics has advanced: separation of powers, legislative checks and balances, independent courts, and elected representation are all improvements unknown to the ancients.

He introduces the idea of a "confederate republic" — a union of smaller republics, each internally republican, governed at the federal level by representative institutions — as the answer to the size problem.

*Response to the Articles:* The Articles created exactly such a confederate structure, but one without any of the internal republican features Hamilton is about to describe.

---

## No. 10 — Madison
### *The Same Subject Continued: The Union as a Safeguard Against Domestic Faction and Insurrection*

This is the most celebrated and most analyzed essay in the collection. Madison defines "faction" as a group of citizens "united and actuated by some common impulse of passion, or of interest, adverse to the rights of other citizens, or to the permanent and aggregate interests of the community."

He argues there are two ways to cure factionalism: destroy its causes (either by destroying liberty or by making all citizens have identical interests — both impossible and undesirable) or control its effects.

In a small republic, a majority faction can easily form and oppress minorities. In a large republic, the greater diversity of interests makes it harder for any one faction to form a majority; and the larger electoral districts will — in theory — filter out demagogues and elevate statesmen of broader vision.

This is the direct inversion of Montesquieu and the Anti-Federalists: size is a *benefit*, not a liability, for republican government. The extended republic becomes a self-correcting machine.

*Scholarly note:* Douglass Adair (1943) demonstrated that this argument derives from David Hume's "Idea of a Perfect Commonwealth." Robert Dahl (*A Preface to Democratic Theory*, 1956) offered a landmark critical analysis, arguing that Madison's "extended republic" was less a theory of democracy than a theory of how to prevent majorities from acting — a conservative brake on popular government. More recently, Larry Kramer (*The People Themselves*, 2004) argues that Madison's design was meant to work alongside popular constitutionalism, not replace it.

---

## No. 11 — Hamilton
### *The Utility of the Union in Respect to Commercial Relations and a Navy*

Hamilton argues for the commercial benefits of union: a unified America can negotiate trade treaties with foreign powers from a position of strength (threatening retaliation against discriminatory tariffs), maintain a common navigation policy, and build a navy that will eventually allow America to tip the balance of power in the Western Hemisphere away from European colonial powers.

He invokes the "superiority of American genius" and envisions America as an eventually dominant Atlantic commercial power — a strikingly ambitious nationalistic vision.

*Response to the Articles:* Under the Articles, Congress had no power to regulate foreign commerce, and states enacted competing, inconsistent trade policies.

---

## No. 12 — Hamilton
### *The Utility of the Union in Respect to Revenue*

Commercial prosperity generates tax revenue; tax revenue funds government; adequate government revenue is the prerequisite of national defense and effective administration. Hamilton argues that union maximizes commerce and thus maximizes the revenue available to fund federal government.

He also makes a practical argument about tax collection: import duties, the primary source of federal revenue, are far easier to collect at a few coastal ports under a unified customs regime than under thirteen separate systems with thousands of miles of land borders between them.

---

## No. 13 — Hamilton
### *Advantage of the Union in Respect to Economy in Government*

A single national government is cheaper than multiple confederacies. The fixed costs of governance — a navy, a diplomatic corps, a judicial system — are largely the same whether shared among one nation or duplicated across three or four confederacies. Hamilton estimates that three confederacies would cost nearly as much as the current union, but with far less combined power.

---

## No. 14 — Madison
### *Objections to the Proposed Constitution from Extent of Territory Answered*

Madison directly refutes the Montesquieu argument that a large republic is ungovernable by distinguishing between a *democracy* (where citizens govern directly, requiring small territory) and a *republic* (where citizens govern through representatives, which can function over a large territory). America is a republic, not a democracy; its representatives can travel to a central legislature; and the actual distances involved, while large, are manageable.

He ends with an eloquent patriotic peroration: America should not timidly stick to inherited models of government but should, as a new people in a new world, "rear the fabrics of governments which have no model on the face of the globe."

---

## No. 15 — Hamilton
### *The Insufficiency of the Present Confederation to Preserve the Union*

Hamilton launches the sustained direct attack on the Articles. The core defect: the Articles operate on *states*, not on *individuals*. A government that can only command states must use force against states when they disobey — which means civil war. The only solution is a government that reaches individuals directly, commanding and coercing them through laws applied by courts.

He catalogs the specific failures of the Articles: unpaid debts, violated treaties, deteriorating military, national disgrace. "We have neither troops, nor treasury, nor government."

*This essay is the pivot of the entire collection.* Everything before it argues for the value of union; everything after argues for the specific constitutional mechanisms that will make union effective.

---

## No. 16 — Hamilton
### *The Same Subject Continued: The Insufficiency of the Present Confederation to Preserve the Union*

Hamilton develops the argument of No. 15: a government that operates on states must ultimately resort to military coercion of recalcitrant states — which is indistinguishable from war. But military coercion among states is incompatible with a union of equals. Therefore any government that operates on states rather than individuals must collapse into either anarchy or military dominance of the weak by the strong. The only alternative is a government of laws binding individuals.

---

## No. 17 — Hamilton
### *The Same Subject Continued: The Insufficiency of the Present Confederation to Preserve the Union*

Hamilton addresses the Anti-Federalist fear that the new federal government will encroach on the states. He argues that the danger of usurpation runs the other way: local attachments are stronger than federal ones; citizens feel more connected to their states than to a distant federal government; state officials will always have greater influence over the people than federal ones.

This argument will be revisited and elaborated in *No. 45* by Madison. It is, in retrospect, one of the predictions of *Publius* that has proved least durable.

---

## No. 18 — Madison and Hamilton
### *The Same Subject Continued: The Insufficiency of the Present Confederation to Preserve the Union*

The Amphictyonic League of ancient Greece is examined as a case study. Despite its role as a religious and political confederacy, it was constantly paralyzed by the interventions of dominant member-states (especially Macedon) and the manipulation of its deliberative processes by powerful factions. The lesson: confederacies without adequate central authority are prey to both internal faction and external domination.

---

## No. 19 — Madison and Hamilton
### *The Same Subject Continued: The Insufficiency of the Present Confederation to Preserve the Union*

The German Empire (Holy Roman Empire) and the Polish republic are examined. Germany's Imperial Diet was perpetually gridlocked, its princes effectively sovereign, its territory repeatedly ravaged by foreign armies. Poland's *liberum veto* — which allowed any single member of the Diet to block legislation and dissolve the body — had made it ungovernable and easy prey for its neighbors (it had been partitioned in 1772 and 1793). The parallel to the Articles' unanimity requirement is direct.

---

## No. 20 — Madison and Hamilton
### *The Same Subject Continued: The Insufficiency of the Present Confederation to Preserve the Union*

The United Provinces of the Netherlands (the Dutch Republic) are examined. Despite commercial prosperity, the Dutch confederacy was perpetually riven by conflict between the Stadtholders and provincial authorities, required unanimous consent for major decisions, and had been repeatedly saved from collapse only by foreign intervention or internal military coups. The lesson: no confederacy has solved the problem of how to act decisively when member-states disagree.

*Scholarly note:* These three historical essays (18–20) represent *Publius* at its most empirical — an early attempt at comparative constitutional history. Gordon Wood (*The Creation of the American Republic*, 1969) notes that the framers were intensely engaged with classical and modern history as laboratories for political science.

---

## No. 21 — Hamilton
### *Other Defects of the Present Confederation*

Hamilton catalogs three additional defects of the Articles: (1) no power of sanction — there is no penalty for state noncompliance; (2) no power to raise revenue by taxation; (3) no regulation of commerce. He also notes the absurdity of apportioning requisitions by land value, which no one could accurately assess.

---

## No. 22 — Hamilton
### *The Same Subject Continued: Other Defects of the Present Confederation*

More defects: (1) no federal power to raise armies — states could withhold troops; (2) no power over foreign commerce, leaving American merchants vulnerable to discrimination; (3) equality of state votes in Congress regardless of population — tiny states had the same weight as large ones; (4) the unanimity requirement for amendment, which gave any single state veto power.

Hamilton closes with a powerful statement about democratic legitimacy: the Constitution, unlike the Articles, will derive its authority from "the pure, original fountain of all legitimate authority" — the people themselves, not the state legislatures.

---

## No. 23 — Hamilton
### *The Necessity of a Government as Energetic as the One Proposed to the Attainment of the National Ends*

Hamilton argues that the powers granted to the federal government must be proportionate to the ends it is designed to achieve. If the ends include national defense, the common welfare, and regulation of commerce, then the means must be adequate to those ends — which requires the broad powers the Constitution grants. To grant the ends but withhold the means is a contradiction.

This essay introduces the logic that will underlie the "necessary and proper" clause argument in *No. 33* and *No. 44*.

---

## No. 24 — Hamilton
### *The Powers Necessary to the Common Defense Further Considered*

Hamilton defends the absence of a constitutional prohibition on standing armies in peacetime. The Anti-Federalists demanded such a prohibition. Hamilton argues: (1) state constitutions only two of thirteen had such prohibitions; (2) the danger of foreign threats in North America makes some standing force necessary; (3) Congress controls military appropriations (the two-year limit on army appropriations is the key safeguard, not a categorical ban).

---

## No. 25 — Hamilton
### *The Same Subject Continued: The Powers Necessary to the Common Defense Further Considered*

Hamilton argues that it is neither practical nor constitutional to restrict military power to the states. If the federal government cannot maintain standing forces, it must rely on state militias — which are unreliable, inconsistently trained, and subject to state rather than national command. Moreover, confining the war power to states simply shifts the danger: states would maintain their own armies, more likely to be turned against each other than against foreign threats.

---

## No. 26 — Hamilton
### *The Same Subject Continued: The Idea of Restraining the Legislative Authority in Regard to the Common Defense Considered*

Hamilton argues that legislative oversight — specifically, the two-year limit on military appropriations — is a more reliable safeguard against military tyranny than constitutional prohibitions. The legislature, elected by the people and jealous of its own authority, will scrutinize military spending and resist executive overreach. Parchment prohibitions, by contrast, are easily circumvented.

*Scholarly note:* This argument is important for separation of powers analysis: Hamilton here emphasizes Congress's role as a structural check on military power.

---

## No. 27 — Hamilton
### *The Same Subject Continued: The Idea of Restraining the Legislative Authority in Regard to the Common Defense Considered*

Hamilton anticipates the concern that the federal government will be a distant, alien entity with no hold on popular loyalty. He argues that as citizens interact with federal officers — paying taxes, sitting on juries, serving in the federal military — they will develop loyalty to the federal government just as they have to their states. Over time, federal affection will grow.

He also argues that because state officials swear oaths to the Constitution, they effectively become agents of the federal system, extending its reach into every community.

---

## No. 28 — Hamilton
### *The Same Subject Continued: The Idea of Restraining the Legislative Authority in Regard to the Common Defense Further Considered*

If the federal government becomes tyrannical, Hamilton argues, the states provide a counterweight — and ultimately, the people can rise in resistance. This essay is important because it explicitly contemplates the possibility of federal overreach and identifies *the states* as a structural check. It supports a vision of federalism as a mutual checking system, not a hierarchy.

*Scholarly note:* This essay is often cited in discussions of the Second Amendment's potential relationship to resistance to tyranny — though Hamilton's argument is structural and political, not specifically about individual gun rights.

---

## No. 29 — Hamilton
### *Concerning the Militia*

Hamilton defends the constitutional arrangement for the militia: Congress has the power to organize, arm, and discipline it, but states retain the appointment of officers and training responsibility. A fully nationalized militia would be impractical. A select corps of well-trained militia is preferable to either an undertrained mass militia or a large standing army.

---

## No. 30 — Hamilton
### *Concerning the General Power of Taxation*

Hamilton argues that the federal government must have "an unqualified power of taxation" — the power to impose any kind of tax on any subject. The Anti-Federalists argued for limiting federal taxation to import duties, leaving direct taxes to states. Hamilton responds: national needs are inherently unlimited (especially in wartime); tying national revenue to a single source is dangerous; and the experience of the Articles demonstrates that requisition systems do not work.

---

## No. 31 — Hamilton
### *The Same Subject Continued: Concerning the General Power of Taxation*

Hamilton defends the proposition that the principles of taxation follow from clear axioms of political theory, just as geometric theorems follow from axioms. He briefly addresses the fear that concurrent state and federal taxation will be oppressive, arguing that competition between the two levels of government for the same tax base is a feature, not a bug — each level will restrain the other's excesses.

---

## No. 32 — Hamilton
### *The Same Subject Continued: Concerning the General Power of Taxation*

Hamilton makes a technical constitutional argument: the federal government's taxation power is *concurrent* with the states', not *exclusive*. The Constitution only deprives states of the exclusive power over imports and exports; it does not strip them of all taxation authority. This is important for maintaining the dual-sovereignty structure of federalism.

---

## No. 33 — Hamilton
### *The Same Subject Continued: Concerning the General Power of Taxation*

Hamilton defends the necessary and proper clause and the supremacy clause against Anti-Federalist attacks. He argues that both clauses simply make explicit what would be implied anyway: a government with certain powers must have the means to execute them, and the laws of a supreme government must be superior to inconsistent state laws. "Without the SUBSTANCE of this power, the whole Constitution would be a dead letter."

*Scholarly note:* The necessary and proper clause debate is central to the Hamilton-Madison split of the 1790s. Hamilton's broad reading — authorizing any law that is convenient and useful to executing federal powers — was contested by Madison and Jefferson, who argued it must be *essential*, not merely *helpful*.

---

## No. 34 — Hamilton
### *The Same Subject Continued: Concerning the General Power of Taxation*

Hamilton responds to the objection that concurrent federal and state taxation will lead to conflict, noting that in the Roman Republic both consuls and tribunes had overlapping authority without the system collapsing. He argues that the prospect of federal and state tax conflicts is speculative, while the demonstrated failure of the Articles' requisition system is concrete.

---

## No. 35 — Hamilton
### *The Same Subject Continued: Concerning the General Power of Taxation*

Hamilton addresses the question of representation in taxation: will Congress, dominated by merchants and lawyers, adequately represent the interests of farmers and manufacturers? He argues that merchants understand commercial interests broadly (including agriculture, since they deal in agricultural products), that lawyers understand all interests (since they represent all clients), and that the population will naturally elect those with the expertise to legislate on complex economic matters.

*Scholarly note:* This essay reveals the elite, Whig character of Hamilton's political theory — he expects deference to trained experts rather than direct popular control. Anti-Federalists like "Federal Farmer" sharply criticized this conception of representation.

---

## No. 36 — Hamilton
### *The Same Subject Continued: Concerning the General Power of Taxation*

Hamilton concludes the taxation argument by addressing the concern that Congress will use its tax power to displace state tax authority entirely. He argues that federal revenue needs will be satisfied primarily by import duties; direct taxes will be used only in emergencies; and in any emergency, the states' and federal governments' interests in raising revenue will be aligned, not competitive.

---

## No. 37 — Madison
### *Concerning the Difficulties Which the Convention Must Have Experienced in the Formation of a Proper Plan*

This transitional essay begins the analysis of the specific constitutional structure. Madison asks readers to appreciate the difficulty of the framers' task: they had to balance energy with republican liberty, stability with republican representation, federal authority with state autonomy, and define the boundaries between branches — all without adequate models or precedents.

He makes an important epistemological point: language is inherently imprecise; even the clearest constitutional text will generate disputes at the margins. This is a subtle argument *against* strict constructionism — the text cannot be self-executing.

*Scholarly note:* This essay is important for those, like Lawrence Lessig, who argue that constitutional interpretation must be purposive and adaptive rather than purely textual.

---

## No. 38 — Madison
### *The Same Subject Continued, and the Incoherence of the Objections to the New Plan Exposed*

Madison catalogs the Anti-Federalist objections to the Constitution and argues they are mutually contradictory: some complain of too much federal power, others of too little; some object to the Senate being too strong, others to it being too weak; some want a stronger executive, others fear the existing one. A plan attacked from all sides simultaneously cannot be consistently wrong.

He also argues that even Solon, Lycurgus, and Numa — the legendary lawgivers of Athens, Sparta, and Rome — produced imperfect constitutions that required revision. The Constitution can be amended.

---

## No. 39 — Madison
### *Conformity of the Plan to Republican Principles*

This is one of Madison's most important essays. He provides a careful definition of a republic: a government that derives its powers from the great body of the people, administered by persons holding office at the pleasure of the people (directly or indirectly), for a limited term. He then argues that the Constitution satisfies this definition.

He also addresses the federal vs. national character of the new government — one of the most important theoretical questions in the document. The Constitution is a mixture: national in some respects (it operates on individuals, the House is elected by popular vote), federal in others (the Senate represents states equally, ratification was by state conventions, amendment requires state approval). This mixed character was deliberate and is defended as a virtue.

---

## No. 40 — Madison
### *The Powers of the Convention to Form a Mixed Government Examined and Sustained*

Madison defends the Convention against the charge of having exceeded its mandate. The delegates were commissioned only to revise the Articles, not to scrap them. Madison's response: (1) the instruction to "render the federal constitution adequate to the exigencies of government" was broad enough to encompass what they did; (2) even if they exceeded their formal commission, the people's right of self-governance authorized what they did; (3) they submitted their work to the people for ratification, not to the state legislatures, which is precisely what a democratic process requires.

---

## No. 41 — Madison
### *General View of the Powers Conferred by the Constitution*

Madison surveys the powers granted to Congress and argues that each is genuinely necessary. He focuses on the war powers, military powers, and treaty powers, arguing that a nation unable to defend itself or conduct foreign policy is not really a nation. He addresses the "general welfare" language and argues it is merely a description of the purposes of the enumerated powers that follow — not an independent, unlimited grant of power to pursue any public good.

*Scholarly note:* This is a key text in the continuing dispute over the scope of the spending power. Hamilton read "general welfare" as an independent grant of authority; Madison did not. Modern constitutional law has largely followed Hamilton.

---

## No. 42 — Madison
### *The Same Subject Continued, and the Powers Conferred by the Constitution Further Considered*

Madison discusses powers over foreign commerce, naturalization, bankruptcy, coinage, weights and measures, post offices, patents and copyrights, and the governance of the capital district. He defends each as genuinely necessary for a functional national government and notes the confusion that resulted under the Articles when these matters were handled inconsistently by thirteen different states.

---

## No. 43 — Madison
### *The Same Subject Continued, with a Review of the Powers Conferred by the Constitution*

Madison addresses the powers to define and punish piracy and felonies on the high seas, declare war, grant letters of marque, govern federal territories, guarantee republican government to states, suppress insurrections, and amend the Constitution. The guarantee clause and the amendment clause receive particular attention — the former protecting states against internal coups or monarchical usurpation, the latter providing a mechanism for peaceful constitutional change.

---

## No. 44 — Madison
### *Restrictions on the Authority of the Several States*

Madison defends the restrictions on states in Article I, Section 10 and discusses the necessary and proper clause in detail. His defense of the latter: if you want the ends (execute constitutional powers), you must accept the means; the only alternatives to the necessary and proper clause are to specify every possible implementing law (impossible) or to leave the Constitution powerless to adapt to unforeseen circumstances (dangerous).

He acknowledges the clause's breadth but argues that it is constrained by its purpose (executing enumerated powers), checked by Congress's political accountability to the people, and limited by the good sense of elected representatives.

---

## No. 45 — Madison
### *The Alleged Danger from the Powers of the Union to the State Governments Considered*

Madison directly addresses the Anti-Federalist fear that the states will be swallowed by the federal government. He argues that the federal government's powers are "few and defined," concerned only with external affairs (war, peace, negotiation, foreign commerce); while the states' powers are "numerous and indefinite," encompassing all the ordinary affairs of daily life. The states will remain closer to the people, command greater loyalty, and will always check federal overreach.

*Scholarly note:* This is one of the most-cited essays in the federalism debates, and also one where the predictive accuracy of *Publius* has been most contested. The expansion of federal power over the past two centuries — especially after the New Deal — suggests that Madison's structural prediction was overly optimistic.

---

## No. 46 — Madison
### *The Influence of the State and Federal Governments Compared*

Madison develops the argument of No. 45: not only do states have structural advantages, but citizens are more attached to their state governments, state officials are more numerous than federal ones, and any federal usurpation would face coordinated state resistance. If the federal government "should possess a military force...the State governments, with the people on their side, would be able to repel the danger."

He envisionsizes a kind of competitive federalism in which states and federal government check each other through political and institutional rivalry — a vision quite different from the cooperative federalism or federal supremacy models that have dominated in practice.

---

## No. 47 — Madison
### *The Particular Structure of the New Government and the Distribution of Power Among Its Different Parts*

Madison addresses the separation of powers principle, famously quoting Montesquieu: "there can be no liberty where the legislative and executive powers are united in the same person." But he argues that Montesquieu did not mean total separation — the British constitution, which Montesquieu admired, has extensive overlaps between branches. What Montesquieu meant was that the *whole* of one branch's power should not be in the hands of another.

He surveys state constitutions and finds that all of them mix powers to some degree — the question is whether the mixing is excessive and dangerous.

---

## No. 48 — Madison
### *These Departments Should Not Be So Far Separated as to Have No Constitutional Control Over Each Other*

Madison argues that *parchment barriers* — written constitutional statements of separation — are insufficient to maintain actual separation. What is needed are structural, institutional means. His key insight: the legislative branch is the most dangerous in a republic, because it draws its power directly from popular elections and can claim democratic legitimacy for expansions of its authority. Therefore the executive and judicial branches need real tools to resist legislative encroachment.

*Scholarly note:* This essay was written with Jefferson's experience as Virginia governor in mind — during the Revolutionary War, the Virginia legislature had effectively subordinated the governor, nearly destroying effective executive action.

---

## No. 49 — Madison
### *Method of Guarding Against the Encroachments of Any One Department of Government by Appealing to the People Through a Convention*

Madison considers Jefferson's proposal (from *Notes on the State of Virginia*) that constitutional disputes between branches be resolved by periodic popular conventions. He respectfully but firmly rejects it. Frequent appeals to the people would disturb the constitutional order, undermine respect for government, and be dominated by the legislative branch (which has the most connections with the people). Stability and reverence for the Constitution require that it not be constantly re-litigated.

---

## No. 50 — Madison
### *Periodic Appeals to the People Considered*

Madison continues the argument of No. 49, examining the Pennsylvania Council of Censors (1783–84) — a body established to review constitutional compliance every seven years — as a case study in why periodic popular conventions fail. The Council was dominated by the same factional conflicts it was supposed to adjudicate and became a forum for partisan politics rather than dispassionate constitutional review.

---

## No. 51 — Madison
### *The Structure of the Government Must Furnish the Proper Checks and Balances Between the Different Departments*

One of the two or three most important essays in the collection. Madison articulates the theory of structural constitutionalism: "Ambition must be made to counteract ambition." Each branch must be given the "necessary constitutional means and personal motives to resist the encroachments of the others."

He explains the double security: first, the separation of powers within the federal government; second, the federal-state division of power. Together, these create a "double security...to the rights of the people."

He also makes the famous argument about faction and justice: in a society with many different groups, no single faction can dominate, and weak factions will combine against any group that attempts dominance — making justice the only stable equilibrium.

*Scholarly note:* This essay is the theoretical core of the entire project. Robert Dahl, David Truman, and pluralist political scientists of the 1950s–60s used it as the theoretical foundation of their account of American democracy. But critics note that Madison's vision assumes rough equality among competing factions — an assumption that organized wealth and political inequality continuously undermine.

---

## No. 52 — Madison
### *The House of Representatives — Qualifications of Members*

Madison begins the detailed examination of the House of Representatives. He defends the biennial election cycle (Anti-Federalists wanted annual elections) and the minimal constitutional qualifications for members and voters. He argues that broad suffrage — using whatever qualifications each state uses for its most popular legislative chamber — is the appropriate republican standard.

---

## No. 53 — Madison
### *The Same Subject Continued: The House of Representatives*

Madison defends two-year terms over one-year terms. Representatives need time to learn federal business — the laws of other states, foreign affairs, commerce — which is far more complex than local legislation. Annual rotation would mean perpetual ignorance in the legislature; biennial terms allow for competence while still ensuring frequent accountability to voters.

---

## No. 54 — Madison
### *The Apportionment of Members Among the States*

Madison addresses the Three-Fifths Compromise — one of the Constitution's most notorious provisions — in what is considered one of the most uncomfortable essays in the collection. He acknowledges the awkwardness of treating enslaved people as both property (not citizens, not voters) and as partial persons for apportionment purposes, and offers a convoluted defense: enslaved people are persons in the sense that criminal law applies to them, so some recognition of their personhood in the apportionment is appropriate.

*Scholarly note:* Most modern scholars regard this essay as an intellectual failure — an attempt to rationalize an indefensible compromise that gave slaveholding states extra representation for their enslaved population without giving those people any political rights. Paul Finkelman (*Slavery and the Founders*, 1996) is among the scholars who have analyzed this most critically.

---

## No. 55 — Madison
### *The Total Number of the House of Representatives*

Anti-Federalists complained that 65 representatives (the initial House size) were far too few to represent millions of Americans. Madison argues that the key is not the absolute number but whether the representatives possess sufficient information, confidence, and integrity. Small deliberative bodies can function better than large, unwieldy ones; the number will grow as population grows; and state legislatures will provide additional representative capacity.

---

## No. 56 — Madison
### *The Same Subject Continued: The Total Number of the House of Representatives*

Madison continues the defense of the House's size, arguing that federal representatives need only understand broad national interests — taxation, commerce, military affairs — not the particular local details that state legislators must master. A relatively small body is adequate for federal purposes.

---

## No. 57 — Madison
### *The Alleged Tendency of the New Plan to Elevate the Few at the Expense of the Many Considered in Connection with Representation*

Madison argues that the House will not be an aristocratic body of the wealthy and well-connected. The broad suffrage requirements and the competitive nature of elections will elevate men of genuine distinction — not of birth or wealth — and keep them accountable. Representatives must win elections every two years; they will be replaced by a "constitutional road to the preferment of merit" that is open to all.

*Scholarly note:* This essay, read alongside No. 35, reveals a tension in *Publius*: in No. 35, Hamilton argues that merchants and lawyers will and should dominate Congress; in No. 57, Madison argues that any citizen of merit can rise. This reflects real differences between Hamilton's more elite vision and Madison's more democratic one.

---

## No. 58 — Madison
### *Objection That the Number of Members Will Not Be Augmented as the Progress of Population Demands Considered*

Madison addresses the concern that Congress will not increase House seats as population grows. He argues that population growth naturally increases the political power of more populous states, who will demand reapportionment; and that the constitutional requirement of a decennial census and reapportionment creates a structural mechanism for keeping representation proportional.

---

## No. 59 — Hamilton
### *Concerning the Power of Congress to Regulate the Election of Its Members*

Hamilton defends Congress's power to regulate federal elections (Article I, Section 4). The Anti-Federalists feared that Congress would use this power to manipulate elections in its own favor. Hamilton argues: (1) some authority must have this power; (2) state control is more dangerous (a hostile state could simply refuse to hold elections for federal offices); (3) a national body has less incentive to gerrymander than a state body.

---

## No. 60 — Hamilton
### *The Same Subject Continued: Concerning the Power of Congress to Regulate the Election of Its Members*

Hamilton argues that Congress is unlikely to abuse its election-regulation power because the diverse interests represented in Congress — large states vs. small states, commercial vs. agricultural interests, northern vs. southern — would make any self-serving manipulation of election rules impossible to achieve as a majority coalition.

---

## No. 61 — Hamilton
### *The Same Subject Continued: Concerning the Power of Congress to Regulate the Election of Its Members*

Hamilton concludes the election-regulation argument by noting that the Constitution already requires elections to be held in every state, prohibits Congress from requiring that Senators be chosen by specific localities, and that any attempted abuse would generate immediate political resistance.

---

## No. 62 — Madison
### *The Senate*

Madison begins the analysis of the Senate, addressing its composition (two senators per state, regardless of population), the qualifications of senators (age 30, nine years citizenship), and the mode of election (by state legislatures — the original provision, before the Seventeenth Amendment). He defends equal state representation as a necessary compromise between large and small states and argues it has an additional benefit: it gives each state equal weight in treaty ratification and provides a conservative check on hasty legislation.

He defends six-year terms as providing stability, institutional memory, and the ability to pursue long-term policy goals — Senate as a "temperate and respectable body of citizens."

---

## No. 63 — Madison
### *The Senate Continued*

Madison addresses the lack of a direct democratic mandate for the Senate (it is elected by state legislatures, not directly by the people). He argues that a body with some insulation from immediate popular passions is actually necessary in a republic — it provides a stable, long-serving counterweight to the more volatile House. He invokes the Roman Senate as a historical model of how a stable deliberative body can preserve republican liberty against both popular passion and executive tyranny.

He also addresses the accountability concern: the Senate is still answerable to state legislatures, which are themselves answerable to the people.

---

## No. 64 — Jay
### *The Powers of the Senate*

Jay defends the Senate's role in treaty-making. The treaty power requires secrecy, dispatch, and expertise in foreign affairs — qualities better found in a small, experienced, long-serving body than in the full legislature or the people at large. He argues that the President and Senate together constitute an appropriate treaty-making authority.

---

## No. 65 — Hamilton
### *The Powers of the Senate Continued*

Hamilton defends the Senate as the court of impeachment. He acknowledges this is an unusual arrangement — the Senate will be judging officers who were, in many cases, appointed with its advice and consent. But he argues there is no perfect institutional solution: the Supreme Court lacks the political standing; a special tribunal could not be constituted; the Senate, despite its partial entanglement with executive officers, is the most appropriate available body because of its size, deliberative character, and insulation from direct electoral pressure.

---

## No. 66 — Hamilton
### *Objections to the Power of the Senate to Sit as a Court for Impeachments Further Considered*

Hamilton continues the defense of Senate impeachment, addressing specific objections: that the Senate will be biased by its role in advice and consent; that it will be judges in their own cause (by judging officers whom Senators may have confirmed); that giving both legislative and judicial power to the Senate violates separation of powers. He argues each objection is overstated and that practical necessity overrides theoretical purity.

---

## No. 67 — Hamilton
### *The Executive Department*

Hamilton begins the analysis of the presidency by taking aim at what he considers a gross distortion by Anti-Federalists: the claim that the President resembles an elective king. He notes that the President's power to fill Senate vacancies during recesses is the specific power most attacked, and he corrects the Anti-Federalist misreading of the relevant constitutional text.

---

## No. 68 — Hamilton
### *The Mode of Electing the President*

Hamilton defends the Electoral College as an elegant solution to a difficult problem: how to select a president who possesses the knowledge and judgment to exercise executive power, while remaining accountable to the people. The electors — chosen by the people, meeting separately in each state, existing only for this purpose — will deliberate without corruption or intrigue; they will be knowledgeable; and the system prevents any one faction from seizing the presidency by surprise.

*Scholarly note:* Hamilton's defense of the Electoral College is historically ironic given how differently it has functioned in practice — dominated by parties, winner-take-all state rules, and now the possibility of faithless electors — from his description.

---

## No. 69 — Hamilton
### *The Real Character of the Executive*

Hamilton directly compares the constitutional president to the British king and to the New York governor. The President: serves a four-year term; can be removed by impeachment; cannot dissolve Congress; has a conditional (not absolute) veto; is commander-in-chief but cannot declare war; can make treaties only with Senate concurrence; and can appoint officers only with Senate consent. The British king is hereditary, irremovable, not subject to impeachment, has an absolute veto, can declare war unilaterally, and can make appointments without parliamentary approval.

*Scholarly note:* This essay is crucial for the unitary executive debate. Hamilton's careful enumeration of the President's limited powers, especially compared to the king, suggests he did not envision the expansive presidential prerogative that later theorists have claimed.

---

## No. 70 — Hamilton
### *The Executive Department Further Considered*

The famous essay on executive "energy." Hamilton argues that energy in the executive is essential to good government — essential to protect the community from external threats and to the steady administration of laws. Energy requires: unity (a single executive, not a council); duration (adequate length of term); adequate powers; adequate support (salary). Unity is especially important: multiple executives cannot be held accountable, tend toward mutual obstruction, and weaken executive authority by constant internal disagreement.

He argues for a single president rather than an executive council or committee, comparing the proposed Constitution favorably to both the Roman dual consulship (where both consuls had to agree) and executive councils of contemporary state governments (which diluted accountability and weakened decision-making).

*Scholarly note:* Scholars like Steven Calabresi and Christopher Yoo (*The Unitary Executive*, 2008) heavily cite this essay for the unitary executive theory. Critics like Peter Shane (*Madison's Nightmare*, 2009) and Jon Michaels (*Constitutional Coup*, 2017) argue that Hamilton's argument was about the choice between a single executive and an executive council — not about whether Congress could structure executive agencies with independent leadership. The latter question was simply not raised in No. 70.

---

## No. 71 — Hamilton
### *The Duration in Office of the Executive*

Hamilton argues for an adequate presidential term — long enough to allow policy implementation, learning from experience, and the development of a stable administration. A president who knows they face imminent departure has reduced incentive to act boldly or develop long-term plans. He defends reeligibility: the prospect of reelection gives the president an incentive to govern well, and it would be wasteful to constitutionally exclude a good president from continued service.

---

## No. 72 — Hamilton
### *The Same Subject Continued, and Re-eligibility of the Executive Considered*

Hamilton continues the defense of presidential reeligibility against arguments for mandatory term limits. He anticipates the objections — that eligible re-election will corrupt the president, who will manipulate power to ensure victory — and responds that term limits would make lame-duck presidents dangerously irresponsible, that presidents would have less incentive for careful administration if they knew departure was inevitable, and that excluding good presidents from re-service wastes talent.

---

## No. 73 — Hamilton
### *The Provision for the Support of the Executive, and the Veto Power*

Hamilton defends the president's fixed, unchangeable salary (Congress cannot reduce or increase it during a term) and the qualified veto. The salary provision ensures executive independence from legislative pressure. The veto: (1) gives the president a constitutional tool to defend against legislative encroachments on executive prerogatives; (2) provides a check on hasty or unjust legislation; (3) requires a two-thirds supermajority to override — sufficient for legitimate correction but high enough to prevent legislative dominance.

---

## No. 74 — Hamilton
### *The Command of the Military Forces and the Pardoning Power of the Executive*

Hamilton defends presidential command of the military (as civilian commander-in-chief) and the pardon power. On the military: civilian command is necessary for unity of command and civil supremacy. On pardons: justice sometimes requires mercy that rigid law cannot supply; and in cases of rebellion, the executive's pardon power can end a crisis without prolonged punishment.

---

## No. 75 — Hamilton
### *The Treaty-Making Power of the Executive*

Hamilton defends the treaty power: shared between President (who negotiates) and Senate (which ratifies with two-thirds majority). He argues against lodging the treaty power solely in the legislature (too slow, too partisan, too prone to factional manipulation) or solely in the executive (too dangerous a concentration of power). The combination of presidential negotiating skill and senatorial deliberation and accountability is the optimal arrangement.

---

## No. 76 — Hamilton
### *The Appointing Power of the Executive*

Hamilton defends presidential nomination with Senate confirmation for major officers (ambassadors, judges, department heads). A single nominator is more accountable than a committee; presidential nominations will be made on merit because the president's reputation depends on the quality of his administration; and Senate confirmation provides a check on obviously unfit nominations without micromanaging executive appointments.

---

## No. 77 — Hamilton
### *The Appointing Power Continued and Other Powers of the Executive Considered*

Hamilton addresses whether the Senate must consent to *removals* as well as appointments. He argues — here taking a position he would later abandon in the "Decision of 1789" — that Senate consent *is* required for removals, because the same body that advised on an appointment should have a voice in its reversal.

*Scholarly note:* This is one of the most important passages in the Papers for executive power debates. Hamilton's position here — that removal requires Senate consent — is directly contrary to the unitary executive theory's claim that the president has unilateral removal power over all executive officers. Hamilton himself later changed his view.

---

## No. 78 — Hamilton
### *The Judiciary Department*

One of the great essays. Hamilton defends the judiciary: lifetime tenure during good behavior (to ensure independence), and — crucially — the power of courts to declare legislation unconstitutional. He argues that the Constitution is a superior form of law enacted by the people; that ordinary legislation cannot override it; and that courts, as interpreters of law, must give effect to the superior law. This is the foundational constitutional argument for what would become *Marbury v. Madison* (1803).

He also argues that the judiciary is the "least dangerous branch" — it has neither the sword (executive) nor the purse (legislative), only judgment.

*Scholarly note:* Raoul Berger (*Congress v. The Supreme Court*, 1969) argued that Hamilton's argument in No. 78 did not necessarily endorse strong judicial review of the scope modern courts exercise. Others, like Saikrishna Prakash and John Yoo, have argued Hamilton envisioned a more muscular judicial role. The essay has been read to support both judicial restraint and judicial activism depending on how one reads the relationship between "superior law" and judicial judgment.

---

## No. 79 — Hamilton
### *The Judiciary Continued*

Hamilton addresses judicial tenure (during good behavior — effectively lifetime) and compensation (cannot be reduced during tenure, providing independence from Congress). He defends these provisions as necessary for judicial independence and compares the proposed federal judiciary favorably to British judicial arrangements.

---

## No. 80 — Hamilton
### *The Powers of the Judiciary*

Hamilton argues that the federal judicial power must extend to all cases arising under the Constitution, federal laws, and treaties; to admiralty and maritime cases; to controversies between states and between citizens of different states; and to cases affecting foreign diplomats. Each category of jurisdiction corresponds to a genuine need for a neutral federal adjudicator.

He specifically defends federal jurisdiction over state-citizen controversies: states cannot be impartial judges in disputes where they are parties.

---

## No. 81 — Hamilton
### *The Judiciary Continued, and the Distribution of the Judicial Authority*

Hamilton defends the structure of the federal judicial system: a Supreme Court with appellate jurisdiction over all federal questions, and inferior federal courts established by Congress. He addresses the concern that the Supreme Court's appellate jurisdiction over both law and fact will undermine jury trial rights, and responds that Congress can regulate the scope of appellate jurisdiction by statute.

He also addresses state court jurisdiction over federal questions: state courts can hear federal cases in the first instance, but federal appellate review is available to ensure uniform interpretation.

---

## No. 82 — Hamilton
### *The Judiciary Continued*

Hamilton addresses the relationship between federal and state courts, arguing for a concurrent jurisdiction model: state courts retain their general jurisdiction, including over federal questions that arise in their courts; the federal judicial system is overlaid on top of, not substituted for, state courts.

---

## No. 83 — Hamilton
### *The Judiciary Continued in Relation to Trial by Jury*

Hamilton's longest essay addresses one of the most politically sensitive Anti-Federalist objections: that the Constitution does not guarantee jury trials in civil cases. He argues: (1) the Constitution does guarantee jury trials in criminal cases (Article III); (2) the omission in civil cases reflects the complexity and variation of civil jury practice across states, not a hostility to juries; (3) Congress can establish jury rights in civil cases by statute; (4) the absence of a guarantee is not the same as a prohibition.

---

## No. 84 — Hamilton
### *Certain General and Miscellaneous Objections to the Constitution Considered and Answered*

Hamilton addresses the most important remaining objections, including the lack of a bill of rights. His argument against a bill of rights is one of the most controversial positions in the Papers: bills of rights historically constrained kings (Magna Carta, the English Bill of Rights) who wielded power the people had not granted; the American Constitution grants only enumerated powers to a government that has no authority to do what the bill of rights would prohibit. A bill of rights is therefore not only unnecessary but *dangerous* — listing rights implies that any rights not listed are not retained.

He also anticipates a slippery slope: a bill of rights could be construed to give the federal government implied powers to regulate the subjects it restricts.

*Scholarly note:* This argument failed to persuade the ratifying states — the promise of a bill of rights was essential to securing ratification in several states. The Ninth Amendment was specifically designed to address Hamilton's concern about implicit limitation. Today, Hamilton's argument is largely rejected in practice; the Bill of Rights is universally regarded as a foundation, not a limitation, of rights.

---

## No. 85 — Hamilton
### *Concluding Remarks*

Hamilton's conclusion makes the case for ratification on practical grounds: the Constitution is not perfect, but it is far better than the Articles; it can be amended; the opportunity to establish good government from reflection may not recur; and the specific objections raised by Anti-Federalists, while sometimes valid, do not justify rejection of the whole instrument. He invokes the authority of David Hume: in political institutions, one must weigh imperfections against realistic alternatives, not ideal ones.

He closes with a note of urgency: New York's ratification is necessary; delay risks the formation of a new union without New York, leaving New York isolated and vulnerable.

---

# PART THREE: THEMATIC ANALYSIS — THE PAPERS AS A RESPONSE TO ALTERNATIVE MODELS

## I. The Articles of Confederation: The Central Target

Virtually every essay in *The Federalist* responds, directly or indirectly, to the failures of the Articles. The specific deficiencies *Publius* catalogs are:

**Requisition finance** (Nos. 12, 21, 22, 30, 36): The Articles gave Congress no power to tax — only to request funds from states, which routinely declined. By 1787, the national debt was unpaid, soldiers were owed back wages, and creditors of the United States had been abandoned. Hamilton's entire discussion of federal taxation is a direct response to this failure.

**Commercial anarchy** (Nos. 7, 11, 42): States imposed tariffs against each other, competed for foreign trade, and negotiated conflicting arrangements with foreign powers. The inability to present a unified commercial front cost American merchants billions in discriminatory foreign duties. Madison's interest in the Commerce Clause was directly shaped by his experience watching Virginia and other states impose retaliatory trade restrictions on each other.

**Treaty violations** (Nos. 3, 22, 42, 64): The Articles gave Congress the power to make treaties but not to enforce them. States routinely violated the 1783 peace treaty — refusing to restore Loyalist property, blocking debt collection by British creditors — giving Britain its pretext for retaining northwestern forts and refusing to evacuate. Jay's essays on foreign affairs are direct responses to this failure.

**No executive, no judiciary** (Nos. 15, 16, 23, 47): The Articles concentrated all federal power in a single body — Congress — which had no executive department and no federal courts. Madison's discussion of separation of powers and the dangers of legislative dominance (Nos. 47–51) is informed by the experience of the Articles' congressional government, where everything ground to a halt because legislation, administration, and adjudication were fused in a single body with no independent power to act.

**The unanimity problem** (Nos. 19, 22, 38): The Articles required unanimous state consent for amendment. Every reform effort — including giving Congress a 5% import duty — was blocked by a single state (Rhode Island). Hamilton's discussion of the Polish liberum veto is not an antiquarian diversion but a direct analogy.

**Military weakness** (Nos. 8, 23, 24, 25): The Articles gave Congress the power to raise armies but only by requisitioning troops from states. Shays' Rebellion (1786–87) — a debtor revolt in Massachusetts — had to be suppressed by a privately funded state militia because there was no federal army. Washington's Continental Army had been perpetually undermanned and undersupplied for the same structural reasons.

---

## II. The Classical Confederacies: Historical Cautionary Tales

*Publius* uses ancient history as empirical evidence, drawing on Polybius, Thucydides, Livy, and the historical tradition to demonstrate that confederate forms of government have a consistent failure mode: they cannot act, they are dominated by their strongest members, and they collapse into either tyranny or anarchy.

**The Amphictyonic League** (No. 18): Greece's religious and political confederacy was manipulated by powerful members (especially Philip of Macedon), incapable of collective defense, and a vehicle for strong-state imperialism rather than genuine collective governance.

**The Achaean League** (No. 18): A more successful model — closer to a genuine federal republic — but still ultimately dependent on the protection of Rome and unable to maintain independence. For *Publius*, even the best ancient confederacy was insufficient.

**The Lycian Confederacy**: Cited by Madison (No. 9, drawing on Montesquieu) as an example of a reasonably successful confederacy, but one that worked precisely because it had proportional voting and some real federal authority — closer to the proposed Constitution than to the Articles.

**Rome**: Used throughout as both model and warning. The Roman Senate as a stable, long-serving deliberative body (Nos. 62, 63); Roman consular government as an example of the problems of divided executive authority (No. 70); Roman tribunes and consuls as an example of overlapping authority that can function without systemic collapse (No. 34).

---

## III. Contemporary European Models

**The British Constitution** is the closest thing to an approved model in *The Federalist*, but *Publius* consistently argues that the American republic improves upon it. The British system has a hereditary monarch (No. 69's entire comparison is designed to show how different the American president is), an unrepresentative Parliament, and a lack of formal constitutional supremacy over ordinary legislation.

Hamilton in particular (Nos. 9, 26) draws on Blackstone's account of the British constitution — separation of powers, independent judiciary, parliamentary control of finance — but strips it of its monarchical and aristocratic elements, producing a rationalized republican version.

**The German Empire** (No. 19): Cited as the supreme example of confederal failure. The Holy Roman Empire's diet was gridlocked, its princes sovereign in practice, and its territory a perpetual battlefield for foreign powers. Karl Friedrich's *The Holy Roman Empire* and other eighteenth-century accounts of German constitutional dysfunction were well-known to educated Americans.

**The Swiss Confederation** (Nos. 19, 20): Like Germany, cited as a cautionary tale. The Swiss cantons were perpetually quarrelsome, the confederation's diet ineffective, and Swiss stability owed more to geographic isolation and great-power indifference than to sound constitutional structure.

**The Dutch Republic** (No. 20): The most developed cautionary tale. The Dutch Republic was the closest contemporary analog to what the Articles created — a commercial republic, a confederacy of provinces, a leadership position (Stadtholder) that was not quite executive and not quite monarchy. Its internal conflicts between Orangist (stadtholderian) and Republican (States' Party) factions, its recurrent paralysis, and its eventual conquest by France in 1795 (just years after the Federalist Papers were written) validated *Publius*'s warnings.

---

## IV. Montesquieu: The Most Important Interlocutor

No single intellectual source receives more sustained engagement in *The Federalist* than Montesquieu, and the engagement is deeply ambivalent. *Publius* simultaneously cites Montesquieu as an authority on separation of powers and refutes his most famous constitutional claim.

Montesquieu's claim (*Spirit of the Laws*, Book VIII) that republican government is only suited to small territories was the single most powerful intellectual weapon in the Anti-Federalist arsenal. The Anti-Federalists — drawing directly on Montesquieu — argued that the scale of the proposed American republic made it necessarily tyrannical or necessarily ineffective.

Madison's response in Nos. 9, 10, and 14 is to turn Montesquieu's own argument against him. Montesquieu approved of confederate republics as a solution to the scale problem — and the American Constitution, *Publius* argues, is precisely such a confederate republic. Moreover (in No. 10), the extended republic argument reverses the small-republic preference: large scale *helps* republican government by multiplying the number of competing factions.

The scholarly consensus, established by Douglass Adair and elaborated by scholars including Paul Rahe (*Republics Ancient and Modern*, 1992), is that *Publius*'s inversion of Montesquieu was one of the most original intellectual contributions of the founding era.

---

## V. Locke, Social Contract, and the Legitimacy Argument

*Publius* does not cite Locke extensively by name, but Lockean premises permeate the collection. The most important is the idea that political authority derives from the consent of the governed — and that this consent must be express, not merely tacit.

This is the basis of Madison's argument in Nos. 39 and 40 that the Constitution, though it exceeded the Philadelphia Convention's formal mandate, was legitimate because it was submitted to the people for ratification by popular conventions. The people — not state legislatures — are the ultimate source of constitutional authority.

This Lockean foundation has important implications for constitutional interpretation. If the Constitution derives its authority from popular consent, and if that consent was expressed through a ratifying process, then the meaning of the Constitution must be understood in terms of what the ratifying public understood it to mean — not simply what the framers intended. This is Rakove's argument in *Original Meanings* and also the foundation for "original public meaning" as distinct from "original intent" originalism.

---

## VI. Hume and the Science of Politics

The most underappreciated intellectual source in *The Federalist* is David Hume. As Adair's 1943 article established, Madison's argument in *Federalist No. 10* derives directly from Hume's essay "Idea of a Perfect Commonwealth," in which Hume argued that a large, well-structured republic might be more stable than a small one because the greater diversity of interests would make it harder for any single faction to capture the government.

More broadly, *Publius* shares Hume's skepticism about purely normative or virtue-based theories of republican government. The classical republican tradition — and some Anti-Federalists — believed that republics required virtuous citizens and civic engagement to survive. *Publius* largely abandons this premise: the constitutional design should work even among self-interested actors. This is the meaning of the famous phrase in No. 51: "If men were angels, no government would be necessary."

This shift — from virtue-based to interest-based constitutional theory — is one of the most important intellectual contributions of the founding era, and it continues to structure debates about constitutional design. Gordon Wood (*The Creation of the American Republic*, 1969) traces this shift from classical republicanism to liberal constitutionalism as the central development of the Revolutionary period.

---

## VII. The Scholarly Debate: Was the Constitution Counter-Revolutionary?

A persistent line of scholarship, beginning with Charles Beard (*An Economic Interpretation of the Constitution*, 1913) and continuing through Merrill Jensen, Jackson Turner Main, and others, has argued that the Constitution was a conservative reaction to the more democratic state constitutions of the 1776–87 period — a counter-revolution against the populism of the Revolution.

Beard argued that the framers were primarily large creditors, land speculators, and merchants who stood to benefit from a stronger national government — one that would enforce debt payments, secure property rights, and create a stable commercial environment. Forrest McDonald (*We the People*, 1958) largely demolished Beard's specific economic claims through detailed analysis of framers' holdings, but the broader structural argument — that the Constitution deliberately insulated policy-making from popular majorities — remains influential.

Robert Dahl's critique of *Federalist No. 10* is in this tradition: Madison's "extended republic" was not a theory of democracy but a theory of how to prevent majority action. The multiplication of competing factions creates a *veto-heavy* system in which organized minorities can block popular majorities on almost any issue.

The counter-tradition, represented by scholars like Carol Berkin (*A Brilliant Solution*, 2002) and Richard Beeman (*Plain, Honest Men*, 2009), emphasizes the genuine idealism and intellectual seriousness of the framers, acknowledging their class interests while crediting their sincere commitment to republican self-government.

---

## VIII. The Living Constitution and the Papers

Against originalist uses of *The Federalist*, a tradition of living constitutionalism represented by scholars like David Strauss (*The Living Constitution*, 2010), Laurence Tribe (*American Constitutional Law*, 3rd ed., 2000), and Jack Balkin (*Living Originalism*, 2011) argues that the constitutional design *anticipated* evolutionary interpretation.

Evidence from *The Federalist* itself:
- Madison's acknowledgment in No. 37 that language is inherently imprecise and that constitutional text cannot be self-executing
- Madison's argument in No. 49 against frequent appeals to the founding for constitutional settlement
- Hamilton's argument in No. 78 that the Constitution is a framework for ongoing governance, not a complete legal code
- The Ninth Amendment's explicit protection of unenumerated rights
- The Necessary and Proper Clause's deliberate flexibility

The framers designed a constitution intended to endure across changing circumstances — and they knew, as Madison wrote in No. 37, that they could not foresee those circumstances with precision.

### A Note on Authorship Disputes

Twelve papers are disputed between Hamilton and Madison: Nos. 49–58, 62, and 63. Hamilton's list (submitted to his executor before he died in 1804) assigned these to himself. Madison, in his later years, claimed them. Modern computational and stylometric analysis (most recently by Glenn Fung and other scholars using statistical methods) has generally supported Madison's authorship of most of the disputed essays, and the consensus attribution now assigns the majority of disputed papers to Madison.
