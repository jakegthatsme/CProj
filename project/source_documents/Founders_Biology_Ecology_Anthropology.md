# The Founders' Incomplete Science
## Constitutional Theory Examined Through Modern Biology, Ecology, Anthropology, and Behavioral Science

---

# PREFACE: THE NATURE OF THE INQUIRY

The founders were, by the standards of their era, unusually sophisticated empiricists. They read history as a laboratory, drew conclusions from comparative constitutional analysis, and — especially Madison — attempted to derive the principles of political design from observation of human behavior across time and cultures. They explicitly invoked "the science of politics" and believed they were doing something analogous to what Newton had done for physics: identifying stable laws of political nature from empirical observation.

But the empirical resources available to them were radically incomplete. They had no genetics, no evolutionary biology, no neuroscience, no systematic anthropology, no ecology as a discipline, no behavioral economics, and no cognitive psychology. Their model of human nature was drawn from classical history, philosophical speculation (Locke, Hobbes, Hume, Rousseau), Christian theology, and the political experience of Western Europe. It was, by the standards of what we now know, a profoundly parochial and frequently wrong account of what human beings are, how they behave, how they organize themselves socially, and what kinds of institutions they can successfully sustain.

This document examines, systematically, which foundational conclusions of the constitutional project were built on assumptions that modern science has substantially revised, complicated, or falsified. The exercise is not to diminish the founders — it is to understand their work with clearer eyes, and to identify where the design they built may be systematically misaligned with the biological, psychological, and social realities it was meant to govern.

---

# PART ONE: THE RATIONAL ACTOR AND THE BEHAVIORAL REVOLUTION

## I. The Core Assumption: Homo Politicus as Rational Agent

The entire architecture of *The Federalist* rests on a specific model of human motivation. In No. 51, Madison writes: "If men were angels, no government would be necessary." The design assumes humans are neither angels (acting from pure altruism) nor demons (acting from pure destructive impulse), but rational self-interested actors whose ambitions can be predicted, channeled, and made to check each other.

Hamilton in No. 6 dismissed the "visionary" notion that commercial republics are naturally peaceful: "Has it not... invariably been found that momentary passions, and immediate interests, have a more active and imperious control over human conduct than general or remote considerations of policy, utility, or justice?" This is a reasonably sophisticated observation — immediate interests dominate long-term calculations.

But the Madisonian-Hamiltonian model of rational self-interest is far more coherent and predictable than the human beings it is meant to describe. It assumes:

- That actors can identify their interests accurately
- That they will pursue those interests consistently across contexts
- That institutional incentive structures will reliably shape behavior toward constitutional ends
- That "ambition counteracting ambition" is a stable equilibrium rather than an escalating arms race

Modern behavioral science has comprehensively revised each of these assumptions.

---

## II. Cognitive Biases and Constitutional Design

### The Availability Heuristic and Risk Assessment

Daniel Kahneman and Amos Tversky's foundational work on cognitive biases (*Judgment Under Uncertainty: Heuristics and Biases*, 1974; *Thinking, Fast and Slow*, 2011) established that human judgment systematically departs from rational calculation in predictable ways. The availability heuristic — the tendency to estimate probability based on how easily examples come to mind — means that vivid, recent, or emotionally salient threats are systematically overweighted while statistical risks are underweighted.

This directly affects constitutional reasoning. The founders' intense focus on standing armies, executive tyranny, and factional takeover was driven substantially by the availability of historical examples (the English Civil War, Cromwell's Protectorate, Caesar crossing the Rubicon) that were vivid and politically potent. The threats they did not design against as carefully — systemic economic inequality, environmental degradation, technological monopoly, slow-moving administrative corruption — were less available as historical examples and were correspondingly underweighted in constitutional design.

The result: a constitution with elaborate formal protections against the specific eighteenth-century failure modes (executive tyranny, religious establishment, general warrants, quartering of troops) and relatively weak structural defenses against the failure modes that actually challenged twentieth and twenty-first century governance.

### Loss Aversion and Constitutional Stability

Kahneman and Tversky also established that humans are approximately twice as sensitive to losses as to equivalent gains — "loss aversion." This has important constitutional implications that the founders did not anticipate.

Madison's theory in No. 51 assumes that institutional actors will rationally deploy their constitutional powers to resist encroachment. But loss aversion predicts something more specific: actors will fight much harder to recover powers they have lost than to prevent losing powers they currently hold. This asymmetry helps explain the ratchet effect in executive power — presidents fight fiercely to recover authority lost by predecessors, but congressional institutions are slower to resist losses than to restore them, because the loss of any specific power is diffuse across 535 members while the fight for its return is concentrated in the executive.

### Present Bias and Long-Term Constitutional Maintenance

The behavioral economics concept of "hyperbolic discounting" — the tendency to heavily discount future costs and benefits relative to immediate ones — creates a systematic problem for constitutional maintenance. Constitutional protections often impose immediate costs (slower decision-making, procedural requirements, protection of unpopular minorities) in exchange for long-term benefits (stable rule of law, protection against tyranny). Present-biased actors — which is what humans consistently are — will be perpetually tempted to accept constitutional erosion in exchange for immediate policy gains.

Madison in No. 49 was acutely aware of this: he argued against frequent constitutional revision precisely because he understood that immediate political pressures would overwhelm long-term constitutional considerations. But he had no systematic framework for understanding *why* this would be so persistent. The behavioral economics literature provides that framework: it is not merely weakness of will or political corruption but a deep feature of human temporal cognition.

## III. Social Identity Theory and the Failure of Faction Theory

### The Realist Assumptions About Faction

Madison's No. 10 theory of faction rests on an essentially economic model of human motivation: people divide into factions primarily based on material interests — "a landed interest, a manufacturing interest, a mercantile interest, a moneyed interest." The multiplicity of these interests in a large republic will prevent any single one from achieving dominant majority status.

This is partly correct but fundamentally incomplete. It misses the most powerful and persistent source of faction in human societies: *social identity*.

Henri Tajfel and John Turner's social identity theory (1979) and subsequent self-categorization theory established through experimental psychology what political anthropologists had long observed in the field: humans categorize themselves into groups based on criteria that may have nothing to do with material interest, and these identity-based groups generate strong in-group loyalty and out-group hostility spontaneously and persistently.

Tajfel's "minimal group paradigm" experiments — in which subjects were randomly assigned to groups based on trivial criteria (preference for Klee vs. Kandinsky paintings) and immediately showed in-group favoritism and out-group discrimination — demonstrated that the tendency toward factional behavior is not reducible to material interest. It is a deep feature of human social cognition.

### Tribalism as a Constitutional Challenge

The founders' model of faction assumed that competing material interests would divide society in cross-cutting ways — a farmer in Virginia would share some interests with a merchant in Massachusetts and oppose him on others — making it impossible to form stable majority coalitions. What Madison did not adequately account for was identity-based political sorting: the tendency of societies to divide into two or a small number of stable, comprehensive identity coalitions that align material, cultural, religious, and geographic interests into opposing camps.

This is exactly what the American party system has produced. The development of a stable two-party system organized around comprehensive identity coalitions — in which geography, religion, education, race, cultural preference, and economic interest are all correlated — creates exactly the factional dynamics Madison feared: majority factions (in the electoral sense) sufficiently large and unified to overwhelm minority rights, and minority factions sufficiently alienated and cohesive to reject the legitimacy of majority outcomes.

Jonathan Haidt's moral foundations theory (*The Righteous Mind*, 2012) provides a psychological underpinning: people's political judgments are driven primarily by intuition (Kahneman's "System 1" processing), not deliberation, and the moral intuitions that generate those judgments cluster into recognizable patterns (care/harm, fairness/cheating, loyalty/betrayal, authority/subversion, sanctity/degradation, liberty/oppression) that differ systematically between political orientations. Political disagreement is often not a failure of information or reasoning but a genuine difference in moral psychology — differences that are partially heritable and resistant to rational persuasion.

This means that Madison's hope that a large, diverse republic would produce pragmatic compromise among competing material interests was misplaced. A large, diverse republic may instead produce intensifying moral-identity conflict in which each side views the other not as a legitimate competitor for policy outcomes but as a fundamental threat to the social order.

## IV. The Neuroscience of Deliberation and Democratic Governance

### The Prefrontal Cortex and Democratic Ideals

Democratic governance, as the founders conceived it, requires citizens and representatives capable of: receiving information, evaluating it rationally, weighing long-term consequences against short-term gains, controlling emotional reactions, and reaching considered judgments through deliberation. This is essentially a description of what neuroscientists associate with prefrontal cortex function — the executive control system of the brain.

What neuroscience has established since the 1990s is that prefrontal cortex function is systematically undermined by precisely the conditions under which political judgment is most consequential:

**Stress:** Acute stress shifts information processing from the prefrontal cortex (deliberative) to the amygdala (threat-response), producing faster but less nuanced judgment, greater in-group/out-group discrimination, and more aggressive responses to perceived threat. Political crises — wars, economic collapses, pandemics — are exactly the conditions under which democratic deliberation is most needed and most neurologically impaired.

**Social threat:** The perception of social status threat or group identity threat activates the same neural systems as physical threat, producing the same degradation of deliberative capacity. Political campaigns that activate identity threat — making voters feel their group is under attack — systematically undermine the rational deliberation on which democratic governance depends.

**Information overload:** Working memory capacity is severely limited (approximately 7 ± 2 items; Miller 1956), and the information environment of modern governance vastly exceeds this capacity. Voters facing complex policy choices under information overload systematically fall back on heuristics — party identification, candidate likeability, racial or gender identity — rather than policy evaluation.

The founders designed a republic for citizens who would receive information through newspapers and deliberate through town meetings, county assemblies, and representative bodies. They had no conception of information environments in which citizens are simultaneously exposed to thousands of competing claims, sophisticated behavioral manipulation (modern political advertising, social media algorithms), and continuous status-threat messaging. The constitutional design's dependence on informed deliberative citizenship was built on a model of cognition that the modern information environment has made nearly impossible to sustain.

### Mirror Neurons, Empathy, and Representation

The discovery of mirror neurons (Rizzolatti et al., 1992) and subsequent research on neural empathy systems has complex implications for the Anti-Federalist representation argument. Genuine political empathy — the ability of a representative to accurately model and respond to constituents' interests and experiences — appears to depend on shared social experience, cultural familiarity, and direct social contact. Neurological research suggests that empathy across large social distance (geographic, cultural, economic) is systematically weaker than empathy within familiar social groups.

The Anti-Federalist "Federal Farmer" argued that representatives must "resemble" those they represent to govern well. Modern neuroscience provides a biological mechanism for this intuition: representational accuracy depends on shared experiential frameworks that activate common neural representations. A representative whose life experience, economic circumstances, and social environment are radically different from those of constituents faces not merely a political gap but a neurological one — a genuine incapacity for accurate modeling of others' interests and experiences.

# PART TWO: ANTHROPOLOGY AND THE STATE OF NATURE

## I. The Social Contract as History

### Hobbes, Locke, and the Pre-Political State

The entire theoretical foundation of the Constitution — and of Enlightenment political philosophy generally — rests on the concept of the "state of nature": a pre-political condition from which individuals voluntarily emerge to form political society through a social contract, surrendering some liberty in exchange for security and governance.

This is presented as a logical argument, not a historical one (Locke explicitly says it need not have actually happened), but it carries profound empirical assumptions about human nature and social organization:

- That the "natural" human condition is one of independent individuals
- That political society is a deliberate construction, not an organic development
- That rights and freedoms are pre-political, not social products
- That the appropriate baseline for political theory is the isolated, rights-bearing individual

Modern anthropology has comprehensively demolished each of these assumptions as descriptions of human social reality.

### Human Beings Are Obligatorily Social

Humans are not facultatively social — beings who could survive alone but choose to cooperate. We are *obligatorily* social — our biology, cognition, immune function, emotional regulation, and cognitive development all require sustained social embeddedness. The research of Louis Cozolino (*The Neuroscience of Human Relationships*, 2006), Matthew Lieberman (*Social: Why Our Brains Are Wired to Connect*, 2013), and John Cacioppo and William Patrick (*Loneliness*, 2008) establishes that:

- Social isolation is as physiologically damaging as smoking 15 cigarettes a day (Holt-Lunstad et al., 2015)
- The default mode network — the brain's "resting state" — is organized around social cognition, not individual self-reference
- Human cognitive development requires social scaffolding; isolated individuals do not develop full human cognitive capacity (feral child cases, extreme isolation studies)
- The human immune system is calibrated by social experience — chronic loneliness produces measurable changes in gene expression affecting immune function (Cole et al., 2007)

The "individual" of social contract theory — the pre-political rights-bearer who rationally consents to governance — is a biological fiction. There is no pre-social human individual. The isolated, rights-bearing person is an artifact of social life, not its precondition.

This has profound implications for constitutional theory. If individual rights are not pre-political — if they emerge from and are sustained by social relationships and institutions — then the constitutional framing of rights as natural pre-political possessions that the state must not infringe may be systematically misleading. Rights may be better understood as social achievements that require ongoing institutional maintenance, not natural endowments that government merely protects or violates.

## II. The Actual Scale of Human Political Organization

### Dunbar's Number and the Constitutional Scale Problem

The British anthropologist Robin Dunbar (*Grooming, Gossip, and the Evolution of Language*, 1996) derived from comparative primate neurology the prediction that the human neocortex is sized for stable social groups of approximately 150 individuals. This "Dunbar's number" has been confirmed by analysis of: hunter-gatherer band sizes, Neolithic village sizes, effective military unit sizes, corporate department sizes, academic citation networks, and the size of personally-known social networks across cultures.

The significance for constitutional theory is profound. The founders were designing governance for populations ranging from thousands to millions — scales that are literally outside the range of human social cognition evolved over millions of years. Human beings have reliable social intuitions, trust calibrations, and cooperative instincts for groups of roughly 5 (intimate), 15 (close social circle), 50 (overnight camp), and 150 (village/band). Beyond 150, we have no evolved social machinery; we rely on institutional substitutes — laws, money, contracts, formal hierarchies, abstract identities — that are cognitively demanding, emotionally thin, and subject to exploitation by those who understand them better than most.

The Anti-Federalists' argument for small-scale republican government was not merely a preference for local control — it was, in anthropological terms, an argument for governance at scales within the range of evolved human social cognition. Montesquieu's observation that republics require small territory was an empirical observation about the limitations of human social cooperation, not merely a theoretical principle.

Madison's response — that an extended republic would generate a beneficial multiplicity of factions — was correct in a narrow sense (large scale does multiply interests) but missed the deeper anthropological point: the cognitive and emotional resources available for political cooperation thin out rapidly as scale increases, and the institutional substitutes that replace them are far more manipulable and fragile than the evolved social machinery they replace.

### Kinship, Reciprocity, and the Foundations of Political Order

Marshall Sahlins (*Stone Age Economics*, 1972), David Graeber (*Debt: The First 5,000 Years*, 2011), and Christopher Boehm (*Hierarchy in the Forest*, 1999) have established through comparative anthropology that human political organization has historically rested on three primary mechanisms:

**Kinship networks:** Political obligations mediated by family relationships — real, fictive, or metaphorical. The language of political loyalty throughout history has been the language of family: "founding fathers," "motherland," "brothers in arms," "the family of nations." Constitutional theory, by contrast, is grounded in the language of contract — rational agreement among strangers. The mismatch between the emotional power of kinship-based political loyalty and the thin, rational foundation of constitutional obligation is a persistent source of constitutional fragility.

**Generalized reciprocity:** The expectation of long-term mutual aid within communities, operating on open-ended time scales without formal accounting. This is the foundation of what the founders called "civic virtue" and what Robert Putnam (*Bowling Alone*, 2000) calls "social capital." Constitutional democracy depends on citizens and officials who feel obligations of reciprocity toward strangers and institutions — but this kind of generalized reciprocity appears to depend on conditions (relatively equal social status, repeated interaction, shared fate) that industrial capitalism systematically erodes.

**Dominance hierarchy:** Status-based organization in which authority flows from demonstrated superiority in relevant capacities (strength, skill, knowledge, age). The founders were trying to design institutions that replaced dominance hierarchy with legitimate authority — rule by law rather than rule by strength. But dominance hierarchy is deeply embedded in primate social organization (primatologist Frans de Waal's work on chimpanzee and bonobo politics provides the comparative context), and human political behavior persistently reasserts it. The appeal of "strong leader" politics — documented across cultures and eras by political psychologists (Bob Altemeyer on authoritarianism, Stanley Feldman on need for security) — reflects the persistent pull of dominance hierarchy against the constitutional alternative.

## III. The State of Nature Reconsidered — Violence, Cooperation, and Human Prehistory

### Was Pre-State Society Nasty, Brutish, and Short?

Hobbes's claim that pre-political human life was "solitary, poor, nasty, brutish, and short" — and Locke's somewhat less dire version — provided the motivational foundation for the social contract: the state of nature is so bad that any stable government is preferable to it, and even an imperfect government is worth the costs of its maintenance.

The empirical status of this claim has been extensively debated in modern anthropology. The debate has two sides:

**The Hobbesian view** (supported by Lawrence Keeley, *War Before Civilization*, 1996; Steven Pinker, *The Better Angels of Our Nature*, 2011): Archaeological and ethnographic evidence shows that pre-state societies had high rates of interpersonal and intergroup violence. Skeletal evidence of trauma from Neolithic and earlier sites suggests that a significant fraction of adults — sometimes 10-20% or more — died violently. Headhunting, raiding, and endemic intergroup warfare were common features of many small-scale societies. State formation, whatever its costs, dramatically reduced per-capita rates of violent death.

**The revisionist view** (supported by Douglas Fry, *War, Peace, and Human Nature*, 2013; R. Brian Ferguson, *The Anthropology of War*, 1984; David Graeber and David Wengrow, *The Dawn of Everything*, 2021): The Keeley-Pinker interpretation significantly overstates prehistoric violence, conflates different periods and contexts, and uses a biased sample of archaeological sites. Hunter-gatherer societies show enormous variation in violence rates. Many small-scale societies are genuinely peaceful. And the violence that does exist in pre-state societies is largely reactive to specific ecological pressures (population density, resource scarcity) rather than a baseline feature of human social life.

Graeber and Wengrow's synthesis is the most recent and most comprehensive: early human societies were extraordinarily diverse in their political organization, ranging from egalitarian seasonal hunter-gatherer bands to complex hierarchical societies and back again. The single-trajectory narrative (band → tribe → chiefdom → state) on which social contract theory depends is an oversimplification that conceals this variation. Many societies deliberately oscillated between hierarchical and egalitarian modes depending on season and context — suggesting that political organization is far more flexible and experimentally diverse than the founders' model assumed.

The constitutional implication: if the state of nature was not uniformly Hobbesian, then the legitimacy argument for the social contract — that *any* stable government is preferable to the pre-political alternative — is weaker than the founders assumed. The question is not simply whether to have a state, but what kind of state, what kind of governance, and at what scale — questions the founders addressed but on an empirically thinner foundation than they believed they had.

## IV. Race, Gender, and the Biology of Inclusion

### Scientific Racism and the Founders' Assumptions

The founders' exclusion of enslaved and free Black Americans, Native Americans, and non-property-owning white men from full political participation was rationalized partly by appeal to natural hierarchy — the idea that some human beings were, by nature, incapable of the rational self-governance that republican citizenship required.

Jefferson, the most scientifically oriented of the major founders, provided the most explicit statement of this reasoning in *Notes on the State of Virginia* (1785). In Query XIV, he speculated — carefully hedged as tentative — that Black people might be "inferior to the whites in the endowments both of body and mind." He cited as evidence: the "dull, tasteless, and anomalous" poetry of Phillis Wheatley (which he had read), the "miserable" prose of Ignatius Sancho, and his own observation that enslaved people seemed to reason but not to imagine.

This was bad science built on motivated reasoning. Jefferson was observing the effects of brutal social oppression — denial of literacy, systematic destruction of cultural achievement, the psychological trauma of enslavement — and attributing those effects to inherent biological incapacity. Modern genetics has comprehensively established that:

- There is more genetic variation *within* conventionally defined racial groups than *between* them (Lewontin, 1972 — still robust despite some subsequent refinement)
- The concept of discrete biological races has no validity in modern human population genetics; "race" is a social classification, not a biological one (American Anthropological Association Statement on Race, 1998)
- Cognitive capacity differences between populations, where measurable, are attributable to environmental, social, and developmental factors — not to inherent biological differences
- The "Flynn effect" — the systematic increase in IQ scores in all measured populations over the twentieth century (Flynn, 1987) — demonstrates that measured cognitive performance is highly responsive to environmental conditions, making any claim of inherent group-level cognitive differences untenable

The founders' exclusion of non-white people from constitutional citizenship was not grounded in biology — it was grounded in the social and economic interests of the people doing the excluding, rationalized by a pseudoscience whose empirical foundations were always thin and have since collapsed entirely.

### Gender and Political Capacity

The exclusion of women from political participation — absolute in 1787, only formally remedied by the Nineteenth Amendment in 1920 — was rationalized by a theory of natural gender difference that located women's nature in domesticity, emotional volatility, and dependence on male judgment.

This theory was not merely the prejudice of ignorant men. It was the dominant view of virtually every major political philosopher in the Western tradition, including those the founders drew on most heavily. Locke excluded women from the social contract. Rousseau's *Emile* (1762) explicitly argued that women's education should be designed to produce compliant wives, not independent citizens. Even the most egalitarian Enlightenment thinkers drew the line at female political participation.

Abigail Adams's famous letter to John Adams (March 31, 1776) — "Remember the Ladies, and be more generous and favourable to them than your ancestors" — was met with condescension: "I cannot but laugh... Depend upon it, We know better than to repeal our Masculine systems."

Modern biology, psychology, and anthropology have established:

- **No systematic cognitive differences** between sexes relevant to political judgment. The well-documented differences (female advantage in verbal memory and social cognition, male advantage in certain spatial tasks) are small in magnitude, massively overlapping in distribution, and largely attributable to developmental and socialization differences rather than innate biology (Rippon, *The Gendered Brain*, 2019)
- **Cross-cultural variation in gender roles** is so extensive (Mead, *Sex and Temperament in Three Primitive Societies*, 1935; despite subsequent critiques, the basic finding of cultural variation is robust) that no specific gender trait can be identified as universal and "natural" in the sense the founders assumed
- **Hormonal and neurological research** shows that the traits associated with political leadership — competitive drive, risk tolerance, decisive action — are not male-specific; they vary enormously among both men and women and are substantially responsive to social context and role assignment
- **Evolutionary theory** does not predict female political incapacity. Many primate species (bonobos, some lemurs) are female-dominated. Hunter-gatherer societies show wide variation in female political participation. The claim that female subordination is "natural" in any useful evolutionary sense is not supported by comparative biology.

The exclusion of women was a political choice that happened to align with the economic interests of propertied men (women could not individually hold property under coverture doctrine) and with social conventions rooted in agricultural property relationships — not a fact about biology.

# PART THREE: ECOLOGY, CARRYING CAPACITY, AND THE INVISIBLE CONSTITUTION

## I. The Founders Had No Ecology

Perhaps the most consequential gap in the founders' scientific knowledge was the complete absence of ecological thinking. Ecology as a discipline did not exist — the word was coined by Ernst Haeckel in 1866, and the systematic study of ecosystem dynamics, carrying capacity, energy flows, and the dependence of human societies on natural systems was entirely outside the conceptual vocabulary of 1787.

The Constitution is a document designed to govern relations among persons and institutions. It contains no framework for governing relations between human society and the natural systems that sustain it. "Property" appears repeatedly; "commons" never appears as a constitutional category; "environment" never appears; there is no recognition that human economies are embedded in and dependent on natural systems that can be depleted, degraded, or destroyed.

This was not mere oversight. It reflected a coherent worldview: Enlightenment political economy understood nature as a resource — the raw material of labor and production, essentially infinite in its capacity to absorb human exploitation. John Locke's labor theory of property (labor mixed with unimproved land creates legitimate private property) presupposes that there is essentially unlimited unimproved land available — a presupposition valid on the eastern seaboard in 1689, increasingly false as the continent was settled, and catastrophically false at a global scale.

The Jefferson-Hamilton debate over agrarian vs. commercial republicanism both assumed environmental abundance. Jefferson's vision of a republic of independent yeoman farmers expanding indefinitely westward presupposed infinite arable land. Hamilton's vision of an industrial commercial republic presupposed infinite resource inputs and infinite capacity to absorb the outputs of industry. Both were ecologically impossible in the long run.

---

## II. Carrying Capacity and Republican Virtue

Jefferson's political theory was explicitly ecological in structure, even if not in vocabulary. His argument for agrarian republicanism rested on the claim that small-scale farming produced the independence, self-sufficiency, and civic virtue that republican citizenship required. He wrote to John Jay (August 23, 1785):

> Cultivators of the earth are the most valuable citizens. They are the most vigorous, the most independent, the most virtuous, and they are tied to their country and wedded to its liberty and interests by the most lasting bonds.

This is an early version of what we would now call a social-ecological argument: specific types of human-environment relationships produce specific types of social and political character. The argument is not entirely wrong — the relationship between economic independence and political independence is real and well-documented. But it rests on assumptions about ecological possibility (unlimited arable land, sustainable small-scale agriculture indefinitely extended) that the ecological history of the United States has not borne out.

The Dust Bowl of the 1930s — caused largely by the kind of independent small-farm agriculture Jefferson idealized, breaking sod prairie and depleting topsoil through practices that maximized short-term yield at the cost of long-term ecological stability — was the ecological refutation of Jeffersonian agrarianism. The "independent yeoman farmer" whose ecological virtue Jefferson celebrated was, in aggregate, one of the most ecologically destructive forces in American history, clearing forests, plowing grasslands, draining wetlands, depleting soils, and extirpating wildlife at an unprecedented rate.

---

## III. The Commons Problem and Constitutional Design

The most important ecological concept missing from the founders' intellectual toolkit was what Garrett Hardin called "the tragedy of the commons" (*Science*, 1968) — the tendency of shared resources to be depleted when each individual actor rationally pursues their own interest without coordination, even when collective restraint would benefit everyone.

The constitutional design addresses the commons problem in some contexts — the Commerce Clause was partly motivated by the problem of interstate commercial commons (navigable waters, interstate trade routes) being managed destructively by competing state interests. But the design has no general framework for governing commons at any scale.

The results have been the familiar litany of constitutional inadequacy in the face of commons problems:

- Atmospheric carbon: A global commons whose degradation imposes diffuse, long-term, global costs that are systematically underweighted by the short-term, geographically bounded interests that Madison's factional system represents
- Fisheries, groundwater, topsoil, old-growth forest: Commons managed destructively under private property regimes that the Constitution protects but that ecology requires to be collectively managed
- Biodiversity: The genetic commons of the biosphere has no constitutional standing; its depletion imposes costs on future generations who have no representation in the constitutional system

Elinor Ostrom's Nobel Prize-winning work (*Governing the Commons*, 1990) established that commons can be sustainably managed — but that doing so requires institutional arrangements (clearly defined boundaries, rules matched to local conditions, collective choice arrangements, effective monitoring, graduated sanctions, conflict resolution mechanisms, recognition of rights to organize) that are most easily developed at small scales with repeated social interaction among participants. The constitutional design's preference for large-scale governance and formal legal mechanisms is particularly poorly suited to commons management.

The founders' political economy assumed that private property and market exchange were the appropriate governance mechanisms for most resources. Modern ecology establishes that many critical resources — atmosphere, oceans, shared aquifers, biodiversity — cannot be effectively governed by either private property or market exchange and require collective institutional arrangements that neither the constitutional text nor its interpretive traditions have been adequate to create.

---

## IV. Future Generations and Constitutional Representation

Jefferson's "nineteen year" theory — that each generation should govern itself under its own laws — was, ironically, the most ecologically sophisticated constitutional argument any founder made, even though Jefferson himself had no ecological framework. The core insight was correct: present-biased political systems systematically discount future costs and benefits, imposing burdens on future generations who have no political voice.

Modern ecology makes this problem vastly more acute than Jefferson could have anticipated. The time scales of ecological consequence — the half-life of atmospheric carbon dioxide is roughly 100 years; the recovery time of depleted fisheries is decades; the restoration of degraded soil takes centuries; the evolutionary response to mass extinction events takes millions of years — are entirely outside the time horizon of any constitutional system designed around two-year congressional terms, four-year presidential terms, and six-year senatorial terms.

Constitutional systems that respond primarily to the interests of currently living, voting constituents will systematically under-invest in long-term ecological stability. This is not a failure of political will or constitutional fidelity — it is the structural outcome of applying a rights-based, present-focused constitutional framework to problems that operate on geological time scales.

No founder designed constitutional institutions for intergenerational or ecological governance. Some contemporary scholars (Bruce Ackerman and Anne Alstott, *The Stakeholder Society*, 1999; Edith Brown Weiss, *In Fairness to Future Generations*, 1989) have proposed constitutional frameworks for representing future generations, but none has been adopted.

# PART FOUR: EVOLUTIONARY BIOLOGY AND POLITICAL INSTITUTIONS

## I. Evolution Did Not Optimize for Democracy

### The Evolved Psychology of Political Behavior

The human brain is the product of approximately 300,000 years of anatomically modern *Homo sapiens* evolution, layered on 2 million years of earlier *Homo* evolution, layered on 65 million years of primate evolution. For the overwhelming majority of that evolutionary history, humans lived in small, kin-based, hierarchical bands in which political behavior was organized around:

- Coalition formation and maintenance within groups of 20–150
- In-group/out-group discrimination (essential for group survival in competitive environments)
- Status hierarchies based on demonstrated capability, age, and coalition size
- Reciprocal altruism within groups, hostility toward competitors outside groups
- Dominance behavior — the assertion of status through aggression, display, and coalition politics

Democratic self-governance — rule by rational deliberation among approximate equals, constrained by abstract principles of rights and procedural fairness — is not what human evolved psychology is designed to do. It is a recent cultural invention (Athens, 508 BCE; modern democracy, approximately 200 years old) attempting to override evolved political instincts that served a very different adaptive function.

This does not mean democracy is impossible or undesirable. It means that democratic institutions face permanent pressure from the evolved psychological tendencies they are designed to constrain — and that constitutional designers who do not account for those tendencies will produce designs that are structurally vulnerable to them.

### Dominance Hierarchy and the Persistence of Aristocracy

The founders were deeply concerned about the emergence of hereditary aristocracy in the new republic. They prohibited titles of nobility (Article I, Sections 9 and 10), designed the Senate to prevent oligarchic entrenchment (though Anti-Federalists disputed this), and relied on competitive elections to prevent any individual or family from accumulating permanent political power.

What they did not account for was the interaction between evolved dominance hierarchy and economic inequality. Frans de Waal's research on primate dominance (*Chimpanzee Politics*, 1982; *The Age of Empathy*, 2009) and Robert Sapolsky's work on stress biology in hierarchical primates (*Why Zebras Don't Get Ulcers*, 1994; *Behave*, 2017) establish that:

- Dominance hierarchy is a universal feature of social primate organization
- High-status individuals accumulate preferential access to resources, mates, and information
- Low-status individuals show chronic stress physiology that degrades cognition, health, and life expectancy
- Status is transmitted across generations through both material inheritance and developmental environment

Economic inequality, in this framework, is not merely a distributive problem — it is a status hierarchy problem with direct biological consequences. The founders' constitutional design explicitly protected economic inequality (property rights, contracts, no redistribution mechanism) while trying to prevent political hierarchy. But in a society where economic power can be converted to political power — through campaign finance, media ownership, lobbying, elite network effects — economic inequality generates political hierarchy regardless of the formal constitutional prohibition on hereditary aristocracy.

The landed aristocracy of 1787 has been replaced not by hereditary titles but by dynastic wealth and corporate power — a form of aristocracy that the Constitution's formal anti-aristocracy provisions were not designed to address.

---

## II. Kin Selection, Nepotism, and the Civil Service

### Inclusive Fitness and Political Corruption

William Hamilton's theory of kin selection (*The Genetical Evolution of Social Behaviour*, 1964) established that organisms will sacrifice individual fitness to benefit genetic relatives in proportion to the degree of genetic relatedness — "inclusive fitness." In social species, this produces powerful nepotistic tendencies: preferential treatment of kin over non-kin in the distribution of resources, power, and protection.

In political institutions, kin selection and its cultural extension (preferential treatment of in-group members based on shared identity — ethnic, religious, tribal) produces nepotism and corruption. The founders were acutely aware of this problem — the patronage system, in which political positions were distributed to supporters' relatives and allies, was a major concern — but they did not have a biological framework for understanding how deep and persistent the tendency would be.

The merit-based civil service, created by the Pendleton Civil Service Reform Act (1883), was the structural response to nepotism in federal employment — a recognition that evolved in-group preferential behavior would corrupt public administration unless countered by explicit procedural constraints. The ongoing battle over the civil service — between merit-based selection and politically-based appointment (the current administration's expansion of Schedule F being the most recent episode) — is in part a battle between institutional constraints on evolved nepotistic behavior and the reassertion of that behavior under political cover.

---

## III. Group Selection and the Paradox of Civic Virtue

### The Evolution of Cooperation

One of the most important developments in evolutionary biology since the 1960s is the formalization of the conditions under which cooperation evolves among self-interested organisms. Robert Axelrod's *The Evolution of Cooperation* (1984), building on game-theoretic work by John Maynard Smith and others, established that cooperative strategies (tit-for-tat) are stable in repeated interactions among identifiable partners — but that cooperation is fragile in large-scale, anonymous interactions where defection cannot be punished reliably.

This has direct implications for constitutional theory. Madison's reliance on "ambition counteracting ambition" is essentially a model of competitive non-cooperation (each branch pursuing its own interests) that produces constitutional stability as an emergent outcome. But constitutional democracy also requires a significant level of *cooperative* behavior — officials who defer to constitutional norms even when defection would be personally advantageous, citizens who accept unfavorable electoral outcomes, judges who apply law they personally disagree with.

David Sloan Wilson and Elliott Sober's multilevel selection theory (*Unto Others*, 1998) argues that genuine altruism and civic virtue can evolve through group selection — groups whose members cooperate outcompete groups whose members defect. Christopher Boehm (*Moral Origins*, 2012) argues that human moral psychology — including the norms against free-riding, cheating, and domineering behavior that constitutional democracy requires — evolved precisely through this mechanism: ancestral human groups who enforced cooperative norms survived and reproduced better than those who did not.

The implication: constitutional civic virtue is not merely a cultural achievement — it has evolutionary foundations in human moral psychology. But those foundations evolved in small groups (Dunbar's number again), and they are activated most reliably by conditions of repeated interaction, shared fate, and mutual transparency — conditions that large-scale anonymous society and modern institutional complexity systematically erode.

---

## IV. Behavioral Ecology, Agriculture, and the Jeffersonian Myth

### The Hunter-Gatherer Legacy

The founders — especially Jefferson — assumed that agricultural society was the natural and ideal basis for republican citizenship. Jefferson's agrarianism was partly a response to Hamilton's commercialism, partly a reflection of Virginia's plantation economy, and partly a philosophical argument about the relationship between economic independence and civic virtue.

Modern behavioral ecology and anthropology have established a more complicated picture. Hunter-gatherer societies — which represent the vast majority of human evolutionary history — are, on average:

- More egalitarian than agricultural societies (Boehm, *Hierarchy in the Forest*, 1999)
- Characterized by more leisure time per calorie obtained (Sahlins, *Stone Age Economics*)
- More nutritionally diverse and often better nourished than early agricultural populations (archaeological evidence from skeletal analysis shows that the transition to agriculture was associated with decreased stature, increased tooth decay, and signs of nutritional stress)
- Organized around active mechanisms for preventing the accumulation of power by any individual — "reverse dominance hierarchies" in which coalitions actively level any individual who attempts to dominate (Boehm)

The transition to agriculture approximately 10,000 years ago introduced the material conditions for: surplus accumulation, social stratification, state formation, warfare at larger scale, and the patriarchal nuclear family (as a unit of agricultural production and property transmission). The "independent yeoman farmer" that Jefferson idealized was not a pre-political natural, but a creature of a specific historical and ecological context — one that depended on ample arable land, particular climate conditions, a specific technological level, and particular property institutions.

Jefferson's argument that agricultural independence produces civic virtue was not entirely wrong, but it was contingent on conditions (land abundance, smallholder property, roughly equal starting positions) that were already being eroded by commercial agriculture in the 1790s and have continued to erode ever since.

# PART FIVE: HUMAN BEHAVIORAL ECOLOGY AND CONSTITUTIONAL SUSTAINABILITY

## I. Status, Inequality, and Democratic Stability

Richard Wilkinson and Kate Pickett's *The Spirit Level* (2009) synthesized a vast literature on the relationship between economic inequality and social outcomes. The research establishes that more unequal societies have worse outcomes on virtually every social measure — mental illness, physical health, educational attainment, social mobility, violence, drug abuse, and imprisonment — than more equal societies at comparable levels of average wealth.

The constitutional implications run deep. Madison's faction theory assumed that economic inequality was politically manageable through institutional design — that competing material interests would cancel out rather than compound into stable hierarchies of power. But the behavioral ecology of status competition suggests that high inequality produces:

- Increased status anxiety and status competition at all levels of the hierarchy
- Erosion of social trust (Putnam's social capital research confirms this)
- Increased political polarization (higher-inequality societies show more intense political conflict)
- Reduced political participation by lower-status groups (participation rates correlate with income and education)
- Greater conversion of economic power into political power

The founders' constitutional protection of property rights and contract, combined with their failure to include any redistributive or anti-monopoly mechanisms, created conditions under which economic inequality would naturally compound over time into political inequality — exactly the aristocratic outcome they claimed to want to prevent.

---

## II. Tribalism, Exclusion, and the Costs of Out-Group Hostility

Henri Tajfel's minimal group experiments and subsequent research on in-group/out-group dynamics have established that the costs of tribalism extend far beyond the direct harm to excluded groups. Societies organized around strong in-group/out-group distinctions are less innovative (reduced information exchange across group boundaries), less economically productive (restricted labor markets, reduced investment in excluded groups' human capital), more prone to collective action failures (inability to cooperate across group lines), and more vulnerable to elite manipulation (divide-and-rule strategies exploit in-group/out-group dynamics).

The founders' constitutional accommodation of racial slavery and the exclusion of women and non-property-holders from political participation was presented as a practical compromise — the price of Southern ratification, the concession to existing social arrangements. What the founders did not have the anthropological or economic tools to calculate was the long-term institutional cost of building the constitutional order on a foundation of systematic exclusion.

That cost included: the Civil War (620,000-750,000 dead by modern estimates); a century of Jim Crow that produced persistent intergenerational poverty and political disempowerment whose effects are directly measurable in current economic and health data; the economic loss from excluding women from labor force participation and political decision-making; and the ongoing political instability generated by the enduring conflict between the constitutional promise of equality and the social reality of structured exclusion.

Robert Fogel and Stanley Engerman's *Time on the Cross* (1974) and subsequent economic histories have attempted to quantify the economic costs of slavery; the more important and harder-to-quantify costs are the institutional, political, and psychological ones that persist across generations (Darity and Mullen, *From Here to Equality*, 2020).

---

## III. Behavioral Genetics and Political Attitudes

One of the most surprising findings of modern behavioral genetics is that political attitudes are substantially heritable. Twin studies consistently find heritability estimates for political orientation (conservative vs. liberal) of approximately 40-60% (Alford, Funk, and Hibbing, "Are Political Orientations Genetically Transmitted?", *American Political Science Review*, 2005; subsequent replications).

This does not mean there is a "liberal gene" or "conservative gene" — the genetic architecture is enormously complex, involving thousands of variants each with tiny individual effects, interacting with developmental environment in ways that are not yet understood. But it does mean that political attitudes are substantially driven by stable dispositional traits that are in part biologically determined — not merely by rational calculation of self-interest or by education and exposure to political argument.

The constitutional implications are significant. If political orientation is substantially heritable — if some fraction of the population is, by stable biological disposition, more sensitive to threat, more authoritarian in political preference, more deferential to hierarchy, more suspicious of out-groups — then Madison's theory that political disagreement can be resolved through deliberation and compromise may be systematically optimistic. Deliberation can change minds at the margin, but the underlying distributions of political disposition may be relatively stable features of the population, not variables that institutional design can substantially shift.

This finding does not invalidate democratic governance — it suggests that democratic systems need to be designed for populations with genuine, stable diversity of political disposition, not for populations that will converge on shared values through deliberation.

# PART SIX: SYNTHESIS — THE CONSTITUTION'S BIOLOGICAL BLINDSPOTS

## I. What the Science Implies

The cumulative picture from behavioral science, anthropology, evolutionary biology, and ecology suggests that the constitutional design has the following systematic vulnerabilities, each rooted in incomplete understanding of human biology and social ecology:

**Scale mismatch:** The Constitution governs populations at scales vastly exceeding the range of evolved human social cognition. The institutional substitutes for evolved social machinery (law, money, formal hierarchy, abstract identity) are cognitively demanding, emotionally thin, and systematically exploitable by those who understand them better than most. The Anti-Federalists were right about this, even if they lacked the anthropological vocabulary to say why.

**Rationality assumption:** Constitutional checks and balances work as designed only if institutional actors rationally pursue their institutional interests. Behavioral science establishes that systematic cognitive biases — availability heuristics, present bias, loss aversion, identity-motivated reasoning — predictably cause actors to depart from rational self-interest in ways that undermine constitutional equilibria.

**Faction theory incompleteness:** Madison's model of material-interest-based faction misses identity-based tribalism, which is more psychologically primitive, more emotionally powerful, more resistant to deliberation, and more capable of producing stable two-camp configurations than the multiplying-interests model predicts.

**Social contract fiction:** The pre-social individual of social contract theory does not exist. Rights are social achievements, not natural endowments. This does not make rights less important, but it changes the constitutional frame: rights require social maintenance, not merely state restraint.

**Ecological blindness:** The Constitution contains no framework for governing the natural systems on which human society depends. The omission was invisible in 1787; it is catastrophic in 2025, as the temporal mismatch between constitutional decision-making time horizons and ecological consequence time horizons produces systematic under-investment in ecological stability.

**Inequality dynamics:** The founders' protection of property without redistributive or anti-concentration mechanisms created conditions for the compounding of economic inequality into political inequality — recreating in economic form the aristocratic hierarchy they had formally prohibited.

**Exclusion costs:** The constitutional accommodation of systematic exclusion (slavery, coverture, property qualifications) imposed costs that the founders lacked the analytical tools to calculate. Those costs — in lost human capital, institutional damage, persistent inequality, and political instability — have been enormous and are still being paid.

---

## II. What the Science Does Not Imply

This analysis should not be misread as a dismissal of the constitutional project. Several important qualifications:

**Biology is not destiny.** The fact that humans have evolved tendencies toward tribalism, dominance hierarchy, nepotism, and present bias does not mean these tendencies cannot be constrained. They can — through institutions, norms, education, and deliberate cultural practice. The point is that constraining them requires understanding them accurately, which the founders could not do with the science available to them.

**Imperfect institutions can work.** Constitutional democracy, despite its misalignment with evolved human psychology, has proven more durable and more protective of human welfare than the alternatives. The record of non-democratic governance is catastrophically worse. Understanding constitutional vulnerabilities is an argument for careful institutional maintenance, not for abandoning the project.

**The founders were not uniquely biased.** Every political thinker before the twentieth century worked without the scientific resources now available. The founders were, by the standards of their time, unusually sophisticated, unusually empirical, and unusually honest about the limitations of their own knowledge. Madison in No. 37 explicitly acknowledged the incompleteness of the founding generation's wisdom. Jefferson explicitly designed the University of Virginia to provide future generations with the scientific education that would enable constitutional improvement.

**The amendment process is the appropriate response.** Madison's insistence that constitutional change must go through the formal amendment process — rather than through judicial reinterpretation, executive action, or simple legislative override — reflects an insight that remains valid: informal constitutional change is far more susceptible to manipulation and less durable than formal amendment. The scientific findings catalogued in this document are arguments for constitutional amendment where the design is inadequate — not for abandoning the framework the founders built.
