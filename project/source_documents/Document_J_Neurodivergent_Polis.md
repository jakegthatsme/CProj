## Part One — The Neurodiversity Paradigm

### The Paradigm Articulated

The neurodiversity paradigm is the analytical-political framework that treats the human cognitive and neurological landscape as constitutively diverse, the diversity as a characteristic feature of the species rather than as a pattern of pathology, and the social and architectural arrangements that produce disability for some neurotypes as the products of architectural design rather than of neurology. The paradigm emerged in the late 1990s in the autistic-self-advocacy community — Judy Singer is generally credited with the term's earliest articulation in the late 1990s autistic-community discussions, with substantial precursor work in the autistic-self-advocacy organizing of the 1980s and early 1990s that the paradigm formalized — and has been developed in the subsequent decades through the work the present part engages.

Nick Walker's *Neuroqueer Heresies: Notes on the Neurodiversity Paradigm, Autistic Empowerment, and Postnormal Possibilities* (2021) is the foundational scholarly articulation of the paradigm as analytical-political framework. The book collects and extends Walker's earlier essays — most consequentially "Throw Away the Master's Tools: Liberating Ourselves from the Pathology Paradigm" (originally 2012, revised in the *Neuroqueer Heresies* collection) and "Neurodiversity: Some Basic Terms and Definitions" (originally 2014, revised in the collection) — and develops the analytical-political extension of the paradigm Walker calls "neuroqueer," the practice of consciously refusing the pathology paradigm's prescriptive demands on cognitive and embodied performance. Walker's analytical contribution that bears centrally on the constitutional architecture is the demonstration that the neurodiversity paradigm is not a thesis about the empirical reality of neurological variation alone — though the empirical reality of neurological variation is part of the paradigm's analytical foundation — but a thesis about the political-architectural conditions under which constitutional and social participation has been organized and the political-architectural reconstruction the recognition of neurological variation requires.

The three principles of the neurodiversity paradigm Walker articulates and the broader scholarly tradition has developed are foundational for the constitutional architecture the present document advances. The first principle is that neurodiversity — the diversity of human neurology and cognition — is a natural and valuable form of human variation comparable to biodiversity in ecological systems. The variation is not pathological. It is constitutive of the species, has analytical and creative consequences whose substantive content the contemporary literature engages, and is not appropriately analyzed under medical-diagnostic frameworks whose substantive content presumes a normative neurology against which divergences are deficits. The second principle is that the medical-pathology model of neurological variation is a social construction whose specific historical, institutional, and political-economic conditions of emergence the contemporary scholarship has documented and whose substantive content does not survive the analytical scrutiny the paradigm advances. The construction has had specific political-economic functions — the regulation of labor markets, the organization of educational institutions, the pharmaceutical and medical industries' organizational structures, the broader political-economic configuration of the contemporary period — and the analytical case for its displacement is structural-architectural rather than principally clinical. The third principle is that the disability and political-architectural disadvantage neurodivergent persons experience are produced by the structural-architectural configuration rather than by the neurology, and the analytical-political response is the structural-architectural reconstruction rather than the neurological-individual remediation.

The constitutional implications of the three principles operate at three levels that the present document develops in subsequent parts. At the level of constitutional rights, the principles support the rights architecture Part Three develops. At the level of constitutional participation, the principles support the participatory and e-democracy architecture Parts Four and Five develop. At the level of constitutional design, the principles support the educational, labor, and decision-science architectures Parts Six and Seven develop and the integrative-architectural commitments Part Nine engages.

### The Empire of Normality and Its Political Economy

Robert Chapman's *Empire of Normality: Neurodiversity and Capitalism* (2023) extends the neurodiversity paradigm into the political-economic analysis the constitutional architecture the present document advances requires. Chapman's analytical contribution that bears centrally on the present argument is the demonstration that the contemporary "empire of normality" — the structural-architectural configuration that produces and enforces a narrowly defined normative neurology and embodiment as the precondition of full social, economic, and political participation — is constitutive of contemporary capitalism rather than incidental to it. The argument is structurally homologous to Cedric Robinson's *Black Marxism* (1983) argument that contemporary capitalism is racial capitalism rather than capitalism with racial inflections layered onto it, and the argument operates through the parallel analytical move: the construction and maintenance of the normative neurology has been integral to the political-economic configuration's structural operation rather than separable from it.

Chapman's analysis develops three architectural commitments the present document engages. The first is the demonstration that the standardization of work tempo, of educational pacing, of cognitive-performance metrics, and of the broader social architecture under industrial and post-industrial capitalism has produced specific exclusions and harms for neurodivergent populations whose substantive content the disability-justice and neurodivergent traditions have documented in detail. The second is the demonstration that the medicalization and pharmaceuticalization of neurodivergent persons has been part of the political-economic configuration rather than a neutral medical response to a clinical condition; the contemporary structure of pharmaceutical-industry profit, the structure of clinical psychiatry's relationship to insurance and Medicaid reimbursement architecture, and the broader political-economic configuration of the medical-industrial complex are the institutional conditions under which the medical-pathology model has been constructed and maintained. The third is the demonstration that the architectural reconstruction the neurodiversity paradigm authorizes operates at the political-economic level and not solely at the clinical or institutional levels. The architectural response engages the political-economic configuration the contemporary capitalism the project's diagnostic identifies and engages it through the constitutional and architectural commitments Document E on currency architecture, Document D on constitutional architecture, and Document H on multi-generational rectification develop.

### The Historical Apparatus

Steve Silberman's *NeuroTribes: The Legacy of Autism and the Future of Neurodiversity* (2015) supplies the principal contemporary historical-narrative apparatus for understanding the trajectory through which the neurodiversity paradigm has emerged from the longer history of autistic and broader neurodivergent communities' experience under the medical, educational, and broader institutional architectures of the twentieth century. The book's principal contributions for the present argument include the documentation of Hans Asperger's wartime work in Vienna and the subsequent contested historiography around it (Edith Sheffer's 2018 *Asperger's Children: The Origins of Autism in Nazi Vienna* substantially revised and complicated Silberman's account, and the contemporary scholarly engagement engages both works under the analytical commitment that the historiographical record itself is contested terrain whose specific content matters for the contemporary politics); the documentation of Leo Kanner's work at Johns Hopkins in the 1940s and the broader American clinical-research configuration that produced the contemporary diagnostic categories; the documentation of the parents' organizations and the contested politics through which the autism advocacy and broader neurodivergent advocacy fields developed; the documentation of the autistic-self-advocacy movement's emergence in the 1980s and 1990s and its institutional development through Autism Network International, the Autistic Self Advocacy Network, the Autistic Women and Nonbinary Network, and the broader constellation of autistic-led organizations; and the documentation of the broader cultural and political shifts through which the contemporary neurodiversity paradigm has emerged.

The historical apparatus is consequential for the constitutional architecture the present document advances because it supplies the analytical-political case that the contemporary neurodivergent communities are constituted political-historical subjects whose articulation has been forged through a specific and contested historical experience, and that the constitutional architecture must engage this historical experience as load-bearing rather than as background context. The architectural commitments of Part Nine — particularly the multi-generational rectification commitments the present document supports through engagement with Document H — engage the historical apparatus directly.

### The Double Empathy Problem

Damian Milton's articulation of the double empathy problem in his 2012 essay "On the Ontological Status of Autism: The 'Double Empathy Problem'" in *Disability and Society* is the foundational analytical contribution the present argument engages on the question of cross-neurotype communication and its constitutional implications. Milton's analytical case is that the communication breakdowns between autistic and neurotypical persons that the standard clinical literature has historically attributed to autistic deficit are bidirectional rather than unidirectional. The autistic communicator's failure to comprehend neurotypical communication is structurally symmetric to the neurotypical communicator's failure to comprehend autistic communication, and the asymmetric attribution of the breakdown to autistic deficit is a structural-ideological feature of the dominant tradition rather than an accurate empirical finding.

The double empathy problem has been substantially confirmed by subsequent empirical research on cross-neurotype communication. Catherine J. Crompton, Sue Fletcher-Watson, and colleagues have published a series of empirical studies between 2019 and the contemporary period documenting the empirical reality of the double empathy problem — that autistic-to-autistic communication exhibits comparable or higher communicative efficiency than autistic-to-neurotypical communication, that neurotypical-to-neurotypical communication exhibits comparable communicative efficiency to neurotypical-to-autistic communication, and that the empirical pattern is not consistent with the unidirectional-deficit account that the historical clinical literature articulated. The empirical confirmation has substantial constitutional-architectural implications: the deliberative-democratic and participatory-architectural designs that have presumed a unidirectional model of communicative competence whose center is neurotypical communication are designs whose empirical foundation does not survive the bidirectional analysis the double empathy problem articulates. The architectural reconstruction Part Four and Part Five develop engages the double empathy problem as load-bearing.

### Autistic Rhetoric and the Deficit Model

M. Remi Yergeau's *Authoring Autism: On Rhetoric and Neurological Queerness* (2018) develops the analytical-philosophical case for understanding autistic rhetoric, communication, and authorship on autistic terms rather than through the deficit frameworks of the dominant rhetorical and educational traditions. Yergeau's analytical contribution that bears centrally on the present argument is the demonstration that autistic rhetorical and communicative practice is rhetoric — substantive, agentic, and politically consequential rhetorical-communicative activity — rather than the absence of rhetoric or the symptom of rhetorical-communicative deficit. The deficit model that has organized the historical clinical and educational literatures has presumed the absence; Yergeau's analysis demonstrates the presence and develops the analytical-philosophical case for engaging autistic rhetoric on its own constitutive terms.

The constitutional implications of Yergeau's analysis operate at the level of the participatory and deliberative architectures the present document develops. The standard deliberative-democratic literature, developed principally through Jürgen Habermas's *Between Facts and Norms* (1992, English translation 1996) and *The Theory of Communicative Action* (1981, English translation 1984), James Fishkin's body of work on deliberative polling and citizens' assemblies, Jane Mansbridge's work on representation and deliberation, and Cristina Lafont's *Democracy without Shortcuts* (2020), has presumed a model of communicative competence whose center is neurotypical communication — verbal-fluency-centered, eye-contact-mediated, real-time-synchronous, executive-function-intensive, sensory-environment-tolerant communication. Yergeau's analysis identifies the structural-political effect of this presumption: the structural exclusion of neurodivergent participants from full participation in the deliberative architecture the standard literature has constructed, and the structural displacement of neurodivergent rhetorical practice from the deliberative architecture's analytical recognition. The architectural reconstruction Part Four develops engages Yergeau's analysis as load-bearing for the participatory-architectural reconstruction.

### The Decolonial Question Flagged

Vishnu Karthik Krishnan Nair, Warda Farah, and Mildred Boveda's 2024 paper articulates the decolonial-neurodiversity question whose substantive engagement Part Eight of the present document develops at length. The question is whether the neurodiversity paradigm as currently constituted in the principal Anglo-American scholarly literature operates as a Global-Northern white framework whose categorical commitments and political-economic assumptions have been structured by the specific cultural-political-economic context of its emergence, and whose substantive engagement with Black, brown, and Indigenous neurodivergent traditions has been peripheral or absent. The question is not the question of whether neurological variation exists across Black, brown, and Indigenous populations (it manifestly does), nor the question of whether persons in those populations have experienced specific forms of neurodivergent subordination (they manifestly have); it is the question of whether the paradigm's categorical apparatus, its political-economic analysis, and its constitutional-architectural commitments have been adequately constituted through engagement with the analytical and political traditions of those populations' neurodivergent communities.

The honest articulation of the present document's position is that the question is well-taken and that the substantive engagement Part Eight develops is part of the analytical work of the document rather than an external caveat to it. The categorical apparatus the present part has developed — the neurodiversity paradigm articulated through Walker, Chapman, Yergeau, Milton, and Silberman — is principally the apparatus of the Anglo-American neurodiversity paradigm, and the cross-traditional reconstruction Part Eight develops is the substantive engagement the multi-eyed seeing commitment requires. The reconstruction does not displace the Anglo-American paradigm; it specifies the categorical apparatus through cross-traditional engagement and identifies the architectural commitments that the cross-traditional engagement supports. The substantive content of that engagement is developed in Part Eight at length.

### The Constitutional Implications of the Paradigm

The constitutional implications of the neurodiversity paradigm for the present argument operate at the foundational level. The paradigm's recognition that human neurological variation is constitutive of the species rather than a pattern of pathology supports the constitutional commitment that the constitutional subject the architecture serves is plurally embodied and plurally neurologically constituted, and that the architectural design must serve the plural subject rather than presume a singular neurological default. The paradigm's political-architectural analysis of the structural exclusions the contemporary architecture has produced supports the constitutional commitment that the architectural reconstruction the project advances must engage the structural exclusions at the structural level rather than through the addition of accommodations to a design whose neurotypical defaults are preserved. The paradigm's empirical and analytical apparatus on cross-neurotype communication supports the participatory-architectural reconstruction Parts Four and Five develop. The paradigm's political-economic extension through Chapman's *Empire of Normality* supports the integration with the political-economic architectural reconstruction Document E develops and the broader integration with the project's political-economic analysis.

The architectural commitments the constitutional implications support are developed in detail in Parts Three through Nine. Part Two engages the operation of the pathology paradigm against which the neurodiversity paradigm articulates its alternative.

## Part Two — The Pathology Paradigm and Its Constitutional Operation

### The Pathology Paradigm Articulated

The pathology paradigm is the analytical-political framework against which the neurodiversity paradigm Part One developed articulates its alternative. The paradigm treats the human cognitive and neurological landscape as a normative landscape with specific neurotypes as standard and divergences from the standard as deficits, dysfunctions, or disorders to be diagnosed, treated, remediated, prevented, and ideally cured. The paradigm has organized the principal institutional architectures through which the constitutional and social standing of neurodivergent persons has been determined across the twentieth and early twenty-first centuries — the medical and psychiatric architecture, the educational architecture, the labor and employment architecture, the criminal-justice architecture, the conservatorship and guardianship architecture, the residential-services architecture — and the paradigm's operation through these architectures has produced the specific constitutional harms whose architectural-political analysis the present part develops.

The argument advanced in this part is that the pathology paradigm's institutional operation constitutes a constitutional-architectural pattern of structural subordination whose substantive content is comparable to the patterns of structural subordination the project's diagnostic identifies on the caste, oligarchy, racial, and broader embodiment axes. The pathology paradigm is not an isolated medical-clinical configuration whose effects are confined to the clinical domain; it is a constitutional-architectural configuration whose operation extends across multiple institutional domains and whose political-economic substrate the foundation infrastructure, the institutional and ideological apparatus, and the broader political-economic configuration the project's diagnostic engages have constructed and sustained. The architectural response Parts Three through Nine develop is therefore architectural rather than clinical, and the substantive content of the response engages the constitutional-architectural configuration directly.

### The Institutionalization Regime: Willowbrook and Its Successors

The institutionalization regime is the foundational constitutional-architectural site at which the pathology paradigm's operation has been most starkly visible across the twentieth century. The regime's historical paradigm in the American context is Willowbrook State School, operated by the State of New York on Staten Island from 1947 to 1987 and at its peak housing approximately 6,000 residents — children and adults categorized as having intellectual or developmental disabilities, including substantial numbers of persons whom the contemporary clinical apparatus would categorize as autistic, ADHD, or otherwise neurodivergent. The institution's conditions were extensively documented by Geraldo Rivera's 1972 ABC News exposé and the broader contemporary investigative-journalistic coverage, by the litigation in *New York State Association for Retarded Children v. Rockefeller* (later *Carey*), filed in 1972 and settled in 1975 in the consent decree commonly cited as the Willowbrook Consent Decree, and by the historical-documentary record the contemporary scholarly literature has subsequently developed.

The conditions documented at Willowbrook included substantial physical neglect and abuse of residents, sustained understaffing, overcrowding (the institution's nominal capacity was approximately 4,000 and at its peak it held approximately 6,000), the systematic absence of educational and developmental services, sustained sexual abuse of residents by staff and other residents, and the conduct of the Saul Krugman hepatitis vaccine experiments at the institution between 1956 and 1972 in which children who were residents at the institution were deliberately infected with hepatitis virus as part of the research protocol. The Krugman experiments were defended at the time on the grounds of parental consent and the eventual development of the hepatitis B vaccine, and they have been subsequently engaged in the bioethics literature as paradigmatic of the structural-architectural conditions under which institutional populations have been subjected to medical experimentation that the broader population would not have accepted. The constitutional-architectural significance of the Willowbrook record is that the conditions were not the failure of an otherwise-functioning institutional architecture; they were the structural-architectural product of the institutionalization regime under which the institutional architecture operated, and the regime's operation across the broader American institutional landscape produced comparable conditions across multiple institutions whose substantive content the contemporary historical record has documented.

The institutionalization regime's contemporary operation has been substantially constrained by the deinstitutionalization movement that emerged in the 1960s and 1970s, the litigation through *Olmstead v. L.C.*, 527 U.S. 581 (1999), which articulated the constitutional principle that unjustified institutional segregation of disabled persons constitutes discrimination under Title II of the Americans with Disabilities Act of 1990, and the broader political-architectural shift toward community-based services. The structural implementation of the deinstitutionalization commitment has been incomplete, and the contemporary architecture of disability and neurodivergent services in the United States retains substantial institutional-confinement architecture whose reform has been politically and economically constrained. The contemporary architecture includes the state-operated residential institutions for persons with intellectual and developmental disabilities (the institutions historically operated under the labels "state school" or "developmental center" — the contemporary national population is substantially smaller than the institutionalization regime's peak but remains substantial); the private-residential-facility architecture that includes facilities such as the Judge Rotenberg Educational Center in Canton, Massachusetts, whose use of contingent electric shock as a behavioral intervention on autistic and other disabled residents has been the subject of substantial scholarly and political contestation across the contemporary period; the nursing-home architecture in which substantial numbers of younger disabled persons have been placed in facilities designed for elderly populations with significantly different care requirements; and the broader residential-services configuration whose substantive content the contemporary scholarship documents.

The contemporary institutionalization regime is the structural-architectural infrastructure through which the pathology paradigm's most consequential physical operation has been conducted. The architectural reconstruction Part Three develops engages the regime through the constitutional rights architecture the document specifies, and the integration with the multi-generational rectification architecture Part Nine develops engages the regime as one of the principal historical sites of constitutional injustice the rectification architecture must address.

### The Conservatorship Architecture

The conservatorship and guardianship architecture is the legal-doctrinal apparatus through which the substantive autonomy of persons categorized as cognitively impaired or otherwise disabled has been transferred to court-appointed conservators or guardians. The architecture's substantive content varies across jurisdictions and across the categorical distinctions the legal-doctrinal apparatus has constructed (conservatorship of the person versus conservatorship of the estate; full versus limited conservatorship; voluntary versus involuntary conservatorship; the related but distinct guardianship architecture), and the architecture's operation has produced substantial constitutional harms whose substantive content the contemporary legal-doctrinal and disability-rights scholarship has documented. The contemporary national conservatorship population is substantial — the Guardian's 2021 reporting cited 1.3 million Americans under conservatorship, and subsequent reporting has confirmed a population in the range of 1.3 to 1.5 million — and the architecture's operation has been the subject of substantial political-architectural contestation across the contemporary period.

The Britney Spears conservatorship case, in operation from February 1, 2008 to November 12, 2021, is the contemporary paradigmatic example of conservatorship abuse and is engaged in the present argument on the public record the case's litigation, the singer's own testimony, and the contemporary investigative-journalistic and documentary coverage have produced. The conservatorship was imposed in February 2008 following an involuntary psychiatric hold under California's Welfare and Institutions Code section 5150 and was structured in the standard California configuration as conservatorship of the person and conservatorship of the estate, with James "Jamie" Spears (the singer's father) and attorney Andrew M. Wallet initially serving as co-conservators. The conservatorship was extended from its initial temporary status to permanent status against the singer's objections, and the singer's substantive autonomy over her finances, personal life, professional activity, and medical decisions was transferred to the conservators across the period of the conservatorship's operation. The conservatorship's operation generated substantial public attention principally through the FreeBritney movement that emerged from fan communities in the late 2010s, through the New York Times documentary *Framing Britney Spears* (February 2021) and its sequel *Controlling Britney Spears* (September 2021), and through the singer's June 2021 testimony before Judge Brenda Penny in which she described the conservatorship's operation in terms that included specific allegations of abuse, coercion, and conflict of interest. The conservatorship was terminated by Judge Penny on November 12, 2021. California Senate Bill 724, the FreeBritney Bill, was signed by Governor Gavin Newsom on September 30, 2021, and modified the California conservatorship architecture in directions including the right of conservatees to obtain counsel of their choosing, financial-conflict-of-interest provisions, termination-procedure reforms, and financial-penalty provisions for abusive conservators.

The constitutional-architectural significance of the Spears case is consequential for the present argument and operates at multiple levels. First, the case illustrates the structural-architectural conditions under which a person who, on the public record, was sustaining an active professional career across substantial portions of the conservatorship period — recording multi-platinum albums, conducting multi-year touring residencies, generating estate revenues that the conservators and their associates received substantial fees from administering — was nevertheless held under the legal-doctrinal apparatus of the conservatorship as a person whose cognitive capacity required the transfer of her substantive autonomy to her conservators. The structural-architectural inconsistency between the empirical reality of the conservatee's professional and financial productivity and the legal-doctrinal premise of the conservatorship's operation is consequential for the analytical-political case the disability-rights and broader contemporary scholarship advances on conservatorship abuse generally. Second, the case illustrates the structural-architectural conditions under which the conservatorship architecture's operation produced specific structural harms — including the singer's inability to retain counsel of her own choosing for substantial portions of the conservatorship's operation, the financial structure through which substantial portions of the singer's earnings were directed to the conservators and their associates, the constraints on the singer's reproductive autonomy that the singer described in her June 2021 testimony, and the broader structural-political configuration of the conservatorship's operation — whose substantive content the SB-724 reform and the broader contemporary conservatorship reform have engaged. Third, the case illustrates the specific structural-political conditions under which the FreeBritney movement operated as a contemporary instance of disability-rights political organizing whose substantive content the contemporary scholarly and journalistic literature has documented.

The Spears case is the celebrity-centered articulation of a broader structural pattern whose substantive content the contemporary disability-rights and conservatorship-reform scholarship has documented across the broader 1.3-to-1.5 million-person conservatorship population. The contemporary scholarship — including the National Council on Disability's 2018 report *Beyond Guardianship: Toward Alternatives That Promote Greater Self-Determination*, the broader work of the contemporary disability-rights legal organizations, and the contemporary scholarly engagement with supported decision-making as alternative architecture — develops the structural-architectural case for the constitutional reconstruction of the conservatorship architecture in directions consistent with the constitutional rights architecture Part Three develops.

### The Sub-Minimum-Wage Employment Regime: Section 14(c)

The sub-minimum-wage employment regime under section 14(c) of the Fair Labor Standards Act of 1938 is the legal-doctrinal apparatus through which employers may obtain certificates from the United States Department of Labor's Wage and Hour Division authorizing the payment of below-federal-minimum-wage compensation to workers categorized as having disabilities that impair their productive capacity. The provision was enacted in 1938 on the legislative theory that the sub-minimum-wage authorization was necessary to prevent the curtailment of employment opportunities for disabled workers, and it has remained in operation across the subsequent eighty-seven years.

The contemporary operation of section 14(c) has been documented in detail by the United States Department of Labor's administrative records, by the National Council on Disability's reports of 2012 (*Subminimum Wage and Supported Employment*) and 2018 (*National Disability Employment Policy, From the New Deal to the Real Deal*), and by the broader contemporary scholarly and policy literature. As of fiscal year 2024, approximately 38,000 to 40,579 workers were employed under section 14(c) certificates, of whom approximately 90 percent had intellectual or developmental disabilities. The Department of Labor's Wage and Hour Division administrative data for the first two quarters of fiscal year 2024 indicated that approximately 49 percent of section 14(c) workers were paid less than $3.50 per hour, that approximately 10 percent were paid $1.00 per hour or less, and that approximately 2 percent were paid 25 cents per hour or less, against the federal minimum wage of $7.25 per hour. The contemporary section 14(c) population is substantially smaller than its peak of approximately 424,000 in 2001, reflecting the cumulative effect of state-level legislative phase-outs (eighteen states have ended or have no active section 14(c) programs as of contemporary reporting, including California whose phase-out commitment culminated in January 2025) and the broader contemporary disability-rights political-architectural movement against the regime.

The federal-architectural status of section 14(c) as of contemporary reporting reflects substantial political contestation. The Department of Labor under the Biden administration published a Notice of Proposed Rulemaking in the Federal Register on December 4, 2024, proposing to phase out the issuance of new section 14(c) certificates and to permit existing certificate holders to continue operating for up to three years after the rule's effective date. The Department of Labor under the second Trump administration formally withdrew the proposed rule on July 7, 2025, citing the absence of statutory authority for the unilateral termination of the program. The proposed federal Transformation to Competitive Integrated Employment Act would establish the legislative architecture for the federal phase-out and has been advocated by the Autistic Self Advocacy Network, the National Council on Disability, the broader contemporary disability-rights political movement, and substantial portions of the contemporary disability-services field.

The constitutional-architectural significance of section 14(c) for the present argument is consequential and operates at multiple levels. First, the provision articulates the political-architectural premise that the labor of disabled and neurodivergent workers may be valued at less than the federal minimum-wage threshold the broader American labor architecture treats as the floor against the substantive subordination of labor; the premise is the structural-political articulation of the pathology paradigm's operation in the labor architecture. Second, the empirical content of the wages section 14(c) workers receive — the documented prevalence of wages below $3.50 per hour, the documented prevalence of wages below $1.00 per hour, the documented existence of wages in the range of cents per hour — articulates the structural-political content of the regime in terms that the abstract legal-doctrinal categories obscure. Third, the structural-political configuration of the section 14(c) employment — typically conducted in segregated "sheltered workshop" settings that limit interaction with non-disabled co-workers, that operate on production-quota systems whose pacing is structurally inaccessible for many of the workers the system employs, and that are substantively integrated with the residential-services architecture in ways that constrain the workers' substantive autonomy — articulates the structural-architectural integration of the labor architecture with the broader institutional architecture the present part has engaged. The architectural response Part Three develops engages section 14(c) as one of the principal sites at which the constitutional rights architecture the document specifies must operate.

### The Educational Segregation Regime

The educational segregation regime is the institutional-architectural configuration through which neurodivergent students have been substantially separated from neurotypical students in the educational architecture. The regime's contemporary content has been substantially shaped by the Education for All Handicapped Children Act of 1975 (now the Individuals with Disabilities Education Act, IDEA), which articulated the principle of education in the "least restrictive environment" and the right of students with disabilities to a "free and appropriate public education" supported by an Individualized Education Program (IEP). The legislative architecture has been foundational for the contemporary educational rights of disabled and neurodivergent students, and the architecture's structural implementation has been incomplete in ways the contemporary scholarship documents.

The contemporary patterns of educational segregation include the substantial placement of neurodivergent students in self-contained classrooms separated from general-education classrooms within otherwise mainstream school buildings; the placement of neurodivergent students in separate special-education schools whose educational programming is structurally separated from general-education programming; the disproportionate use of disciplinary architectures including suspension, expulsion, and seclusion-and-restraint procedures against neurodivergent students; the disproportionate referral of neurodivergent Black, brown, and Indigenous students to special-education and disciplinary architectures whose substantive content the cross-disability-and-race tradition the project's diagnostic engages has documented; and the broader structural-political configuration of educational architecture that has organized the participation of neurodivergent students under the pathology paradigm's operative commitments. Lydia X. Z. Brown's body of work on the school-to-prison pipeline and its disproportionate effects on autistic and otherwise neurodivergent Black, brown, and Indigenous students has been foundational for the contemporary scholarly understanding of these patterns and supplies the analytical apparatus the present argument engages.

The architectural response Parts Three and Six develop engages the educational segregation regime as one of the principal sites at which the constitutional rights architecture and the educational architecture commitments the document specifies must operate. The architectural commitments include the right to inclusive and neurodiversity-affirming education, the architectural commitment to ending educational segregation in its substantive forms, and the integration with the broader rectification architecture Part Nine develops.

### The Criminal-Justice Over-Incarceration

The criminal-justice over-incarceration of neurodivergent persons is the structural-architectural pattern through which the criminal-justice system has produced disproportionate incarceration, prosecution, and police-encounter outcomes for autistic, ADHD, and otherwise neurodivergent persons. The contemporary scholarship — including the work of the Autistic Self Advocacy Network on criminal-justice issues, Lydia X. Z. Brown's body of work on the disability-justice and criminal-justice integration, the Bazelon Center for Mental Health Law's substantial scholarly and policy work, and the broader contemporary criminal-justice and disability-justice scholarly literature — has documented the patterns and developed the analytical-political apparatus for understanding their structural-architectural content.

The structural-architectural patterns include the disproportionate use of force by police against autistic and otherwise neurodivergent persons during police encounters whose substantive content reflects the breakdown the double empathy problem articulates between the neurotypical communicative-behavioral expectations of police officers and the neurodivergent communicative-behavioral patterns of the persons encountered; the disproportionate prosecution of neurodivergent persons under criminal-law architectures whose substantive content has not been calibrated to neurodivergent communicative and behavioral patterns; the disproportionate incarceration of neurodivergent persons in correctional architectures whose substantive content has not been designed to accommodate neurodivergent sensory, cognitive, and communicative requirements; the documented patterns of physical and sexual abuse, of solitary confinement, of psychiatric medication without informed consent, and of the broader structural-architectural conditions under which neurodivergent persons have experienced incarceration; and the broader structural-political configuration of the criminal-justice architecture whose operation against neurodivergent persons articulates one of the principal contemporary sites of the pathology paradigm's constitutional operation.

The architectural response engages the criminal-justice architecture through the constitutional rights architecture Part Three develops, through the integration with the broader criminal-justice abolitionist and reformist scholarship the project's diagnostic engages, and through the integration with the multi-generational rectification architecture Part Nine engages.

### Forced Treatment, ABA, and the Medical-Architectural Operation

The forced-treatment regime is the medical-architectural configuration through which neurodivergent persons have been subjected to specific medical, behavioral, and psychiatric interventions without their substantive informed consent. The regime's contemporary operation includes the involuntary psychiatric hospitalization architecture (the contemporary "5150" architecture in California and its analogs in other states); the forced-medication architecture under court orders; the use of physical and chemical restraints in institutional settings; the conduct of behavioral interventions including Applied Behavior Analysis (ABA) on autistic children whose substantive content has been the subject of substantial contestation in the contemporary autistic-self-advocacy and broader neurodivergent scholarly community; the contemporary architecture of medical experimentation under Institutional Review Board oversight whose substantive content engages neurodivergent populations under conditions whose ethical adequacy is contested; and the broader medical-architectural configuration whose operation against neurodivergent persons articulates one of the principal contemporary sites of the pathology paradigm's constitutional operation.

ABA therapy warrants particular engagement in the present argument. The therapy was developed principally through the work of O. Ivar Lovaas at UCLA in the 1960s and 1970s, articulated in Lovaas's 1987 paper in the *Journal of Consulting and Clinical Psychology* on the use of intensive behavioral intervention with autistic children. The therapy operates through the application of operant-conditioning principles — typically through structured trials, reinforcement of behaviors categorized as desirable, and the systematic reduction of behaviors categorized as undesirable — to autistic children typically beginning in early childhood and continuing for substantial periods (the Lovaas protocol specified forty hours per week of one-on-one intervention). The contemporary autistic-self-advocacy community's substantial critique of ABA — articulated in the work of the Autistic Self Advocacy Network, in scholarly articulations including Henny Kupferstein's 2018 *Advances in Autism* paper documenting evidence of post-traumatic stress symptoms in adults who received ABA in childhood (a study whose methodological reception in the broader literature has been contested but whose substantive concerns the autistic community has continued to articulate), and in the broader autistic-community discussion across the contemporary period — has identified specific structural-architectural concerns that include the therapy's operation in suppressing autistic communicative and behavioral patterns including stimming and atypical communication that the contemporary neurodivergent scholarship has identified as autistic communicative practice rather than as deficit; the therapy's frequent use of aversive interventions in earlier formulations and contested aversive elements in some contemporary formulations; the therapy's focus on producing behavioral conformity to neurotypical norms rather than on supporting autistic flourishing; the therapy's operation in extracting forty or more hours per week of structured intervention from the developmental period of autistic children whose substantive autonomy and self-direction the contemporary neurodivergent scholarship treats as load-bearing; and the broader structural-political configuration of the ABA industry, including its contemporary integration with insurance reimbursement architectures whose substantive content has organized the contemporary American autism-services field. The contemporary defenders of ABA have advanced their own analytical-political case, and the present document engages the contestation as substantive political contestation rather than as settled medical question. The architectural response the document advances supports the constitutional commitment that autistic and otherwise neurodivergent children's substantive autonomy be treated as load-bearing in the architecture of behavioral and developmental interventions, and that the architectural reconstruction the document specifies engage the contestation directly rather than presume the legitimacy of the standing architecture.

## Part Three — The Constitutional Rights Architecture

### The Architectural Move

The constitutional rights architecture the present part develops articulates the substantive rights commitments the integrated diagnostic Parts One and Two support. The rights are not articulated as accommodations to be added to a constitutional architecture whose substantive premises remain unchanged; they are articulated as constitutional rights whose substantive content is constitutive of the architectural design the project advances. The distinction is consequential and tracks the analytical move the neurodiversity paradigm authorizes: where the accommodation framework presumes that the constitutional architecture is substantively adequate and requires modification at its periphery to permit neurodivergent participation, the rights framework treats neurodivergent participation as constitutive of the architectural design and articulates rights whose substantive content is foundational rather than peripheral.

The architectural move is methodologically homologous to the analytical move the project's diagnostic conducts on the embodiment axis generally. Document B Part Five articulated the case for treating embodiment as a load-bearing constitutional axis rather than as a supplementary category accommodating disabled and neurodivergent persons within an otherwise unchanged framework. The rights architecture the present part develops is the substantive specification of the constitutional commitments the diagnostic supports for the neurodivergent dimension. The rights are stated below in scholarly-analytical articulation rather than as draft constitutional language; the constitutional-language drafting Document D conducts will operate from the analytical articulation the present part supplies.

### The Right Against Pathology-Paradigm Subordination

The first right the constitutional architecture articulates is the right against pathology-paradigm subordination. The right's substantive content is the constitutional commitment that no person shall be subjected to the legal-architectural categorization of their cognitive, neurological, or communicative configuration as pathological where that categorization operates as the substantive ground for the limitation of constitutional standing, autonomy, or participation. The right does not displace the medical and clinical apparatus where its operation is consensual and substantively serves the person whose treatment is at issue; it constrains the operation of the apparatus where its operation is structurally or actually non-consensual and operates as the ground for the limitation of constitutional standing.

The right's structural content engages multiple specific architectural commitments. The right against involuntary diagnostic categorization is the commitment that diagnostic categorization shall not be imposed on a person without the person's substantive consent, with the recognition that the consent architecture must engage the substantive autonomy of the person whose categorization is at issue and that the consent architecture's adequacy depends on the broader structural conditions under which the consent is sought. The right against the use of diagnostic categorization as ground for constitutional disability is the commitment that the legal-architectural transfer of constitutional standing, autonomy, or participation rights shall not operate on the basis of diagnostic categorization alone; the architecture must engage the specific substantive considerations the case at issue raises rather than treating the diagnostic categorization as substitutionary for the substantive consideration. The right against the systematic medicalization of neurodivergent persons in the labor, educational, and political-architectural domains is the commitment that the structural-architectural operation through which neurodivergent communicative and behavioral patterns have been pathologized for purposes of these architectures shall be reconstructed in directions consistent with the neurodiversity paradigm's substantive commitments.

The constitutional articulation of the right against pathology-paradigm subordination operates in tandem with the rights articulated in subsequent subsections of the present part and integrates with the broader constitutional rights architecture Document D develops. The right is not a stand-alone provision; it is the foundational analytical commitment from which the more specific rights articulated below are derived.

### The Right to Neurodivergent Communicative Practice

The second right the constitutional architecture articulates is the right to neurodivergent communicative practice. The right's substantive content is the constitutional commitment that the constitutional architecture's communicative and deliberative infrastructures shall accommodate the substantive diversity of human communicative practice rather than presuming a singular normative communicative model whose substantive content is the neurotypical-default model the contemporary architecture has presumed.

The right's substantive content engages M. Remi Yergeau's *Authoring Autism* (2018) analytical case for understanding autistic rhetorical and communicative practice as substantive rhetorical-communicative activity rather than as the absence or symptom of communicative deficit, and engages Damian Milton's double empathy problem as the substantive analytical-empirical case for the bidirectional rather than unidirectional model of communicative competence. The architectural-political case is that the contemporary deliberative-architectural infrastructure has presumed a unidirectional model of communicative competence whose substantive content is the neurotypical-default model, and that the bidirectional model the contemporary scholarship supports requires the substantive reconstruction of the deliberative-architectural infrastructure.

The substantive architectural commitments the right supports include the commitment to multiple communicative modalities in deliberative architecture (text-based, visual, gestural, augmentative-and-alternative-communication-mediated, asynchronous, sustained over extended time periods), with the recognition that the multiple modalities are not optional accommodations to be added to a verbal-real-time-default architecture but constitutive of the architectural design; the commitment to the right to use augmentative and alternative communication (AAC) in all constitutional-participatory contexts, including the right to the AAC technology, the right to AAC training and support, the right to AAC use without discriminatory inference about cognitive capacity from AAC use; the commitment against the structural-architectural privileging of verbal fluency, eye-contact-mediated communication, and real-time-synchronous communication in constitutional-participatory contexts; the commitment to the structural-architectural recognition of stimming, scripting, echolalia, atypical prosody, atypical gaze patterns, and the broader spectrum of autistic and neurodivergent communicative practice as substantive communicative activity rather than as symptoms requiring suppression or remediation; and the commitment to the structural-architectural reconstruction of the educational architecture's communicative pedagogy in directions consistent with the neurodivergent commitment.

The right to neurodivergent communicative practice integrates with the participatory and e-democracy architectures Parts Four and Five develop. The substantive architectural commitments the right supports are foundational for the architectural reconstruction those parts specify.

### The Right to Sensory and Cognitive Accommodation

The third right the constitutional architecture articulates is the right to sensory and cognitive accommodation. The right's substantive content is the constitutional commitment that the constitutional architecture's physical, sensory, and cognitive design shall accommodate the substantive diversity of human sensory and cognitive configurations rather than presuming a singular normative configuration whose substantive content is the neurotypical-default sensory-and-cognitive configuration the contemporary architecture has presumed.

The right's structural content engages multiple specific architectural commitments. The substantive commitment to sensory accommodation in the design of physical and digital constitutional-participatory spaces is the commitment that the lighting, acoustic, olfactory, and broader sensory configuration of the spaces shall be designed to accommodate the substantive diversity of human sensory configurations rather than presuming the sensory configuration the neurotypical-default architecture has assumed. The substantive content includes the commitment to the availability of low-stimulation environments in all constitutional-participatory contexts, the commitment to the reduction of fluorescent lighting and other sensory-load-intensive features in constitutional-architectural design, the commitment to the availability of sensory-accommodation tools (noise-canceling headphones, weighted blankets, sensory-regulation tools) in constitutional-participatory contexts, and the broader substantive commitment to the structural-architectural reconstruction of physical and digital spaces in directions consistent with the neurodivergent commitment.

The substantive commitment to cognitive accommodation in the design of constitutional-participatory architectures is the commitment that the cognitive load, the executive-function demands, the working-memory requirements, the attentional demands, the time-sensitivity, and the broader cognitive configuration of the architectures shall be designed to accommodate the substantive diversity of human cognitive configurations rather than presuming the cognitive configuration the neurotypical-default architecture has assumed. The substantive content includes the commitment to the availability of asynchronous-participation modalities in constitutional-participatory contexts, the commitment to extended time-frames for substantive constitutional-participatory activity (rather than presuming the synchronous-real-time configurations the contemporary architecture has presumed), the commitment to the availability of cognitive-load-reduction tools (visual-organization aids, executive-function-supporting interfaces, processing-time accommodations), the commitment to the reduction of unnecessary cognitive complexity in constitutional-participatory architectures, and the broader substantive commitment to the structural-architectural reconstruction of cognitive-architectural design in directions consistent with the neurodivergent commitment.

The right to sensory and cognitive accommodation integrates with the educational and labor architectures Part Six develops, with the participatory and e-democracy architectures Parts Four and Five develop, and with the decision-science architecture Part Seven develops. The substantive architectural commitments the right supports are foundational for the architectural reconstruction those parts specify.

### The Right Against Sub-Minimum-Wage Employment

The fourth right the constitutional architecture articulates is the right against sub-minimum-wage employment. The right's substantive content is the constitutional commitment that no person shall be paid less than the federal minimum wage on the basis of disability or neurodivergence categorization. The right operates as the constitutional commitment that the architectural reconstruction Part Two engaged in the section on section 14(c) requires.

The right's substantive content engages the structural-architectural conditions Part Two documented. The contemporary section 14(c) employment of approximately 38,000 to 40,579 workers, of whom approximately 90 percent have intellectual or developmental disabilities and substantial portions are autistic or otherwise neurodivergent, at wages whose contemporary content includes substantial portions paid less than $3.50 per hour, $1.00 per hour, or 25 cents per hour, articulates the structural-political configuration the right addresses. The constitutional commitment that no person shall be paid less than the federal minimum wage on the basis of disability or neurodivergence categorization operates as the substantive ground for the elimination of section 14(c), the substantive ground for the architectural reconstruction the proposed Transformation to Competitive Integrated Employment Act would establish, and the substantive ground for the broader political-architectural commitment to ending the sub-minimum-wage regime.

The right operates in tandem with the broader architectural commitments to competitive integrated employment that Part Six develops at length. The architectural reconstruction must engage not only the substantive elimination of the sub-minimum-wage authorization but the affirmative commitment to the structural-architectural conditions under which neurodivergent and disabled workers may participate in competitive integrated employment with appropriate workplace accommodations, supported employment services where requested, and the broader political-economic configuration that supports the substantive employment outcomes the architectural reconstruction commits to.

### The Right Against Educational Segregation

The fifth right the constitutional architecture articulates is the right against educational segregation. The right's substantive content is the constitutional commitment that no neurodivergent or disabled student shall be subjected to educational segregation that is not the substantive choice of the student (or, where the student's age or substantive autonomy makes the parental decision the operative one, the substantive informed choice of the parents acting consistently with the student's substantive interests) and that is not consistent with the student's substantive flourishing.

The right's substantive content engages multiple specific architectural commitments. The commitment to inclusive education as the default educational configuration is the commitment that the educational architecture's default placement shall be in general-education classrooms with appropriate supports and accommodations, with the substantive recognition that the contemporary architecture's substantial use of self-contained classrooms, separate special-education schools, and other segregated educational configurations is not the consequence of the substantive needs of the students placed in those configurations but the consequence of the structural-architectural configuration of the contemporary educational system. The commitment to the architectural reconstruction of the educational system in directions consistent with the inclusive-education default engages the broader contemporary scholarship on inclusive education, on the structural-architectural reforms that would enable the inclusive-education default to operate substantively rather than nominally, and on the integration with the broader educational architectural commitments Part Six develops.

The commitment against the disproportionate use of disciplinary architectures including suspension, expulsion, seclusion-and-restraint procedures, and the school-to-prison pipeline against neurodivergent students is the commitment that the structural-political configuration through which the contemporary architecture has produced disproportionate disciplinary outcomes for neurodivergent students — particularly Black, brown, and Indigenous neurodivergent students whose substantive treatment Lydia X. Z. Brown's body of work has documented — shall be substantively reconstructed.

The right against educational segregation integrates with the broader rectification architecture Part Nine develops and with the educational architectural commitments Part Six develops at length.

### The Right Against Medical Experimentation and Forced Treatment

The sixth right the constitutional architecture articulates is the right against medical experimentation and forced treatment. The right's substantive content is the constitutional commitment that no neurodivergent or disabled person shall be subjected to medical, psychiatric, or behavioral interventions without their substantive informed consent, with the recognition that the consent architecture must engage the substantive autonomy of the person whose treatment is at issue and that the consent architecture's adequacy depends on the broader structural conditions under which the consent is sought.

The right's substantive content engages the historical record Part Two engaged. The Saul Krugman hepatitis vaccine experiments at Willowbrook State School between 1956 and 1972, the broader historical pattern of medical experimentation on institutionalized populations whose contemporary scholarly engagement has documented the structural-political conditions under which the experimentation has been conducted, the contemporary architecture of medical experimentation under Institutional Review Board oversight whose substantive content engages neurodivergent populations under conditions whose ethical adequacy is contested, and the broader medical-architectural configuration whose operation against neurodivergent persons articulates the structural-political content of the right are the historical and contemporary baseline against which the right operates. The substantive constitutional commitment is that the architectural reconstruction shall foreclose the structural-political conditions under which the experimentation has been conducted and shall require substantive informed consent under conditions whose adequacy is structurally rather than nominally established.

The right's content on forced treatment engages the contemporary involuntary-psychiatric-hospitalization architecture, the forced-medication architecture under court orders, the use of physical and chemical restraints in institutional settings, the conduct of behavioral interventions including ABA on autistic children, and the broader forced-treatment regime Part Two engaged. The substantive constitutional commitment is that the architectural reconstruction shall require substantive informed consent under conditions whose adequacy is structurally rather than nominally established, shall provide for substantive due-process and substantive-review architectures where the consent is contested or where the substantive autonomy of the person is at issue, and shall reconstruct the architecture in directions consistent with the supported-decision-making alternatives the contemporary disability-rights scholarship has developed.

The right against medical experimentation and forced treatment integrates with the conservatorship-reform commitments engaged in the next subsection and with the broader rectification architecture Part Nine develops.

### The Right Against Conservatorship Abuse

The seventh right the constitutional architecture articulates is the right against conservatorship abuse. The right's substantive content is the constitutional commitment that the conservatorship and guardianship architecture shall be reconstructed in directions consistent with the substantive autonomy of the persons whose participation in the architecture is at issue, with the substantive availability of supported-decision-making alternatives, with the substantive procedural protections against abuse, and with the substantive reconstruction of the structural-political conditions under which the architecture's contemporary operation has produced the abuses Part Two engaged.

The right's substantive content engages the contemporary conservatorship-reform scholarship and the architectural alternatives the contemporary scholarship has developed. The supported-decision-making (SDM) architecture, articulated in detail in the contemporary scholarly and policy literature including the National Council on Disability's 2018 report *Beyond Guardianship: Toward Alternatives That Promote Greater Self-Determination* and the broader work of the National Resource Center for Supported Decision Making, supplies the principal architectural alternative to the contemporary conservatorship architecture. The SDM architecture treats the cognitive support some persons require for substantive decision-making as a structural-architectural feature rather than as the ground for the legal-architectural transfer of substantive autonomy to a third party; it provides for the substantive participation of supporters in the decision-making process while preserving the substantive autonomy of the person whose decision is at issue. The architectural reconstruction the present right supports operates through the substantive adoption of SDM as the default architectural alternative, with the substantive constraint of conservatorship to circumstances in which SDM is genuinely inadequate and with substantive procedural protections in those circumstances.

The right's substantive content engages the procedural-architectural reforms the FreeBritney Act (California SB-724, 2021) and analogous state-level reforms have begun to articulate, including the right of conservatees to obtain counsel of their choosing regardless of cognitive-capacity determinations, financial-conflict-of-interest provisions, termination-procedure reforms, financial-penalty provisions for abusive conservators, and the substantive procedural-due-process commitments that the contemporary scholarship has identified as constitutionally required. The architectural reconstruction the present right supports extends these reforms in the directions consistent with the broader constitutional commitment.

The right against conservatorship abuse integrates with the broader rectification architecture Part Nine develops, with the substantive autonomy commitments the broader rights architecture supports, and with the integration with the multi-generational rectification commitments Document H develops.

### Olmstead and the ADA Architecture Engaged

The Americans with Disabilities Act of 1990 and the Supreme Court's decision in *Olmstead v. L.C.*, 527 U.S. 581 (1999), supply the contemporary doctrinal-architectural baseline against which the constitutional rights architecture the present part articulates operates. *Olmstead* held that unjustified institutional segregation of persons with disabilities constitutes discrimination under Title II of the ADA, and the decision has been foundational for the contemporary deinstitutionalization movement and for the broader contemporary disability-rights legal architecture.

The constitutional rights architecture the present document advances does not displace the ADA and *Olmstead* architecture; it specifies the substantive constitutional commitments the broader architectural reconstruction engages. The substantive limits of the ADA framework — the framework's operation principally through individual-accommodation mechanisms, the structural reach of the framework into the design of the broader social architecture has been substantially constrained by judicial interpretation and by the limits of administrative enforcement, the political-economic resistance of regulated entities to the framework's substantive implementation — articulate the substantive case for the constitutional articulation the present document advances. The constitutional rights architecture operates at the structural-architectural level rather than at the individual-accommodation level, and its substantive content is the architectural reconstruction the integrated diagnostic supports.

The structural implementation of *Olmstead* has been incomplete in ways the contemporary scholarship has documented. The principal structural patterns include the contemporary institutionalization of substantial disabled and neurodivergent populations in residential facilities whose substantive integration with community life is constrained, the contemporary nursing-home placement of substantial younger disabled populations in facilities whose substantive content has been designed for elderly populations with significantly different care requirements, and the broader structural-architectural conditions Part Two engaged. The constitutional rights architecture the present document advances supports the substantive completion of the *Olmstead* architectural commitment through the structural reconstruction of the residential-services and broader disability-services architectures.

### The CRPD as Constitutional Baseline

The Convention on the Rights of Persons with Disabilities (CRPD), adopted by the United Nations General Assembly on December 13, 2006, and entered into force on May 3, 2008, is the principal contemporary international-law articulation of the substantive constitutional commitments the present part advances. The CRPD has been ratified by 191 states parties as of contemporary reporting; the United States signed the Convention on July 30, 2009, but the Senate has not ratified it (the most recent ratification vote, in December 2012, fell short of the two-thirds majority required for treaty ratification).

The CRPD's substantive content articulates the constitutional commitments the present document supports. The Convention's Article 12 on equal recognition before the law articulates the substantive autonomy commitments the conservatorship-reform commitments engage. The Convention's Article 19 on living independently and being included in the community articulates the substantive deinstitutionalization commitments the institutionalization-regime reform engages. The Convention's Article 27 on work and employment articulates the substantive employment commitments the section 14(c) reform and the broader employment commitments engage. The Convention's Article 29 on participation in political and public life articulates the substantive participatory commitments the participatory and e-democracy architectures engage. The Convention's broader substantive content articulates the constitutional commitments the rights architecture the present part advances supports.

The constitutional commitment the present document advances is that the architectural reconstruction the project develops shall operate consistently with the CRPD's substantive content as constitutional baseline rather than as international-law commitment subject to political-architectural contestation. The architectural commitment includes the substantive ratification of the CRPD as part of the constitutional reconstruction Document D advances, the substantive incorporation of the CRPD's substantive content into the constitutional architecture's domestic-law operation, and the substantive operation of the architectural reconstruction in directions consistent with the CRPD's substantive commitments.

## Part Four — The Participatory Architecture

### The Architectural Move and the Deliberative-Democracy Tradition

The participatory architecture the present part develops articulates the substantive specification of constitutional participation under the neurodivergent commitment. The architectural move is the substantive reconstruction of the deliberative-democratic and broader participatory architecture in directions consistent with the constitutional rights architecture Part Three articulated, with specific attention to the neurotypical-default assumptions in the standing deliberative-democracy literature and to the architectural alternatives the neurodiversity paradigm authorizes.

The standing deliberative-democracy literature has been developed principally through the work of Jürgen Habermas, James Fishkin, Jane Mansbridge, Cristina Lafont, and the broader contemporary scholarly tradition. The substantive content of this literature has been foundational for the contemporary scholarly and political-architectural understanding of deliberative democracy, and the project's substantive commitments to deliberative architecture — articulated in detail in Document F on e-democracy as structural public check — engage this literature substantively. The argument advanced in the present part is not that the standing literature is to be displaced wholesale; it is that the standing literature has presumed neurotypical-default assumptions whose structural-political effect has been the systematic exclusion of neurodivergent participants from full participation in the deliberative architecture the literature has constructed, and that the substantive architectural reconstruction the neurodivergent commitment authorizes operates through the engagement of these assumptions and the articulation of the architectural alternatives the engagement supports.

### The Habermasian Tradition Engaged

Jürgen Habermas's *Between Facts and Norms: Contributions to a Discourse Theory of Law and Democracy* (1992, English translation 1996) and *The Theory of Communicative Action* (1981, English translation 1984) supply the foundational scholarly articulation of the contemporary deliberative-democracy tradition. Habermas's analytical contribution is the demonstration that democratic legitimacy depends on the substantive participation of affected persons in the deliberative processes through which the binding norms governing their lives are produced, and that the substantive adequacy of the participation depends on the structural conditions under which the deliberation operates — conditions Habermas articulates through the concept of the "ideal speech situation" in which participants engage one another on terms of substantive equality, freedom from coercion, and orientation toward mutual understanding.

The neurotypical-default assumptions in the Habermasian tradition operate at multiple structural levels. The communicative-action framework presumes a communicative model whose substantive content is the verbal-real-time-synchronous communication the neurotypical-default architecture has assumed. The orientation toward mutual understanding presumes a communicative-cognitive configuration in which participants can substantively engage one another's communicative content under the time and cognitive constraints the standard deliberative configuration imposes. The ideal speech situation presumes a structural-political configuration whose substantive operation has been organized around the neurotypical-default communicative configuration. The substantive content of the assumptions is consequential for the participatory-architectural reconstruction the present argument advances: the standard Habermasian deliberative configuration has been substantively inaccessible to substantial neurodivergent populations, not because of the neurology of those populations but because of the structural-architectural conditions under which the deliberation has been organized.

The reconstruction the present argument advances does not displace the Habermasian commitment to substantive participation, to substantive equality, to freedom from coercion, and to orientation toward mutual understanding; it reconstructs the structural-architectural conditions under which these commitments operate in directions consistent with the substantive diversity of human communicative practice. The substantive content of the reconstruction engages Yergeau's analytical case for understanding autistic rhetorical and communicative practice on autistic terms, Milton's double empathy problem as the analytical-empirical case for the bidirectional model of communicative competence, and the broader contemporary neurodivergent scholarship's substantive contributions on the architectural alternatives the neurodiversity paradigm authorizes. The substantive Habermasian commitments are sustained under the reconstruction; the structural-architectural conditions under which the commitments operate are transformed.

### The Fishkin Tradition Engaged

James Fishkin's body of work on deliberative polling and citizens' assemblies — articulated principally in *When the People Speak: Deliberative Democracy and Public Consultation* (2009), in *Democracy When the People Are Thinking: Revitalizing Our Politics through Public Deliberation* (2018), and in the broader contemporary scholarly literature on deliberative polling — supplies the foundational scholarly articulation of the deliberative-polling and citizens'-assembly architecture the contemporary deliberative-democracy field has developed. Fishkin's analytical contribution is the demonstration that substantive public deliberation under structured architectural conditions produces outcomes substantively different from the outcomes the broader political-architectural configuration produces, and that the substantive content of the deliberative architecture is the appropriate institutional response to the substantive limits of the contemporary political-architectural configuration.

The deliberative-polling and citizens'-assembly architecture has been foundational for the contemporary deliberative-democracy field, and the substantive contributions of the Fishkin tradition have been substantial. The neurotypical-default assumptions in the Fishkin tradition operate at multiple structural levels and warrant substantive engagement. The structured-deliberation format that the contemporary deliberative-polling and citizens'-assembly architecture has employed has typically operated through real-time small-group deliberation under time-constrained conditions, with the substantive cognitive and executive-function demands the configuration imposes.
