# Multi-Eyed Seeing as Constitutional Method: Methodology, Epistemology, and the Architecture of Plural Authority

This installment makes explicit and defends the methodological commitment that has been load-bearing across the project arc. The standing research-mode prompt names the commitment in each document's opening paragraph; this document develops it into a substantive analytical contribution at the level of method, epistemology, and constitutional theory. The commitment is multi-eyed seeing: the simultaneous engagement of multiple analytical traditions whose authority the project recognizes as co-equal. The traditions are the Western constitutional-democratic tradition; the Indigenous constitutional traditions of Turtle Island, Aotearoa, Sápmi, the Andes, and Africa; the Black-radical constitutional tradition; the dalit and South Asian anti-caste tradition; the disability-justice tradition; the neurodivergent tradition; the feminist and Black-feminist tradition; the Latinx and Chicanx tradition; the Asian-American tradition; the decolonial-theory tradition; and the climate-justice tradition. The commitment extends Albert and Murdena Marshall's Two-Eyed Seeing (Etuaptmumk) — the bilateral practice of viewing the world through Indigenous and Western analytical traditions in genuine integration, originally articulated in Cheryl Bartlett, Murdena Marshall (Mi'kmaq), and Albert Marshall's (Mi'kmaq) "Two-Eyed Seeing and Other Lessons Learned within a Co-Learning Journey of Bringing Together Indigenous and Mainstream Knowledges and Ways of Knowing" (2012) — into the multilateral architecture the project's equal-weight commitment requires, and is deployed with explicit care to honor the Marshall framework as "a learning process and not a perfected outcome" rather than to displace or supersede it. The document proceeds in ten parts: the conceptual development from Two-Eyed Seeing to multi-eyed seeing; the Indigenous research-methodology tradition that supplies the procedural and ethical architecture for the commitment; standpoint epistemology and epistemic plurality as the philosophical foundation in feminist and Black-feminist thought; epistemic injustice and epistemic resistance as the analytical apparatus for diagnosing whose voices constitutional architectures hear; decolonial theory and the pluriverse as the framework for understanding the modern/colonial entanglement of knowledge with power; the neurodivergent contribution to epistemic plurality; multi-eyed seeing operationalized as constitutional method; the renovation-versus-decolonial question engaged substantively as the document's most consequential analytical move; the application of the commitment to the project's seven structural commitments and to the e-democracy and rectification architectures; and a closing integration that names the methodological commitment as the project arc's binding thread.

## Part One — From Two-Eyed Seeing to Multi-Eyed Seeing: The Conceptual Development

### The Marshall Family's Primary Articulation

The intellectual debt of this document begins in a specific place: the cooperative Integrative Science program at Cape Breton University and the long collaboration of Mi'kmaq Elders Albert Marshall and Murdena Marshall with the non-Indigenous biologist Cheryl Bartlett. The published articulation that the academic literature now treats as the origin point of the framework is Bartlett, Marshall, and Marshall's 2012 paper in the *Journal of Environmental Studies and Sciences*, "Two-Eyed Seeing and Other Lessons Learned within a Co-Learning Journey of Bringing Together Indigenous and Mainstream Knowledges and Ways of Knowing." The paper does not present Etuaptmumk as a method to be applied; it presents it as the codification of a relational practice that emerged through years of working together. The Mi'kmaq word Etuaptmumk — translated by Albert Marshall as "the gift of multiple perspective" or "to see with two eyes" — names a stance toward knowledge in which one eye is trained to see the strengths of Indigenous knowledge and ways of knowing and the other eye is trained to see the strengths of Western knowledge and ways of knowing, and the practitioner learns to use both eyes together for the benefit of all.

The framework's force in the original articulation comes from three commitments that are easy to soften and important not to. First, the Marshall articulation refuses the integration-as-merger move that has dominated cross-cultural knowledge frameworks from the colonial period forward. Etuaptmumk does not produce a single hybrid knowledge by absorbing one tradition into the other. The two eyes remain two eyes; the binocular vision they together produce is not a third tradition but a relational practice. Second, the framework is explicitly directional in a way that its later popularizations have sometimes obscured. The Marshalls articulate the commitment from within a Mi'kmaq worldview that has been subjected to centuries of colonial epistemic violence, and the framework's first commitment is to the survival, integrity, and authority of Indigenous knowledge in a world structured to delegitimize it. The bilateral move toward Western knowledge is offered from a position of relative epistemic vulnerability and is conditional on the integrity of the Indigenous half being honored. Third — and this is the commitment that this document will lean on most heavily — the Marshalls describe Two-Eyed Seeing as "a learning process and not a perfected outcome." The framework names a stance and a practice; it does not deliver a method that, properly applied, will produce correct cross-traditional knowledge. The honest commitment is to keep working at it, to keep listening, to keep recognizing where the practitioner's eyes have failed.

The Marshall articulation is foundational primary text in this project's bibliography on its own constitutional standing. The project recognizes the framework as Indigenous intellectual property whose authority is primary; the engagement of non-Indigenous scholarship with the framework is commentary, and the commentary must be answerable to the primary articulation rather than supplanting it. Where this document develops or extends the framework, it does so with explicit acknowledgment that the Marshalls' description of Two-Eyed Seeing as a learning process binds the extension as well: the multi-eyed seeing this document advances is itself a learning process, not a perfected outcome, and the project must say so.

### The Contemporary Scholarship's Extension

The decade following the 2012 paper produced a substantial scholarly literature working out what Two-Eyed Seeing means in practice. The literature is now sufficient to support a developed methodological argument; it is also sufficient to expose the patterns by which non-Indigenous scholarship can domesticate, dilute, or instrumentalize an Indigenous framework. Both deserve attention.

Andrea J. Reid, Lauren E. Eckert, John-Francis Lane, Nathan Young, Scott G. Hinch, Chris T. Darimont, Steven J. Cooke, Natalie C. Ban, and Albert Marshall (Mi'kmaq) extended the framework into fisheries research and management in their 2021 paper in *Fish and Fisheries*, "'Two-Eyed Seeing': An Indigenous Framework to Transform Fisheries Research and Management." The paper is significant for the present argument because it is co-authored with Marshall and because it directly addresses what Two-Eyed Seeing requires institutionally rather than only methodologically. The authors specify that Two-Eyed Seeing in fisheries practice means more than incorporating Indigenous knowledge into Western management frameworks as supplementary data: it means recognizing Indigenous nations as governing authorities with primary jurisdiction in their territories, sharing decision rights at the institutional rather than the consultative level, and rebuilding the procedural architecture of fisheries science around relational accountability to ecosystems and to Indigenous communities. The Reid et al. paper is the principal source the present document draws on for the move from methodological commitment to institutional architecture.

Carolyn Smith, Sibyl Diver, and Ron Reed's (Karuk) 2023 paper in *Environment and Planning F*, "Advancing Indigenous Futures with Two-Eyed Seeing: Strategies for Restoration and Repair through Collaborative Research," develops the framework specifically in connection with collaborative research on the Klamath River and Karuk territory restoration. The paper makes three contributions that the present document carries forward. First, it links the methodological commitment to the material work of land and waterway restoration, tying epistemology to the rectification architecture. Second, it specifies how the commitment operates when the research is initiated, governed, and authorized by an Indigenous nation rather than by a settler institution that invites Indigenous participation; the procedural directionality matters for what the framework can and cannot do. Third, it engages the question of compensation, attribution, and benefit-sharing — the material political economy of cross-traditional research — in a way that subsequent applications of the framework cannot ignore.

Andrea L. Wright, Cara Gonzalez, Albert Marshall (Mi'kmaq), Cheryl Bartlett, and Murdena Marshall (Mi'kmaq) authored a 2019 integrative review in the *International Journal of Qualitative Methods*, "Using Two-Eyed Seeing in Research with Indigenous Peoples: An Integrative Review." The paper surveys the literature and identifies the patterns by which the framework has been used well and the patterns by which it has been instrumentalized as a license for non-Indigenous researchers to claim Indigenous authority for projects that do not honor the framework's commitments. The review's specification of those failure patterns is essential for the present argument: a commitment to multi-eyed seeing that does not internalize the failure patterns will reproduce them at the constitutional level.

Lisa Hall, Cheryl Bartlett, Murdena Marshall (Mi'kmaq), and Albert Marshall (Mi'kmaq), in their 2015 paper in the *International Indigenous Policy Journal*, "Research as Cultural Renewal: Applying Two-Eyed Seeing in a Research Project about Cultural Interventions in First Nations Addictions Treatment," develop the framework's application in health and healing contexts. The paper is significant for this document for what it shows about the framework's capacity to operate where the question is not only knowledge production but social repair — addictions treatment is a domain in which colonial history is materially present in the bodies of those receiving care, and the paper shows how Two-Eyed Seeing must hold that material presence rather than abstract from it.

Across this scholarship, four extensions of the original Marshall framework recur in ways that the present document treats as foundational. The framework moves from a dyadic relational practice toward institutional architectures of shared governance; from a research-methodology commitment toward a commitment that binds the institutions in which research occurs; from a question of how knowledge is produced toward a question of how knowledge authority is allocated; and from an epistemological framework toward a political-economic framework whose commitments include material restitution and the recognition of Indigenous jurisdiction.

### Why the Bilateral Architecture Must Become Multilateral

The standing research-mode prompt of this project names not two traditions but at least eleven, and the prompt's commitment is that the load-bearing scholarship on every major analytical move is drawn from multiple traditions in roughly co-equal weight. This is a different commitment from the Marshall articulation in three respects, each of which requires care.

The first is substantive. The constitutional theory the project pursues is not a question that can be adequately addressed by Indigenous-and-Western bilateral engagement alone, because the constitutional history of the United States is constituted by multiple racial-caste, class, gender, ability, and embodiment hierarchies whose interaction is irreducible to a single Indigenous-Western axis. The Black-radical tradition's analysis of the constitutional architecture of slavery and its afterlives, the dalit tradition's analysis of constitutional caste, the disability-justice tradition's analysis of the constitutional erasure of disabled people from the body politic — these are not subsumable under a generalized Indigenous-Western frame, and to subsume them would domesticate them in exactly the way the Marshall framework warns against. The substantive content of the project's question demands more than two eyes.

The second is positional. The Marshall articulation is grounded in a specific Mi'kmaq positionality and addressed to the specific colonial relation between Indigenous nations and the Canadian and United States settler states. The project's positionality is different: the project is being written from within the constitutional theory of the settler state, by a participant in that constitutional theory, with the explicit purpose of producing a constitutional architecture that the settler state's institutions might adopt. The project does not have the standing to extend the Marshall framework on its own authority; it can only extend the framework with explicit acknowledgment of where its authority comes from and where it does not. The multi-eyed seeing this document advances is, accordingly, articulated as a commitment of the project — a commitment the project takes on and tries to honor — not as a method the project is qualified to teach.

The third is architectural. The Marshall framework's bilateral architecture has the elegance of a relational practice that two parties can sustain through long collaboration. The multilateral architecture this document advances cannot have that elegance, because eleven or more traditions cannot enter into the same kind of dyadic long collaboration. What can replace the dyadic relational practice as the framework's binding mechanism, when the relata are not two but many, becomes the central methodological question of this document. Part Seven specifies the answer the project advances: the binding mechanism becomes a procedural architecture in which the load-bearing analytical authority on each major question is co-produced by simultaneous engagement of relevant traditions, and in which the categories of analysis are themselves specified through cross-traditional engagement rather than imported from a Western default and supplemented from the others.

### The Risk of Domestication and How the Framework Holds It

The most serious objection to the multi-eyed seeing extension is that it domesticates the Marshall framework by incorporating it into a settler-led project's methodological apparatus. The objection has force. Audra Simpson's (Mohawk) work on refusal in *Mohawk Interruptus* (2014) and Glen Coulthard's (Yellowknives Dene) work on the politics of recognition in *Red Skin, White Masks* (2014) both develop the analysis that settler-state offers of inclusion characteristically operate as instruments of dispossession by other means. The settler institution invites the Indigenous tradition to the table; the table remains the settler institution's; the invitation is itself an exercise of the authority it does not relinquish. A multi-eyed seeing that operates this way reproduces the structure it pretends to escape.

The project's response to this objection has three parts, each substantive rather than gestural. First, the project specifies the architectural conditions under which the renovation move it advances is legitimate and the conditions under which it is not. Part Eight develops these conditions in detail; they include the multi-generational rectification commitment with material Land Back, the Indigenous co-governance with FPIC standard at constitutional level, the engagement of Indigenous foundational documents on their own constitutional standing, the seventh-generation criterion as constitutional decision rule, and the multi-eyed seeing methodological commitment treated as substantive rather than ornamental. The conditions are architectural rather than aspirational; under them, the renovation move is the project's answer; without them, the abolitionist or decolonial alternatives become the more honest move and the project must say so. Second, the project takes the directionality of authority seriously: the load-bearing primary texts of the Indigenous constitutional tradition are engaged as primary texts on their own authority, with non-Indigenous scholarly engagement treated as secondary commentary. The Kaianerekowa, the Cherokee Constitution of 1827, the Matike Mai Aotearoa report, the Uluru Statement from the Heart, and comparable documents are not invoked as comparative reference for Western architecture to draw on; they are invoked as primary text whose constitutional authority sets the analytical agenda. Third, the project names the failure mode in advance and asks to be held to it: the multi-eyed seeing framework can fail by domesticating Indigenous and underrepresented traditions even when it nominally honors them, and the failure pattern is identifiable. The project commits to the corrective work of identifying when the failure is happening and refusing to proceed under the failed terms.

### Multi-Eyed Seeing and the Architecture of Plural Authority

The conceptual contribution this document offers, named in its title, is multi-eyed seeing as the architecture of plural authority. The phrase "plural authority" is specific: it names a constitutional architecture in which authority is not allocated to a single tradition that incorporates others as advisory, but is allocated co-equally across traditions whose categories, primary texts, and analytical commitments structure the constitutional question. Plural authority is not pluralism in the standard liberal sense, in which a single overarching framework hosts a diversity of substantive views. It is not multiculturalism in the standard sense, in which a dominant cultural framework hosts and accommodates minority cultural traditions. It is the more demanding commitment that the framework itself is co-constituted, that the categories of analysis are themselves contested across traditions, and that the constitutional architecture must be capable of operating without resolving the contestation by absorption.

This commitment has direct implications for institutional design that subsequent parts of this document develop. The procedural architecture of constitutional drafting, ratification, amendment, and implementation operates under the commitment by ensuring that the load-bearing decisions on each constitutional question are co-produced by representatives of relevant traditions with primary authority — not by a unitary authority that consults representatives of other traditions. The substantive architecture of the constitutional categories (the human, the political, the economic, the ecological, the rational, the deliberative, the just) is specified through cross-traditional engagement, with the recognition that the categories themselves are not neutral and that imported Western categories will distort what the constitution can do. The institutional architecture allocates authority to multiple constitutional bodies — Indigenous nations operating under their own constitutional traditions with FPIC over decisions affecting their territories and peoples; deliberative mini-publics operating under sortition and citizen assembly procedures; elected representative bodies operating under the renovated electoral architecture; commissioners and officers operating under the multi-generational, ecological, and disability-justice commitments — without subordinating any to an apex authority that adjudicates among them.

The shift this represents is large and is worth naming directly. The Madisonian constitutional architecture of 1787 is monolingual in a sense that has been culturally invisible because the language has been the medium of constitutional discourse: the architecture's categories are Western Enlightenment categories deployed in English, with Latin and Greek philosophical antecedents, and the conceptual furniture of the document is taken for granted as the conceptual furniture of constitutional thought as such. The multi-eyed seeing commitment denies that taking-for-granted. It says that the conceptual furniture of constitutional thought is constructed through cross-traditional engagement when constitutional thought is honest about whom the constitution is for and from, and that a constitution adequate to the present crisis and durable for at least the next 250 years cannot be built from monolingual conceptual materials.

### The Commitment as a Learning Process

The final element of Part One returns to the Marshalls' description of Two-Eyed Seeing as "a learning process and not a perfected outcome." The phrase is doing important work in the original articulation, and it must do equally important work in the extension. The honest version of multi-eyed seeing is not a methodology that, when correctly applied, produces multi-traditional knowledge. The honest version is a stance and a practice that recognizes its own incompleteness and that commits to keep working at the points where its eyes have failed. The project's adoption of the multi-eyed seeing commitment carries this commitment forward.

In practice, this means several specific things. It means that the document's claims are open to correction by the traditions whose authority the document recognizes, and that the project's response to such correction must be substantive rather than rhetorical — the document changes when the correction lands, rather than appending a footnote acknowledging the correction without changing what it says. It means that the absence of a tradition's representation on a particular question is not evidence that the tradition has nothing to say on the question; it is evidence that the project has not yet done the work of engaging that tradition. The seven-Black-radical-and-Indigenous-women-historians-of-the-United-States objection — that any constitutional theory not centrally engaging the work of Black women historians of the United States cannot be a serious constitutional theory of the United States — is not a slogan but a recurring reminder of the work the project has not done and is committed to doing. It means that the multi-eyed seeing commitment cannot be used as a shield against criticism that the project has imported unexamined Western conceptual furniture into a particular argument; the commitment is binding only insofar as the project actually responds to such criticism by changing what it says. And it means that the document's authority on the multi-eyed seeing commitment itself is limited by the project's own positionality: the document advances a stance that the traditions whose authority it recognizes are entitled to refuse, modify, or supersede, and the project does not have standing to settle on the extension without the ongoing assent of those traditions' authorized voices.

This is the conceptual and ethical foundation on which the rest of the document builds.
## Part Two — The Indigenous Research-Methodology Tradition

The conceptual extension developed in Part One requires a procedural architecture if it is to be operationalized rather than gestural. The procedural architecture this document inherits is supplied by a generation of Indigenous methodologists whose work has reconstituted the protocols of research from within Indigenous intellectual traditions. The literature is now sufficient to support a developed methodological argument, and the present document treats it as load-bearing rather than as a supplementary methodological appendix. Four primary works anchor the engagement: Linda Tuhiwai Smith's (Ngāti Awa / Ngāti Porou) *Decolonizing Methodologies* (1999, second edition 2012); Shawn Wilson's (Opaskwayak Cree) *Research Is Ceremony* (2008); Margaret Kovach's (Plains Cree / Saulteaux) *Indigenous Methodologies* (2009); and the contemporary Indigenous data sovereignty literature, anchored in Stephanie Russo Carroll's (Ahtna) "CARE Principles for Indigenous Data Governance" (2020) and the Maori and United States Indigenous Data Sovereignty Networks. Each contributes a specific component of the procedural architecture this document operationalizes in Part Seven.

### Linda Tuhiwai Smith and the Reconstitution of the Research Protocol

Linda Tuhiwai Smith's *Decolonizing Methodologies* is the foundational text of the contemporary Indigenous research-methodology tradition. The book's first edition, published in 1999, made the argument that the research relationship between Indigenous peoples and the academy has historically been a relationship of extractive colonial science, in which Indigenous communities have been the objects of research conducted by non-Indigenous researchers serving institutional and disciplinary commitments that did not include the wellbeing or sovereignty of the communities being researched. The second edition, published in 2012, extends the argument and engages the substantial scholarly response the first edition provoked.

Smith's central methodological contribution is to specify that the question of how research is conducted cannot be separated from the question of who has authority over the research, who benefits from it, and who is accountable to whom for what it produces. The methodological move is not the development of a new research technique but the reconstitution of the research relationship under Indigenous-led terms. The book's structure makes this commitment material: it works through the question of what research has historically done to Indigenous peoples, what an Indigenous-led research practice would look like, and what protocols and ethical commitments such a practice requires. The argument is not that non-Indigenous researchers cannot work with Indigenous communities; the argument is that the conditions under which they can do so legitimately are stringent, specifiable, and require a reconstitution of authority over the research at the institutional level.

For the present document's argument, three of Smith's contributions are most consequential. First, the framework's commitment to research-as-relationship, in which the protocol of relationship rather than the technique of data collection structures the research, supplies the procedural architecture for the multi-eyed seeing commitment. The constitutional analog of research-as-relationship is constitution-as-relationship: the constitutional architecture cannot be the unilateral product of one tradition's drafting committee, however well-intentioned, but must emerge from sustained relationships of co-authorship across the traditions whose authority the constitution recognizes. Second, Smith's specification of the twenty-five Indigenous projects — projects of remembering, indigenous self-knowing, claiming, intervening, revitalizing, connecting, reading, writing, representing, and so forth — names a set of activities that an Indigenous-led research program can pursue independently of its relation to the settler academy. The projects are not derivable from a Western research framework; they emerge from the substantive concerns of communities engaged in the work of survival, recovery, and self-determination. The constitutional analog is that the Indigenous traditions whose authority the project recognizes have constitutional projects of their own, and that the project's commitment to multi-eyed seeing must include the commitment to support those projects on their terms rather than to integrate them into the settler-state's constitutional project on its terms. Third, the book's persistent attention to the question of accountability — to whom the researcher is accountable, by what mechanism, with what consequences — names an architectural feature that constitutional frameworks under multi-eyed seeing must possess. The accountability cannot be only formal-procedural; it must be substantive, it must be enforceable, and the consequences of failure must be material rather than rhetorical.

Smith's work is engaged in the second edition with explicit attention to what has and has not changed in the two decades since the first edition. The second edition is honest about the academy's continuing failure to internalize the framework even as the language of decolonization has become widespread, a failure that the present document must not replicate. The 2012 edition's chapter on the politics of indigenous research explicitly engages the failure pattern in which Indigenous methodologies are taken up at the level of vocabulary while the institutional structures that reproduce extractive colonial science remain unchanged. The present document's commitment to multi-eyed seeing operates under that warning: a project that adopts the language of Indigenous methodological commitment without the institutional restructuring that the commitment requires is part of the failure pattern, not a corrective to it.

### Shawn Wilson and Research as Ceremony

Shawn Wilson's *Research Is Ceremony*, published in 2008, develops a methodological framework grounded specifically in Cree intellectual tradition, with parallel engagement of related Indigenous intellectual traditions in Australia. The book's central methodological argument is that research from within Indigenous intellectual traditions is not a means to produce knowledge that is then deployed in the world; it is itself a relational practice that constitutes and reconstitutes relationships, including relationships with knowledge, with the entities being researched, with the communities the research concerns, and with the cosmos within which the research occurs. The metaphor of ceremony is not decorative but methodological: ceremony is the practice through which relationships are constituted and made present, and research properly conducted is itself such a practice.

Wilson's framework specifies an axiology and an ontology under which research must operate. The axiology is relational: the value of research is determined by the quality of the relationships it constitutes and maintains, not by the production of decontextualized knowledge that can be extracted from those relationships. The ontology is also relational: what exists, in this framework, exists in relation, and the entities of research are not isolated objects but nodes in webs of relationship. The methodological consequence is that research cannot be reductionist in the standard Western scientific sense without doing violence to what is being researched. What is researched comes into the research with its relationships intact, and the research methodology must be capable of holding those relationships rather than abstracting from them.

For the constitutional question, Wilson's framework supplies a specification of what relational accountability would mean at the level of constitutional architecture. The Western liberal constitutional tradition has its own relational architectures — the social contract, federalism, separation of powers — but each of these architectures operates within a frame in which the fundamental units are individuals or institutions whose relationships are constituted by contract, statute, or constitutional text. The relational ontology Wilson articulates is more demanding: the fundamental units are themselves constituted in and through relationship, and a constitutional architecture that presumes the prior individuation of its units imports a metaphysical commitment that the Indigenous traditions whose authority the project recognizes do not share. The implication is not that the constitutional architecture must adopt the Indigenous relational ontology and abandon the Western contractual one; it is that the architecture must be capable of holding both ontologies and operating across the categorial difference rather than resolving the difference by importing one ontology and treating the other as figurative.

Wilson's framework also supplies a specification of what accountability looks like when relational ontology is taken seriously. Accountability is not contractual performance; it is the maintenance of relationship through time, with all the obligations, commitments, and reciprocities the relationship sustains. The constitutional architecture under the multi-eyed seeing commitment must include accountability mechanisms of this kind, not only the contractual-procedural mechanisms the Western liberal tradition specifies. Part Seven of this document develops the institutional implementations.

### Margaret Kovach and Indigenous Methodologies as Disciplined Practice

Margaret Kovach's *Indigenous Methodologies* (2009) develops a methodological framework grounded in Plains Cree and Saulteaux intellectual traditions, with attention to the structural challenges of operating these methodologies inside settler-academic institutions. Kovach's contribution to the present argument is the specification of what disciplined Indigenous methodological practice looks like — the protocols, the analytical moves, the writing practices — when the practitioner is operating in institutional contexts that do not by default support such practice.

Kovach articulates a set of methodological commitments that can be summarized as follows. Knowledge is grounded in specific Indigenous intellectual traditions whose categories must be respected on their own terms; the knowledge-keepers of those traditions are not informants but authoritative voices whose teaching is the methodology's substance; the methodologies operate through specific practices — story, dream, conversation, ceremony — whose epistemic standing in the traditions is foundational rather than supplementary; the writing of research must be capable of holding these practices and their epistemic standing rather than reducing them to data; and the evaluation of the research must be conducted in relationship with the traditions whose authority the research recognizes, not by external academic peer review whose criteria are derived from a different tradition.

The constitutional analog is direct. The constitutional architecture under the multi-eyed seeing commitment cannot evaluate its own legitimacy by criteria internal to a single tradition. The Madisonian framework's criteria of legitimacy — popular sovereignty as expressed through ratification, the consent of the governed as expressed through majoritarian institutions, the protection of rights as specified in the Bill of Rights and the Reconstruction Amendments — are criteria internal to the Western liberal tradition. The Indigenous traditions whose authority the project recognizes have their own criteria of constitutional legitimacy: the Kaianerekowa specifies criteria internal to the Haudenosaunee tradition; the Cherokee Constitution of 1827 operates under criteria internal to the Cherokee tradition; the Matike Mai Aotearoa report develops criteria internal to the Maori tradition; the Uluru Statement from the Heart articulates criteria internal to the Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander traditions of Australia. The multi-eyed seeing commitment requires that the constitutional architecture be evaluated by criteria of legitimacy that operate across traditions, not by criteria internal to one. Kovach's specification of cross-traditional evaluation in research methodology is the procedural template for the constitutional move.

Kovach is also clear that operating Indigenous methodologies in settler-academic institutions imposes ongoing strain that the methodology itself cannot resolve. The institutional context of the methodology — the funding structures, the disciplinary norms, the publication systems, the credentialing apparatus — is constituted by traditions other than the ones the methodology operates from, and the practitioner must navigate that strain without pretending it does not exist. The constitutional analog is that the multi-eyed seeing commitment, operationalized in a constitutional architecture that emerges in the settler-state's political institutions, will face the analogous strain at the institutional level. The constitutional architecture cannot pretend the strain away. Part Seven specifies how the procedural architecture must be designed to make the strain visible and to make it actionable rather than dispositive of the project's viability.

### The Indigenous Data Sovereignty Movement

The fourth component of the procedural architecture this document inherits is the Indigenous data sovereignty movement, which has produced specific governance frameworks, principles, and institutional architectures that the constitutional question can draw on directly. The movement's principal published articulation is Stephanie Russo Carroll's (Ahtna) "CARE Principles for Indigenous Data Governance," published in *Data Science Journal* in 2020. The CARE principles — Collective Benefit, Authority to Control, Responsibility, and Ethics — were developed in response to the FAIR principles (Findable, Accessible, Interoperable, Reusable) that have governed open-data science in the past decade. The CARE authors observed that FAIR principles, applied to Indigenous data, treat the data as a resource that should be made widely available without specifying the terms of authority that should govern its use, and that the unintended consequence of FAIR-only governance has been the further extraction of Indigenous data from Indigenous communities by researchers and institutions whose access to it does not include accountability for its use. The CARE principles do not replace FAIR; they specify the additional commitments that operate alongside FAIR when the data concerns Indigenous peoples.

The Maori Data Sovereignty Network, anchored in the work of Tahu Kukutai, John Taylor, and the Te Mana Raraunga collective, has developed parallel frameworks specific to the Aotearoa context. The United States Indigenous Data Sovereignty Network, developing the framework for tribal nations within the United States, has produced specific governance architectures for tribal data governance that operate within the existing legal architecture of tribal sovereignty. The frameworks share a commitment that data about Indigenous peoples is held under Indigenous authority rather than under the unilateral authority of the institutions that collect it.

The constitutional implications are direct. A constitutional architecture under the multi-eyed seeing commitment must specify governance frameworks for data and information in which the authority over data about Indigenous peoples, communities, and territories is held by those peoples and communities, and in which the institutions that produce, store, or analyze such data operate under that authority. The implications extend beyond Indigenous data: the constitutional architecture should specify analogous frameworks for data about other communities whose authority over their own information has been historically denied, including disabled communities, neurodivergent communities, Black communities, and other communities whose data has been deployed in ways that have not respected the authority of those communities over their own information.

This is one of several places where the procedural architecture of Indigenous methodology, developed through long practice in research contexts, supplies a template that the constitutional architecture can adopt directly. The CARE principles, applied at constitutional scale, become the foundation for an information governance architecture under which the constitutional state operates. The procedural-architectural move is from the research relationship to the constitutional relationship, with both relationships structured by the same underlying commitments to authority, accountability, and ethics. Part Seven elaborates the application; the present part establishes that the template exists and is mature enough to be load-bearing.

### What the Procedural Architecture Requires

The four works engaged in this part collectively specify a procedural architecture that the constitutional commitment to multi-eyed seeing can operationalize. The architecture has the following components: a commitment that research and constitutional drafting are relationships rather than products; a specification that the relationships are governed by protocols internal to the traditions the research and constitution recognize; an axiology under which the value of the practice is determined by the quality of the relationships it constitutes and maintains; an ontology under which the entities of the practice are constituted in and through relationship rather than prior to it; an accountability framework whose mechanisms are substantive and enforceable rather than rhetorical; an information governance framework under which authority over data about communities is held by those communities; and a recognition that the practice operates under institutional strain that cannot be resolved by the practice itself but must be made visible and actionable through the architecture.

The architecture is not derived from Western research methodology and supplemented by Indigenous concerns; it is constituted by Indigenous methodologists working from within Indigenous intellectual traditions, with the awareness that the architecture must operate in institutional contexts whose default frameworks are different. The architecture's transferability to constitutional design is the methodological move this document advances; the transferability is not automatic, and the next several parts develop the philosophical, epistemological, and decolonial-theoretical resources that the move requires.

One further matter is essential before proceeding. The procedural architecture this part develops carries a directional commitment that the constitutional move must preserve: the architecture is articulated by Indigenous methodologists working from positions of relative epistemic vulnerability, and the authority of the architecture is grounded in the authority of those methodologists and the traditions they speak from. The constitutional move that adopts this architecture must operate under the same directional commitment: the architecture is not the project's to authorize or revise on its own terms; it is the project's to honor in the way the source traditions specify, with whatever modifications the source traditions authorize. The multi-eyed seeing commitment requires that this directionality be maintained at every level of the architecture's operation; otherwise the architecture is being deployed in service of a project whose authority structure remains unilateral, and the deployment reproduces the failure pattern Smith identifies in the politics of Indigenous research.

Part Three turns to the philosophical resources — standpoint epistemology and epistemic plurality — that supply the deeper grounding for the commitment.
## Part Three — Standpoint Epistemology and Epistemic Plurality

The procedural architecture developed in Part Two requires philosophical grounding if it is to operate at constitutional scale. The grounding cannot come from a single tradition; it must be co-produced across traditions whose commitments converge on the question of whose perspectives count as knowledge-producing under what conditions. This part develops the philosophical foundation through engagement with four bodies of work that the project treats as load-bearing on the standpoint-epistemological move: Sandra Harding's articulation of standpoint epistemology and the science question in feminism, particularly *Whose Science? Whose Knowledge?* (1991) and *Sciences from Below* (2008); Patricia Hill Collins's development of Black feminist standpoint theory in *Black Feminist Thought* (2nd edition 2000) and *Intersectionality as Critical Social Theory* (2019); Donna Haraway's articulation of situated knowledges in "Situated Knowledges: The Science Question in Feminism and the Privilege of Partial Perspective" (1988); and Helen Longino's pluralist philosophy of science in *The Fate of Knowledge* (2002). The four traditions are not interchangeable; each contributes a distinct analytical move that the multi-eyed seeing commitment depends on.

### Sandra Harding and the Science Question

Sandra Harding's standpoint epistemology, developed across more than three decades of writing, is the most extensively elaborated philosophical articulation of the position that knowledge is shaped by the social location of the knower and that the knowledge-producing potential of those whose social locations have been marginalized is not less than the knowledge-producing potential of those whose locations have been dominant — and that, on questions where the marginalized location supplies analytical access the dominant location denies, the marginalized location is epistemically privileged with respect to those questions. The position is sometimes summarized as "the privileged standpoint of the oppressed," but Harding's articulation is more careful than that summary suggests. The privilege is not automatic; it is conditional on the marginalized location being engaged through the appropriate analytical practice, and the analytical practice requires that the location's experience be brought into theoretical articulation through what Harding calls "strong objectivity."

Strong objectivity is Harding's central methodological concept, and it is the concept the present document leans on most heavily. The conventional positivist understanding of objectivity treats objectivity as the maximization of distance between the knower and the known — the elimination of the knower's perspective so that what remains is the unmediated character of the known. Harding's argument is that this conventional understanding is incoherent because the elimination of the knower's perspective is impossible — knowledge always has a knower, and the knower is always located — and the conventional understanding's effect is not the elimination of perspective but the masking of the dominant perspective as no perspective. Strong objectivity rejects this masking. It treats the knower's location as a constitutive feature of the knowledge produced and asks how knowledge can be produced under conditions that make the knower's location explicit and accountable. The answer requires that the knowledge-producing community include knowers from locations that supply analytical access the dominant locations deny, and that the knowledge produced be evaluated by whether it can hold those locations' analytical contributions rather than by criteria internal to the dominant location.

Harding's *Sciences from Below* (2008) extends the framework specifically to engage non-Western knowledge traditions. The book argues that the Western scientific tradition, when honest about its own historical formation, must recognize that the knowledge it has produced has been shaped by its formation in colonial and imperial contexts and that the knowledge it has marginalized has been knowledge that did not serve those contexts. The recovery of marginalized knowledge traditions is therefore not a charitable accommodation of cultural diversity; it is a corrective to a knowledge formation that has been distorted by the social locations of its dominant practitioners. The argument is consequential for the present document because it specifies a framework under which the engagement of Indigenous, Black-radical, dalit, disability-justice, and neurodivergent traditions in constitutional theory is not the addition of perspectives to a complete framework but the corrective recovery of analytical resources that the dominant framework's social location has caused it to lack.

The position has been contested. The principal philosophical objection — that standpoint epistemology either commits to relativism or smuggles in a privileged perspective by another name — has been developed in detail in the analytic philosophy literature, and Harding has engaged the objection in subsequent work. The response specifies that strong objectivity is not relativism: it does not treat all standpoints as equally good for all questions; it specifies that for any given question, some standpoints supply better analytical access than others, and the analytical question is which standpoints those are. The privileged standpoint is not a metaphysical privilege but a methodological consequence of the question being asked. For questions about the operation of caste in constitutional architecture, dalit standpoints are epistemically privileged; for questions about the operation of race in policing, Black standpoints are; for questions about the operation of ability and embodiment in constitutional design, disability-justice standpoints are. The claim is not that other standpoints have nothing to say on the question; it is that the load-bearing analytical access is supplied by the standpoints that have lived under the structures the question concerns.

### Patricia Hill Collins and Black Feminist Standpoint Theory

Patricia Hill Collins's articulation of Black feminist thought in the book of that name — first published in 1990, with the second edition in 2000 substantially extending the argument — develops standpoint epistemology specifically through Black feminist intellectual tradition. Collins's contribution operates at three levels that the present document depends on.

At the first level, Collins specifies that the social location relevant to standpoint analysis is not univariate. The Black feminist standpoint is not a Black standpoint and a feminist standpoint added together; it is constituted by the simultaneous operation of race, gender, class, and other axes of structural location, and the analytical access it supplies is not derivable from the sum of access supplied by single-axis standpoints. The argument is the foundation of what later becomes the analytical move of intersectionality, developed in Crenshaw's work and elsewhere; Collins's contribution to the project's argument is to specify that the analytical privilege of standpoint operates at the level of intersection rather than at the level of single axes. The constitutional implication is direct. A constitutional architecture under the multi-eyed seeing commitment cannot operate by allocating one tradition's voice to one constitutional question and another tradition's voice to another; the load-bearing analytical contributions on most constitutional questions of consequence are intersectional, and the architecture must be capable of holding the intersection.

At the second level, Collins specifies the architecture of Black feminist intellectual tradition itself: its lineages, its primary articulators, its institutional carriers, its modes of theoretical production, its relation to Black women's everyday lives. The argument is that Black feminist thought is not a contemporary academic specialization but a coherent intellectual tradition with its own canonical texts, its own modes of theoretical articulation, and its own criteria of validity. The historical specification — through Maria Stewart, Sojourner Truth, Ida B. Wells, Anna Julia Cooper, the Combahee River Collective, Audre Lorde, June Jordan, Toni Cade Bambara, bell hooks, Audrey Lorde, and the contemporary scholars Collins works alongside — is not antiquarian; it establishes that the analytical authority of Black feminist thought is grounded in a tradition whose history and continuity are integral to its standing. The constitutional implication is that the Black-radical tradition's analytical authority is not derivable from the contemporary academic engagement with that tradition; it is grounded in the tradition's own continuous practice of theoretical articulation, and the constitutional architecture under the multi-eyed seeing commitment must engage that continuous practice rather than only its contemporary academic articulations.

At the third level, Collins's *Intersectionality as Critical Social Theory* (2019) develops the framework as a critical social theory in its own right rather than as a methodological commitment within other frameworks. The argument is consequential for this document because it answers a question that has appeared in the project's prior installments and continues to appear: whether intersectionality is best understood as a methodological commitment internal to other social theories or as a coherent social theory in its own right. Collins's answer is the latter, and the answer carries with it a specification of what intersectionality as social theory commits to: a relational rather than additive understanding of social structure; a specification of structural intersections as constitutive rather than incidental; a methodological commitment to analytical practice that operates at the level of intersection rather than at the level of single axes; and a normative commitment to the analytical authority of those whose social locations are constituted by the intersections in question. The Black feminist tradition's authority on questions of intersectional constitutional analysis is, on this account, the authority of a coherent social theory, not a vocabulary borrowed from a more authoritative theoretical framework.

The position has its critics, including from within the Black-radical tradition itself, and the present document engages those critics with the seriousness they deserve. Adolph Reed Jr., for example, has developed extensive critique of what he treats as the displacement of class analysis by intersectional analysis in contemporary scholarship; Reed's argument is engaged in this project's analytical work in connection with caste analysis (Document B) and in connection with the political-economic critique developed across Documents Seven and Nine. The present document records that the engagement is part of the project's analytical work; it does not adjudicate the contested question of how the relation between intersectional and class analysis should be theorized. The multi-eyed seeing commitment is compatible with multiple positions on that question; what it requires is that the question itself be engaged honestly rather than resolved by assertion.

### Donna Haraway and Situated Knowledges

Donna Haraway's "Situated Knowledges" (1988) supplies a third philosophical foundation. Haraway's argument is best read as a parallel articulation to Harding's strong objectivity, with somewhat different emphasis. Where Harding develops the analytical machinery of standpoint epistemology in the context of feminist philosophy of science, Haraway develops a metaphor and an ethical orientation: knowledges are situated in specific bodies, contexts, and histories; the knowledge-producing relation is not a view from nowhere but a view from somewhere; the epistemological commitment is to take responsibility for the somewhere from which one views and to recognize the views from elsewhere as views, not as data or noise.

The "privilege of partial perspective" — the phrase that appears in Haraway's subtitle and that has been central to her reception — is not an apology for partial perspective but a recognition that all perspective is partial and that the recognition of partiality is the precondition for honest knowledge production. The view from nowhere is incoherent; the view from somewhere, taken responsibly, is the only view available, and the question of what knowledge production looks like when this is recognized is the methodological question Haraway pursues. The answer involves an ethical commitment to responsibility-taking, an analytical commitment to the explicit specification of one's own location, and a relational commitment to engagement with views from elsewhere as views with their own locations rather than as comparison points or correctives.

For the present document, Haraway's contribution is the articulation of an ethical orientation that the multi-eyed seeing commitment requires the project to take. The project's location is specifiable: the project is being written from within the United States constitutional theory tradition, by a participant in that tradition, with the explicit purpose of producing a constitutional architecture that the United States constitutional state might adopt. The view from this location is partial; the project does not pretend it is not. The commitment to multi-eyed seeing is the commitment to engage the views from elsewhere — the views from Indigenous constitutional traditions, from Black-radical constitutional traditions, from dalit constitutional traditions, from disability-justice and neurodivergent traditions — as views with their own locations, and to take responsibility for the location from which the project's own articulation is made. The articulation is not the only possible articulation; it is the articulation made from the project's location, and the views from other locations may displace, modify, or supersede this articulation in ways the project does not have authority to adjudicate.

Haraway's framework has been read by some as undermining the realist commitments of feminist science studies; the reading misunderstands the position. The privilege of partial perspective is not a denial that there is a world that knowledge is about; it is a specification that knowledge of the world is constituted in the relation between the world and located knowers, and that the relation is constitutive of what counts as knowledge of the world. The commitment is realist in the sense that there is something the knowledge is of; it is non-foundationalist in the sense that there is no view from nowhere that can adjudicate among the views from somewhere. The commitment is the philosophical analog of Two-Eyed Seeing's relational practice: the binocular vision is the vision from somewhere, taken responsibly, in relation with the visions from elsewhere.

### Helen Longino and the Social Constitution of Scientific Knowledge

Helen Longino's *The Fate of Knowledge* (2002) develops a distinct but compatible position: a social epistemology under which the constitution of knowledge depends on social processes of critical engagement among knowers from diverse perspectives. Longino's argument is that the cognitive content of scientific knowledge cannot be separated from the social processes of its production; the social processes are constitutive of cognitive content because the very specification of what counts as evidence, what counts as a good hypothesis, what counts as a successful explanation depends on processes of critical engagement that take place in scientific communities. The argument has direct philosophical force against the conventional separation of the context of discovery from the context of justification.

For the present document, Longino's contribution is the philosophical specification of why diverse knower-communities are not optional in the production of reliable knowledge. The conventional view treats the diversity of knower-communities as relevant to questions of fairness or political legitimacy in scientific institutions but as irrelevant to questions of scientific reliability. Longino's argument is that the conventional view is incoherent: the reliability of scientific knowledge depends on the capacity of scientific communities to subject hypotheses to critical engagement from perspectives that can identify the limitations of the dominant perspectives, and the absence of such perspectives compromises reliability rather than only fairness. The argument is the philosophical analog of strong objectivity in Harding's framework, and it converges with Haraway's situated knowledges and Collins's Black feminist standpoint theory on the substantive position that diverse knower-communities are constitutively necessary to reliable knowledge production.

The constitutional implication is that the multi-eyed seeing commitment is not only an ethical or political commitment of the project; it is an epistemological commitment that the reliability of the constitutional architecture depends on. A constitutional architecture produced under conditions that exclude or marginalize the analytical contributions of relevant knower-communities is not only less just than one produced under inclusive conditions; it is less reliable, in the sense that it will fail to anticipate and to address the structural conditions whose analysis depends on those marginalized communities' analytical access. The constitutional architecture's capacity to address constitutional questions of caste, race, embodiment, neurodivergence, ecological catastrophe, and intergenerational obligation depends on the analytical contributions of communities whose lived analytical engagement with these questions exceeds what any single tradition can supply. The exclusion of these contributions is not only a moral failure but a reliability failure.

### The Convergence and the Constitutional Implication

The four bodies of work engaged in this part — Harding's strong objectivity, Collins's Black feminist standpoint theory, Haraway's situated knowledges, Longino's social epistemology — operate from distinct intellectual traditions and are not reducible to a single position. The convergence is not a unified philosophical theory but a recognition that knowledge production under conditions of structural marginalization requires analytical practices that the conventional view cannot supply. The convergence is what makes the multi-eyed seeing commitment philosophically coherent rather than only ethically attractive.

For the constitutional question, the convergence specifies that the procedural architecture developed in Part Two — the architecture of relational research, accountability, Indigenous data sovereignty, and the protocols of disciplined Indigenous methodological practice — is not a culturally specific accommodation that the project happens to undertake out of respect for Indigenous traditions. The architecture is the operationalization of an epistemological commitment that the project undertakes because the constitutional question's reliability depends on it. The standpoint-epistemological grounding of the procedural architecture is essential because it specifies that the procedural architecture is not optional, not contingent, not a matter of cultural style. It is constitutively required by the nature of the question the project pursues.

The grounding has limits, and the document specifies them. Standpoint epistemology, even in its strongest articulations, does not specify how disagreements among standpoints should be adjudicated when they arise. The framework specifies that the multi-eyed seeing commitment requires engagement of multiple standpoints in roughly co-equal weight; it does not, on its own, specify what to do when the standpoints diverge on a question of constitutional consequence. The next part — Part Four, on epistemic injustice and resistance — develops the analytical apparatus for diagnosing whose voices are being heard, whose are being dismissed, and whose interpretive frameworks are being credited under what conditions. The apparatus is not a decision procedure for adjudicating among standpoints; it is the diagnostic infrastructure that makes the question of adjudication operate in good faith rather than under the masking of the dominant perspective as no perspective.

A final orienting point. The standpoint-epistemological commitment this part develops is sometimes received as a position primarily concerned with whose voices count, treated as a question of political legitimacy. The position is more demanding than that. It specifies that the categories of analysis themselves — the human, the political, the economic, the ecological, the rational, the deliberative, the just — are constituted in social processes that include some communities and exclude others, and that the categories developed under conditions of exclusion will fail to capture what the included categories can capture only when the previously excluded communities' analytical contributions are taken into the constitutive process. The constitutional architecture under multi-eyed seeing must therefore be willing to renegotiate its own categories in response to the analytical contributions of communities whose constitutive contribution has been excluded. This is the substantive content of the commitment that Part Seven operationalizes; the present part has supplied the philosophical grounding for why the substantive content is what it is.
## Part Four — Epistemic Injustice and Epistemic Resistance

The standpoint-epistemological grounding developed in Part Three specifies why diverse knower-communities are constitutively necessary to reliable knowledge production. It does not, on its own, supply the diagnostic apparatus by which constitutional architectures can be evaluated for their treatment of those communities' analytical contributions. The diagnostic apparatus this part develops is supplied by the literature on epistemic injustice and epistemic resistance, anchored in three primary works: Miranda Fricker's *Epistemic Injustice: Power and the Ethics of Knowing* (2007); José Medina's *The Epistemology of Resistance: Gender and Racial Oppression, Epistemic Injustice, and Resistant Imaginations* (2013); and Kristie Dotson's "Tracking Epistemic Violence, Tracking Practices of Silencing" (2011). The diagnostic apparatus these works provide is essential to the constitutional question because it permits the explicit naming of failure modes — the specific ways in which constitutional decision-making can fail to credit the analytical contributions of marginalized communities — and thereby permits constitutional architecture to be designed against those failure modes.

### Miranda Fricker's Twofold Diagnosis

Miranda Fricker's *Epistemic Injustice* (2007) developed a now-canonical analytical framework for naming two distinct forms of injustice in the epistemic relations between knowers. The first, testimonial injustice, occurs when the credibility of a speaker's testimony is unjustly deflated because of identity prejudice on the hearer's part. The hearer hears the speaker through the lens of stereotypes that distort the hearer's reception of what the speaker is saying; the speaker's testimony is discounted, not because of any defect in the testimony itself, but because of the hearer's prejudicial reception. The second, hermeneutical injustice, occurs when a community lacks the conceptual resources to make sense of a significant portion of its members' social experience because the conceptual resources have been generated under conditions that have excluded the relevant members from the conceptual production. Members of the community whose experience is unintelligible to the community's available concepts cannot make their experience intelligible even to themselves through those concepts; the experience is "hermeneutically marginalized," in Fricker's phrase, even when it is widely shared.

The constitutional applications of the twofold diagnosis are direct and consequential. Testimonial injustice operates throughout the constitutional decision-making architecture: in the differential reception of testimony from witnesses, plaintiffs, advocates, and parties before legislative committees, regulatory bodies, and courts; in the differential reception of constituents' communications with elected representatives; in the differential reception of expert testimony from experts whose authority is grounded in non-dominant traditions. The Black-radical tradition's analysis of the differential reception of Black women's testimony in legal and political institutions, the dalit tradition's analysis of the differential reception of dalit voices in Indian constitutional and legal institutions, the disability-justice tradition's analysis of the differential reception of disabled persons' testimony about their own experience, the Indigenous tradition's analysis of the differential reception of Indigenous testimony in settler-state legal systems — all are forms of the testimonial injustice Fricker names, each with its own specific features and its own analytical literature.

Hermeneutical injustice operates at the level of constitutional concept itself. The categories the constitutional architecture deploys — citizenship, property, person, contract, sovereignty, due process, equal protection, reasonable accommodation — are categories generated under conditions that have systematically excluded the analytical contributions of marginalized communities, and the categories' inability to do justice to those communities' constitutional concerns is a form of hermeneutical injustice operating at constitutional scale. The Black-radical critique of property that Cheryl Harris develops in "Whiteness as Property" (1993) is centrally a critique of hermeneutical injustice at the level of constitutional concept. The disability-justice critique of "reasonable accommodation" — which assumes that the standard institutional architecture is the unmarked default and that disabled persons' accommodation requires modification of an otherwise complete architecture — is a form of hermeneutical injustice at the level of constitutional concept. The neurodivergent critique of "competence" as it operates in legal frameworks of capacity is a form of hermeneutical injustice at the level of constitutional concept. The Indigenous critique of "sovereignty" as it operates in settler-state legal frameworks for tribal nations is a form of hermeneutical injustice at the level of constitutional concept. In each case, the critique specifies that the conceptual category itself has been generated under conditions that exclude the analytical contributions of the community whose experience the category is supposed to describe, and that the category therefore cannot do justice to that community's constitutional concerns without renegotiation.

Fricker's framework has limits, and the present document engages them rather than pretending otherwise. The framework's principal limit, identified in subsequent literature most pointedly by Kristie Dotson, is that the framework operates at the level of individual epistemic relations and treats the prejudicial dispositions of individual hearers as the primary site of testimonial injustice. The framework can be extended to institutional contexts, but the extension is not automatic, and the framework's individualist starting point can mask structural features of epistemic injustice that operate independently of any particular hearer's dispositions. Dotson's contribution, developed in the section below, supplies the corrective.

### José Medina and the Epistemology of Resistance

José Medina's *The Epistemology of Resistance* (2013) develops an extension of Fricker's framework specifically oriented to the analytical practices through which marginalized communities resist the epistemic injustices they suffer. Medina's argument is that the epistemic agency of the oppressed is not exhausted by the diagnosis of how that agency has been distorted; it includes the active analytical practices through which the oppressed develop the conceptual resources, the testimonial networks, and the resistant imagination that enable them to articulate the injustices they suffer and to develop alternatives. The epistemology of resistance is the philosophical study of these practices.

Medina specifies four features of resistant epistemic practice that the present document carries forward. First, resistant epistemic practice is collective: it operates through the analytical and interpretive networks of communities that have developed the resources for articulating their experience under conditions of marginalization. The Black-radical tradition's interpretive networks, the dalit tradition's interpretive networks, the disability-justice tradition's interpretive networks, the Indigenous traditions' interpretive networks — each is a collective epistemic practice in Medina's sense, with its own modes of analytical production and its own criteria of validity. Second, resistant epistemic practice operates against the testimonial and hermeneutical structures that have produced the marginalization; the practice is not the passive reception of marginalization-produced concepts but the active production of alternative concepts that the dominant framework has not been able to produce. Third, resistant epistemic practice is grounded in lived experience and material conditions; it is not derivable from the dominant framework's analytical categories alone, because the dominant framework's categories have been constituted under conditions that exclude the experience and conditions in question. Fourth, resistant epistemic practice has its own internal disciplines and standards; it is not the absence of methodological rigor but the presence of methodological rigor under conditions the dominant framework has not been able to recognize as such.

For the constitutional question, Medina's framework supplies a specification that the analytical contributions of marginalized communities to constitutional theory are not derivable from the dominant framework's analytical categories and supplemented by the marginalized communities' lived experience as data. The contributions are produced through resistant epistemic practices whose authority is grounded in the practices themselves, and the constitutional architecture under the multi-eyed seeing commitment must engage those practices on their own terms. The Black-radical tradition's analysis of constitutional racial subordination is not data that the dominant constitutional theory can incorporate as supplementary case material; it is a coherent analytical framework with its own primary texts, its own canonical works, its own methodological commitments, and its own criteria of validity, and the dominant constitutional theory's engagement with it must be on the terms the tradition specifies rather than on the terms the dominant theory specifies.

The argument has consequential implications for citation practice and for the procedural architecture of constitutional drafting. The citation practice this project commits to — load-bearing citation in roughly co-equal weight across traditions — is a methodological consequence of the recognition that the analytical contributions of marginalized traditions are not derivable from the dominant tradition. The procedural architecture this project advances — co-equal authority on constitutional questions across traditions whose analytical contributions are constitutive — is a methodological consequence of the same recognition. Without the recognition, the multi-eyed seeing commitment can be operated in form while being violated in substance; the analytical contributions of marginalized traditions can be cited and incorporated without the dominant tradition giving up its position as the framework that hosts and adjudicates the contributions.

### Kristie Dotson and the Structures of Epistemic Violence

Kristie Dotson's "Tracking Epistemic Violence, Tracking Practices of Silencing" (2011) develops a framework that operates at a different level from Fricker's and Medina's, though in compatible direction. Dotson's analytical move is to specify that epistemic injustice operates through structural practices of silencing that are not reducible to the prejudicial dispositions of individual hearers and that are constitutive of the institutional contexts in which speakers and hearers operate. The principal failure modes Dotson names are testimonial quieting — in which the speaker is structurally prevented from offering testimony — and testimonial smothering — in which the speaker, anticipating the hearer's incompetence to receive the testimony, withholds the testimony. Both failure modes operate independently of any particular hearer's dispositions and are produced by the structural features of the contexts in which speakers and hearers operate.

The constitutional applications are extensive. The architecture of constitutional decision-making in the Madisonian framework is structured by mechanisms of testimonial quieting that operate through formal procedural exclusions: the pre-1965 disenfranchisement of Black voters in the South was a mechanism of testimonial quieting at constitutional scale; the Slaughter-House Cases (1873) and the subsequent Reconstruction-era jurisprudence quieted the Reconstruction Amendments' transformation of the constitutional architecture by reading the amendments narrowly; the contemporary jurisprudence on standing in environmental and Indigenous-rights cases — most pointedly *Lujan v. Defenders of Wildlife* (1992) — operates as testimonial quieting by structurally excluding plaintiffs whose injury cannot be specified in the framework's preferred terms; the contemporary jurisprudence on tribal jurisdiction — most pointedly *Oliphant v. Suquamish Indian Tribe* (1978) — operates as testimonial quieting by structurally excluding tribal authority over non-Indians whose conduct affects tribal members within tribal territory.

Testimonial smothering operates throughout the constitutional decision-making architecture as well. The phenomenon by which marginalized communities anticipate the incompetence of dominant institutions to receive their testimony and accordingly withhold the testimony or modify it for those institutions' reception is widely documented across the literatures the project engages: the documentation in the Black-feminist tradition by Patricia Hill Collins, in the disability-justice tradition by Mia Mingus and Leah Lakshmi Piepzna-Samarasinha, in the Indigenous research-methodology tradition by Linda Tuhiwai Smith and Margaret Kovach, in the dalit tradition by B.R. Ambedkar's analysis of the structural conditions under which the testimony of untouchables is received in Indian legal and political institutions. The constitutional implication is that the architecture under which testimony is offered to constitutional decision-making bodies is currently structured to produce testimonial smothering at scale, with the result that the bodies receive a systematically distorted record of the constitutional concerns the marginalized communities actually have. The architecture must be redesigned if the bodies are to receive testimony under conditions of competent reception.

Dotson's framework also engages the structural conditions under which hermeneutical injustice operates. The conceptual resources available to a community are not products of individual conceptual production but of the institutional, political, and cultural infrastructure through which concepts are produced, circulated, and validated. The infrastructure is structurally biased toward the conceptual production of dominant communities and structurally hostile to the conceptual production of marginalized communities. The remedy for hermeneutical injustice is therefore not the addition of marginalized communities' concepts to a continuing dominant framework but the restructuring of the conceptual infrastructure to permit the conceptual production of marginalized communities to operate on the same terms as the conceptual production of dominant communities. The constitutional analog is that the constitutional architecture's conceptual production — its categories, its protocols of category-revision, its institutional structures for the development of new concepts — must be restructured under the multi-eyed seeing commitment to permit conceptual production from marginalized traditions to operate on the same terms as conceptual production from the dominant tradition.

### The Constitutional Diagnostic

The three works engaged in this part collectively supply a diagnostic apparatus that the constitutional architecture under the multi-eyed seeing commitment can use to evaluate its own operation. The diagnostic operates at four levels.

At the first level, the architecture's operation should be evaluated for testimonial injustice in Fricker's sense: are the testimonial contributions of marginalized communities being received with the credibility they merit, or are they being discounted through identity-prejudicial reception? The evaluation requires that the architecture include mechanisms for tracking the differential reception of testimonial contributions and for correcting the differential reception when it is identified. The mechanisms cannot be only formal-procedural; they must be substantive, with the capacity to identify when prejudicial reception is operating and to disrupt the operation when it is.

At the second level, the architecture's operation should be evaluated for hermeneutical injustice: are the constitutional concepts the architecture deploys capable of doing justice to the constitutional concerns of marginalized communities, or are they generating systematic categorical mismatch between the concerns the communities have and the concerns the architecture can address? The evaluation requires that the architecture include mechanisms for the renegotiation of constitutional concepts in response to the analytical contributions of marginalized communities. The mechanisms cannot be only marginal-amendment; they must include the capacity to revisit foundational concepts when foundational mismatch is identified.

At the third level, the architecture's operation should be evaluated for testimonial quieting and smothering in Dotson's sense: are there structural features of the architecture that prevent testimony from being offered or that lead to the modification of testimony for the architecture's reception? The evaluation requires that the architecture include mechanisms for identifying structural quieting and smothering and for redesigning the structural features that produce them. The mechanisms cannot be only individual-correction; they must include the capacity to redesign procedural and institutional architecture in response to identified structural failure.

At the fourth level, the architecture's operation should be evaluated for resistant epistemic practice in Medina's sense: are the analytical contributions of marginalized communities being engaged on the terms the resistant epistemic practices specify, or are they being absorbed into the dominant framework on the dominant framework's terms? The evaluation requires that the architecture include mechanisms for the recognition of resistant epistemic practices as authoritative on their own terms and for the architecture's accountability to those practices' criteria of validity rather than to the dominant framework's criteria.

The diagnostic apparatus is consequential because it specifies the failure modes the constitutional architecture under the multi-eyed seeing commitment must be designed against. Without the diagnostic apparatus, the multi-eyed seeing commitment can be operated in form while being violated in substance. With the diagnostic apparatus, the operation of the commitment can be tracked, the failures can be identified, and the architecture can be redesigned in response. The next part — Part Five, on decolonial theory and the pluriverse — develops the theoretical resources for understanding why the failure modes are not accidental but constitutive of the modern/colonial framework within which constitutional theory has historically operated, and why the multi-eyed seeing commitment requires a deeper architectural transformation than the diagnostic alone can deliver.

### A Note on the Project's Own Operation

Before turning to that next part, the document records that the diagnostic apparatus must be applied to the project's own operation. The project is being written from within the dominant constitutional theory tradition, by a participant in that tradition, with the resources of the dominant tradition's institutional, political, and cultural infrastructure. The project's operation is therefore subject to the same failure modes the diagnostic apparatus identifies in constitutional architectures generally. The project's testimonial reception of analytical contributions from marginalized traditions can be distorted by identity prejudice operating through the project's own analytical practice; the project's hermeneutical resources can be inadequate to the constitutional concerns of marginalized communities even when the project nominally engages those concerns; the project's procedural architecture can produce testimonial quieting and smothering of the contributions of marginalized communities even when the project nominally invites those contributions; the project can absorb the analytical contributions of marginalized communities into its own framework on its own terms even when the project nominally engages those communities on their terms.

The acknowledgment is not a confession that excuses the failures; it is a specification that the diagnostic apparatus binds the project as well as the architectures the project examines. The project's operation under the multi-eyed seeing commitment must be subject to the same diagnostic that the constitutional architectures must be subject to, and the project commits to the corrective work of identifying when its operation is failing and of changing what it does when the failure is identified. The commitment cannot be honored in this single document; it must be honored in the project's continuing operation, in the project's responsiveness to correction, and in the project's willingness to change its analytical practice when the analytical practice is identified as failing.
## Part Five — Decolonial Theory and the Pluriverse

The diagnostic apparatus developed in Part Four supplies the analytical machinery for identifying the failure modes the constitutional architecture under the multi-eyed seeing commitment must be designed against. The diagnostic does not, on its own, specify why the failure modes are not accidental — why they recur across architectures, across institutions, across centuries — and what deeper architectural transformation the multi-eyed seeing commitment ultimately requires. The theoretical resources for that deeper analysis are supplied by the decolonial tradition, which the present document treats as load-bearing alongside the standpoint-epistemological and Indigenous-methodological traditions. Four works anchor the engagement: Aníbal Quijano's "Coloniality of Power, Eurocentrism, and Latin America" (2000); Walter Mignolo's *The Darker Side of Western Modernity: Global Futures, Decolonial Options* (2011); Catherine Walsh's "Decoloniality In/As Praxis," published in the joint volume with Mignolo (2018); and Arturo Escobar's *Designs for the Pluriverse: Radical Interdependence, Autonomy, and the Making of Worlds* (2018).

### Aníbal Quijano and the Coloniality of Power

Aníbal Quijano's articulation of the coloniality of power, developed across a substantial body of work and condensed in the 2000 article that has become the most-cited statement of the position, is the foundation of the contemporary decolonial tradition. Quijano's central analytical move is to specify that what is conventionally called "colonialism" — the formal political domination of colonized territories by colonizing states — was a historically specific phase of a deeper structural formation, the coloniality of power, which extends beyond the formal colonial period and continues to operate after formal decolonization. The coloniality of power names a structural relationship in which the constitution of modernity is inseparable from the constitution of coloniality: modernity is not a phenomenon of European intellectual history that subsequently spread to colonized regions, but a phenomenon co-constituted with coloniality such that the categories of modernity — race, capitalism, the state, the human, the rational — are themselves products of the colonial relation rather than universal categories that the colonial relation imported.

The argument has several specific components that the present document depends on. Race is articulated by Quijano not as a pre-existing biological or cultural category that colonialism deployed but as a category that was produced through the colonial encounter and that has structured the global division of labor, the global organization of knowledge, and the global organization of political authority since the sixteenth century. The category did not exist in its modern form before the colonial encounter; it was produced in and through the encounter and has been the structural matrix within which modernity has organized itself. The capitalist world-system is articulated similarly: not as an economic phenomenon that subsequently took on racial and colonial dimensions but as a system constituted from its origins by the racial-colonial division of labor, with the racial-colonial division being a structural feature of capitalism rather than a contingent overlay. The categories of modern political philosophy — sovereignty, state, citizenship, the rational individual — are articulated as products of the same historical formation, with their content shaped by the colonial relation rather than by abstract philosophical inquiry whose results were subsequently applied to the colonial situation.

The constitutional implications are extensive and consequential. The constitutional theory of the United States, on Quijano's analysis, cannot be understood as the application of universal Enlightenment principles to a particular political situation; it must be understood as a specific articulation of a colonial-modern formation whose categories are themselves products of that formation. The "consent of the governed," "the rational individual," "the social contract," "natural rights" — each of these categories carries with it the historical specificity of its formation in a colonial-modern world, and each of them does conceptual work that includes the structural features of the world from which it emerged. The work of constitutional renovation, on this analysis, cannot proceed by appealing to the foundational principles as if they were universal and applying them more thoroughly; it must proceed by recognizing that the principles themselves are constituted by the colonial-modern formation and that the renovation must include the renegotiation of the principles' content under conditions that take seriously the analytical contributions of the traditions the colonial-modern formation has marginalized.

Quijano's framework is not without contestation, including from within the decolonial tradition itself. The principal contestations concern the framework's treatment of class analysis, its specification of the relation between race and capitalism, its treatment of gender, and its specification of what alternatives to coloniality might consist in. The present document engages those contestations as part of the analytical work without adjudicating them; the multi-eyed seeing commitment is compatible with multiple positions on the internal contestations of the decolonial tradition, and the project's authority does not extend to settling internal contestations within the traditions whose authority the project recognizes.

### Walter Mignolo and Decolonial Options

Walter Mignolo's *The Darker Side of Western Modernity* (2011) develops the decolonial framework in directions that the present document depends on heavily. Mignolo's articulation of decolonial options — the alternative pathways that decolonial thought makes available — is structured by a distinction between de-westernization and decoloniality. De-westernization names the political-economic project of states, regions, and movements that seek to exit the dominance of Western political-economic frameworks while operating substantially within the categorial framework of modern political economy: alternative capitalisms, alternative state-forms, alternative regional orderings. Decoloniality names the deeper analytical and ontological project of articulating the categories of political life from outside the colonial-modern formation, with the recognition that the categories are not infinitely malleable within the formation but require the formation's transformation to be transformed.

The distinction is consequential for the project's argument because it permits the specification that the renovation move the project advances is itself a position within decolonial thought rather than an alternative to decolonial thought. The renovation of constitutional architecture under the multi-eyed seeing commitment is not de-westernization in Mignolo's sense — it is not the assertion of an alternative framework within the categorial structure of modern political economy. It is, on the project's specification, an attempt at decoloniality conducted with awareness that the attempt is conducted from within the colonial-modern formation and that the attempt's success cannot be measured by the formation's criteria. The attempt is therefore necessarily partial, necessarily contestable, and necessarily subject to the analytical contributions of those whose authority extends beyond the formation's categories.

Mignolo's specification of pluri-versality — the recognition that there are multiple worlds within the world, and that the modern/colonial framework's claim to universality is itself the imposition of one world over others — is the conceptual hinge that connects Mignolo's framework to Escobar's pluriverse and to the broader decolonial articulation of constitutional alternatives. Pluri-versality is not pluralism in the standard liberal sense, in which multiple substantive views are held within a single overarching framework. Pluri-versality is the recognition that the framework itself is plural, that the categories are contested across worlds, and that the constitutional architecture under decolonial conditions must be capable of operating across plural framings rather than within a single dominant one.

The constitutional implications converge with what Part One of this document specified about plural authority. The architecture cannot allocate authority to a single tradition that incorporates others as advisory; it must allocate authority across traditions whose categorial frameworks are recognized as constitutive rather than as subsumable under a single overarching framework. The pluri-versality commitment is not an aspirational addition to the constitutional architecture but a structural feature without which the architecture cannot operate under decolonial conditions.

### Catherine Walsh and Decoloniality as Praxis

Catherine Walsh's contribution, particularly in "Decoloniality In/As Praxis" in the joint volume with Mignolo, develops the decolonial framework in directions that emphasize the practical, material, and pedagogical dimensions of the commitment. Walsh's articulation grounds decoloniality in the lived practices of communities engaged in the work of decolonial transformation — the practices of Black, Indigenous, and popular movements in the Andean region and across Latin America — rather than in the philosophical articulations alone. The argument is that decoloniality is not a theoretical position that can be fully articulated philosophically and then applied; it is a praxis that is constituted in the practices of communities engaged in the material work of decolonial transformation, and the philosophical articulations are accountable to those practices.

The constitutional implications include a specification that the constitutional architecture under the multi-eyed seeing commitment is accountable to the lived practices of communities engaged in decolonial work, and that the architecture's authority is grounded in the relation it sustains with those practices rather than in its own internal coherence. The architecture is not a finished philosophical product that the practices are then asked to operate under; it is a structural support for the practices, accountable to the practices' continuing development, and subject to revision when the practices' development calls for it. The position is consonant with what Part Two specified through engagement with the Indigenous research-methodology tradition: research-as-relationship and constitution-as-relationship are forms of the same commitment, and the commitment binds the constitutional architecture in the same way it binds the research practice.

Walsh's emphasis on the pedagogical dimensions of decolonial praxis is also consequential. The constitutional architecture under the multi-eyed seeing commitment is itself a pedagogical formation in the sense that the architecture's operation includes the formation of the political subjectivities, capacities, and practices through which it is operated. The architecture is not a structure that operates over a pre-existing population; it is a structure that participates in the formation of the population, and the formation can be conducted under decolonial conditions only when the pedagogical dimensions are recognized as such. Part Seven of the present document develops the implications for institutional design; the present part has supplied the analytical specification.

### Arturo Escobar and Designs for the Pluriverse

Arturo Escobar's *Designs for the Pluriverse* (2018) is the most extensively elaborated articulation of how the decolonial commitment to pluri-versality can be operationalized in design practice — design here understood broadly to include institutional design, constitutional design, and the design of the relations through which communities organize their material and political life. Escobar's argument is that the dominant design tradition, which has emerged from the modern/colonial framework, treats design as the application of universal principles to particular contexts, with the universal principles being articulated abstractly and the particular contexts being treated as instantiations to which the universal principles apply with appropriate modification. The design tradition under decolonial conditions must operate differently: it must treat the specific worlds within which design occurs as themselves constitutive of the design rather than as instances to which abstract principles apply.

The Latin American autonomist movements, the Black quilombola communities of Brazil and the broader Afro-Latin American region, the Indigenous communities of the Andes and Amazonia, and the popular movements that Escobar engages across the book provide the specific material from which the framework develops. The design practices these communities have developed are not derivable from the dominant design tradition's universal principles; they emerge from the specific worlds within which the communities live and through which their practices are constituted. The design practices include practices of territorial organization, communal economic organization, governance, conflict resolution, and intergenerational continuity that have been constituted under conditions distinct from those of the modern/colonial framework. The practices are not reducible to the framework's categories; they constitute the analytical material from which alternative frameworks emerge.

For the constitutional question, Escobar's framework supplies a specification of how design under decolonial conditions can be conducted across plural worlds. The constitutional architecture under the multi-eyed seeing commitment is design under decolonial conditions; it operates across plural worlds whose categorial frameworks are constitutive of the architecture rather than incidental to it. The procedural architecture of constitutional drafting must therefore include mechanisms for the participation of the worlds whose constitutive contribution is required, with the participation operating on the worlds' own terms rather than on terms set by a unitary design authority. Escobar's specification of relational design, autonomist design, and pluriversal design supplies the conceptual apparatus that the constitutional architecture's procedural design can adopt.

The pluriverse, in Escobar's articulation, is not a liberal-pluralist accommodation of cultural diversity within a continuing modern/colonial framework; it is the recognition that the framework itself is one world among many and that the others are not derivable from it. The recognition is not anti-modern in the sense of rejecting modernity in favor of pre-modern alternatives; it is post-modern in the sense of operating beyond the modern/colonial formation while engaging the formation's intellectual products with the seriousness their continuing operation merits. The recognition is not anti-Western in the sense of rejecting Western traditions; it is post-Western in the sense of operating beyond the singular authority of the Western tradition while engaging the tradition's intellectual products with the seriousness their continuing operation merits. The constitutional architecture under the multi-eyed seeing commitment is, on this analysis, post-Western in Escobar's sense, with the post specifying not the abandonment of the Western tradition but the abandonment of its singular authority.

The renovation move — the project's commitment to producing a constitutional architecture that the United States constitutional state might adopt, with the architecture incorporating the analytical contributions of the traditions the project recognizes as co-equal — is a move that operates within the modern/colonial formation and that asks the formation to be transformed under conditions the formation does not yet support. The decolonial framework's commitment to operating beyond the formation rather than transforming it from within raises a structural question about whether the renovation move is consistent with the framework's analytical commitments.

A further matter requires explicit engagement before turning to Part Six. The decolonial framework's commitment to pluri-versality and to operating across plural worlds raises a question about whether the framework's articulation in academic philosophical literature is itself a form of incorporation of the worlds it engages into the modern/colonial framework's intellectual apparatus. The question is internal to the decolonial tradition and has been engaged extensively by Indigenous scholars, Black-feminist scholars, and decolonial scholars themselves. The position the present document takes is that the decolonial tradition's philosophical articulation is one engagement among possible engagements with the worlds the tradition seeks to make space for, and that the engagement's legitimacy depends on its accountability to the worlds' own articulations rather than on the philosophical articulation's adequacy to the worlds. The decolonial tradition's philosophers have generally been explicit about this dependence; the present document records the dependence and operates under it.

## Part Six — The Neurodivergent Contribution to Epistemic Plurality

The standpoint-epistemological, decolonial, and Indigenous-methodological resources developed in Parts One through Five supply much of the philosophical and procedural architecture the multi-eyed seeing commitment requires. They do not, on their own, supply the analytical resources that the neurodivergent tradition contributes — resources that are essential to the constitutional question because the architecture of constitutional decision-making operates through cognitive and deliberative practices whose default specification is neurotypical, and the neurotypical default has been culturally invisible in constitutional theory for the same reason it has been culturally invisible elsewhere. This part develops the neurodivergent contribution through engagement with five primary works and one extension that the project treats as load-bearing on the deliberative-democracy and constitutional-method questions: Nick Walker's *Neuroqueer Heresies: Notes on the Neurodiversity Paradigm, Autistic Empowerment, and Postnormal Possibilities* (2021); Robert Chapman's *Empire of Normality: Neurodiversity and Capitalism* (2023); M. The project's prior installments — particularly Document Seven on minds, markets, and the sacred, and the engagement of Luke et al. ### The Neurodiversity Paradigm and the Critique of the Neurotypical Default

The neurodiversity paradigm, articulated principally in Nick Walker's *Neuroqueer Heresies* (2021) and developed in convergent directions by autistic, ADHD, and other neurodivergent scholars and movements, names a position that the project treats as foundational. The paradigm's central commitment is that the variation in human neurological organization is not pathological deviation from a neurotypical norm but constitutive variation comparable to other forms of human diversity — sexual, gender, ethnic, racial — and that the framing of the variation as pathology is a historical-political product rather than an empirical-scientific finding. The paradigm distinguishes between the recognition that some neurodivergent individuals experience disability or distress (a recognition the paradigm shares with the medical model) and the conclusion that the neurodivergent organization itself is the source of the disability or distress (a conclusion the paradigm rejects). The disability or distress, on the paradigm's analysis, emerges in the relation between the neurodivergent individual and the social environments organized around the neurotypical default; the relation is alterable, and the alteration is properly the object of social and political work rather than only of clinical intervention.

The constitutional implications of the paradigm are extensive. The architecture of constitutional decision-making — committee deliberation, public hearings, town meetings, courtroom testimony, electoral participation, jury service — is organized around cognitive and deliberative practices whose specification has been neurotypical. The practices include rapid verbal response, sustained attention to extended deliberation in which the analytical content is delivered through speech, navigation of social environments structured by neurotypical conventions of facial expression and body language, response to the implicit and explicit social hierarchies of the deliberative space, and management of the sensory and attentional load of the deliberative environment. Each of these practices is more demanding for many neurodivergent individuals than for the neurotypical default; the demand has been culturally invisible because the default has been culturally invisible; and the constitutional architecture's reception of the analytical contributions of neurodivergent participants has been distorted as a consequence.

The phenomenon is the constitutional analog of the testimonial injustice and the testimonial smothering Part Four developed: neurodivergent participants' testimonial contributions are distorted by the neurotypical structure of their reception, and the structural conditions of the reception lead neurodivergent participants to withhold or modify their testimonial contributions for the architecture's reception. The constitutional architecture under the multi-eyed seeing commitment must be redesigned to receive testimonial contributions under conditions that do not impose neurotypical default structures on their offering and reception; the redesign cannot be only cosmetic accommodation of neurodivergent participants in an otherwise neurotypical architecture but must include the architectural transformation that the disability-justice tradition and the neurodivergent tradition specify.

### Robert Chapman and the Political Economy of Normality

Robert Chapman's *Empire of Normality: Neurodiversity and Capitalism* (2023) develops the neurodiversity paradigm in directions that connect it to the political-economic critique the project's installments on macroeconomic theory and on transformative legislation have developed. Chapman's argument is that the construction of normality — the organization of human variation around a default that is treated as healthy, productive, and contributing while deviation from the default is treated as deficient, unproductive, and burdensome — is not a free-standing cultural phenomenon but a structural feature of the political economy of capitalist accumulation. The construction of the normal worker, normal student, normal citizen has emerged in tandem with the labor-market and educational architecture of capitalist economies, and the categorization of neurodivergent humans as deficient relative to that construction is the categorial product of the political economy rather than a finding about human variation that the political economy has discovered.

The argument is consequential for the project's engagement with the neurodivergent tradition because it specifies that the neurodivergent contribution to constitutional theory cannot be separated from the political-economic critique. The accommodation of neurodivergent participants within an otherwise unchanged political-economic architecture cannot deliver the constitutional contribution the neurodivergent tradition can make, because the political-economic architecture's construction of normality is itself the structural condition under which the marginalization operates. The constitutional renovation must include the renegotiation of the political-economic architecture's construction of normality, which requires the renegotiation of the architecture's categorial commitments to productivity, citizenship, and contribution. The connection between the neurodivergent contribution and the political-economic critique is not coincidental; it is constitutive, and the project's engagement with the neurodivergent tradition must operate at the level of the connection rather than at the level of accommodation alone.

Chapman's framework also engages the question of how the neurodivergent contribution should be theorized in relation to other forms of structural marginalization. The argument resists the tendency, present in some neurodiversity-paradigm scholarship, to treat neurodivergence as a free-standing axis of structural marginalization comparable to but separate from race, class, gender, and disability. Chapman's specification is that the political economy of normality operates intersectionally with these other axes; the construction of the normal is constituted by the construction of the white, the male, the able-bodied, the heterosexual, the propertied as default categories, and the construction of the neurotypical operates within and through these other constructions rather than as a separate axis. The position is consonant with the intersectional analytical move developed in the Black-feminist tradition; the neurodivergent contribution to constitutional theory is intersectional in the same sense, and the constitutional architecture's reception of the contribution must be capable of operating at the intersection rather than at the level of single-axis accommodation.

### M. Remi Yergeau and the Rhetorical Constitution of the Autistic Subject

M. Remi Yergeau's *Authoring Autism: On Rhetoric and Neurological Queerness* (2018) develops a contribution that is distinct from but compatible with the neurodiversity paradigm and the political-economic critique. Yergeau's argument is that the dominant cultural construction of autism has operated through specific rhetorical structures that have constituted the autistic subject as the figure of failed rhetoric — the human whose communication is impaired, whose social cognition is deficient, whose capacity for the rhetorical practices that constitute the political subject is questionable — and that the rhetorical construction has been the structural condition under which autistic persons have been excluded from the political-rhetorical practices that constitute the political subject. The construction is not an empirical finding about autistic persons; it is a rhetorical-political move whose effect has been the constitution of the political subject around the neurotypical default while pathologizing the neurodivergent.

The argument is consequential for the constitutional question because it specifies that the rhetorical practices of constitutional decision-making — the practices through which testimonial contributions are offered and received, through which deliberation occurs, through which political subjectivity is constituted — are themselves the site of the marginalization the constitutional architecture must address. The architecture cannot be redesigned only at the level of formal procedure; it must be redesigned at the level of rhetorical practice, with the recognition that the rhetorical practices are themselves productive of the political subjectivities through which the architecture operates. The redesign is the redesign of the constitution of political subjectivity itself, and it cannot be conducted under the assumption that the constitution of political subjectivity is settled and that the redesign concerns only the procedural architecture through which already-constituted subjects participate.

Yergeau's framework also engages the question of what autistic rhetorical practice contributes that the dominant rhetorical practices cannot. The argument is that autistic rhetorical practice has its own internal commitments, its own modes of articulation, its own criteria of validity, and that the practices' analytical contribution is not derivable from the dominant rhetorical practices supplemented by autistic experience. The contribution is constitutive in the same sense the contributions of the Indigenous, Black-radical, and dalit traditions are constitutive: it is not data that the dominant framework can incorporate but a framework with its own analytical authority, and the constitutional architecture's engagement with it must be on the framework's own terms.

### Damian Milton and the Double Empathy Problem

Damian Milton's articulation of the double empathy problem in his 2012 paper, "On the Ontological Status of Autism: The 'Double Empathy Problem,'" is a contribution of methodological significance that the project's prior installments have engaged and that the present part develops further. The argument is that the cultural construction of autism as a deficit in social cognition or empathy has been one-sided in a specific way: the deficit has been articulated as autistic persons' difficulty in understanding neurotypical persons, while the parallel difficulty of neurotypical persons in understanding autistic persons has been treated as the absence of a deficit rather than its presence. The asymmetry, on Milton's analysis, is not empirical but cultural: the social cognition of the dominant group is treated as the standard against which the social cognition of the marginalized group is measured, and the social cognition of the marginalized group is treated as deficient by the standard rather than as a different cognitive style that the dominant group has its own difficulty in understanding.

The argument has direct philosophical analog to the standpoint-epistemological position Part Three developed: the conventional view that treats the dominant perspective as no perspective and the marginalized perspective as a perspective is incoherent, because the dominant perspective is a perspective whose status as default is a cultural-political product rather than an empirical-philosophical finding. Milton's articulation of the double empathy problem is the application of the standpoint-epistemological move to the specific case of autistic-neurotypical social cognition; the analytical move is the same as the move Harding, Collins, and Haraway develop in different contexts.

The constitutional implications converge with what Yergeau's framework specifies. The constitutional architecture's reception of testimonial contributions from autistic and other neurodivergent participants has been structured by the asymmetry the double empathy problem names: the autistic contributions are treated as testimony from a marginalized cognitive style whose adequacy to the architecture's reception standards is questionable, while the architecture's own cognitive style is treated as no style. The redesign of the architecture under the multi-eyed seeing commitment must include the recognition that the architecture's cognitive style is a style — a specifiable, contestable, alterable style — and that the architecture's reception of testimonial contributions must operate under conditions that do not impose the style as the default. The redesign cannot be only the addition of accommodations for neurodivergent participants to an unchanged architecture; it must be the transformation of the architecture's cognitive style into one that operates across plural cognitive styles rather than within a single dominant one.

### Decolonial Neurodiversity and Multi-Traditional Engagement

The most recent contribution the present part engages is the decolonial-neurodiversity framework articulated in Vishnu KK Nair, Warda Farah, and Mildred Boveda's "Toward a Critical Praxis of Decolonized Neurodiversity" (2024). The contribution is significant for the project because it specifies that the neurodiversity paradigm, as developed primarily in white-Western academic and movement contexts, has its own racial and colonial limitations that require correction through engagement with Black, Indigenous, and global-South perspectives on neurodivergence. The argument is consonant with the methodological commitments the project has developed throughout: the neurodiversity paradigm's contribution is itself the contribution of a specific tradition with specific historical-political conditions of formation, and the constitutional architecture's engagement with the contribution must include engagement with the contribution's own conditions of formation rather than treating the contribution as universally articulated.

Nair, Farah, and Boveda's framework specifies several specific points that the project's engagement with the neurodivergent tradition requires. First, the dominant neurodiversity paradigm has been articulated primarily in contexts where neurodivergence has been the principal axis of marginalization; in contexts where neurodivergence operates intersectionally with racial, colonial, and class marginalization, the analytical resources required are not derivable from the paradigm's dominant articulation alone. Second, the medicalization of neurodivergence operates differently in different racial-colonial contexts: the categorization of Black children as neurodivergent has historically operated as a mechanism of exclusion from educational and political resources, while the categorization of white children as neurodivergent has more recently operated as a mechanism for the receipt of educational and political resources. The differential operation requires that the neurodiversity paradigm's analytical resources be developed with attention to the differential operation rather than abstracting from it. Third, the global-South contributions to the analysis of neurodivergence — including contributions from disability-justice scholars in India, Brazil, South Africa, and elsewhere — have been underrepresented in the dominant articulation of the paradigm, and the project's engagement with the paradigm must include engagement with the global-South contributions as constitutive rather than as supplementary.

The framework's contribution to the present document is to specify that the neurodivergent contribution to the multi-eyed seeing commitment is itself plural, that the plurality requires engagement of multiple traditions within the neurodivergent contribution, and that the constitutional architecture's reception of the contribution must operate at the intersection of neurodivergence and other axes of structural marginalization rather than at the level of single-axis neurodivergence. The architectural implications converge with what the Black-feminist intersectional tradition has specified: the architecture must be capable of operating at intersection rather than at the level of single-axis accommodation. The convergence is itself an instance of the multi-eyed seeing commitment operating substantively rather than only formally.

### A Final Methodological Note Before Part Seven

The neurodivergent contribution to the multi-eyed seeing commitment carries a specific reflexive demand on the project's own operation. The project is being authored by a participant in the constitutional theory tradition who has identified as neurodivergent (specifically, as a person with attention-deficit hyperactivity disorder), and the methodological commitments the project advances must operate at the relation between the project's authorial standpoint and the analytical contributions the project engages. The relation cannot be resolved in either direction: the project does not have standing to speak for the neurodivergent tradition as such, because the tradition is plural and the project's authorial standpoint is one neurodivergent standpoint among many; and the project does have standpoint-epistemological access to certain analytical contributions that operate from neurodivergent positionality, and that access is part of the analytical material the project can engage.

The relation is not unique to the neurodivergent contribution; analogous relations operate for other traditions the project engages where the project's authorial standpoint includes some standpoint-epistemological access to the tradition's contributions. The project's authorial standpoint includes some access to the standpoint of those identified or identifying as members of the United States' professional-managerial class, and that access is part of the analytical material the project can engage when the project examines the operation of class in constitutional architecture. The project's authorial standpoint does not include access to the standpoints of dalit, Black-radical, Latinx, Asian-American, or Indigenous traditions, and the project's engagement with those traditions is engagement from outside the relevant standpoint, with the implications for the project's authority that have been specified throughout this document.

The reflexive demand is that the project be honest about the relations between its authorial standpoint and the analytical contributions it engages, that it acknowledge the limits of the standpoint-epistemological access its authorial position supplies, and that it be answerable to the corrections of those whose standpoint-epistemological access exceeds the project's. The demand is part of the multi-eyed seeing commitment as the present document specifies it, and it binds the project's continuing operation as well as this document's individual articulation. ## Part Seven — Multi-Eyed Seeing as Constitutional Method

The conceptual, procedural, philosophical, diagnostic, decolonial, and neurodivergent resources developed in Parts One through Six collectively supply the analytical material from which the multi-eyed seeing commitment can be operationalized as constitutional method. The operationalization is the substantive contribution this document offers to constitutional theory: the specification of how a constitutional architecture can be designed and operated under the commitment that the load-bearing analytical authority on every major constitutional question is co-produced by traditions whose authority the architecture recognizes as co-equal. The specification proceeds at three levels — procedural, substantive, and institutional — each of which the present part develops in turn.

### The Procedural Architecture

The procedural architecture under multi-eyed seeing concerns how constitutional drafting, ratification, amendment, implementation, and revision operate. The architecture must satisfy several specific requirements that the prior parts have established, and the present part specifies them in operational terms.

The drafting of constitutional text under the commitment cannot be conducted by a unitary drafting authority that consults representatives of relevant traditions. The drafting must be conducted by a multi-traditional drafting body whose constitution includes representatives of the traditions whose authority the architecture recognizes, with the representatives operating under their own traditions' protocols of authorization rather than under the drafting body's protocols of selection. The Indigenous representation must include representatives selected by Indigenous nations operating under their own constitutional traditions, with the selection operating under the FPIC standard developed in international Indigenous-rights law. The Black-radical representation must include representatives selected by Black-radical institutional and movement traditions whose authority is grounded in the tradition's own continuous practice rather than in the drafting body's selection procedures. The disability-justice representation must include representatives selected by disability-justice institutional and movement traditions, with the selection operating under the disability-justice tradition's own protocols of cross-disability solidarity and accountability. The neurodivergent representation must include representatives selected by neurodivergent institutional and movement traditions, with the selection operating under protocols that do not impose neurotypical default cognitive and rhetorical structures on the selection process. The dalit, Latinx, Chicanx, Asian-American, feminist, and decolonial-theory representations must operate under analogous protocols.

The drafting process must operate under temporal and procedural conditions that permit the substantive participation of the represented traditions on their own terms. The conditions include: extended timelines that accommodate the deliberative practices of traditions whose protocols include extended consultation, ceremonial process, and intergenerational consultation rather than only rapid consensus-formation; multiple modalities of participation that accommodate cognitive and rhetorical styles other than the neurotypical default; financial and material support that permits participation by representatives whose institutional positions do not include the resources for unsupported participation; protections against the testimonial smothering Part Four specified, including procedures for the protection of representatives from retaliation for testimony that the dominant tradition's institutions might find unwelcome; and a process for the documentation and transmission of the drafting record that is itself accountable to the traditions whose participation it documents.

The ratification of constitutional text under the commitment cannot be conducted by a unitary popular sovereignty whose specification operates within the dominant tradition's framework. The ratification must operate under multi-traditional protocols whose specifications include: ratification by the Indigenous nations whose territories and peoples the constitutional architecture concerns, with the ratification operating under each nation's own constitutional protocols rather than under the settler state's protocols of popular ratification; ratification by representatives of the other traditions under their own protocols of authorization; and ratification by the populations that the dominant tradition's protocols of popular sovereignty specify, with the recognition that the populations include members of all the represented traditions and that the populations' ratification is constituted in part by the ratification of the traditions whose authority the architecture recognizes. The ratification protocol cannot collapse the multiple ratifications into a single unitary ratification; the multi-traditional character of the ratification is itself constitutive of the architecture's operation.

The amendment of constitutional text under the commitment must operate under analogous multi-traditional protocols. The Madisonian framework's amendment protocol — supermajority approval in Congress and the states — is a unitary-popular-sovereignty protocol that does not satisfy the multi-traditional requirements the commitment specifies. The amendment protocol under the commitment must include: amendment proposed by representatives of the relevant traditions operating under their own protocols of proposal; amendment ratified under multi-traditional protocols analogous to those for ratification; and a protocol for the recognition that some constitutional provisions concern specific traditions (Indigenous treaty rights, for example) whose amendment must operate under the protocols of the traditions whose authority the provisions concern.

The implementation of constitutional architecture under the commitment must include institutional mechanisms for the continuing participation of the represented traditions in the operation of the architecture. The implementation cannot be the unilateral operation of the constitutional state's institutions over a population whose constitutional contribution was exhausted in the ratification; it must be the continuing relational practice of the constitutional state's institutions with the institutional carriers of the represented traditions, with the relational practice being constitutive of the architecture's operation. The institutional mechanisms include: standing bodies whose composition includes representatives of the traditions; protocols for the consultation of the traditions on policy decisions affecting their members; protocols for the renegotiation of the architecture's categorial commitments in response to the analytical contributions of the traditions; and procedures for the protection of the traditions' authority over their own institutional and intellectual carriers.

The revision of constitutional text under the commitment must include the recognition that the architecture is itself a learning process and not a perfected outcome — the Marshall framework's foundational commitment, transferred to the constitutional level. The architecture must include mechanisms for the recognition that its operation is failing the multi-eyed seeing commitment when the failure occurs, and for the corrective revision of the architecture in response to the recognition. The mechanisms cannot be only formal-procedural; they must include substantive accountability to the traditions whose authority the architecture recognizes, with the accountability being enforceable rather than rhetorical.

### The Substantive Architecture

The substantive architecture under multi-eyed seeing concerns how the constitutional categories — the human, the political, the economic, the ecological, the rational, the deliberative, the just — are specified in the architecture's operation. The categories cannot be imported from a single dominant tradition and supplemented from the others; they must be co-produced through cross-traditional engagement, with the recognition that the categories themselves are not neutral and that imported categories will distort what the constitution can do.

The category of the human under the commitment is not the abstract individual of liberal political theory, whose pre-political existence is presumed and whose entry into political relation is contractual. The category is constituted across plural articulations: the relational person of Indigenous traditions whose existence is constituted in and through relationship with kin, ancestors, descendants, and the more-than-human world; the racial-historical person of Black-radical tradition whose existence is constituted in and through the structural relation of racialization that has constituted the modern world; the caste-historical person of dalit tradition whose existence is constituted in and through the structural relation of caste hierarchy; the embodied and disabled person of disability-justice tradition whose existence is constituted in and through the embodiment that the dominant constructions of personhood have abstracted from; the neurodivergent person of the neurodivergent tradition whose existence is constituted in and through the cognitive variation that the dominant constructions of personhood have pathologized; the gendered and gendered-historical person of feminist tradition whose existence is constituted in and through the structural relations of gender hierarchy; and the universal person of liberal tradition whose abstract status is recognized as one articulation of the human among many rather than as the framework within which the others operate. The constitutional architecture must be capable of operating across these articulations rather than collapsing them into a single dominant articulation.

The category of the political under the commitment is similarly co-produced. The category is not the public sphere of liberal political theory, defined by its distinction from the private sphere whose specification has been gendered, racialized, and classed. The category is constituted across plural articulations: the relational politics of Indigenous traditions whose specification operates through ceremony, ongoing consultation, and the maintenance of relationships across generations and across human and more-than-human relations; the resistance politics of Black-radical tradition whose specification operates through the analysis and dismantling of structural racial subordination; the abolitionist politics of contemporary Black abolitionist tradition whose specification operates through the dismantling of the carceral and policing infrastructure of the constitutional state; the disability-justice politics whose specification operates through the analysis of access, interdependence, and care as constitutive political practices; the neurodivergent politics whose specification operates through the analysis of cognitive and rhetorical plurality as constitutive of political life; the dalit politics whose specification operates through the analysis and dismantling of structural caste subordination; the decolonial politics whose specification operates through the analysis of the modern/colonial formation and the articulation of alternatives that operate beyond it; and the liberal politics whose specification operates through the architecture of representative institutions, separation of powers, and individual rights. The constitutional architecture must be capable of operating across these articulations.

The category of the economic under the commitment is constituted by similar plural articulations and has been the principal subject of the project's prior installments on macroeconomic theory and on transformative legislation. The economic is not the market sphere of liberal political-economic theory whose specification operates through prices, exchange, and individual maximization. The category is constituted across plural articulations: the embedded economy of Karl Polanyi's substantive economics, the human economy of feminist economics, the gift economy of Indigenous traditions, the doughnut economy of Kate Raworth's ecological economics, the metabolic economy of John Bellamy Foster's metabolic-rift analysis, the political economy of Black-radical tradition's analysis of racial capitalism, the development economy of Andean *buen vivir* tradition, and the market economy of liberal political-economic theory. The constitutional architecture's specification of the economic — including the dual-currency architecture the project has developed in Document E — must operate across these articulations rather than within a single dominant one.

The category of the ecological under the commitment is constituted across plural articulations that the project's prior installments on biomimicry and ecology and on the constitution against the climate have developed. The ecological is not the environment of conservation biology whose specification operates through the abstraction of nature from human relation. The category is constituted across plural articulations: the relational ecology of Indigenous traditions whose specification operates through the recognition of human-ecological relationships as constitutive; the rights-of-nature ecology whose specification operates through the recognition of ecological entities as bearers of constitutional rights; the planetary-boundary ecology whose specification operates through the recognition of biophysical limits to human economic activity; the buen vivir ecology whose specification operates through the recognition of the well-being of human and more-than-human relation as the proper object of political and economic activity; the metabolic ecology whose specification operates through the recognition of the metabolic relations between human society and the broader biosphere; and the conservation-biology ecology whose specification operates through scientific analysis of biophysical processes. The constitutional architecture must be capable of operating across these articulations.

The category of the rational under the commitment is constituted across plural articulations that the present document and the project's installment on minds, markets, and the sacred have developed. The rational is not the calculating individual of decision-theoretic articulation whose specification operates through subjective expected utility. The category is constituted across plural articulations: the relational rationality of Indigenous traditions whose specification operates through the recognition of relationships and reciprocities as the substance of rational practice; the situated rationality of feminist epistemology whose specification operates through the recognition of social location as constitutive of rational practice; the cognitively plural rationality of the neurodivergent tradition whose specification operates through the recognition of multiple cognitive styles as constitutive of rational practice; the bounded rationality of decision-science articulation whose specification operates through the recognition of cognitive limitations as constitutive of rational practice; the resistant rationality of epistemic-resistance articulation whose specification operates through the recognition of the analytical practices through which marginalized communities articulate their experience; and the calculating rationality of decision-theoretic articulation whose specification operates through subjective expected utility. The constitutional architecture must be capable of operating across these articulations.

The category of the deliberative under the commitment is constituted across plural articulations. The deliberative is not the rational discourse of Habermasian articulation whose specification operates through the ideal speech situation. The category is constituted across plural articulations: the council deliberation of Indigenous traditions whose specification operates through extended consultation, ceremonial process, and consensus-seeking practices that operate over generational time horizons; the deliberative mini-public of contemporary deliberative-democracy articulation whose specification operates through random selection and structured deliberation; the resistant deliberation of Black-radical tradition whose specification operates through the analytical practices of communities organized in opposition to structural subordination; the disability-justice deliberation whose specification operates through accessible deliberative practices that accommodate cognitive and embodied diversity; the neurodivergent deliberation whose specification operates through cognitive and rhetorical plurality; and the rational discourse of Habermasian articulation whose specification operates through the ideal speech situation. The constitutional architecture must be capable of operating across these articulations.

The category of the just under the commitment is constituted across plural articulations. The just is not the procedural justice of liberal political theory whose specification operates through formal equality and due process. The category is constituted across plural articulations: the restorative justice of Indigenous traditions whose specification operates through the restoration of right relationship; the abolitionist justice of contemporary abolitionist tradition whose specification operates through the dismantling of the carceral state and the construction of alternative responses to harm; the reparative justice of Black-radical tradition whose specification operates through the recognition and material redress of historical and continuing structural injustice; the disability justice whose specification operates through the recognition of access, interdependence, and the dismantling of ableist hierarchy; the climate justice whose specification operates through the recognition of intergenerational and global obligations; the buen vivir justice whose specification operates through the well-being of human and more-than-human relation; and the procedural justice of liberal political theory whose specification operates through formal equality and due process. The constitutional architecture must be capable of operating across these articulations.

### The Institutional Architecture

The institutional architecture under multi-eyed seeing concerns the bodies, processes, and authorities through which the constitutional state operates. The architecture must satisfy several specific requirements that the prior parts have established.

The architecture must include constitutional bodies that operate under each of the represented traditions' authorities rather than under a unitary apex authority that incorporates them. The Indigenous nations whose territories and peoples the constitutional architecture concerns must operate as constitutional authorities under their own constitutional traditions, with the authorities' operation being constitutive of the architecture rather than contingent on the architecture's recognition. The Indigenous nations' authority is grounded in the nations' own constitutional traditions and predates the constitutional state's existence; the constitutional architecture's recognition of the nations' authority is the recognition of an existing authority rather than the creation of a new one. The architecture must include institutional protocols for the operation of the nations' authority on questions affecting their territories and peoples, with the protocols operating under the FPIC standard.

The architecture must include deliberative mini-publics constituted under sortition with multi-traditional protocols of selection and operation. The mini-publics are not unitary bodies whose composition reflects population-weighted random selection from a unitary population; they are constituted under protocols that ensure substantive participation across the represented traditions and that include the procedural conditions for participation specified above. The mini-publics' authority operates on specific constitutional questions for which their deliberative contribution is required, with the questions being specified by the constitutional architecture and with the mini-publics' deliberation being constitutive of the architecture's operation on those questions.

The architecture must include elected representative bodies whose electoral architecture operates under the renovated electoral commitments the project's prior installments on the constitutional architecture for the next 250 years and on transformative legislation have developed. The elected bodies' authority is not the unitary popular-sovereignty authority of the Madisonian architecture; it is one institutional carrier of constitutional authority among the others, with the authority operating across the represented traditions rather than within a unitary popular sovereignty.

The architecture must include commissioners and officers operating under the multi-generational, ecological, and disability-justice commitments the project has developed. The Future Generations Commissioner the project's installment on the constitution against the climate has specified is one such officer; analogous officers must operate under the disability-justice, neurodivergent, dalit, Black-radical, and other relevant commitments. The officers' authority is not contingent on the elected bodies' authorization; it is constitutive of the architecture's operation, with the authority operating under the protocols of the traditions whose authority the officers represent.

The architecture must include accountability mechanisms whose operation is substantive rather than rhetorical. The mechanisms include the diagnostic apparatus Part Four developed, with the apparatus operating institutionally rather than only analytically; the protocols of relational accountability the Indigenous research-methodology tradition has specified, with the protocols operating institutionally rather than only methodologically; and the mechanisms for the continuing renegotiation of the architecture's categorial and procedural commitments in response to the analytical contributions of the represented traditions, with the renegotiation operating substantively rather than only formally.

The architecture must include the e-democracy infrastructure the project's installment on e-democracy has developed, with the infrastructure's operation being structured by the multi-eyed seeing commitment. The infrastructure is not a neutral technological architecture whose deployment supplements other constitutional bodies' operation; it is itself a constitutional body whose operation is constitutive of the architecture, with the operation being subject to the commitments specified throughout this document. The infrastructure's design must include the procedural conditions for participation that this document has specified, the diagnostic apparatus Part Four developed, the cognitive-and-rhetorical plurality the neurodivergent contribution requires, and the relational accountability the Indigenous methodological tradition specifies.

### The Architecture as a Learning Process

The institutional, substantive, and procedural architecture this part has specified is not a finished design that will operate correctly when properly constructed. It is a structural specification of an architecture that must operate as a learning process under the foundational commitment of the Marshall framework. The architecture must include the institutional mechanisms for the recognition of its own failures, for the corrective revision of its operation in response to the failures, and for the accountability of its continuing operation to the traditions whose authority it recognizes. The mechanisms cannot resolve the underlying analytical questions; they can only ensure that the questions remain operative, that the architecture's responses to them are subject to correction, and that the architecture's commitments are honored in the continuing operation rather than only in the founding articulation.

The specification supplies the constitutional method that the multi-eyed seeing commitment requires. The method is demanding, contestable, and incomplete. It is the method that the analytical resources developed across Parts One through Six collectively support; it is the method whose substantive content the rest of this document — particularly Part Eight's engagement with the renovation-versus-decolonial question — defends and refines. The next part turns to the most consequential analytical move the document makes, the engagement of the four most demanding critiques of the renovation move under which the multi-eyed seeing commitment operates.
## Part Eight — The Renovation-Versus-Decolonial Question Engaged Substantively

The methodological commitment developed in the preceding parts raises a structural question that the project cannot evade and that this part engages substantively. The commitment to honoring Indigenous constitution-like documents on their own terms; to engaging Black-radical, abolitionist, and settler-colonial-studies critiques as load-bearing rather than as adversarial interlocutors; to operating under the analytical authority of dalit, disability-justice, neurodivergent, decolonial, and other underrepresented traditions in roughly co-equal weight — this commitment, taken seriously, generates a question that the project must answer rather than gesture toward. The question is whether the project's renovation-of-American-constitutional-architecture move is legitimate at all, or whether the more honest response to the analytical contributions the project recognizes would be the abolitionist or decolonial move that operates against the constitutional state rather than for its renovation. The question has been raised, in different formulations, throughout the project's installments; it has been engaged in pieces; it has not yet been engaged as the central analytical question the multi-eyed seeing commitment requires it to be. The present part conducts that engagement.

The engagement proceeds through five movements: the four most demanding critiques of the renovation move developed in the abolitionist, decolonial, settler-colonial-studies, and Black-pessimist literatures; the four critiques of the multi-eyed seeing commitment that the project's prior installments have begun to engage; the project's analytical answer to the renovation-versus-decolonial question; the conditions under which the answer holds and the conditions under which it does not; and the implications for the project's continuing operation when the conditions are not met.

### The Four Critiques of the Renovation Move

The first critique is the Black-pessimist or Afro-pessimist critique, articulated most influentially in Frank Wilderson's *Afropessimism* (2020) and developed in convergent directions by Jared Sexton, Saidiya Hartman, and others. The critique's central analytical move is that anti-Blackness is not a contingent feature of the modern world that can be addressed through reform, restitution, or renovation but is constitutive of the modern world's foundational categories. The category of the human, on this analysis, has been constituted in distinction from the Black, with the Black functioning not as a marginalized version of the human but as the constitutive outside whose marginalization defines the human as such. The political itself, on this analysis, has been constituted in distinction from the position of the Black, with Black persons not having the standing of political subjects in the constitutive architecture of the modern political. The constitutional architecture of the United States, on this analysis, is not a constitutional architecture that has marginalized Black persons and that can be renovated to include them; it is a constitutional architecture whose constitutive structure includes the position of the Black as the position outside the constitutional, and the renovation of the architecture to include Black persons as constitutional subjects requires the dissolution of the architecture's constitutive structure rather than the renovation of its accidental features.

The critique's force, on the present document's reception, is in the analytical specification of how anti-Blackness operates structurally rather than only contingently. The Wilderson articulation, particularly in *Red, White, and Black: Cinema and the Structure of U.S. Antagonisms* (2010) and *Afropessimism* (2020), develops the position through close engagement with the historical record of slavery, the post-Reconstruction order, the carceral state, and contemporary anti-Black violence in police-civilian encounters. The argument is not that Black persons cannot be political agents — Wilderson's own articulation includes engagement with Black political agency — but that the political space within which Black persons can be agents is not the political space the constitutional architecture has constituted. The political space the constitutional architecture has constituted is the space in which the Black is the constitutive outside, and the Black political agency that operates within that constitution is agency within a structure of antagonism that the renovation of the structure cannot resolve.

Sexton's published work develops the position in directions that engage the relations between Afro-pessimism and other analytical traditions. The articulation in "People-of-Color-Blindness: Notes on the Afterlife of Slavery" (2010) and "The Vel of Slavery: Tracking the Figure of the Unsovereign" (2014) specifies that the Afro-pessimist position is not a position within the broader category of the structurally marginalized that contests with other positions for analytical privilege; it is a position whose specification of the structural marginalization of the Black is non-analogous to the specifications of marginalization in other traditions, with the non-analogy being the position's central analytical claim. The constitutional implication is that the multi-eyed seeing commitment's articulation of co-equal authority across multiple traditions cannot, on the Afro-pessimist analysis, do the analytical work the project requires of it: the position of the Black is not one position among many whose authority can be co-equal with the others; it is the position whose marginalization is the constitutive condition of the others' political and human standing, and the co-equal allocation of authority across positions in which the Black is one position among many reproduces the constitutive marginalization rather than addressing it.

The critique is the most demanding the project engages in this part, and the project's answer to it operates with the seriousness the critique deserves. The project does not adjudicate the analytical question of whether the Afro-pessimist position is correct in its specification of the structural location of the Black; the question is not the project's to adjudicate, and the project's authority does not extend to settling it. The project's response is at the level of architectural specification: the constitutional architecture under the multi-eyed seeing commitment must include institutional mechanisms for the recognition of the Afro-pessimist analytical contribution as load-bearing, must include the structural commitments to Black-radical material rectification that the position requires (including reparations on the scale and architecture the California Reparations Task Force final report and Darity and Mullen's *From Here to Equality* specify), and must operate under the recognition that the architecture's success at the level of structural transformation cannot be evaluated by the architecture's own internal criteria but must be evaluated by the analytical contributions of the Black-radical and Afro-pessimist traditions whose authority the architecture recognizes. The architecture's success is conditional on the recognition; without the recognition, the architecture is operating under a different commitment from the one it claims, and the project must say so.

The second critique is the settler-colonial-studies critique, articulated most influentially in Patrick Wolfe's *Settler Colonialism and the Transformation of Anthropology* (1999) and *Traces of History: Elementary Structures of Race* (2016) and developed in convergent directions by Lorenzo Veracini, J. Kēhaulani Kauanui, Sarah Maddison, and others. The critique's central analytical move is that settler colonialism is not a historical event that occurred in the past and that has subsequently been concluded but a structure that operates in the present, with the structure being constituted by the continuing relation between the settler society and the Indigenous peoples whose lands the settler society has occupied. The structure's logic is the logic of elimination, on Wolfe's specification: settler colonialism's structural project is the elimination of the Indigenous as Indigenous, with the elimination operating through multiple mechanisms (genocide, assimilation, land dispossession, denial of sovereignty, denial of treaty obligations) but with the underlying logic being constant. The settler society's constitutional architecture, on this analysis, is not a constitutional architecture that has marginalized Indigenous peoples and that can be renovated to include them; it is a constitutional architecture whose constitutive operation is the elimination of Indigenous sovereignty over Indigenous lands, and the renovation of the architecture to include Indigenous peoples as constitutional subjects requires the dissolution of the architecture's constitutive operation rather than the renovation of its accidental features.

The critique's force, on the present document's reception, is in the analytical specification of how the constitutive operation of settler colonialism cannot be separated from the constitutional architecture of the settler state. The constitutional architecture's specification of the territorial sovereignty of the United States is itself the specification of the elimination of Indigenous territorial sovereignty over the lands the United States claims; the recognition of Indigenous nations as "domestic dependent nations" in *Cherokee Nation v. Georgia* (1831) is itself the specification of the subordinate status of Indigenous sovereignty within the broader sovereignty of the settler state; the contemporary jurisprudence on tribal jurisdiction is itself the specification of the limited operation of Indigenous sovereignty within the constitutional architecture of the settler state. The constitutional architecture's renovation to include Indigenous peoples as constitutional subjects within the architecture, on the settler-colonial-studies analysis, does not address the constitutive operation of settler colonialism; it incorporates the Indigenous as subordinate constituents of the settler state's continuing sovereignty, with the subordination being the elimination's continuation by other means.

The critique requires the project's substantive engagement, and the project's answer operates at the level of architectural specification. The constitutional architecture under the multi-eyed seeing commitment must include the structural recognition of Indigenous nations as constitutional authorities under their own constitutional traditions, with the recognition operating not through incorporation into the settler state's constitutional framework but through the constitutional architecture's accountability to the Indigenous nations' authority. The recognition must include the FPIC standard at constitutional level, the material restitution of Indigenous lands through Land Back, the recognition of Indigenous treaty obligations as constitutionally binding rather than as politically discretionary, and the constitutional architecture's accountability to the seventh-generation criterion the Indigenous traditions specify. The architecture's success at the level of structural transformation cannot be evaluated by the architecture's own internal criteria but must be evaluated by the analytical contributions of the Indigenous traditions and the settler-colonial-studies tradition whose authority the architecture recognizes. The architecture's success is conditional on the recognition; without the recognition, the architecture is operating under a different commitment from the one it claims, and the project must say so.

The third critique is the constitutional-bind critique, articulated most directly in Aziz Rana's *The Constitutional Bind: How Americans Came to Idolize a Document That Fails Them* (2024) and developed in convergent directions by Rana's earlier *The Two Faces of American Freedom* (2010), by Adrienne Davis, by Robert Tsai, and by the broader contemporary constitutional-theory critique. The critique's central analytical move is that the project of American constitutionalism — the project of treating the 1787 Constitution and its subsequent amendments as the framework within which American political life operates — has been the obstacle rather than the vehicle of multiracial, multi-traditional democratic life in the United States. The constitutional architecture, on Rana's analysis, has been constituted by specific political-economic interests in the founding period, has been preserved by specific political-economic interests in the post-Reconstruction period, and has been idolized by specific political-economic interests in the contemporary period; the idolization operates as a mechanism for the prevention of constitutional transformation that would address the structural failures the project of constitutionalism has produced.

The critique's force, on the present document's reception, is in the analytical specification of how the constitutional veneration that the project of American constitutionalism has produced operates as a structural obstacle to constitutional transformation. The veneration treats the constitutional architecture as the framework within which political reform must occur, with the architecture's foundational features being treated as fixed; the result is that political reforms that would require the renegotiation of the architecture's foundational features are framed as constitutional violations rather than as constitutional improvements. The structural effect is that the architecture's failures — including the structural failures the project's prior installments have specified — are treated as features the architecture must be operated under rather than as failures the architecture must be renovated to address. The renovation move, on this analysis, can itself be a form of constitutional veneration if it operates within the architecture's foundational frame and accepts the frame's limits on what can be reformed.

The critique requires the project's substantive engagement at the level of architectural specification. The constitutional architecture under the multi-eyed seeing commitment must operate at the level of foundational categorial revision rather than only at the level of marginal amendment within the existing categorial frame. The categorial revision the project has developed across Parts One through Seven — the recategorization of the human, the political, the economic, the ecological, the rational, the deliberative, the just under multi-traditional articulation — is the categorial revision the constitutional-bind critique requires. The project's renovation move is, on the project's specification, not a renovation that operates within the existing constitutional veneration; it is a renovation that operates against the veneration by requiring the categorial revision the veneration prevents. The renovation's success is conditional on the categorial revision; without the categorial revision, the renovation operates within the constitutional veneration and reproduces the structural failures the veneration produces, and the project must say so.

The fourth critique is the abolitionist critique, articulated most directly in Mariame Kaba's *We Do This 'Til We Free Us: Abolitionist Organizing and Transforming Justice* (2021) and Ruth Wilson Gilmore's *Abolition Geography: Essays Towards Liberation* (2022) and developed in convergent directions by Angela Davis, Beth Richie, Critical Resistance, the broader contemporary abolitionist movement, and the historical Black-radical abolitionist tradition. The critique's central analytical move is that the carceral state — the policing, prison, and surveillance infrastructure of the contemporary United States — is not a feature of the constitutional architecture that can be reformed but is constitutive of the architecture's continuing operation, and that the abolition of the carceral state requires the dissolution of the architecture's constitutive infrastructure rather than the renovation of its accidental features. The abolitionist tradition's analytical contribution is not principally negative; it is constructive, with the construction operating at the level of community-based alternatives to the carceral response to harm, the building of the infrastructure for collective survival outside the carceral state, and the development of the political subjectivities through which abolition can be sustained as a continuing project rather than as a one-time political event.

The critique's force, on the present document's reception, is in the analytical specification of how the carceral infrastructure operates as constitutive of the constitutional architecture rather than as accidental to it. The constitutional architecture's specification of due process, equal protection, and the rights of the criminally accused operates within an infrastructure whose constitutive features include mass incarceration, racialized policing, and the surveillance of marginalized communities; the rights operate within the infrastructure rather than against it. The renovation of the architecture's specification of rights, on the abolitionist analysis, cannot address the constitutive operation of the infrastructure; it can only modify the infrastructure's marginal operations while the constitutive operation continues. The constitutional architecture under the multi-eyed seeing commitment must therefore include the abolitionist specification of the carceral infrastructure as constitutive, the abolitionist commitment to the dissolution of the infrastructure, and the abolitionist construction of community-based alternatives to the carceral response to harm.

The critique requires the project's substantive engagement at the level of architectural specification. The constitutional architecture under the multi-eyed seeing commitment must include the structural commitment to the abolitionist project, with the commitment operating not as a reform within the existing carceral infrastructure but as the structural transformation of the infrastructure itself. The commitment must include the recognition that the abolitionist project is a continuing political project rather than a one-time constitutional event, that the abolitionist construction of community-based alternatives requires the material support and structural protection that the constitutional architecture must supply, and that the architecture's success at the level of structural transformation cannot be evaluated by the architecture's own internal criteria but must be evaluated by the analytical contributions of the abolitionist tradition whose authority the architecture recognizes. The architecture's success is conditional on the commitment; without the commitment, the architecture is operating under a different commitment from the one it claims, and the project must say so.

The fifth critique is the decolonization-not-as-metaphor critique, articulated most directly in Eve Tuck and K. Wayne Yang's "Decolonization Is Not a Metaphor" (2012). The critique's central analytical move is that the contemporary academic and political adoption of the language of decolonization has frequently operated as the metaphorical extension of the term to projects that do not engage the material conditions of colonization — the dispossession of Indigenous peoples from Indigenous lands and the continuing settler-state occupation of those lands — and that the metaphorical extension functions as a form of "settler moves to innocence" that permits the continuation of settler colonization while signaling apparent commitment to its undoing. The decolonization that operates as metaphor is, on Tuck and Yang's analysis, the failure pattern by which the language of structural transformation is appropriated to forestall the structural transformation; the decolonization that does not operate as metaphor is the material project of the return of Indigenous lands to Indigenous nations, with the return being literal rather than figurative.

The critique's force is at the level of the project's own operation. The project has, throughout its installments, used the language of decolonization, of multi-traditional engagement, of the recognition of Indigenous constitutional authority on its own terms. The Tuck-and-Yang critique requires that the language be cashed out in material rather than only figurative terms: the multi-eyed seeing commitment, taken seriously, requires the structural commitment to Land Back as material restitution rather than as figurative recognition; requires the constitutional architecture's specification of the conditions under which the return of Indigenous lands operates; requires the project's accountability to the Indigenous nations whose authority the project recognizes for the material follow-through of the commitments the project has articulated. The critique is the structural test by which the project's renovation move can be evaluated for its material seriousness, with the test operating at the level of the project's actual commitments rather than at the level of the project's articulation of commitments.

The critique requires the project's substantive engagement at the level of architectural specification. The constitutional architecture under the multi-eyed seeing commitment must include the structural commitment to Land Back as material restitution, with the commitment operating through specific institutional architectures for the return of Indigenous lands to Indigenous nations. The commitment must include the recognition that the return is not a discretionary act of the settler state but a structural obligation that the constitutional architecture must specify and operationalize; the commitment must include the protection of the return from the political and economic interests that have historically opposed it; the commitment must include the architectural specification of the relations between the returned lands and the broader constitutional architecture of the settler state, with the relations operating under the FPIC standard and under the Indigenous nations' authority over their own constitutional traditions. The commitment is not aspirational; it is architectural, and without it, the project's renovation move operates as decolonization-as-metaphor in Tuck and Yang's sense, and the project must say so.

### The Four Critiques of the Multi-Eyed Seeing Commitment

In addition to the four critiques of the renovation move developed above, the project's prior installments have begun to engage four critiques of the multi-eyed seeing commitment itself that the present part must engage substantively. The four critiques are the conservative-incoherence critique, the radical-domestication critique, the practical-demandingness critique, and the colonial-residue critique. Each is engaged in turn.

The conservative-incoherence critique holds that multi-traditional engagement produces incoherence rather than integration. The argument is that traditions whose categorial commitments are non-identical cannot be engaged in roughly co-equal weight without producing analytical contradiction, and that the resulting analysis will be either incoherent — operating across mutually inconsistent commitments — or covertly unitary — operating under one tradition's commitments while gesturing at engagement with the others. The critique is internal to the analytical traditions of the project; it is articulated most pointedly by analytical philosophers and constitutional theorists working within the dominant Western tradition who treat coherence as a primary criterion of theoretical adequacy.

The project's response operates at the level of the meta-theoretical specification of what coherence means under the multi-eyed seeing commitment. The conservative-incoherence critique presupposes that coherence is properly specified by the criteria internal to the dominant Western analytical tradition; the multi-eyed seeing commitment denies that presupposition. The criteria of coherence are themselves specified across traditions, with different traditions specifying different criteria. The Western analytical tradition specifies coherence through the avoidance of formal contradiction in propositional commitments; the Indigenous traditions specify coherence through the maintenance of right relationship across human and more-than-human relata; the Black-radical tradition specifies coherence through the analytical articulation of the structural relations of racial subordination; the disability-justice tradition specifies coherence through the maintenance of access and interdependence; the abolitionist tradition specifies coherence through the analytical relation between current practice and the abolitionist horizon. The constitutional architecture under the multi-eyed seeing commitment is coherent insofar as it operates under each tradition's coherence criteria where the tradition's authority is constitutive; it is not coherent insofar as it is evaluated by a single tradition's coherence criteria when the tradition's authority is one among many. The conservative-incoherence critique is therefore not the demonstration that multi-traditional engagement produces incoherence but the assertion of one tradition's coherence criteria as the meta-criteria by which coherence is to be evaluated, and the assertion is itself an instance of the dominance the multi-eyed seeing commitment seeks to displace.

The radical-domestication critique holds that multi-traditional engagement domesticates Indigenous and underrepresented traditions by incorporating them rather than allowing them to displace. The argument is the most serious objection the project engages, and it has been engaged throughout this document. The critique's force is in the analytical specification that incorporation, however nominally co-equal, operates within an architecture whose continuing operation is the dominant tradition's operation, and that the incorporation accordingly reproduces the dominance it claims to displace. The Audra Simpson and Glen Coulthard articulations of refusal develop the position with particular force in the Indigenous context; the Wilderson and Sexton articulations develop convergent positions in the Afro-pessimist context.

The project's response, developed in Part One and reinforced throughout, has three components. First, the project specifies the architectural conditions under which the renovation move it advances is legitimate and the conditions under which it is not, with Part Eight as the specification of those conditions. The conditions include the multi-generational rectification commitment with material Land Back, the Indigenous co-governance with FPIC standard at constitutional level, the engagement of Indigenous foundational documents on their own constitutional standing, the seventh-generation criterion as constitutional decision rule, and the multi-eyed seeing methodological commitment treated as substantive rather than ornamental. Second, the project takes the directionality of authority seriously: the load-bearing primary texts of the Indigenous, Black-radical, dalit, disability-justice, and other traditions are engaged as primary texts on their own authority. Third, the project names the failure mode in advance and asks to be held to it: the multi-eyed seeing commitment can fail by domesticating the traditions even when it nominally honors them, and the failure pattern is identifiable. The project's commitment is to the corrective work of identifying when the failure is happening and refusing to proceed under the failed terms. The response does not resolve the radical-domestication critique; it operates under the critique, and the critique remains operative as a condition of the project's continuing accountability.

The practical-demandingness critique holds that the multi-eyed seeing commitment is too demanding for actual constitutional decision-making. The argument is that constitutional decision-making operates under temporal, institutional, and political constraints that prevent the substantive engagement of multiple traditions on every question of consequence, and that the commitment, taken seriously, cannot be operationalized in any constitutional architecture that can actually function as such. The critique is internal to the analytical traditions of the project; it is articulated most pointedly by constitutional theorists and political scientists working within the dominant Western tradition who treat operability as a primary criterion of architectural adequacy.

The project's response operates at three levels. First, the practical-demandingness critique presupposes that the operational constraints under which constitutional decision-making currently operates are fixed; the multi-eyed seeing commitment is part of the project of changing those constraints. The constraints include the temporal compression of contemporary constitutional decision-making, the institutional centralization of constitutional authority in the settler-state's institutions, and the political pressure for rapid constitutional resolution; each of these constraints is alterable, and the project's renovation move includes the alteration of the constraints to permit the substantive engagement the commitment requires. Second, the practical-demandingness critique presupposes that the constitutional architecture's primary operation is rapid decision-making on questions of consequence; the multi-eyed seeing commitment specifies that the architecture's primary operation is the maintenance of relationships, the renegotiation of categories, and the substantive engagement of analytical contributions, with rapid decision-making being a secondary operation that occurs only when the primary operation has produced sufficient cross-traditional consensus to support it. Third, the practical-demandingness critique presupposes that the alternative to the commitment is a constitutional architecture that produces rapid decisions of adequate quality; the project's prior installments have specified the structural failures of the existing architecture's rapid decision-making, with the failures operating across the climate, racial-caste, oligarchic, neurodivergent, and other domains the project has examined. The alternative to the commitment is not adequate rapid decision-making; it is rapid decision-making that produces structural failures of the magnitude the project has specified, and the practical-demandingness critique therefore operates as the defense of an architecture whose failures the project has documented rather than as the defense of an architecture whose adequacy is presumed.

The colonial-residue critique holds that the framing of separable "traditions" reproduces the structure that produced the separation. The argument is that the categorization of analytical contributions into "the Indigenous tradition," "the Black-radical tradition," "the dalit tradition," "the disability-justice tradition," "the neurodivergent tradition" operates under categorial commitments that have been generated under colonial-modern conditions, with the categories carrying the structural features of those conditions. The categories presume a separability that is not internal to the traditions themselves; the traditions have been constituted in relation to and against one another, with the relations being constitutive of the traditions rather than incidental to them. The framework that engages the traditions as separable and then calls for their integration reproduces the structure of separation that has produced the failure the integration is supposed to address.

The project's response operates at the level of the meta-theoretical specification of how the traditions are engaged. The colonial-residue critique is correct that the traditions are not separable in the way the framework's articulation might suggest; the traditions have been constituted in relation to and against one another, and the relations are constitutive. The framework's articulation of the traditions as analytically distinct does not deny the relations; it specifies that the analytical contributions of each tradition are not derivable from the contributions of the others, with the non-derivability being the substance of the engagement. The framework operates by holding the relations between the traditions as constitutive while engaging the contributions of each tradition on the tradition's own terms; the holding of the relations is itself the methodological move that the multi-eyed seeing commitment specifies. The colonial-residue critique nonetheless operates as a continuing condition of the framework's accountability: the framework's articulation of the traditions can fail by treating the traditions as separable in ways that do not honor the relations, and the failure pattern is identifiable. The project's commitment is to the corrective work of identifying the failure pattern when it occurs and refusing to proceed under the failed terms.

### The Project's Analytical Answer

The four critiques of the renovation move and the four critiques of the multi-eyed seeing commitment, taken together, specify the analytical conditions under which the project's renovation move is legitimate. The conditions are not aspirational; they are architectural, and they bind the project's continuing operation. The conditions are: the multi-generational rectification commitment with material Land Back as the constitutive condition rather than as a discretionary outcome; the Indigenous co-governance with FPIC standard at constitutional level; the engagement of Indigenous foundational documents on their own constitutional standing rather than as comparative reference for Western architecture; the seventh-generation criterion as constitutional decision rule; the abolitionist commitment to the dissolution of the carceral infrastructure with the construction of community-based alternatives; the structural commitment to reparations on the scale and architecture the Black-radical tradition has specified; the structural commitment to the dismantling of caste hierarchy with the architecture the dalit tradition has specified; the structural commitment to disability-justice transformation of the constitutional architecture's specification of access, interdependence, and the dismantling of ableist hierarchy; the structural commitment to the architectural transformation specified by the neurodivergent tradition's analysis of cognitive and rhetorical plurality; and the multi-eyed seeing methodological commitment treated as substantive rather than ornamental, with the substantive operation specified through the procedural, substantive, and institutional architecture Part Seven developed.

Under these conditions, the renovation move is the project's answer to the renovation-versus-decolonial question: the constitutional architecture's renovation under the conditions specified is the project's specification of how the multi-traditional commitments can be honored substantively in an architecture that the United States constitutional state might adopt. The renovation is not the alternative to the abolitionist or decolonial moves; it is the architectural specification under which the moves can be sustained as constitutional commitments rather than only as oppositional political projects. The architecture's success at the level of structural transformation cannot be evaluated by the architecture's own internal criteria; it must be evaluated by the analytical contributions of the traditions whose authority the architecture recognizes, with the evaluation operating substantively rather than rhetorically.

Without these conditions, the renovation move is not the project's answer. The project specifies, with the seriousness the question deserves, that the conditions are not optional features of the architecture but constitutive features without which the architecture is operating under a different commitment from the one it claims. If the architecture's operation does not include the material rectification commitment, the architecture is reproducing the structural failures the rectification is required to address, and the project must say so. If the architecture's operation does not include the Indigenous co-governance and Land Back commitments, the architecture is reproducing the constitutive operation of settler colonialism, and the project must say so. If the architecture's operation does not include the abolitionist commitment, the architecture is reproducing the constitutive operation of the carceral state, and the project must say so. If the architecture's operation does not include the substantive multi-eyed seeing commitment, the architecture is reproducing the constitutive operation of the dominant tradition's monocular framework, and the project must say so.

The project's answer to the renovation-versus-decolonial question is therefore conditional rather than categorical. The renovation is legitimate under specifiable conditions; the renovation is illegitimate without those conditions; the conditions are architectural rather than aspirational; and the project's authority on the question extends to the specification of the conditions and the project's accountability to them, not to the assertion that the conditions are or are not in fact met by any particular architecture's operation. The accountability for the conditions' actual operation is to the traditions whose authority the architecture recognizes, and the project's authority does not extend to settling the question of whether the conditions are in fact met when the traditions' authoritative voices specify that they are not.

### The Implications for the Project's Continuing Operation

The conditional answer specified above has implications for the project's continuing operation that the present part records before turning to Part Nine. The first implication is that the project's renovation move is itself a continuing project rather than a one-time articulation: the project's commitments must be operationalized in the project's continuing engagement with the traditions whose authority the project recognizes, with the operationalization being subject to correction, modification, and supersession by the traditions' authoritative voices. The project's articulation of the renovation move in this document is one articulation among possible articulations; the articulation is subject to revision as the project's relationships with the traditions develop.

The second implication is that the project's authority on the question of whether the renovation conditions are met is limited. The project does not have the authority to assert that the conditions are met when the traditions' authoritative voices specify that they are not; the project does not have the authority to assert that the conditions can be partially met when the traditions specify that the conditions must be fully met; the project does not have the authority to assert that the conditions can be operationalized through the architecture the project has specified when the traditions specify that the architecture is inadequate to the operation. The project's authority is to specify the conditions and to articulate the architecture under which the project commits to operating; the authority for the conditions' actual operation is the traditions' authority.

The third implication is that the project's continuing operation includes the corrective work of identifying when the renovation move's conditions are not being met and of refusing to proceed under the failed terms. The corrective work cannot be conducted by the project alone; it must include the engagement of the traditions whose authority the project recognizes, with the engagement operating under the protocols of the traditions rather than under the project's protocols. The project's commitment to the corrective work is part of the multi-eyed seeing commitment; the commitment is binding on the project's continuing operation, not only on the project's articulation in this document.

The fourth implication is that the renovation move's success or failure is not the project's to declare. The success or failure is the traditions' authority to declare, with the traditions' declaration operating under the protocols the traditions specify and being authoritative for the project's continuing operation. The project's renovation move is, in this sense, an offer that the traditions are entitled to refuse, modify, or supersede; the project's continuing operation must be conducted under the recognition that the offer's reception is the traditions' authority rather than the project's.

This is the most consequential analytical move the document makes, and the document specifies it with the seriousness the move deserves. ## Part Nine: Application to the Project's Architecture

The methodological commitment the document has specified across Parts One through Eight is consequential for the project's architectural proposals only insofar as it operates as the constitutive method of those proposals' specification, evaluation, and continuing operation. Part Nine accordingly turns to the application of the multi-eyed seeing commitment to the architectural elements the project's prior installments have developed: the seven structural commitments specified in the revised project outline (the unidirectional dual currency, the usable-but-reversible amendment process, judicial review of executive orders, e-democracy as immediate public check, the technological-critique response, the public-trust threshold for private organizations, and publicly funded research returns), the e-democracy structural infrastructure developed at length in Document F, and the multi-generational rectification architecture developed at length in Document H. ### The Unidirectional Dual Currency Under Multi-Eyed Seeing

The unidirectional dual currency, specified in detail in Document E and integrated into the constitutional architecture proposed in Document D, is the project's structural response to the constitutional incompatibility between the Declaration's commitments to life, liberty, and the pursuit of happiness and the operation of a monetary architecture under which accumulated wealth controls the channels of governance, the channels of information, and the conditions of material subsistence. The architecture specifies a public-trust currency, denominated in publicly issued units, whose unidirectional convertibility (publicly issued units may be converted into private currency but not the reverse) prevents the accumulated private wealth from purchasing the public goods the public-trust currency denominates. The Modern Monetary Theory tradition (Wray's *Modern Money Theory* in 2015, Kelton's *The Deficit Myth* in 2020, Mosler's *Soft Currency Economics* in 1995) and the sovereign-money tradition (the Benes-Kumhof IMF Working Paper of 2012, Huber's *Sovereign Money* of 2017) provide the analytical foundation in the heterodox Western monetary tradition.

The multi-eyed seeing commitment operates as the architecture's constitutive method in three respects. The first is the commitment's specification of the architecture's foundational categories. The category of "currency" is not specified by the dominant Western monetary tradition's articulation of currency as a medium of exchange, store of value, and unit of account; the category is specified through cross-traditional engagement that includes the Indigenous gift-economy traditions (the Anishinaabe gift economies, the wampum tradition of the Haudenosaunee Confederacy, the potlatch tradition of the Pacific Northwest peoples), the Black-radical political-economy tradition's analysis of money's relation to racial subordination (Mehrsa Baradaran's *The Color of Money* of 2017 on the structural exclusion of Black communities from the monetary architecture, William Darity Jr. and A. Kirsten Mullen's *From Here to Equality* of 2020 on the monetary architecture's relation to the reparations question), the dalit-economic tradition's analysis of money's relation to caste subordination (Ambedkar's analysis of the monetary architecture's role in maintaining caste hierarchy), and the ecological-economic tradition's analysis of money's relation to biophysical limits (Daly's steady-state economics, Hickel's degrowth analysis, Raworth's doughnut framework). The architecture's specification of "currency" is co-produced through engagement with these traditions; the resulting category is not a Western monetary category supplemented from elsewhere but a multi-traditional category whose specification draws on each tradition's analytical contribution as load-bearing.

The second respect is the commitment's specification of the architecture's evaluative criteria. Whether the dual-currency architecture operates adequately is not a question answered by the dominant Western tradition's criteria of monetary adequacy alone; the question is answered through the simultaneous operation of multiple traditions' evaluative criteria. The Western monetary tradition's criteria — price stability, employment effects, exchange-rate effects, monetary-aggregate effects — operate alongside the Indigenous gift-economy tradition's criteria (does the architecture support the maintenance of right relationships across human and more-than-human relata, does it operate under reciprocity rather than under accumulation), the Black-radical tradition's criteria (does the architecture operate as part of the rectification of historical economic subordination or does it reproduce the structural exclusions of the existing architecture), the dalit tradition's criteria (does the architecture's operation support the dismantling of caste-economic hierarchy or does it reproduce it), the disability-justice tradition's criteria (does the architecture support access to the conditions of material subsistence for disabled communities or does it reproduce the structural exclusions of the existing architecture), and the ecological-economic tradition's criteria (does the architecture operate within the planetary boundaries Rockström and colleagues have specified, does it support the steady-state or degrowth transitions Daly and Hickel have specified). The architecture's evaluative adequacy is the joint product of the multiple traditions' criteria operating together rather than the product of the Western tradition's criteria operating alone.

The third respect is the commitment's specification of the architecture's continuing modification. The architecture's operation is subject to correction, modification, and supersession by the analytical authority of the traditions whose authority the architecture recognizes; the correction operates through the institutional architecture Part Seven specified, with the Indigenous Constitutional Council, the equivalent councils of the other traditions, and the Inter-Traditional Methodological Council operating with binding authority over the architecture's continuing operation. The Indigenous gift-economy tradition's analytical authority extends to the question of whether the architecture's unidirectional convertibility is the right structural response or whether the gift-economy alternatives the tradition has developed across millennia provide the more adequate architecture; the Black-radical tradition's analytical authority extends to the question of whether the architecture operates as part of the reparations architecture or whether the architecture's operation must be modified to support the reparations commitment; the dalit tradition's analytical authority extends to the question of whether the architecture supports the dismantling of caste-economic hierarchy or must be modified to support that dismantling. The architecture's continuing operation is accountable to the traditions; the multi-eyed seeing commitment is the methodological specification of how that accountability operates.

### The Usable-but-Reversible Amendment Process Under Multi-Eyed Seeing

The usable-but-reversible amendment process, specified in detail as Structural Commitment Two of the revised project outline, is the project's response to the structural failure of Article V's supermajority entrenchment under the conditions of the contemporary crisis. The architecture specifies asymmetric thresholds for adoption versus entrenchment, with adoption operating under simple-majority-plus-confirmatory-referendum thresholds and entrenchment operating under supermajority-plus-confirmatory-referendum-plus-Citizens' Assembly thresholds; the architecture additionally specifies sunset provisions for amendments adopted under the lower thresholds, with the sunset period operating as an automatic re-evaluation rather than as automatic repeal.

The multi-eyed seeing commitment operates as the architecture's constitutive method in respects parallel to those specified for the dual-currency architecture. The category of "amendment" is specified through cross-traditional engagement: the Anglo-American constitutional tradition's specification of amendment as formal alteration of the constitutional text operates alongside the Haudenosaunee tradition's specification of constitutional adaptation through the Mohawk-Oneida-Onondaga-Cayuga-Seneca-Tuscarora consensus process, the Anishinaabe tradition's specification of constitutional adaptation through the Mide council deliberations, the Maori tradition's specification of constitutional change through the tikanga Maori-grounded processes the Matike Mai Aotearoa report (Margaret Mutu and Moana Jackson, chairs, in 2016) developed, and the African Ubuntu tradition's specification of constitutional change through the relational-ontological processes the *Makwanyane* judgment articulated. The architecture's specification of "amendment" is the joint product of these traditions' specifications, with the Anglo-American articulation being one specification among the multiple specifications the architecture's category integrates.

The architecture's evaluative criteria operate similarly: whether the amendment process is adequate to the constitutional architecture's commitments is evaluated through the simultaneous operation of multiple traditions' criteria, with the Anglo-American tradition's criteria (procedural regularity, democratic legitimacy, constitutional stability) operating alongside the Indigenous traditions' criteria (consensus across the relevant nations, maintenance of right relationship across the constitutional commitments, conformity with the seventh-generation criterion), the Black-radical tradition's criteria (the amendment process's relation to the rectification of historical subordination, the amendment process's accountability to the traditions of Black political organizing and Black-radical constitutionalism), and the disability-justice tradition's criteria (the amendment process's accessibility, the amendment process's accommodation of the cognitive and rhetorical pluralities the neurodivergent and disabled communities specify).

The architecture's continuing modification is subject to the traditions' analytical authority: the question of whether the asymmetric-threshold architecture is the right structural response or whether the consensus-process alternatives the Indigenous traditions have developed across millennia provide the more adequate architecture is one the Indigenous Constitutional Council operates with binding authority to address; the question of whether the sunset-provision architecture supports or undermines the constitutional commitments the Black-radical tradition has specified is one the Black-radical Constitutional Council operates with binding authority to address.

### Judicial Review of Executive Orders Under Multi-Eyed Seeing

The judicial review of executive orders, specified as Structural Commitment Three of the revised project outline, is the project's response to the structural failure of the existing constitutional architecture's response to the unitary-executive theory's expansion of executive authority. The architecture specifies a constitutional standard of review for executive orders, a defined timeline for the review's operation, and a specification of the institutional bodies operating the review. The architecture's response is constitutional rather than statutory: the unitary-executive theory's expansion has operated through statutory and judicial doctrine that is itself amendable through the existing architecture's mechanisms, and the project's specification operates at the constitutional level to prevent the statutory-and-doctrinal pathway from being the architecture's continuing vulnerability.

The multi-eyed seeing commitment specifies that the architecture's category of "executive authority" is not the Anglo-American category alone but a multi-traditional category whose specification draws on the Indigenous traditions' specifications of leadership authority (the Haudenosaunee tradition's specification of leadership authority as bounded by the Confederacy's collective deliberation, the Anishinaabe tradition's specification of leadership authority as bounded by the Mide council's deliberation, the Maori tradition's specification of rangatiratanga as bounded by the tikanga Maori-grounded relations the leadership authority operates within), the Black-radical tradition's specification of leadership authority as bounded by accountability to the constituency the leadership operates on behalf of (Ella Baker's articulation of group-centered leadership as documented in Barbara Ransby's biography of 2003, the Mississippi Freedom Democratic Party's specification of leadership accountability), and the disability-justice tradition's specification of leadership authority as bounded by the "nothing about us without us" principle and the access-and-interdependence commitments the tradition has articulated.

The architecture's evaluative criteria operate through the simultaneous operation of multiple traditions' criteria: whether the judicial-review architecture operates adequately is evaluated through the Anglo-American tradition's criteria (procedural regularity, separation of powers, judicial competence) operating alongside the Indigenous traditions' criteria (the consonance between executive authority and the consensus traditions of the relevant Indigenous nations, the operation of FPIC where the executive orders affect Indigenous nations), the Black-radical tradition's criteria (the executive orders' relation to the rectification of historical subordination, the executive orders' accountability to the traditions of Black political organizing), and the disability-justice tradition's criteria (the executive orders' accessibility and the executive orders' accommodation of the disabled communities the orders affect).

### E-Democracy as Immediate Public Check Under Multi-Eyed Seeing

The e-democracy structural commitment, specified in detail in Document F, is the project's response to the structural failure of representative democracy alone in the contemporary information environment. The architecture specifies direct legislative referendum on legislation crossing a public-petition threshold, recall provisions for legislators on specific votes, public-veto provisions on procedural manipulation, real-time public-deliberation infrastructure, and sortition-based oversight commissions with binding authority on specific question categories.

The multi-eyed seeing commitment is particularly consequential for the e-democracy architecture because the architecture's foundational categories — the category of "the public," the category of "deliberation," the category of "participation" — are categories whose meanings are contested across traditions and whose meanings the architecture must specify through cross-traditional engagement. The category of "the public" is specified through engagement with the Western deliberative-democratic tradition's articulation (Habermas's *Between Facts and Norms* of 1996, Fishkin's *When the People Speak* of 2009, Landemore's *Open Democracy* of 2020) but also through engagement with the Indigenous traditions' articulation of the relevant relational community (the Haudenosaunee tradition's articulation of the relevant community as the Confederacy's constituent nations operating in consensus, the Anishinaabe tradition's articulation of the relevant community as including more-than-human relations, the Maori tradition's articulation of the relevant community as iwi-and-hapu operating under tikanga Maori), the Black-radical tradition's articulation (Ella Baker's tradition of group-centered participation, the Combahee River Collective Statement of 1977 on the constitutive role of intersectional analysis in specifying the relevant public), and the disability-justice tradition's articulation (the structural commitment to including disabled and neurodivergent participants as load-bearing rather than as accommodation cases).

The category of "deliberation" is specified through similar cross-traditional engagement, with the Western tradition's articulation operating alongside the Indigenous traditions' articulation (the Haudenosaunee Confederacy's deliberative protocols, the Anishinaabe Mide council's deliberative protocols, the African palaver tradition's deliberative protocols), the Black-radical tradition's articulation (the participatory traditions documented in Charles Payne's *I've Got the Light of Freedom* of 1995 on the Mississippi Freedom Democratic Party's parallel-democracy architecture, the participatory-budgeting tradition originating in Porto Alegre and now widespread), and the neurodivergent tradition's articulation (the rhetorical-plurality articulation Yergeau developed in *Authoring Autism* of 2018, the double-empathy framework Milton developed in 2012). The architecture's deliberative protocols are accordingly multi-modal: the architecture supports written, oral, embodied, and ceremonial deliberative modes; the architecture supports synchronous and asynchronous deliberation; the architecture supports the temporal structures the different traditions specify, with the rapid temporal structures of contemporary digital deliberation operating alongside the slower temporal structures the consensus traditions require.

The category of "participation" is similarly specified, with the architecture's specification of who counts as a participant operating through cross-traditional engagement that recognizes the more-than-human participants the Indigenous traditions specify (the rivers, mountains, forests, and other entities recognized as legal persons under the Whanganui Te Awa Tupua Act of 2017, the Ecuadorian Constitution's Rights of Nature provisions of 2008, the Bolivian Law of Mother Earth of 2010, and the Atrato River decision of 2016), the future generations the seventh-generation criterion specifies, and the disabled and neurodivergent participants the disability-justice and neurodivergent traditions specify as load-bearing constitutional subjects rather than as accommodation cases. The architecture's specification of "participation" is accordingly broader than the Western liberal tradition's specification; the broader specification is not an extension of the Western specification but a multi-traditional specification whose Indigenous, Black-radical, disability-justice, and ecological dimensions are constitutive rather than additive.

### The Technological-Critique Response Under Multi-Eyed Seeing

The technological-critique response, specified as Structural Commitment Five of the revised project outline, is the project's structural answer to the family of critiques specifying that the e-democracy architecture cannot adequately operate under the conditions of pervasive digital infrastructure, surveillance capitalism, platform-architecture manipulation, biometric-identification regimes, and the broader political economy of contemporary digital technology. The architecture specifies five paired failure-mode-and-response architectures: paper-ballot fallback as constitutional default and constitutional prohibition of internet-based vote tabulation, addressing the election-security-and-verification-audit failure mode (drawing on the National Academies' *Securing the Vote* of 2018 and the technical critique of J. Alex Halderman, Andrew Appel, and Matt Blaze); constitutional guarantee of multi-modal participation infrastructure with constitutional prohibition of digital-only participation requirements, addressing the digital-divide-and-accessibility failure mode (drawing on Jan van Dijk's *The Digital Divide* of 2020 and the Disability Visibility Project's body of work); integration with the public-trust threshold of Structural Commitment Six with constitutional protection of algorithmic transparency, addressing the platform-architecture-manipulation failure mode (drawing on the post-2016 disinformation literature and the Cambridge Analytica record); in-person deliberation requirements and coercion-resistance properties, addressing the voter-coercion and secret-ballot failure mode (drawing on the cryptographic literature on receipt-freeness and the technical critique of Estonian e-voting); and zero-knowledge-proof identity systems with anonymous-credential authentication as architectural alternatives to surveillance-prone designs, addressing the authentication-identity-and-surveillance failure mode (drawing on Bruce Schneier's published work and the W3C verifiable-credentials infrastructure).

The multi-eyed seeing commitment is constitutive of the architecture in respects that distinguish it from the technocratic specifications the failure-mode literature on its own would generate. The category of "the technological" is specified through cross-traditional engagement rather than through the dominant Western technical tradition's specification alone. The Indigenous-data-sovereignty tradition's articulation operates as primary alongside the Western technical articulation: the CARE Principles for Indigenous Data Governance specified by Stephanie Russo Carroll and colleagues in 2020 specify governance protocols for Indigenous knowledge and data that operate under collective rather than individual authority, with the Maori Data Sovereignty Network and the United States Indigenous Data Sovereignty Network developing the protocols at scale. The Black-radical tradition's articulation operates as primary alongside the Western technical articulation: Ruha Benjamin's *Race After Technology* of 2019, Simone Browne's *Dark Matters* of 2015, Safiya Umoja Noble's *Algorithms of Oppression* of 2018, and Charlton McIlwain's *Black Software* of 2019 specify the constitutive relations between contemporary digital infrastructure and racial subordination, with the relations operating as load-bearing rather than as supplementary considerations. The dalit-data tradition's articulation, developed in the empirical record of Aadhaar exclusion that Reetika Khera and Jean Drèze have documented, operates as primary alongside the Western technical articulation: the Aadhaar architecture's operation, even where its technical specifications met Western data-protection criteria, has produced documented exclusion of dalit and other marginalized communities from the social-protection infrastructure the architecture mediates, with the exclusion being not incidental to the architecture's operation but constitutive of its functioning under conditions of caste hierarchy. The disability-rights tradition's articulation operates as primary alongside the Western technical articulation: the disability-rights critique of biometric authentication, of access barriers in digital-only participation infrastructure, and of the broader assumption that "users" can be specified independent of the embodied conditions under which use operates is load-bearing rather than supplementary.

The architecture's evaluative criteria are accordingly the joint product of these traditions' specifications. Whether the technological-critique response operates adequately is not a question the Western privacy-engineering tradition (Helen Nissenbaum's *Privacy in Context* of 2010, Mireille Hildebrandt's *Smart Technologies and the End(s) of Law* of 2015, the Carissa Véliz body of work) answers alone; the question is answered through the simultaneous operation of these criteria alongside the Indigenous-data-sovereignty criteria, the Black-radical-surveillance-critique criteria, the dalit-data-justice criteria, and the disability-rights criteria. The architecture's continuing modification operates under the analytical authority of these traditions: the question of whether the zero-knowledge-proof and anonymous-credential architectures adequately address the surveillance failure mode is one the Indigenous Data Sovereignty Network operates with binding authority to address where the architecture's operation affects Indigenous communities; the question of whether the algorithmic-transparency provisions adequately address the platform-manipulation failure mode is one the Black-radical surveillance-critique tradition operates with binding authority to address where the architecture's operation affects communities the tradition has specified as load-bearing; the question of whether the multi-modal-participation guarantees adequately address the accessibility failure mode is one the disability-rights tradition operates with binding authority to address.

### The Multi-Generational Rectification Architecture Under Multi-Eyed Seeing

The multi-generational rectification architecture, specified in detail in Document H, is the project's response to the constitutional question of how the historical injustices the constitutional architecture's prior operation has produced are to be rectified within the architecture's continuing operation. The architecture specifies the institutional and procedural infrastructure for Indigenous land restoration and Land Back, reparations to the descendants of enslaved Africans, constitutional acknowledgment and apology, truth-and-reconciliation institutional architecture, and the operationalization of the seventh-generation criterion as a constitutional decision rule.

The multi-eyed seeing commitment is constitutive of the rectification architecture in a particularly direct way: the architecture's primary analytical authorities are the traditions whose communities have been the subjects of the historical injustices the architecture is designed to rectify. The Indigenous land restoration architecture operates under the analytical authority of the Indigenous nations whose lands are at issue; the architecture's specification of "land restoration" is not the Anglo-American property-law category but a multi-traditional category whose specification draws on the Indigenous nations' own articulations (the Yellowhead Institute's *Land Back* red paper of Hayden King and Shiri Pasternak in 2019, the LANDBACK Manifesto, the Indigenous-led scholarship of Glen Coulthard's *Red Skin, White Masks* of 2014, Audra Simpson's *Mohawk Interruptus* of 2014, Leanne Betasamosake Simpson's *As We Have Always Done* of 2017, and Nick Estes's *Our History Is the Future* of 2019). The reparations architecture operates under the analytical authority of the Black-radical tradition's articulation of reparations (William Darity Jr. and A. Kirsten Mullen's *From Here to Equality* of 2020, Olúfẹ́mi Táíwò's *Reconsidering Reparations* of 2022, the California Reparations Task Force final report of 2023, the broader scholarship Mehrsa Baradaran's *The Color of Money* of 2017 develops); the architecture's specification of "reparations" is the joint product of the tradition's articulations rather than an Anglo-American articulation supplemented from elsewhere.

The seventh-generation criterion is operationalized as a constitutional decision rule under the analytical authority of the Indigenous traditions whose articulation of the criterion is primary. The criterion is articulated by Oren Lyons (Onondaga), Daniel Wildcat (Yuchi/Muscogee), and Kyle Whyte (Citizen Potawatomi) among others; the criterion's operation as a constitutional decision rule is specified through engagement with the Indigenous traditions' articulations of how the criterion operates within Indigenous decision-making protocols. The criterion is not an Anglo-American category supplemented from Indigenous traditions; the criterion is an Indigenous category whose constitutional specification operates under the Indigenous traditions' authority, with the Anglo-American articulation operating as commentary rather than as primary.

The truth-and-reconciliation institutional architecture is specified through engagement with the comparative experience the South African TRC final report of 1998 to 2003 documents, the Truth and Reconciliation Commission of Canada's final report of 2015 documents, the broader comparative scholarship Priscilla Hayner's *Unspeakable Truths* of 2011 and Martha Minow's *Between Vengeance and Forgiveness* of 1998 develop, and the analytical critiques the Indigenous and Black-radical traditions have developed of the comparative experience (Mahmood Mamdani's *Neither Settler nor Native* of 2020 on the limits of the South African experience under continuing settler-colonial structure, the Indigenous-led critiques of the Canadian TRC that emphasize the gap between the Commission's recommendations and the Canadian state's implementation). The architecture's specification of "truth and reconciliation" is the joint product of these engagements; the architecture's operation is accountable to the traditions whose communities are the subjects of the rectification.

### The Public-Trust Threshold for Private Organizations Under Multi-Eyed Seeing

The public-trust threshold for private organizations, specified as Structural Commitment Six of the revised project outline, is the project's response to the structural failure of the existing constitutional architecture's treatment of the relation between private organizational power and public-trust obligations. The architecture specifies that when a private organization crosses a defined threshold — by market share in markets affecting substantial public interests, by network-effect dominance, by public-function provision, or by systemic risk — it enters the public trust and is subject to public-trust obligations including transparency, non-discrimination, public-purpose accountability, and democratic oversight through the e-democracy mechanisms specified in Structural Commitment Four. The Brandeisian "curse of bigness" tradition (Tim Wu's *The Curse of Bigness* of 2018, Lina Khan's "Amazon's Antitrust Paradox" of 2017, Sabeel Rahman's *Democracy Against Domination* of 2017) and the public-trust doctrinal tradition (the Joseph L. Sax scholarship, Mary Christina Wood's *Nature's Trust* of 2014 on atmospheric trust litigation) provide the analytical foundation in the dominant Western legal tradition.

The multi-eyed seeing commitment operates as the architecture's constitutive method in respects parallel to those specified for the prior commitments. The category of "the public trust" is specified through cross-traditional engagement rather than through the Anglo-American common-law tradition's specification alone. The Indigenous traditions' articulation of stewardship and right-relationship obligations operates as primary alongside the Western legal articulation: the Haudenosaunee tradition's specification of the council's obligations to the seventh generation, the Anishinaabe tradition's specification of the obligations of the human community to the more-than-human community, the Maori tradition's specification of kaitiakitanga as the stewardship obligation tikanga Maori specifies, and the Andean tradition's articulation of the public-trust obligations corresponding to sumak kawsay (buen vivir) and the Pachamama provisions of the Ecuadorian Constitution and the Bolivian Law of Mother Earth. The Black-radical tradition's articulation operates as primary alongside the Western legal articulation: the public-trust obligations of the corporate structures whose operation has been constitutive of racial subordination are specified by the tradition's analytical engagement with the relation between corporate power and racial dispossession (Mehrsa Baradaran's *The Color of Money*, Cedric Robinson's *Black Marxism*, Cheryl Harris's "Whiteness as Property"). The dalit tradition's articulation of the public-trust obligations of corporate structures whose operation has been constitutive of caste hierarchy operates as primary alongside the Western legal articulation, drawing on Ambedkar's analysis of the relations between corporate power and caste subordination and the contemporary anti-caste scholarship that has developed those relations.

The architecture's evaluative criteria operate through the simultaneous operation of these traditions' criteria. Whether a particular threshold-crossing organization is operating under adequate public-trust obligations is evaluated through the Western legal tradition's criteria (procedural regularity, doctrinal consistency, enforceability) operating alongside the Indigenous traditions' criteria (the maintenance of right relationship across the human and more-than-human communities the organization's operation affects, the conformity with the seventh-generation criterion), the Black-radical tradition's criteria (the relation between the organization's operation and the rectification of historical subordination), and the dalit tradition's criteria (the relation between the organization's operation and the dismantling of caste hierarchy). The architecture's continuing modification is subject to the analytical authority of the traditions: the question of whether the threshold criteria adequately specify which organizations enter the public trust is one the multiple traditions operate with binding authority to address, with the question's resolution being the joint product of the cross-traditional engagement.

### The Publicly Funded Research Returns Architecture Under Multi-Eyed Seeing

The publicly funded research returns architecture, specified as Structural Commitment Seven of the revised project outline, is the project's response to the structural failure of the existing constitutional architecture's treatment of the relation between publicly funded research and the private appropriation of the resulting intellectual and material assets. The architecture specifies that the products of publicly funded research — pharmaceuticals developed through National Institutes of Health funding, technologies developed through Defense Advanced Research Projects Agency funding, the foundational digital infrastructure developed through publicly funded research at the National Science Foundation and the public university system — are subject to public-return obligations rather than to unconditional private appropriation through the patent and intellectual-property architecture. The Mariana Mazzucato tradition (*The Entrepreneurial State* of 2013, *The Value of Everything* of 2018) and the broader heterodox economic tradition on public-versus-private value creation provide the analytical foundation in the dominant Western tradition.

The multi-eyed seeing commitment operates as the architecture's constitutive method in respects that diverge consequentially from the dominant Western tradition's specification alone. The category of "research returns" is specified through cross-traditional engagement that includes the Indigenous traditions' articulation of knowledge as relational rather than proprietary. The CARE Principles for Indigenous Data Governance of Carroll and colleagues in 2020 specify the governance architecture for Indigenous knowledge as operating under collective rather than individual authority, with the relational specification of knowledge being non-derivable from the Western intellectual-property tradition's specification of knowledge as alienable individual property. The Indigenous research methodology tradition Linda Tuhiwai Smith, Shawn Wilson, and Margaret Kovach have developed specifies the relational architecture under which research operates within Indigenous communities, with the relational architecture's specification of who has standing to receive research returns and who has standing to evaluate them being constitutive rather than supplementary to the architecture's operation. The Black-radical tradition's articulation of the relations between research and the rectification of historical subordination — including the analytical engagement with the historical record of medical research on Black communities (the Tuskegee record, the Henrietta Lacks record, the broader record Harriet Washington's *Medical Apartheid* of 2007 documents) — operates as primary alongside the Western articulation. The disability-justice tradition's articulation of the "nothing about us without us" principle as a structural commitment operates as primary alongside the Western articulation: research on disabled communities operates under the load-bearing participation of those communities rather than under supplementary consultation. The neurodivergent tradition's articulation of similar load-bearing participation in research on neurodivergent communities operates as primary alongside the Western articulation.

The architecture's evaluative criteria and continuing modification operate under the analytical authority of these traditions. Whether the publicly funded research returns architecture is operating adequately is a question evaluated through the simultaneous operation of multiple traditions' criteria: the Western criteria (efficient allocation of public funding, appropriate balance between public investment and private incentive, procedural regularity in returns assessment) operating alongside the Indigenous criteria (the conformity of returns architecture with the relational specification of knowledge, the conformity with Indigenous data-sovereignty governance), the Black-radical criteria (the architecture's relation to the rectification of historical research-subordination), and the disability-justice and neurodivergent criteria (the architecture's accommodation of load-bearing participation). The continuing modification of the architecture is subject to the traditions' authority, with the architecture's specification being the joint product of the cross-traditional engagement rather than the Western specification operating alone.
