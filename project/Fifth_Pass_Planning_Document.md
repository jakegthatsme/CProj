# Fifth-Pass Planning Document — Biocultural Governance, Eco-Mimetic Policy-Making, Disturbance-Adapted Implementation

## Opening Note

The fifth pass replaces the fourth-pass standing-context framing with a load-bearing **triad**: biocultural governance as constitutional ontology, eco-mimetic policy-making as operational method, and disturbance-adapted implementation as temporal orientation. The colloquial summary phrase "grown, not built" survives for use in chapter openings and synthesis passages where its rhetorical economy is useful; the load-bearing terminology in the constitutional-ontology sense is the triad. The first three documents of the fourth-pass arc — Document 1 (*The Founding Texts and the 1787 Implementation*), Document 2 (*Federalist–Anti-Federalist Records Read Against the Founding Texts*), Document 3 (*Founders' Hindsight*) — are preserved with targeted edits propagating the new framing. All other documents are re-specified. The number and order of documents change. The Roman-numeraled architecture remains at fourteen Parts, but Part II expands from three documents to eight to carry the five disturbance-regime diagnostics plus the interactions document, and a new Document R is added to Part XII to carry the eco-mimetic policy-making method.

The first element of the triad — **biocultural governance** — names the constitutional ontology. The polity is a complex adaptive system cultivated across nested spatial and temporal scales as a biocultural formation: biological substrate and cultural plurality coupled, neither reducible to the other. The biocultural-diversity literature (Maffi's *On Biocultural Diversity* of 2001; the Berkes biocultural-knowledge corpus; Darrell Posey's *Cultural and Spiritual Values of Biodiversity* of 1999) and the biocultural-rights literature (Kabir Bavikatte's *Stewarding the Earth* of 2014; Daniel Robinson on biocultural rights and the Convention on Biological Diversity) provide scholarly grounding in a tradition that already treats biological and cultural diversity as integrated rather than parallel. The Indigenous and Eastern traditions are the principle's primary articulators; the Western complex-adaptive-systems, ecology, biomimicry, and disturbance-ecology canons are recent and partial recovery. Kimmerer's *Braiding Sweetgrass* of 2013 on the honorable harvest, Yunkaporta's *Sand Talk* of 2019 on tending rather than building, Wildcat's *Red Alert!* of 2009 on indigenization, the Haudenosaunee Great Law's cultivation logic, Laozi's *Daodejing* on *wu wei* and *ziran*, Nagarjuna's *Mulamadhyamakakarika* on dependent origination, the Andean ayllu and ayni architecture, the southern African Ubuntu tradition, the Pacific Islander Talanoa and Vā traditions — these are primary articulations; Holling on panarchy, Ostrom on polycentricity, Benyus on biomimicry, Christopher Alexander on the nature of order, Mang and Reed on regenerative development, Pickett and White on disturbance ecology, Folke and Walker on resilience extend the principle into vocabularies the dominant tradition can read.

The second element of the triad — **eco-mimetic policy-making** — names the operational method. Scientific concepts from ecology, complex-adaptive-systems theory, biomimicry, and disturbance ecology inspire the architecture by which laws are passed, written, and tested. The legislative cycle is structured to mimic adaptive-cycle dynamics in the Holling sense: a growth phase in which a proposal is developed and built out, a conservation phase in which the proposal is deliberated and amended, a release phase in which the proposal is voted on and enacted, and a reorganization phase in which the enacted law's implementation is reviewed and the law is sunset, adapted, or re-enacted. Legal drafting specifies, per provision, the scale at which the right operates (the biological-referent scale), the scope of the right (the substantive terrain), the feedback condition (how the right's operation is sensed and corrected), and the sunset-or-adaptation condition (how the right is reviewed). Keystone-species concepts inform the drafting of foundational legal rights; edge-effect concepts inform the drafting of boundary-crossing legal rights; succession-dynamics concepts inform the drafting of transitional legal rights; panarchy-cycle concepts inform the drafting of legal architectures spanning multiple scales. Constitutional and statutory review mimics ecological monitoring: ongoing sensing at multiple scales rather than periodic judicial review at one scale. The fifth pass devotes Document R in Part XII to the substantive elaboration of eco-mimetic policy-making.

The third element of the triad — **disturbance-adapted implementation** — names the temporal orientation. The architecture is cultivated across intergenerational temporal scales to absorb, metabolize, and recover from a plural disturbance landscape. The project's term for the foundational temporal orientation is *intergenerational*; the seventh-generation criterion of the Haudenosaunee Great Law is quoted where the Haudenosaunee tradition is engaged directly and is otherwise referenced as the foundational primary articulation of intergenerational thinking that the project's intergenerational framing extends. Five disturbance regimes are named as load-bearing: the **cultural** regime, which the five-axis diagnostic (caste, oligarchy, media, racism, embodiment) specifies; the **biological** regime, which integrates pandemic dynamics, biodiversity loss, food-web collapse, soil-system breakdown, vector-borne disease shift, antibiotic resistance, and agricultural-pest dynamics; the **physical-environmental** regime, which integrates climate, hydrological regime shift, fire regime shift, geological events, anthropogenic chemical contamination, and atmospheric composition shift; the **cognitive-and-informational** regime, which integrates disinformation cascades, attention-capture architectures, AI-driven epistemic disruption, cognitive-load shocks, and generational cognitive-substrate shifts; and the **political-economic** regime, which integrates currency-and-monetary shocks, supply-chain collapse, trade-war dynamics, debt-crisis cascades, technological labor displacement at scale, and capital-concentration dynamics. The five regimes do not operate in isolation: they compound, amplify, and modulate each other, and the architecture's resilience properties depend on how its constitutional commitments handle the interactions. The compound-disturbance literature (Paine et al., "Compounded Perturbations Yield Ecological Surprises," 1998; Buma on disturbance interactions and synergies; Turner and Dale on landscape disturbance interactions; Folke on resilience under compound disturbance) is integrated as analytical foundation.

What is preserved from the fourth pass: Documents 1, 2, 3 (with targeted edits); the multi-traditional canon at co-equal weight; the twelve structural commitments as substantive content (re-read as bounded decision rights and cultivation conditions at specified scales); the multi-lens checks-and-balances commitment (re-read as multi-scale feedback across the biological-referent levels and the disturbance regimes); the multi-eyed seeing methodological commitment; the intergenerational criterion (under the new term, with the seventh-generation criterion as primary articulation); the bibliography v4 as foundation for v5; the phase-based implementation discipline; the move-don't-regenerate principle for relocations. The fourth-pass question-resolution answers remain authoritative on substantive commitments; they are re-read where they specify designed-architecture framings the fifth pass replaces with cultivation framings.

What shifts: Documents 4 through 10 and A through L are re-specified rather than preserved. The Roman-numeraled architecture maintains fourteen Parts but expands Part II from three documents to eight (with the five disturbance-regime diagnostics, the interactions document, plus Documents 4 and C), and adds Document R to Part XII for eco-mimetic policy-making. The total document count rises to thirty-seven across the fourteen Parts. Document numbering changes: new documents added include M (biocultural-governance ontology, Part III), N, O, P, Q (the material-substrate documents, Part VI), R (eco-mimetic policy-making, Part XII), S (biological-disturbance diagnostic, Part II), T (physical-environmental-disturbance diagnostic, Part II), U (cognitive-and-informational-disturbance diagnostic, Part II), V (political-economic-disturbance diagnostic, Part II), W (disturbance-regime interactions, Part II), and D-Synthesis (cultivation-protocol synthesis, Part XIII). Implementation order shifts to accommodate the expanded Part II and the new dependencies. The renovation-versus-decolonial question is re-engaged under the cultivation-rather-than-design-renovation framing: cultivation honors the multi-traditional canon by maintaining conditions under which traditions continue to grow alongside one another, which sits differently in relation to the decolonial critique of synthesis-as-appropriation than design-renovation did.

The remainder of this planning document specifies, in order: the fourteen-Part Roman-numeraled architecture (Section I); the thirty-seven-document arc (Section II); the cross-cutting threads (Section III); the gap analysis (Section IV); the implementation order (Section V); the per-document execution-plan deliverable list (Section VI); the bibliography v5 additions (Section VII). Per-document execution plans are separate deliverables produced under the `execution-plan-writing` skill.

# I. ROMAN-NUMERALED ARCHITECTURE

## Part I — Foundational Texts and the 1787 Implementation

Part I establishes the foundational normative texts of all canonical traditions against which the 1787 implementation and its subsequent adjustments are measured. The Anglo-American foundational texts — the Declaration of Independence (1776), the Virginia Declaration of Rights (1776), the United States Constitution and amendments — are read alongside the Indigenous foundational texts (the Kaianerekowa, the Cherokee Constitution of 1827, the Choctaw Doaksville Constitution of 1838, the Matike Mai Aotearoa report, the Uluru Statement from the Heart, the Sami parliamentary architecture), the African foundational texts (the Kurukan Fuga Charter, the Constitution of Medina), the international human-rights instruments (the Universal Declaration, the ICCPR, the ICESCR, UNDRIP), and the Reconstruction and Black-radical foundational texts (the Reconstruction Amendments read through the constitutional theory of Frederick Douglass, Charles Sumner, Thaddeus Stevens, and the post-Reconstruction constitutional critique developed by W. E. B. Du Bois in *Black Reconstruction in America* of 1935 and by Eric Foner). Part I is the foundational normative substrate of the entire arc. The 1787 implementation is one partial structural translation of a multi-traditional normative inheritance; the project's renovation move is the cultivation of an implementation more adequate to the foundational texts than the 1787 implementation was.

Documents 1, 2, 3, and A constitute Part I. Documents 1, 2, 3 are preserved with targeted edits propagating the biocultural-governance framing into the founding-era analysis (the agrarian-republican strand in Jefferson read as proto-articulation of biocultural-governance thinking, the Madisonian institutional architecture re-read as proto-articulation of scale-matching and feedback-structure, the Anti-Federalist scale-and-locality critique re-read as proto-articulation of disturbance-adapted scale-matching). Document A is re-specified to integrate the foundational texts of all canonical traditions at the level of constitutional standing.

The Part articulates the principle, demonstrates its analytical superiority to the design-imposition framing the founders worked under, specifies the nested-scales reference frame at the level of constitutional category, and prepares the ground for Parts IV through XIII.

# III. CROSS-CUTTING THREADS

The fifth-pass cross-cutting threads are nine. Each runs through every Part and every document at the depth its substantive bearing requires.

The first thread is **biocultural governance** as constitutional ontology. The thread is load-bearing for the entire arc, articulated in Document M (Part III), and grounded in the biocultural-diversity literature (Maffi, Berkes biocultural-knowledge corpus, Posey) and the biocultural-rights literature (Bavikatte, Robinson, the Indigenous and Local Communities biocultural-heritage literature). The thread runs through every Part because every Part is conducted under the load-bearing ontology. In Part I, the foundational normative texts are read as cultivation specifications rather than as design specifications; in Part II, the disturbance-regime diagnostics specify how the biocultural formation is threatened, captured, or eroded across the five disturbance regimes; in Part IV, the deep substrate is read as cultivation substrate; in Part V, the CAS, emergence, and disturbance-ecology canon is read as recent partial recovery of the cultivation framing the Indigenous and Eastern traditions have carried; in Part VI, the material substrate is read as cultivation terrain; in Part VII, the multi-eyed seeing commitment is read as methodological condition for biocultural cultivation; in Part VIII, the cognitive substrate is read as cultivation substrate at organism-and-organ-system scales; in Part IX, climate is read as paradigmatic stress test of the biocultural polity; in Part X, transformative legislation is read as panarchy-cycle cultivation work; in Part XI, operational layers are read as cultivation infrastructure; in Part XII, structural commitments are read as cultivation conditions and bounded decision rights at specified scales, and eco-mimetic policy-making (Document R) is read as the operational method by which cultivation proceeds; in Part XIII, the constitutional architecture is read as cultivation protocol; in Part XIV, the capstone synthesis reads the arc as a single cultivation-protocol specification.

The second thread is **eco-mimetic policy-making** as operational method. The thread is articulated in Document R (Part XII), with anticipations in Document M (Part III, which provides ontological grounding) and Document K (Part V, which provides systems-science substrate). The thread bears on every Part by specifying how legislative process, legal drafting, and constitutional review are to proceed under the biocultural-governance ontology. In Part II, the disturbance-regime diagnostics specify the empirical conditions eco-mimetic policy-making must respond to; in Part VI, the material-substrate documents specify the substantive policy terrain eco-mimetic method must address; in Parts VIII through XI, the operational-and-political-economic documents specify the implementation conditions eco-mimetic method must work within; in Part XII, the structural-commitment documents specify the bounded decision rights eco-mimetic method instantiates; in Part XIII, eco-mimetic method is integrated into the cultivation-protocol synthesis.

The third thread is **disturbance-adapted implementation** as temporal orientation. The thread is articulated through Part II's disturbance-regime diagnostics and Document W's interactions analysis, with anticipations in Document M (Part III) and operationalization in Document H (Part XII) and Document D-Synthesis (Part XIII). The thread bears on every Part by specifying the disturbance landscape the architecture must absorb. In Part I, the foundational texts are read as articulating normative commitments that the disturbance regimes threaten; in Part IV, the deep-substrate documents specify the evolutionary backdrop against which contemporary disturbance regimes operate; in Part V, the disturbance-ecology canon provides analytical foundation; in Part VI, the material-substrate documents specify cultivation conditions disturbance-adapted implementation must maintain; in Part IX, climate as paradigmatic stress test exemplifies the disturbance orientation; in Parts X and XI, the political-economic and operational documents specify the implementation conditions disturbance-adapted cultivation must work within; in Part XII, the intergenerational document (H) specifies the temporal commitment, and the eco-mimetic-policy-making document (R) specifies the operational method by which disturbance adaptation proceeds; in Part XIII, the cultivation protocol integrates the disturbance-adapted orientation across the architecture.

The fourth thread is **nested-scale biomimicry** as spatial-and-temporal reference frame. The thread is articulated in Document M (Part III) and operationalized in Document K (Part V). The nested scales — biosphere, ecozone, ecosystem, habitat, population, organism, organ system, with their temporal analogs — are reference frames for scale-matching. The thread bears on every Part where decision rights are specified or evaluated.

The fifth thread is **intergenerational thinking and long-term temporal orientation**. The thread is preserved from the fourth pass (where it was called "time and long-term thinking") and integrates the Haudenosaunee seventh-generation criterion as foundational primary articulation; the Andean cyclical time of Pachakuti; the Hopi temporal cosmology through Malotki's *Hopi Time* of 1983; Aboriginal Dreamtime as ontological time; the Greek distinction of *kairos* versus *chronos* versus *aion*; Augustine's *Confessions* Book XI; Bergson's *Duration and Simultaneity*; Heidegger's *Being and Time*; Ricoeur's *Time and Narrative*; Buddhist *anicca* and Nagarjunian critique of temporal substantiality; Daoist temporal flow and *ziran*; Brand on the Long Now; Amara, Dator, Inayatullah on futures studies; Edith Brown Weiss on intergenerational equity; Stephen Gardiner on the ethics of climate-and-time; Rob Nixon on slow violence; John McPhee on deep time; Roy Scranton on the Anthropocene. Under the fifth-pass framing, time is read as the temporal dimension of the nested-scales reference frame.

The sixth thread is **systems thinking and cybernetics**. The thread is preserved from the fourth pass and integrates the Western systems-thinking canon (von Bertalanffy, Forrester, Meadows, Senge, Capra, Sweeney, Sterman, Donella Meadows on leverage points), the Indigenous systems-thinking tradition (Kimmerer, Cajete's *Native Science* of 2000, Yunkaporta, Wildcat, Whyte), the Eastern systems-thinking tradition (the I Ching's combinatorics, Daoist relational ontology, Buddhist dependent origination, Madhyamaka two-truths), and the cybernetic critique (Wiener, Ashby, Beer's viable system model, Pickering's *The Cybernetic Brain* of 2010). Under the fifth-pass framing, systems thinking is read as the operational discipline of cultivation: sensing, feedback, intervention, adaptation as the cybernetic primitives that eco-mimetic policy-making operationalizes.

The seventh thread is **multi-eyed seeing**. The thread is articulated in Document I (Part VII) as the project's methodological commitment.

The eighth thread is **multi-lens checks and balances**. The thread is articulated in Document D-Synthesis (Part XIII) as substantive architectural commitment, with anticipations in Documents E, F, H, J, D-1 through D-4, and R (Part XII). Under the fifth-pass framing, checks and balances are re-read as multi-scale feedback across the biological-referent levels and across the disturbance regimes.

The ninth thread is the **multi-traditional canon at co-equal weight**. The thread is preserved from the fourth pass. Western, Indigenous, Eastern, Black-radical, dalit, disability-justice, neurodivergent, feminist, Latinx, Asian-American, decolonial, climate-justice, Islamic, Liberation-Theology, Pacific Islander, Caribbean, African, Russian/Eastern European, and Jewish traditions are co-equal canonical contributors. Under the fifth-pass framing, the commitment is strengthened: the Indigenous and Eastern traditions are explicitly named as primary articulators of biocultural governance; the Western recovery canon is read as recent partial recovery; the other traditions are read as articulating cultivation under their specific historical conditions.

# IV. GAP ANALYSIS

The fourth-pass-to-fifth-pass gap analysis identifies what the fourth pass treated under design-imposition framing that the fifth pass re-specifies under cultivation framing, plus the substantive shifts the fifth-pass framing introduces.

The first gap is the **load-bearing-ontology document**. The fourth pass had no Part-level home for the constitutional-ontology commitment. The fifth pass fills the gap with Part III and Document M.

The second gap is the **disturbance-regimes architecture**. The fourth pass treated Document B as the structural diagnostic without specifying that the diagnostic concerns one disturbance regime (the cultural) among several the architecture must absorb. The fifth pass expands Part II to eight documents, with the cultural-disturbance diagnostic alongside biological, physical-environmental, cognitive-and-informational, and political-economic diagnostics, plus an interactions document.

The third gap is the **material substrate as constitutional terrain**. The fourth pass treated the material substrate sporadically. The fifth pass fills the gap with Part VI's four new documents (N, O, P, Q).

The fourth gap is the **eco-mimetic-policy-making method**. The fourth pass had biomimicry as a cross-cutting thread without specifying how laws are actually passed, written, and tested under the biomimicry commitment. The fifth pass fills the gap with Document R in Part XII.

The fifth gap is the **structural commitments as bounded decision rights at specified scales**. The fourth pass specified twelve structural commitments as substantive content without re-reading them as bounded decision rights. The fifth pass fills the gap by re-specifying the commitments across Part XII's nine documents.

The sixth gap is the **cross-scale-governance and disturbance-ecology literatures**. The fourth pass had Ostrom's polycentricity but did not engage Cash et al., Lebel et al., Berkes and Folke, the Resilience Alliance, Pickett and White, Turner, Buma, Paine, or Folke at the level the fifth-pass nested-scales reference frame and disturbance-regimes architecture require. The fifth pass fills the gap by integrating both literatures into Document K (Part V) explicitly.

The seventh gap is the **constitutional-architecture-as-cultivation-protocol framing**. The fourth pass had Document D as designed-architecture proposal. The fifth pass re-frames the architecture as cultivation protocol and relocates the synthesis to Part XIII through Document D-Synthesis.

The eighth gap is the **biocultural-diversity literature**. The fourth pass did not engage the biocultural-diversity and biocultural-rights literatures (Maffi, Berkes biocultural-knowledge corpus, Posey, Bavikatte, Robinson) as scholarly grounding. The fifth pass integrates the literature as foundational for Document M and the entire load-bearing triad.

The ninth gap is the **renovation-versus-decolonial question under cultivation framing**. The fourth pass delivered an analytical answer under design-renovation framing. The fifth pass re-engages the question under cultivation framing and delivers a cleaner analytical answer.

The tenth gap is the **temporal-scales dimension of the nested-scales reference frame**. The fourth pass had the time-and-long-term-thinking thread but did not specify temporal scales as a dimension of the nested-scales reference frame. The fifth pass fills the gap.

The eleventh gap is the **terminological shift from "multi-generational" to "intergenerational"**. The fourth pass used "multi-generational" as the project's term. The fifth pass uses "intergenerational" as the academic-philosophy term aligned with Edith Brown Weiss's *In Fairness to Future Generations* of 1989, with the Haudenosaunee seventh-generation criterion as primary articulation quoted where the Haudenosaunee tradition is engaged directly. Document H is renamed accordingly.
