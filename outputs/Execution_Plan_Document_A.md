## 2. Load-Bearing Triad

The fifth-pass project rests on a load-bearing triad: **biocultural governance** as constitutional ontology (the polity is a complex adaptive system cultivated across nested spatial and temporal scales as a biocultural formation, with biological substrate and cultural plurality coupled, grounded in the biocultural-diversity and biocultural-rights literatures); **eco-mimetic policy-making** as operational method (ecology, CAS theory, biomimicry, and disturbance-ecology concepts inspire how laws are passed, written, and tested); **disturbance-adapted implementation** as temporal orientation (intergenerational architecture cultivated to absorb a plural disturbance landscape comprising five named regimes — cultural, biological, physical-environmental, cognitive-and-informational, political-economic — and their interactions). The Indigenous and Eastern traditions are the principle's primary articulators; the Western complex-adaptive-systems, ecology, biomimicry, and disturbance-ecology canons are recent and partial recovery. "Grown, not built" is usable as colloquial summary phrase.

For Document A, the triad bears on what kind of inheritance the foundational normative texts constitute: the texts are read not as parallel design specifications competing for synthesis but as primary articulations, each in its own register, of the cultivation conditions under which a biocultural polity grows. The 1787 implementation is then re-read as one partial structural translation of a multi-traditional cultivation-condition inheritance, and the project's earlier "renovation" move is re-specified as cultivation: the maintenance of conditions under which the canonical traditions continue to grow alongside one another, rather than the engineering of a single revised architectural fixture that subsumes them.

## 4. Substantive Task

The re-specification preserves the analytical spine of the existing document — the Declaration of Independence and the Virginia Declaration of Rights as Anglo-American foundational normative texts of the colonial founding period, the founding-period reception, the immanent-critique tradition through Lincoln, Douglass, the Seneca Falls women, and the long line of those who have held the founding to its own commitments — and re-specifies the document at two load-bearing points. First, the document integrates the foundational texts of the canonical traditions the fifth-pass project carries at co-equal weight, each named in its own primary articulations rather than absorbed into an Anglo-American frame: alongside the Anglo-American substrate, the Indigenous foundational texts already present in the source are extended and refined, and the African, Eastern, Islamic, Latin American and Andean, Caribbean, Pacific Islander, Jewish, and dalit foundational-texts traditions are added at the same constitutional standing as the Anglo-American Declaration and Virginia Declaration. Second, the document re-frames the project's renovation move as cultivation: where the existing source treats the gap between the foundational commitments and the 1787 implementation as ground for a renovation of the implementation, the re-specified document re-reads the gap as failure of cultivation conditions and scale-matching, and re-specifies the project's positive move not as the engineering of a revised fixture but as the cultivation of an implementation more adequate to the foundational normative inheritance than the 1787 implementation was. The cultivation re-frame is the document's central analytical move; it bears on how each tradition's foundational texts are read (as primary articulations of cultivation conditions rather than as competing design proposals), on how the relations among traditions are specified (as co-cultivation under conditions of mutual integrity rather than as synthesis-into-architecture), and on how the gap between inheritance and implementation is diagnosed (as cultivation failure rather than design defect).

The existing source is the substantive substrate for the Anglo-American material (Parts One through Four and Part Eight of the source), for the Indigenous foundational-texts material (Part Five), for the Reconstruction-and-Black-radical foundational-texts material (Part Six), and for the international-human-rights material (Part Seven). The move-don't-regenerate principle governs: existing substantive analysis is preserved and extended rather than rewritten. The additions integrate the African, Eastern, Islamic, Latin American and Andean, Caribbean, Pacific Islander, Jewish, and dalit traditions as substantive new analytical material at the standing the existing source accords to the Indigenous and Reconstruction traditions.

The document operates under the multi-eyed-seeing methodological commitment already articulated in the existing source's Opening (the extension of Albert and Murdena Marshall's Two-Eyed Seeing through Cheryl Bartlett's 2012 articulation), and the re-specification refines that articulation under the cultivation framing: multi-eyed seeing is read not as a synthesis device but as the methodological discipline by which the cultivation conditions of multiple traditions are held in view at once without collapsing them into one architectural register.
