## 2. Load-Bearing Triad

The fifth-pass project rests on a load-bearing triad: **biocultural governance** as constitutional ontology (the polity is a complex adaptive system cultivated across nested spatial and temporal scales as a biocultural formation, with biological substrate and cultural plurality coupled, grounded in the biocultural-diversity and biocultural-rights literatures); **eco-mimetic policy-making** as operational method (ecology, CAS theory, biomimicry, and disturbance-ecology concepts inspire how laws are passed, written, and tested); **disturbance-adapted implementation** as temporal orientation (intergenerational architecture cultivated to absorb a plural disturbance landscape comprising five named regimes — cultural, biological, physical-environmental, cognitive-and-informational, political-economic — and their interactions). The Indigenous and Eastern traditions are the principle's primary articulators; the Western complex-adaptive-systems, ecology, biomimicry, and disturbance-ecology canons are recent and partial recovery. "Grown, not built" is usable as colloquial summary phrase.

For Document D-Synthesis, the triad is the integrative subject matter of the document: D-Synthesis articulates the constitutional architecture as cultivation protocol drawing the entire arc together. The architecture is not a designed institutional fixture; it is a protocol for cultivating a biocultural polity adequate to the nested-scales reference frame, the multi-traditional canonical commitment, the disturbance-regime landscape, and the intergenerational time-horizon. The protocol specifies: foundational normative texts as governing criterion (drawing on Part I and Document A); Indigenous co-governance and FPIC as constitutional commitment (drawing on Parts I and VII); intergenerational rectification as cultivation work (drawing on Part XII through Document H); multi-lens checks and balances as multi-scale feedback (drawing on Parts V and XII); CAS-emergence-disturbance-ecology-informed adaptive governance (drawing on Part V Document K); eco-mimetic policy-making as constitutional method (drawing on Part XII Document R); material-substrate cultivation work as constitutional terrain (drawing on Part VI Documents N, O, P, Q); the twelve structural commitments as bounded decision rights at specified scales (drawing on Part XII Documents D-1 through D-4); the multi-traditional canon at co-equal weight as constitutional method (drawing on Part VII Document I); the intergenerational criterion as decision rule (drawing on Part XII Document H); and the disturbance-adapted implementation orientation (drawing on Part II's disturbance-regime diagnostics through Documents B, S, T, U, V, W, C, 4 and Document W's interactions analysis).
