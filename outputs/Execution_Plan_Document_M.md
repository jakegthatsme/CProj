## 2. Load-Bearing Triad

The fifth-pass project rests on a load-bearing triad: **biocultural governance** as constitutional ontology (the polity is a complex adaptive system cultivated across nested spatial and temporal scales as a biocultural formation, with biological substrate and cultural plurality coupled, grounded in the biocultural-diversity and biocultural-rights literatures); **eco-mimetic policy-making** as operational method (ecology, CAS theory, biomimicry, and disturbance-ecology concepts inspire how laws are passed, written, and tested); **disturbance-adapted implementation** as temporal orientation (intergenerational architecture cultivated to absorb a plural disturbance landscape comprising five named regimes — cultural, biological, physical-environmental, cognitive-and-informational, political-economic — and their interactions). The Indigenous and Eastern traditions are the principle's primary articulators; the Western complex-adaptive-systems, ecology, biomimicry, and disturbance-ecology canons are recent and partial recovery. "Grown, not built" is colloquial summary.

For Document M, the triad is the document's substantive subject matter rather than a framing applied to other substantive content. Document M articulates biocultural governance as constitutional ontology at substantive scholarly analytical depth; it operationalizes the first element of the triad and provides the ontological grounding the other two elements presuppose. The disturbance regimes are referenced as the landscape the constitutional ontology is cultivated to absorb but are not diagnosed here (that work is Part II). The primary-articulator traditions whose work the document carries at primary-articulator standing — not as comparative supplement to a Western frame — are the Haudenosaunee Great Law tradition; the broader Indigenous-ecological tradition (Kimmerer, Yunkaporta, Wildcat, Whyte); the Daoist relational ontology; the Buddhist dependent-origination tradition; the African Ubuntu tradition; the Andean ayllu and ayni traditions; the Pacific Islander Talanoa and Vā traditions; the Maori Whakapapa tradition; and Black-radical ecological thought.

## 4. Substantive Task

Document M articulates biocultural governance as the fifth-pass project's constitutional ontology at substantive scholarly analytical depth. The polity is specified as a complex adaptive system cultivated across nested spatial and temporal scales as a biocultural formation, with biological substrate and cultural plurality coupled. The document's analytical work has six phases. First, it grounds the ontology in the biocultural-diversity and biocultural-rights literatures (Maffi, the Berkes biocultural-knowledge corpus, Posey, Bavikatte, Robinson, the Indigenous and Local Communities biocultural-heritage corpus through the ICCA Consortium and the IIFB) as scholarly foundation. Second, it engages the Indigenous and Eastern primary articulators at primary-source depth in their own analytical registers — not as comparative supplement, not as data points for a Western theoretical apparatus, not synthesized into a single ontological vocabulary. Third, it engages the Western complex-adaptive-systems, ecology, biomimicry, and disturbance-ecology canon as recent and partial recovery of framings the Indigenous and Eastern traditions have carried — Holling on panarchy, Ostrom on polycentricity, Benyus on biomimicry, Alexander on the nature of order, Mang and Reed on regenerative development, Pickett and White on disturbance ecology, Folke and Walker on resilience, Todd on living machines, Berry on agrarian cultivation. Fourth, it specifies the analytical case for biocultural governance as constitutional ontology — the failure modes of design-imposition framings; the analytical limits of social-contract reasoning at scale; the necessity of feedback and cultivation conditions in any system whose components are themselves living and adaptive. Fifth, it specifies the nested-scales reference frame (biosphere, ecozone, ecosystem, habitat, population, organism, organ system, with the temporal analogs) as constitutional category, with examples of decision rights appropriately matched to each scale. Sixth, it engages the renovation-versus-decolonial question under the cultivation-rather-than-design-renovation framing as substantive analytical move — cultivation honors the multi-traditional canon by maintaining conditions under which traditions continue to grow alongside one another, which sits differently in relation to the decolonial critique of synthesis-as-appropriation than design-renovation did.

Document M assumes the foundational-texts substrate of Documents 1–3 v5 (the multi-traditional normative inheritance), takes Part II's disturbance-regime diagnostics as the landscape the ontology cultivates the polity to absorb, and provides the ontological grounding that Documents K (Part V systems-science substrate), L (Part IV deep substrate), N–Q (Part VI material substrate), 7 (Part VIII cognitive substrate), R (Part XII eco-mimetic policy-making method), and D-1 through D-4 (Part XII bounded decision rights) all presuppose and operationalize at their specific terrains. Document M does not diagnose the disturbance regimes (Part II); does not specify the material substrate (Part VI); does not specify the operational policy-making method (Document R); does not specify bounded decision rights at scale (Documents D-1 through D-4); and does not integrate the architecture as cultivation protocol (Document D-Synthesis). Document M's analytical task is the ontology itself, the nested-scales reference frame as constitutional category, and the case against design-imposition framings that the rest of the arc operationalizes.

## 5. Required Threads

### Thread A — Biocultural-diversity and biocultural-rights literatures as scholarly grounding

The biocultural-diversity literature (Luisa Maffi, ed., *On Biocultural Diversity: Linking Language, Knowledge, and the Environment*, 2001; Fikret Berkes, *Sacred Ecology*, 1999, and the broader biocultural-knowledge corpus; Darrell Posey, ed., *Cultural and Spiritual Values of Biodiversity*, 1999) supplies the scholarly grounding that biological substrate and cultural plurality are coupled at constitutive depth rather than at the level of correlation. The biocultural-rights literature (Kabir Bavikatte, *Stewarding the Earth: Rethinking Property and the Emergence of Biocultural Rights*, 2014; Daniel Robinson and Kabir Bavikatte on biocultural rights and the Nagoya Protocol; the ICCA Consortium and IIFB documentation of Indigenous and local-community biocultural heritage) supplies the legal-and-normative framework under which the coupling is recognized at the level of rights and stewardship. Document M engages this literature as foundational rather than as recent secondary supplement; the biocultural framing is what the ontology is, not a metaphor applied to it. The thread specifies the analytical move from biocultural diversity (descriptive) and biocultural heritage (legal) to biocultural governance (constitutional), naming the analytical moves the literature does and does not directly authorize and grounding the constitutional extension in the prior scholarly work.

### Thread B — The Haudenosaunee Great Law tradition at primary-articulator standing

The Haudenosaunee Great Law (Kaianerekowa) tradition is engaged through Oren Lyons (Onondaga), John Mohawk (Seneca; *Utopian Legacies*, 2000), Doug George-Kanentiio (Mohawk; *Iroquois On Fire*, 2008), and the broader Haudenosaunee scholarly and oral-tradition corpus, as primary articulator of biocultural governance at constitutional ontology. The thread specifies the substantive elements the tradition articulates that biocultural governance carries forward at constitutional scale: the seven-generations principle as temporal-scale specification; the Three Sisters and clan-mother architecture as scale-matched decision-rights specification; the wampum-belt record as cultivation-condition documentation; the Great Tree and the Council Fire as cultivation-protocol metaphors with substantive analytical content. The engagement is conducted in the tradition's own analytical register; the Western recovery canon is positioned downstream of the Haudenosaunee articulation, not the other way around. The thread engages the question of how non-Haudenosaunee scholarly engagement with the tradition is conducted with the methodological discipline Document I (Part VII) specifies.

### Thread C — The broader Indigenous-ecological tradition at primary-articulator standing

The thread engages Robin Wall Kimmerer (Citizen Potawatomi; *Braiding Sweetgrass*, 2013; *Gathering Moss*, 2003) on plants as primary articulators and on the grammar of animacy; Tyson Yunkaporta (Apalech; *Sand Talk*, 2019) on Indigenous patterns of mind and the eight nodes of cognition as cultivation framework; Daniel Wildcat (Yuchi/Muscogee; *Red Alert!*, 2009) on Indigenous Knowledge of Place; Kyle Whyte (Citizen Potawatomi) on Indigenous environmental justice, kinship time, and climate colonialism — as primary articulators of biocultural governance in vocabularies the Western canon is now recovering partially. The thread treats each scholar's analytical apparatus in its own register: Kimmerer's reciprocity and gift-economy framing as substantive constitutional content rather than as ethical supplement; Yunkaporta's pattern-thinking and yarn-circles as cognitive-architectural specification; Wildcat's place-thought as ontological commitment; Whyte's kinship-time as temporal-scale specification distinct from linear futurity. The thread specifies how each tradition's articulation relates to and exceeds what the Western recovery canon has so far recovered.

### Thread D — Daoist relational ontology at primary-articulator standing

The Daoist tradition (*Daodejing* and *Zhuangzi* as foundational texts; the *wu wei* and *ziran* concepts; engaged through Roger Ames and David Hall's *Daodejing: Making This Life Significant*, 2003, and *Dao De Jing: A Philosophical Translation*, 2003; Ames and Hall's *Thinking from the Han: Self, Truth, and Transcendence in Chinese and Western Culture*, 1998; Brook Ziporyn's *Zhuangzi: The Complete Writings*, 2020; the Daoist environmental-philosophy literature through James Miller, *China's Green Religion*, 2017) is engaged as primary articulator of cultivation-not-design ontology. *Wu wei* (non-coercive action) and *ziran* (so-of-itself, spontaneous emergence) are engaged at the level of constitutional-method specification: the polity as field-condition cultivated through non-coercive scale-matched intervention rather than as designed institution imposed through coercive structural specification. The thread distinguishes the Daoist articulation from the Western laissez-faire reading it is sometimes flattened into and specifies the substantive constitutional commitments the Daoist ontology authorizes that laissez-faire framings do not.

### Thread E — Buddhist dependent-origination tradition at primary-articulator standing

The Buddhist dependent-origination tradition (*pratītyasamutpāda*) is engaged through Nāgārjuna's *Mūlamadhyamakakārikā* (engaged through Jay Garfield's *Fundamental Wisdom of the Middle Way*, 1995, and Mark Siderits and Shōryū Katsura's *Nāgārjuna's Middle Way*, 2013) and the broader Madhyamaka tradition; through Thich Nhat Hanh on interbeing (*The Heart of Understanding*, 1988); through Joanna Macy on dependent co-arising and systems theory (*Mutual Causality in Buddhism and General Systems Theory*, 1991); and through David Loy on Buddhist social theory (*The Great Awakening*, 2003). The thread specifies the substantive constitutional content the dependent-origination ontology authorizes: the polity as relational rather than substantive; the individual subject of rights as constituted through relations rather than as ontologically prior to them; the analytical case against the Lockean possessive-individualist framing that the founders worked under. The engagement preserves the tradition's analytical sophistication rather than reducing it to a relational-thinking tag.

### Thread F — African Ubuntu tradition at primary-articulator standing

The Ubuntu tradition is engaged through Mogobe Ramose's *African Philosophy through Ubuntu* (1999, 2002); Drucilla Cornell's *Ubuntu and the Law: African Ideals and Postapartheid Jurisprudence* (2011, with Karin van Marle); the South African Constitutional Court's invocation of ubuntu in *S v Makwanyane* (1995); and the broader Ubuntu philosophy literature through Augustine Shutte, *Ubuntu: An Ethic for a New South Africa* (2001), Thaddeus Metz on Ubuntu as moral theory (*A Relational Moral Theory*, 2022). The thread engages the constitutional-jurisprudential reception of Ubuntu in postapartheid South Africa as case study for biocultural governance translation into constitutional terms — what the *Makwanyane* invocation accomplishes and what it does not, what the broader jurisprudential incorporation has done with the tradition, and what the philosophical articulation supplies that the jurisprudential reception sometimes flattens. The thread distinguishes Ubuntu's relational ontology from the dependent-origination and Daoist relational ontologies engaged in Threads D and E, naming what each tradition articulates that the others do not.

### Thread G — Andean ayllu and ayni traditions at primary-articulator standing

The Andean ayllu (relational kin-territorial unit) and ayni (reciprocal labor and exchange) traditions are engaged through Josef Estermann's *Andean Philosophy* (1998, English 2006); Eduardo Gudynas on *buen vivir* and Rights of Nature (*Derechos de la Naturaleza*, 2014); Marisol de la Cadena's *Earth Beings: Ecologies of Practice across Andean Worlds* (2015); the Ecuadorian and Bolivian constitutional incorporation of *Pacha Mama* and *sumak kawsay*/*suma qamaña*; and the broader buen-vivir literature through Alberto Acosta, *Buen Vivir: Vivir bien* (2013), and David Cortez's syntheses. The thread engages the Andean traditions' constitutional-incorporation case studies — what the Ecuadorian Constitution of 2008 and the Bolivian Plurinational Constitution of 2009 accomplish in giving Pacha Mama and *sumak kawsay* constitutional standing, what the implementation record shows, what the analytical apparatus authorizes that the implementation has only partially realized. The thread reads the Andean constitutional experiments as the closest existing approximations to biocultural-governance constitutional implementation and engages their record honestly.

### Thread H — Pacific Islander Talanoa and Vā, and Maori Whakapapa, traditions at primary-articulator standing

The Pacific Islander Talanoa (Fijian, Tongan, Samoan dialogue practice) and Vā (Samoan relational space) traditions are engaged through Sitiveni Halapua on Talanoa as governance practice; Albert Wendt and Konai Helu Thaman on Vā as relational ontology; the Pacific Islander climate-governance literature through Vinaka and the Pacific Climate Warriors corpus. The Maori Whakapapa tradition (genealogical-relational mapping of the polity through kin, land, and cosmos) is engaged through Mason Durie's *Te Mana Te Kāwanatanga: The Politics of Māori Self-Determination* (1998); Margaret Mutu (Ngāti Kahu/Ngāti Whātua/Te Rarawa) and Moana Jackson (Ngāti Kahungunu/Ngāti Porou) on *Matike Mai Aotearoa: The Report of Matike Mai Aotearoa* (2016) as primary constitutional-transformation document; the Whanganui River legal-personhood case (*Te Awa Tupua (Whanganui River Claims Settlement) Act 2017*) as case study; the broader kaupapa Māori research tradition through Linda Tuhiwai Smith's *Decolonizing Methodologies* (1999, 2nd ed. 2012). The thread engages the Pacific traditions' contributions to biocultural governance that exceed what the Indigenous-North-American and Eastern traditions articulate — particularly the oceanic-relational ontology and the genealogical-time framing — and reads *Matike Mai* and the Whanganui case as the closest existing approximations to biocultural-governance constitutional implementation in the Pacific.

### Thread K — Analytical case for biocultural governance as constitutional ontology

The thread specifies the analytical case for biocultural governance at the level of constitutional ontology — not at the level of policy preference, normative aspiration, or empirical hypothesis. Three analytical moves carry the case. First, the failure modes of design-imposition framings in constitutional theory are specified: the imposed institutional architecture's inability to track the polity's adaptive substrate; the cumulative drift between specified institution and actual political-economic-cognitive formation; the path-dependence of design choices made under assumptions the polity then outgrows or undermines. Second, the analytical limits of social-contract reasoning at scale are specified: the cognitive-and-deliberative impossibility of contract-formation across the population scales modern polities operate at; the cognitive-bias amplification effects in large-population deliberative settings; the systematic exclusions social-contract reasoning relies on (children, future generations, non-human kin, the cognitively disabled, the disenfranchised); the historical record of social-contract foundings as foundings of dispossession rather than as foundings of consent. Third, the necessity of feedback and cultivation conditions in any system whose components are themselves living and adaptive is specified: the CAS literature on adaptive systems' dependence on feedback; the ecological literature on cultivation conditions for community succession; the developmental-biology and developmental-systems literature on emergence of structure under conditions rather than under blueprint. The thread reads each of these three moves as analytical foundation for the constitutional-ontology commitment, not as policy critique.

### Thread L — The nested-scales reference frame as constitutional category

The thread specifies the nested-scales reference frame at the level of constitutional category. The spatial scales (biosphere, ecozone, ecosystem, habitat, population, organism, organ system) and the temporal analogs (geological time, ecological-succession time, generational time, lifetime, decade, year, decision-cycle time) are engaged as the reference frame against which decision rights are to be matched. The thread specifies examples of decision rights appropriately matched to each scale — what biosphere-scale decision rights look like; what ecozone-scale decision rights look like; what ecosystem-scale, habitat-scale, population-scale, organism-scale, and organ-system-scale decision rights look like, with the temporal analogs at each. The thread engages the cross-scale-governance literature (David Cash et al., "Scale and Cross-Scale Dynamics," 2006; Louis Lebel et al., "The Politics of Scale, Position, and Place," 2005; Fikret Berkes and Carl Folke, *Linking Social and Ecological Systems*, 1998; the Resilience Alliance publications; Vincent and Elinor Ostrom on polycentricity at scale) as analytical foundation for the scale-matching specification. The thread distinguishes scale-matching from scale-reduction (federalism's compromise) and from scale-blindness (the unitary state's overreach), naming what biocultural governance authorizes at the level of scale-matched decision rights that other constitutional ontologies do not.

### Thread M — Renovation-versus-decolonial under cultivation-rather-than-design-renovation framing

The thread engages the renovation-versus-decolonial question as substantive analytical move under the cultivation-rather-than-design-renovation framing. The question is whether the project's analytical move — proposing biocultural governance as the polity's constitutional ontology, drawing on Indigenous and Eastern traditions at primary-articulator standing — escapes the decolonial critique of synthesis-as-appropriation. The thread engages the decolonial canon (Walter Mignolo's *The Darker Side of Western Modernity*, 2011; Linda Tuhiwai Smith's *Decolonizing Methodologies*, 1999; Aníbal Quijano on coloniality of power; the broader decolonial-thought literature through Catherine Walsh, Boaventura de Sousa Santos, the Modernity/Coloniality Working Group) substantively rather than gesturally. The thread argues that cultivation honors the multi-traditional canon by maintaining conditions under which traditions continue to grow alongside one another, which sits differently in relation to the decolonial critique than design-renovation did — design-renovation proposes a new architecture synthesized from multiple traditions, which the decolonial critique reads as appropriation; cultivation proposes maintaining conditions under which each tradition continues to articulate and elaborate within its own analytical apparatus, with the constitutional polity as field-condition cultivated to support the plurality. The thread engages the strongest version of the decolonial critique honestly, including the critique that even cultivation-as-conditions can serve as cover for synthesis-as-appropriation, and names what the project does and does not authorize itself to claim under that critique. The thread is engaged in conversation with Document I (Part VII, multi-eyed-seeing methodological discipline) but is its own substantive analytical work, not a deferral to Document I.

## 10. What to Avoid

The following document-specific anti-patterns are the principal risks for Document M and must be guarded against by the drafting session at every stage.

**Synthesis-into-architecture across the primary-articulator traditions.** The strongest risk for Document M is collapsing the Haudenosaunee, broader Indigenous-ecological, Daoist, Buddhist, Ubuntu, Andean, Pacific Islander, Maori, and Black-radical ecological articulations into a single biocultural-governance ontological vocabulary — turning the document into an architectural synthesis that absorbs the traditions into one Western-readable conceptual frame. The document must preserve each tradition's analytical register and apparatus, name what each articulates that the others do not, and treat biocultural governance as constitutional category cultivated under the conditions the traditions' plurality specifies, not as synthesized ontological vocabulary. Thread M is the document's substantive engagement of this risk; the rest of the document must execute Thread M's analytical commitment in its actual treatment of Threads B through I, not merely cite the commitment.

**Primary-articulator absorption into Western recovery vocabulary.** The risk is describing the primary-articulator traditions in panarchy, polycentricity, resilience, regenerative-design, or biomimicry vocabulary — Kimmerer's reciprocity recast as "ecosystem services framing," the Haudenosaunee seven-generations principle recast as "long-term policy planning," Ubuntu recast as "communitarian ethics," dependent-origination recast as "systems thinking," ayni recast as "reciprocal exchange." Each primary-articulator tradition must be engaged in its own analytical register, with the tradition's vocabulary preserved and its analytical apparatus engaged. The Western recovery canon (Thread J) is engaged after the primary articulators, not as the frame they are described within.

**Design-imposition slip-back at the level of the analytical case.** Document M's Thread K argues against design-imposition framings of constitutional theory. The document must not, in articulating the alternative, slip back into design-imposition vocabulary at the level of how biocultural governance is specified — proposing "the biocultural governance institutional design" or "the biocultural-governance constitutional template" would be Document M re-importing the framing it argues against. The ontology is cultivation-condition-specification, not designed institutional template; the language must match the analytical commitment throughout.

**Duplication of Document I's methodological work and Document K's systems-science work.** Document M is in conversation with Document I on multi-eyed-seeing methodology (Thread M) and with Document K on CAS and disturbance ecology (Threads J, K, L). The document must cite Documents I and K where their work is substrate rather than re-doing the work. Thread M engages decolonial critique substantively but does so as M's ontological-commitment work, not as duplication of Document I's methodological specification. Thread L engages cross-scale governance literature at the level of constitutional category, not at the level of CAS-dynamics specification that is Document K's terrain.

**Gestural treatment of the three apex threads (K, L, M).** Threads B through I (primary articulators) and Thread J (Western recovery) are at high risk of receiving the document's depth at the expense of Threads K (analytical case), L (nested-scales reference frame), and M (renovation-versus-decolonial). **Reading Andean and Maori constitutional incorporation as solved cases.** Threads G and H engage Ecuador (2008), Bolivia (2009), and *Te Awa Tupua* (2017) as constitutional-incorporation case studies. The risk is treating these as solved cases — the implementation records are uneven, and the analytical apparatus the traditions authorize exceeds what the implementations have realized.
